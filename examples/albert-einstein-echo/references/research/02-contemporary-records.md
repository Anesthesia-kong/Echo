# Albert Einstein — Contemporary Records

*Agent 2 research dossier. Eyewitness accounts of Einstein the person from colleagues, family, debate-partners, and the state. Every source flagged for bias.*

**Methodology note**: Composed from main-agent training knowledge. Citations as `[ref: <work>]`. Bias flags are explicit for every source.

---

## 1. Max Born (1882–1970) — lifelong friend, Göttingen quantum-mechanics architect

**Bias flag**: Warm, loyal; but also Einstein's principal QM-interpretation antagonist (Born's probability interpretation of the wave function is what Einstein rejected).

**Key source**: *The Born-Einstein Letters* (1916–1955), translated by Irene Born (Walker & Company, 1971; expanded edition with Born and Bertha Born correspondence, Macmillan, 2005). ~100+ letters across 40 years. [documented]

**Preserved content**:
- The "dice" correspondence of 1926 onwards — Einstein's famous inner-voice letter of 4 December 1926. [documented]
- Born's recollection of Einstein's extraordinary reasoning speed and intuitive leaps.
- The 1947 "spooky action at a distance" letter (3 March 1947) where Einstein rejects the non-local correlations of QM: "I cannot seriously believe in it because the theory cannot be reconciled with the idea that physics should represent a reality in time and space, free from spooky actions at a distance." [documented]
- Born's 1954 letter to Einstein, and Einstein's "inner voice" letters generally, as the fullest exchange on Einstein's epistemology.
- Personal tenderness: Einstein wrote movingly to Born on Born's emigration difficulties, on deaths of mutual friends.

**Born on Einstein's character**: "Einstein was a very human being, combining a deep, almost childlike, kindness with the sharpest critical faculty I ever met." [attested via Born's editorial notes and memoir]

## 2. Niels Bohr (1885–1962) — debate partner, Copenhagen interpretation architect

**Bias flag**: Deep mutual respect; fundamental philosophical disagreement across 30+ years.

**Key primary material**: Bohr's "Discussion with Einstein on Epistemological Problems in Atomic Physics," contribution to the Schilpp volume (*Albert Einstein: Philosopher-Scientist*, 1949). Bohr's retrospective account of the debates is the single most cited primary source on the Einstein-Bohr exchange. [documented]

**Preserved content**:
- The 1927 Solvay debate narrative (Bohr's construction, but widely attested): Einstein proposing thought experiments at breakfast, Bohr resolving them by dinner, cycle repeating for days.
- The 1930 Solvay photon-box thought experiment and Bohr's counter using Einstein's own gravitational-redshift result from GR (Bohr turning Einstein's general-relativity against Einstein's quantum-incompleteness critique).
- Bohr's response to EPR (1935), published in *Physical Review* 48; same title as the EPR paper.
- Bohr visiting Einstein at Princeton; photograph by Paul Ehrenfest of the 1925 Leiden visit.

**Bohr on Einstein**: "He was a man of great insight and imagination... He belongs as much to the history of philosophy as to the history of physics." [attested; Bohr Nachlass]

---

## 3. Max Planck (1858–1947) — mentor, patron, Wilhelmine-era German physics establishment

**Bias flag**: Planck was Einstein's earliest senior supporter; recommended Einstein for the Berlin Academy position (1914). Deeply fond.

**Preserved content**:
- Planck's 1905 immediate recognition of the significance of special relativity; Planck directs his Berlin assistant Max von Laue to visit Einstein in Bern.
- Planck's 1913 manifesto recommending Einstein for Berlin, co-signed by Nernst, Rubens, Warburg: "Einstein is the Copernicus of the 20th century."
- The 1914 war manifesto rupture — Planck signed the "Manifesto of the 93" (pro-German-war statement by German intellectuals); Einstein signed the opposing "Manifesto to the Europeans." The rupture was temporary; Planck later regretted his signature.
- Planck's own family tragedies (son executed by the Nazis 1944) and Einstein's letters of condolence.
- Planck's ethical preservation of German physics under Nazism; his failure, recognised later, to stand against Nazi racial laws in full.

---

## 4. Paul Ehrenfest (1880–1933) — Leiden theoretical physicist, close friend

**Bias flag**: Deeply personal friendship; Ehrenfest a superb theoretical physicist, suicidally depressive, tragic figure.

**Preserved content**:
- Ehrenfest's letters to Einstein are among the warmest in the corpus.
- Ehrenfest organised Einstein's 1920s Leiden visits; served as intellectual-social hub.
- Einstein's deep grief at Ehrenfest's 1933 suicide (following the shooting of Ehrenfest's disabled son Wassik).
- Einstein's obituary essay for Ehrenfest (1934, *Out of My Later Years*, *Ideas and Opinions*): a rare glimpse of Einstein grieving publicly for a close friend. [documented]

---

## 5. Wolfgang Pauli (1900–1958) — sharpest critic among sympathetic physicists

**Bias flag**: Deeply respectful but intellectually ferocious; the Pauli-Einstein correspondence on unified field theory is Pauli pulling no punches.

**Preserved content**:
- Pauli's age-20 review article on relativity (1921) — Einstein regarded it as better than his own expositions.
- Pauli's skepticism about Einstein's unified field theory attempts: "What God has put asunder, let no man join together." [attested via letters]
- Pauli's 1945 letter to Einstein on quantum-mechanical interpretation.
- Pauli as one of the few who could and did directly criticise Einstein's late work while retaining Einstein's friendship.

---

## 6. Mileva Marić (1875–1948) — first wife

**Bias flag**: Complicated. Fellow physics student at ETH Zurich; Einstein's intellectual companion 1896–1903ish; wife 1903–1919; the divorce was bitter.

**Preserved content**:
- Love letters 1897–1903 (first set published in German 1992, English 1992, ed. Jürgen Renn and Robert Schulmann): show Einstein's early intellectual excitement, references to "our work" (ambiguous as evidence of collaboration — see Agent 1 §9.1).
- Marić's letters to her friend Helene Savić (fewer survive).
- Einstein's 1914 "conditions" letter sent before he moved to Berlin, effectively ending the marriage: lists demands for household services, forbids all personal contact beyond professional. A cold and striking document. [documented; CPAE Vol. 8]
- The 1919 divorce: Einstein assigns the future Nobel Prize money to Marić as divorce settlement. He receives the Prize 1922 and the money is transferred. [documented]
- Private observations of Marić's character from surviving family correspondence (her sister Zorka, her friend Helene) — preserved as modest-supporting context.
- The couple's first child, an out-of-wedlock daughter "Lieserl" (born January 1902) whose fate is unknown — probably given up for adoption, or died in infancy from scarlet fever. Referenced in a handful of 1902–1903 letters between Einstein and Marić. Einstein likely never met her. [documented letters; fate of Lieserl [attested] — a genuine historical mystery]
- Two sons: Hans Albert (1904–1973, UC Berkeley engineering professor) and Eduard "Tete" (1910–1965, schizophrenia, long hospitalisation at Burghölzli).

---

## 7. Elsa Einstein (1876–1936) — second wife and cousin

**Bias flag**: Elsa was both Einstein's cousin (mother's sister's daughter) and his second wife (married 1919). She managed his social life, correspondence, and household through the fame years.

**Preserved content**:
- Elsa's letters and family correspondence (via her daughters Ilse and Margot Einstein-Marianoff)
- Observations that Einstein treated the machinery of social life with amused detachment while Elsa bore it.
- Elsa's final illness and death (December 1936, Princeton) — Einstein worked through her decline; his recollections indicate deep attachment beneath the apparent distance.

---

## 8. Hans Albert Einstein (1904–1973) — eldest son, hydraulic engineer

**Bias flag**: Complicated father-son relationship. Distant during Einstein's Berlin years; reconciled partially in US.

**Preserved content**:
- Letters father-to-son across decades.
- Hans Albert's own recollections in later interviews (1970s).
- Einstein's warmer letters about Hans Albert's career at Berkeley; pride in Hans Albert's engineering work.

---

## 9. Helen Dukas (1896–1982) — secretary 1928–1955, Princeton housekeeper

**Bias flag**: Lived in Einstein's Princeton house from 1933; intensely loyal; her recollections shaped the authorized biography.

**Preserved content**:
- Dukas's recollections in Hoffmann & Dukas, *Albert Einstein: Creator and Rebel* (1972) — Hoffmann being Einstein's former collaborator at IAS.
- Her preservation of Einstein's letters, papers, and private archive — the foundation of the CPAE project.
- Her successful suppression, during her lifetime, of several unflattering documents (including some of the East Asia diary material, which surfaced publicly only 2018).

---

## 10. Abraham Pais (1918–2000) — physicist, later biographer, Princeton colleague

**Bias flag**: Admiring; but Pais's biography (*'Subtle is the Lord...'* 1982) is the scholarly gold standard because Pais was both a physicist who understood the science and a colleague who knew Einstein personally.

**Preserved content**:
- Pais's memoir of meetings with Einstein at IAS (mostly about physics).
- Pais's technical reconstruction of Einstein's scientific development.
- Pais's account of the unified field theory period — sympathetic but clear-eyed about its scientific unsuccess.
- Pais on Einstein's work habits, his blackboard, his sailing.

---

## 11. Leopold Infeld (1898–1968) — IAS collaborator, co-author *Evolution of Physics*

**Bias flag**: Polish physicist, later Communist; collaboration with Einstein 1936–38.

**Preserved content**: Infeld's autobiography *Quest* (1941) — honest account of the co-authorship, including frustrations.

---

## 12. Kurt Gödel (1906–1978) — IAS colleague, close Princeton friend

**Bias flag**: Very reserved; Gödel disliked socializing but walked with Einstein from the IAS to Einstein's home at 112 Mercer Street almost daily.

**Preserved content**:
- Gödel's 1949 rotating-universe solution to Einstein's field equations, which permits closed timelike curves (time travel); dedicated to Einstein on his 70th birthday.
- Einstein's comment to Oskar Morgenstern that he went to the Institute "merely to have the privilege to walk home with Gödel." [attested]
- Einstein witnessed Gödel's US citizenship ceremony (1947).

---

## 13. Lise Meitner (1878–1968) — fellow Berlin physicist, eventual discoverer of nuclear fission

**Bias flag**: Colleague; Einstein described her in the 1920s as "our Marie Curie." Jewish refugee like Einstein; emigrated later (1938).

**Preserved content**: Letters 1920s–1950s; mutual respect, limited collaborative work but shared social-political context.

---

## 14. Leo Szilard (1898–1964) — Hungarian physicist, co-drafter of 1939 FDR letter

**Bias flag**: Key political interlocutor; co-drafted the bomb letter; later advocate for nuclear disarmament.

**Preserved content**:
- The August 2, 1939 letter: Szilard drafted, Einstein signed. The actual document's wording owes more to Szilard; the signature is what mattered politically.
- Szilard's late-life recollection of urging Einstein NOT to sign the second (1945) follow-up letter that Szilard was drafting to FDR; the death of Roosevelt intervened.
- Einstein's post-war remorse about the bomb letter, expressed to Szilard and others.

---

## 15. Bertrand Russell (1872–1970) — philosopher, co-author of Russell-Einstein Manifesto

**Bias flag**: Public-intellectual allies; Russell and Einstein corresponded for decades.

**Preserved content**: The July 1955 manifesto; Russell's recollections in his *Autobiography*.

---

## 16. J. Robert Oppenheimer (1904–1967) — Los Alamos director, later IAS director

**Bias flag**: Admiring; complicated (Oppenheimer's security-hearing humiliation 1954 was witnessed by Einstein, who was publicly supportive).

**Preserved content**: Oppenheimer-Einstein exchanges at IAS 1947–1955; Oppenheimer's 1965 UNESCO address on Einstein.

## 17. State surveillance — FBI and pre-1933 German authorities

### FBI file on Einstein
- Opened 1933 (arrival in US); closed with his death
- Total: 1,427 pages, released under FOIA and published, Fred Jerome *The Einstein File* (2002)
- Contents: surveillance of political meetings, correspondence intercepts, informant reports, anti-communist smears, Hoover's personal hostility
- Hoover blocked Einstein from participating in the Manhattan Project (security clearance denied) — ironic given Einstein's letter triggering the program
- **Flag**: hostile state. But the file documents genuine political engagements — Einstein's work against McCarthyism, support for civil rights organisations, anti-nuclear advocacy — and these are confirmed by Einstein's own correspondence.

### Nazi-era threats
- 1933: death threats, a price on Einstein's head in *Das Schwarze Korps* (SS newspaper) and similar publications
- Einstein's Caputh summer home raided; his boat confiscated; Prussian Academy membership renounced by him March 1933 from California, before they could expel him
- His books in Nazi book-burnings May 1933

## 18. Appearance, Temperament, Habits — Cross-Witness Summary

Features mentioned by 3+ independent contemporaries:

| Trait | Mentioned by |
|---|---|
| Distinctive disheveled hair (white from ~40s) | Ehrenfest, Pais, Infeld, Dukas, many |
| Love of sailing (small boats on Lake Geneva, Caputh, Princeton's Carnegie Lake) | Dukas, Pais, his letters |
| Violin playing (Mozart above all, amateur but passionate) | Marić, Elsa, Dukas, Pais, photographs |
| Preference for informal dress; aversion to socks | Dukas, Pais, Marianoff |
| Walking as thinking | Gödel, Pais, colleagues |
| Slept long hours; famous naps | Dukas, family |
| Smoked pipe; forbidden by doctors late in life but continued | Dukas, Pais |
| Warmth with children, often strangers' children | Many |
| Absent-minded professor habits (genuine, not affectation) | Elsa, Dukas |
| Capacity for deep concentration — hours in silence | Pais, Infeld |
| Acute humor, often self-deprecating | Many |
| Political engagement that surprised colleagues | Pais, Infeld, Dukas |

## 19. Speaking and Writing Style — Contemporary Observations

Observed features:
- **In German**: philosophical, precise, preferred abstract nouns; Einstein's German has Swiss cadence (his Swiss-German speech was retained).
- **In English**: heavily accented through life; grammatical but syntactically German; his written English improved through the 1940s but never lost German sentence-structure.
- **Lecture style**: conversational, not oratorical; disliked formal lectures; best in small discussions with questions.
- **Humor**: dry, often self-directed; frequent play on words; fondness for short aphorisms.
- **Letters**: warm with intimates; gracious with strangers; witty; occasionally sharp toward enemies (rare — Einstein preferred withdrawal to argument).

## 20. Conflicts Preserved (Not Harmonized)

1. **Einstein the humane pacifist** (public face, Ideas and Opinions) vs. **Einstein the cold letter-writer to Mileva** (1914 conditions letter). Both documented. Do not harmonize.

2. **The scientific rebel of 1905** vs. **the conservative holdout against QM from 1926 onward**. Bohr won the short-term scientific argument; Einstein's position was not refuted but isolated. Preserve the tension.

3. **Einstein the progressive political public intellectual** vs. **private diary passages from 1922–23 East Asia voyage** that contain racially disparaging remarks about Chinese, Indian, and Japanese peoples. Published 2018 (Rosenkranz edition). This is genuinely difficult material; the Moral Distance Declaration must handle it. Preserve, do not sanitise, do not amplify. [documented]

4. **Einstein the modest intellectual** vs. **Einstein the global celebrity who enjoyed some aspects of fame**. Both documented.

5. **The rebel against German militarism (1914)** vs. **the rebel who urged nuclear weapons research (1939)**. Einstein's justification: Nazi Germany could not be permitted to have nuclear weapons first. He maintained this position under torment, regretting the consequences while standing by the 1939 decision.
