# Albert Einstein — Letters and Sayings

*Agent 3 research dossier. The personal voice: correspondence, documented utterances, attested sayings. Einstein is one of the most quoted and mis-quoted historical figures; provenance is crucial.*

**Methodology note**: Composed from main-agent training knowledge. Citations as `[ref: <work>]`.

---

## 1. Major Correspondence Collections

### The Born-Einstein Letters (1916–1955)
- Covered in Agent 2 §1. The single most cited single correspondence.
- Published Walker 1971 (Irene Born translation); expanded Macmillan 2005 (including Bertha Born correspondence).
- Central to understanding mature-Einstein views on quantum mechanics, realism, determinism.

### The Einstein-Besso Correspondence (1903–1955)
- Michele Besso, Einstein's best friend from Bern patent office days, continued friendship until Besso's death in March 1955 (Einstein himself died a month later).
- ~230 letters across 52 years. Published Paris 1972 (Pierre Speziali edition).
- Range from private (marriage troubles, fatherhood) to technical (Mach's Principle, GR, the nature of simultaneity).
- Einstein's letter to Besso's family upon Besso's death (March 1955) contains the famous "death" passage: "Now he has gone from this strange world a little ahead of me. That means nothing. For us believing physicists, the distinction between past, present and future is only a stubbornly persistent illusion." *("Für uns gläubige Physiker hat die Scheidung zwischen Vergangenheit, Gegenwart und Zukunft nur die Bedeutung einer wenn auch hartnäckigen Illusion.")* [documented]

### The Einstein-Schrödinger Correspondence (1926–1955)
- Allies on QM-interpretation (both realists, both unhappy with Copenhagen).
- 1935 letters leading up to EPR and Schrödinger's cat.
- Einstein's August 1935 letter to Schrödinger setting out the "radioactive-atom-plus-explosive" thought experiment that inspired Schrödinger's cat. [documented]
- Einstein's letter: "Nobody really doubts that the presence or absence of the cat is something independent of the act of observation." [documented]

### The Einstein-Ehrenfest Correspondence (1912–1933)
- Deep friendship; unguarded scientific and personal exchange.
- Ends with Ehrenfest's 1933 suicide; Einstein's obituary essay is a major grief document.

### The Einstein-Mileva Marić Love Letters (1897–1903)
- ~54 surviving letters (early ones lost).
- Published: Renn & Schulmann 1992 (German); English 1992.
- Reveal Einstein's early intellectual excitement, the "our work" references that have driven the Marić-as-contributor controversy, the pregnancy with Lieserl (1901), the marriage preparation.
- Emotional tenor: ardent then cooling; by 1903 tensions visible.

### The 1914 "Conditions" Letter to Mileva
- Before leaving for Berlin, Einstein sends Mileva a list of conditions for continued marriage: she must ensure his clothes and linen are kept in good order, meals served in his room, desk kept clean, and she must "renounce all personal relations" with him beyond what the public requires.
- One of the hardest primary documents in the corpus. Published in CPAE Vol. 8. [documented]

## 2. Key Public Letters and Documents

### The FDR Letter (August 2, 1939)
- Drafted by Leo Szilard (with Eugene Wigner, Edward Teller consulting)
- Signed by Einstein
- Alerts Roosevelt to the possibility of nuclear chain reactions in uranium, the military significance, the German research already underway, and urges US government research.
- Facsimile widely reproduced. [documented]
- Einstein's later regret: to Linus Pauling (November 1954): "I made one great mistake in my life when I signed the letter to President Roosevelt recommending that atom bombs be made; but there was some justification — the danger that the Germans would make them." [attested via Pauling's contemporary notes; widely quoted]

### The Goldstein Telegram (April 24, 1929)
- Rabbi Herbert Goldstein of the Institutional Synagogue in New York cables Einstein: "Do you believe in God? Stop. Answer paid. 50 words."
- Einstein's reply: "I believe in Spinoza's God, who reveals Himself in the lawful harmony of the world, not in a God who concerns Himself with the fate and the doings of mankind." [documented]

### "Religion and Science" (1930)
- Published in *The New York Times Magazine*, November 9, 1930.
- Introduces Einstein's three-stage theory of religion (fear-based, moral, cosmic).
- Reprinted in *Ideas and Opinions*. [documented]

### "Science and Religion" (1941)
- Symposium address; contains "Science without religion is lame, religion without science is blind."
- Reprinted in *Ideas and Opinions*. [documented]

### "Why Socialism?" (May 1949)
- Published as inaugural issue lead essay of *Monthly Review*.
- Einstein's fullest published statement of his democratic-socialist economic and political views.
- Critiques the alienation produced by capitalism; advocates planned economy with democratic accountability; warns against state bureaucracy (anticipating Cold War abuses). [documented]

### The 1946 Lincoln University Address
- Spoken during honorary degree ceremony at the historically Black Lincoln University, Pennsylvania (May 1946).
- "The separation of races is not a disease of colored people but a disease of white people. I do not intend to be quiet about it."
- Einstein spent time with W.E.B. Du Bois and other Black intellectuals that weekend. [documented]

### The Frauenglass Letter (June 1953)
- To William Frauenglass, Brooklyn English teacher subpoenaed by Senate Internal Security Subcommittee.
- Einstein urges refusal to testify, accepting the risk of jail — invoking Thoreau and Gandhi.
- Published in the *New York Times*, May 12, 1953 [note: Einstein writing June 1953, published retrospectively]; intensified FBI interest. [documented]

### The Russell-Einstein Manifesto (July 9, 1955; drafted spring 1955)
- Einstein signed days before his death (April 1955; manifesto released July 1955 by Russell).
- Co-signatories: 9 other Nobel laureates including Linus Pauling, Hideki Yukawa, Max Born, Percy Bridgman, Leopold Infeld, Frédéric Joliot-Curie, Hermann Muller, Cecil Powell, Joseph Rotblat.
- Call for peaceful resolution of international disputes; foundational document for Pugwash movement. [documented]

## 3. Famous Sayings with Provenance

### "God does not play dice with the universe" (and variants)

**German original**: "*Der Alte würfelt nicht*" — literally "The Old One does not throw dice."

**Provenance**: Einstein to Max Born, letter of 4 December 1926. Full sentence: "Quantum mechanics is certainly imposing. But an inner voice tells me that it is not yet the real thing. The theory says a lot, but does not really bring us any closer to the secret of the 'old one.' I, at any rate, am convinced that He does not throw dice." [documented — Born-Einstein Letters]

Einstein repeats variations in ~30 years of correspondence. The English "God does not play dice with the universe" is a compression and mild retheologisation. Einstein's "Alte" = Spinoza's God, the order of Nature, NOT the personal theistic God.

Bohr's famous retort: "Einstein, stop telling God what to do." [attested — Pais reports this from Bohr's circle; the exact wording varies]

### "Subtle is the Lord, but malicious He is not"

**German**: *Raffiniert ist der Herr Gott, aber boshaft ist Er nicht.*

**Provenance**: Spoken at Princeton in April 1921, during Einstein's first visit to the US; the line was later inscribed on the mantelpiece at Fine Hall (now Jones Hall), Princeton University mathematics department, at Einstein's direction. [documented — Einstein said it publicly in Princeton April 1921; the plaque installation followed]

### "Imagination is more important than knowledge"

**Provenance**: From the October 1929 George Sylvester Viereck interview in the *Saturday Evening Post*. Full context: "I am enough of the artist to draw freely upon my imagination. Imagination is more important than knowledge. Knowledge is limited. Imagination encircles the world." [documented]

### "Everything should be made as simple as possible, but no simpler"

**Provenance**: Paraphrase. Einstein's actual 1933 Herbert Spencer Lecture at Oxford ("On the Method of Theoretical Physics") contains: "It can scarcely be denied that the supreme goal of all theory is to make the irreducible basic elements as simple and as few as possible without having to surrender the adequate representation of a single datum of experience."

The short, popular formulation "as simple as possible, but no simpler" appears in Roger Sessions, *New York Times* 1950, attributing it to Einstein. The *spirit* is Einstein's, the exact wording is a paraphrase. [documented original + [attested] paraphrase]

### "The most beautiful experience we can have is the mysterious"

**Provenance**: "The World As I See It" (1931, Forum and Century), reprinted in *Ideas and Opinions*. Full sentence: "The most beautiful experience we can have is the mysterious. It is the fundamental emotion that stands at the cradle of true art and true science." [documented]

### "Anyone who has never made a mistake has never tried anything new"

**Provenance**: [attributed] — commonly cited but no clear primary source in CPAE has been located. Treat with skepticism. [likely misattribution]

### "If I had an hour to solve a problem..."

**Provenance**: NOT in CPAE; no documented source. Probably apocryphal. [misattribution]

### "Insanity is doing the same thing over and over..."

**Provenance**: NOT Einstein. First documented appearance in Narcotics Anonymous literature (1981), attributed to Einstein only retrospectively by misattribution. [documented misattribution]

### "The hardest thing in the world to understand is the income tax"

**Provenance**: [attributed] — attributed to Einstein as a quip to his accountant Leo Mattersdorf in a 1963 Time magazine piece by Mattersdorf. The attribution is not in Einstein's own writings. [attributed, unverifiable]

### "Only two things are infinite, the universe and human stupidity, and I'm not sure about the former"

**Provenance**: [attributed] — no primary source. Sometimes attributed to Frederick Perls. [misattribution]

### "The true sign of intelligence is not knowledge but imagination"

**Provenance**: Paraphrase of the Viereck interview (see above).

### "Not everything that counts can be counted..."

**Provenance**: NOT Einstein. Sign in William Bruce Cameron's 1963 *Informal Sociology*. Misattributed to Einstein repeatedly. [documented misattribution]

### "Compound interest is the most powerful force in the universe" / "the eighth wonder of the world"

**Provenance**: NOT Einstein. No primary source. [documented misattribution]

### "Everybody is a genius, but if you judge a fish..."

**Provenance**: NOT Einstein. No primary source. Likely 20th-century self-help origin. [documented misattribution]

## 4. Autobiographical Notes (1946) — key passages

Written for Schilpp *Albert Einstein: Philosopher-Scientist* (1949) at age 67. The single most valuable source for how Einstein framed his own intellectual development.

### Opening lines
"Here I sit in order to write, at the age of 67, something like my own obituary." [documented; Schilpp trans.]

### The Hume-Mach liberation
Einstein describes reading Hume (especially the *Treatise*) and Mach (*Mechanics*) as intellectual liberations that loosened his dogmatic trust in space and time. Decisive for the path to relativity. [documented]

### The "happiest thought"
Writing of the 1907 realization: "For an observer falling freely from the roof of a house there exists — at least in his immediate surroundings — no gravitational field. Indeed, if the observer drops some bodies then these remain relative to him in a state of rest or of uniform motion... The observer therefore has the right to interpret his state as 'at rest.' This was the happiest thought of my life." [documented; Schilpp trans.]

### The quantum doubt
"All my attempts to adapt the theoretical foundation of physics to this new type of knowledge failed completely. It was as if the ground had been pulled out from under one, with no firm foundation to be seen anywhere, upon which one could have built." — on the implications of QM for his worldview. [documented]

### The schema of the theoretical scientist
"A theory can be tested by experience, but there is no way from experience to the setting up of a theory. Equations of such complexity as the equations of the gravitational field can be found only through the discovery of a logically simple mathematical condition which determines the equations completely or (at least) almost completely."
— On the role of mathematical intuition and theory-construction. [documented]

### Closing lines
"The essential in the being of a man of my type lies precisely in *what* he thinks and *how* he thinks, not in what he does or suffers." [documented]

## 5. The "Difficult Material" (preserve, do not sanitise or amplify)

Per the Moral Distance Declaration at runtime; research-level: preserve in context.

### 5.1 The 1922–23 East Asia Travel Diaries

Published as *The Travel Diaries of Albert Einstein* (ed. Ze'ev Rosenkranz, Princeton 2018) after being held in the Einstein Archives for nearly a century and only selectively released earlier. The diary entries from the 1922–1923 voyage to Japan, Palestine, and Spain contain racially disparaging remarks about Chinese people ("industrious, filthy, obtuse people" in one passage; noting that Chinese "would supplant all other races" as a concern), Indian people, and reflections on Japanese that are comparatively positive but still exoticising.

The diary was private. These were never published by Einstein in his lifetime. The 2018 publication produced considerable scholarly discussion (Rosenkranz's introduction offers extensive contextualisation). The remarks are the idiom of interwar European racial-hierarchical thinking which Einstein later publicly and repeatedly rejected, but they exist in the 1922–23 private record.

**Echo policy**: preserve, do not sanitise, do not amplify, contextualise within Einstein's intellectual trajectory (the same man who in 1946 gave the Lincoln University address on racism as a disease of white people). The tension is real and instructive.

### 5.2 The 1914 "Conditions" Letter to Mileva

Covered in §1 above. A cold document by any standard. Context: marriage breakdown in progress; Einstein genuinely wanted a clean break and framed it bluntly. Preserve.

### 5.3 The Private Life — Infidelities, the "Lieserl" Daughter

Einstein's private life from the 1920s onward included multiple affairs (documented in correspondence and family papers, especially with Margarete Lebach, Toni Mendel, Estella Katzenellenbogen, Ethel Michanowski, Betty Neumann). The first daughter Lieserl (born January 1902 to Einstein and Marić out of wedlock) was almost certainly given up for adoption or died in infancy; Einstein likely never met her. [documented letters; fate of Lieserl [attested]]

These are private-life facts documented in the correspondence. They do not bear on the scientific or ethical-public work but they are part of the biographical record.

### 5.4 Views on the Soviet Union and Communism

Einstein was a democratic socialist, not a Soviet supporter. He publicly criticised the Moscow trials. But his general left-progressive politics, combined with the McCarthyist atmosphere, made him a long-term FBI target. Preserve this tension; Einstein was neither the innocent apolitical scientist the state pretended he was NOR the Soviet sympathiser Hoover alleged.

### 5.5 Ambivalence about Zionism and Israel

Einstein supported the cultural-Zionist project from 1919 onward — fundraising for Hebrew University, public advocacy. He was skeptical of political Zionism and the nation-state form. Declined the presidency of Israel (1952). His public statements about Palestinian Arabs in the 1948 war period are mixed and need careful contextualising.

## 6. Personal Voice Features (for Expression DNA)

Based on the correspondence and writings:

1. **Plainness with depth**. Einstein avoided pomposity and preferred concrete images. "Falling elevator," "running alongside a light beam," "photon in a box" — physics communicated through pictures.

2. **Warm humor, often at his own expense**. "I have no special talent. I am only passionately curious." (Letter to Carl Seelig, 11 March 1952) [documented]

3. **Precision in distinction**. Einstein distinguished carefully — his epistemological essays are models of drawing fine distinctions (principle-theory vs. constructive-theory; cosmic religious feeling vs. personal-God theism; reality-independent vs. observation-dependent).

4. **A characteristic German-Swiss syntactic density** in original language; survives partially in translation. Long dependent clauses, abstract nouns, philosophical vocabulary.

5. **Gnomic aphorisms** scattered through the correspondence and essays — not forced; emerged naturally when Einstein compressed a thought.

6. **Kindness with strangers' letters**. Einstein replied to enormous quantities of unsolicited correspondence — schoolchildren, clerks, religious seekers, amateur scientists. These replies are often touching; he took the questions seriously.

7. **Sharpness when provoked on scientific method**. Einstein's Reply to Criticisms (Schilpp 1949) is polite but unyielding on points he had worked through carefully.

8. **Reverent humility before nature's intelligibility**. The repeated "cosmic religious feeling" is not a rhetorical posture — it recurs in private letters as in public essays. Einstein's "religious" feeling is a real feature of his thought, not an apologetic tack.

9. **Direct political voice when it mattered**. The Lincoln address, the Frauenglass letter, "Why Socialism?" — Einstein could write with moral force without preaching.

10. **Preference for brief over elaborate expression**. Einstein's essays are remarkably short. *Autobiographical Notes* is ~50 pages; "Why Socialism?" is ~2,500 words; most *Ideas and Opinions* essays are brief.

## 7. What Survives and What Is Lost in Translation

Einstein in English translation:
- **Survives**: the clarity of thought, the structure of argument, the aphoristic moments, the ethical weight.
- **Partly lost**: the specific German philosophical vocabulary (*Anschauung*, *Wesen*, *Gesetzmäßigkeit*, *gemeinverständlich*); the Swiss-German rhythms; the quiet humor of particular word choices.
- **Standard English translations**: *Ideas and Opinions* (Sonja Bargmann translations, Crown 1954); *Autobiographical Notes* (Paul Schilpp translation, 1949); *Born-Einstein Letters* (Irene Born, 1971); *Relativity* (Robert W. Lawson, 1920); *Meaning of Relativity* (various editions).
