# Cao Cao 曹操 (155-220 CE) — Primary Works Research

## Overview

Cao Cao's surviving corpus is fragmentary but remarkable for its range: approximately twenty to twenty-four *yuefu* 樂府 poems [attested], the earliest extant commentary on Sun Tzu's *Art of War* (魏武注孫子 *Wei Wu zhu Sunzi*, often called 孫子略解 *Sunzi lüejie*) [documented], a cluster of politically decisive edicts (the three 求賢令 "Edicts Seeking Talent" of 210, 214, and 217 CE, and the 210 autobiographical 讓縣自明本志令 "Edict Relinquishing Fiefs to Clarify My Ambition") [documented], a notorious 遺令 "Last Testament" (220) [documented], and scattered letters preserved chiefly through the *Sanguozhi* 三國志 plus Pei Songzhi's 裴松之 fifth-century commentary and later Tang-Song encyclopedias (*Yiwen leiju* 藝文類聚, *Taiping yulan* 太平御覽, *Beitang shuchao* 北堂書鈔, *Chuxue ji* 初學記). Much is lost — his original military compendium 兵書接要 *Bingshu jieyao* survives only in fragmentary quotations [documented]. The modern critical standard is the Zhonghua Shuju 中華書局 edition *Cao Cao ji* 曹操集 (Beijing: Zhonghua Shuju, 1959; repr. 1974 and later) [documented]. Across this corpus, recurring arguments — meritocracy over pedigree (唯才是舉), Legalist discipline over Confucian ritual, skepticism of the mandate-of-Heaven rhetoric used against him, and the brevity-of-life / urgency-of-action topos — form a consistent ideological signature [modern-scholarly-inference].

## 1. Cao Cao's Collected Works (*Cao Cao ji* 曹操集)

- The standard modern critical edition is *Cao Cao ji* 曹操集, Beijing: Zhonghua Shuju 中華書局, 1959 (with subsequent reprints, the 1974 reissue being the one commonly cited in Western scholarship) [documented]. This edition is cited directly by the University of Washington's *Chinese Autobiographical Writing* digital project for the text of 讓縣自明本志令 (*Cao Cao ji*, 2.41–43) [web: https://uw.manifoldapp.org/read/698726ee-3a7b-45b8-916d-c76c40bf0081/section/6337064e-3bb6-41f7-8e7d-450777043593].
- A later, more thorough Zhonghua Shuju critical collation appeared in 2013 as a revised *Cao Cao ji* [attested; web: see academia.edu traces above].
- What survives: roughly 20–24 poems; one extensive commentary (Sun Tzu); several dozen edicts, orders, and memorials (*ling* 令, *biao* 表, *jiao* 教); a handful of letters. Even the Zhonghua edition compiles material scattered across the dynastic histories, *Yiwen leiju*, *Yuefu shiji* 樂府詩集, and the *Taiping yulan*, so authorship of individual fragments is frequently debated [modern-scholarly-inference].
- What is lost: the *Bingshu jieyao* 兵書接要 (a general survey of Chinese military literature by Cao Cao himself) is attested but no longer extant except as citations [documented; web: http://chinaknowledge.de (cited in search results on Sunzi bingfa)]. A separate *Bingfa* 兵法 text is also attested [attested].
- Authorship certainty is graded: the major *yuefu* (Haoli xing, Duange xing, Guan canghai, Gui sui shou, Kuhan xing) and the programmatic edicts (the three 求賢令; 讓縣; 遺令) are securely attributed in the *Sanguozhi* and its Pei Songzhi annotations [documented]. Short letter fragments and some minor poems (e.g., "Qiu hu xing" 秋胡行) are less secure and depend on later encyclopedia attestation [modern-scholarly-inference].

## 2. Poetry (~20–24 surviving *yuefu*)

Cao Cao is credited with "over twenty *yuefu* poems," with "24 extant poems" cited as the standard count [attested; web: https://www.clausiuspress.com/assets/default/article/2024/09/17/article_1726589040.pdf; https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry_of_Cao_Cao]. All survive through anthology transmission — chiefly Guo Maoqian's 郭茂倩 (1041–1099) *Yuefu shiji* 樂府詩集 and earlier *leishu* 類書 like *Yiwen leiju* [documented; web: http://chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Poetry/yuefushiji.html].

### Key poems

**蒿里行 Haoli xing ("Ballad of Haoli" / "Marching Among Wormwood" / "Graveyard Song")** — c. 190–192 CE, written in the aftermath of the failed coalition against Dong Zhuo 董卓 [attested]. Yuefu form, five-character lines. "Haoli" was originally a funeral-dirge tune [documented]. Famous closing couplet: 白骨露於野，千里無雞鳴。生民百遺一，念之斷人腸 "White bones lie exposed on the open plain, / For a thousand *li* no rooster crows. / Of every hundred living souls scarcely one remains; / Thinking on this, my bowels break." Opening: 關東有義士，興兵討群凶 "East of the Passes righteous heroes gathered, / Raising troops to stand against the tyrants" (translator: vacantmountain blog, 2013 — non-peer-reviewed) [web: https://vacantmountain.wordpress.com/2013/07/02/cao_cao_%E8%92%BF%E9%87%8C%E8%A1%8C_poem_march_wormwood/]. Another translation by Jean Yuan / Vickie Fang at classicchinesepoetry.com renders the same lines: "Their unburied bones turn white in the wilderness / And for a thousand miles no rooster cries" [web: https://www.classicchinesepoetry.com/tag/cao-cao/].

**薤露行 Xielu xing ("Ballad of the Dew on the Shallots")** — dated to the period around Dong Zhuo's 190 CE sack of Luoyang [attested]. Paired in function with *Haoli xing*: "Xielu" was another old funeral-dirge tune originally sung at the passing of aristocrats [documented; web: http://chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Poetry/yuefushiji.html]. Comments on the failed anti-eunuch coup of He Jin 何進 and the burning of Luoyang [attested]. These two poems are sometimes called Cao Cao's "elegies for the Han" [modern-scholarly-inference].

**短歌行 Duan ge xing ("Short Song Style")** — composed in the 210s, traditionally associated with Red Cliffs (208) though the dating is contested [attested; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry_of_Cao_Cao]. Four-character lines, the "old" *Shijing*-era meter. Opens 對酒當歌，人生幾何 "Facing the wine one should sing: how long, after all, is a human life?" Themes: brevity of life, the yearning for worthy advisers, banquet conviviality, recruitment of talent [documented]. Translated in Stephen Owen, *An Anthology of Chinese Literature: Beginnings to 1911* (New York: W.W. Norton, 1996), p. 280 [attested; web: https://www.coursehero.com/tutors-problems/Literature/53338216-...]. Burton Watson comments: Cao Cao was "the only writer of the period who succeeded in infusing the old four-character meter with any vitality, mainly because he discarded the archaic diction associated with it and employed the ordinary poetic language of his time" (Watson's *Chinese Lyricism: Shih Poetry from the Second to the Twelfth Century*, Columbia University Press, 1971, paraphrased in Wikipedia) [attested; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry_of_Cao_Cao].

**觀滄海 Guan canghai ("Viewing the Blue Sea" / "Gazing at the Sea")** — 207 CE, composed on the return march from the Wuhuan 烏桓 campaign at White Wolf Mountain 白狼山, atop Jieshi Mountain 碣石山 [attested]. This is the first stanza of the four-stanza cycle **步出夏門行 *Bu chu Xiamen xing* "Stepping Out through the Xia Gate"** [documented]. Four-character lines, heroic-cosmic scale — often treated as one of the earliest self-consciously landscape (*shanshui* 山水) poems in Chinese [modern-scholarly-inference; web: https://www.classicchinesepoetry.com/2021/03/gazing-down-at-the-deep-blue-sea/]. Chinese text as transmitted: 東臨碣石，以觀滄海。水何澹澹，山島竦峙……日月之行，若出其中；星漢燦爛，若出其裡 [web: https://s.qiouyi.lu/newsletter/two-takes-on-cao-caos-guan-cang-hai/]. Translations by S. Qiouyi Lu (2020) and David Bowles (2014) are in circulation; a canonical peer-reviewed translation I could locate is not identified — Burton Watson does discuss Cao Cao's four-character style in the 1971 Columbia volume [modern-scholarly-inference].

**龜雖壽 Gui sui shou ("Though the Tortoise Lives Long")** — 207 CE, the fourth (concluding) stanza of *Bu chu Xiamen xing* [documented]. Four-character lines. Famous aging-warrior image: 老驥伏櫪，志在千里；烈士暮年，壯心不已 "The old war-horse may be stabled, / Yet still it longs to gallop a thousand *li*; / And a noble-hearted man though advanced in years / Never abandons his proud aspirations" (translator unattributed in the widely circulated version; appears in Wikipedia citing earlier anthologies) [attested; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry_of_Cao_Cao]. Themes: mortality, defiance, perseverance in late career — widely read as Cao Cao's own self-portrait at 52 [modern-scholarly-inference].

**苦寒行 Kuhan xing ("Ballad of Bitter Cold")** — 206 CE, composed on the winter campaign through the Taihang 太行 range against Gao Gan 高幹 [attested]. Describes bitter terrain: 北上太行山，艱哉何巍巍 "Going north up Mount Taihang — how arduous, how towering!" A rare first-person campaign poem in the Chinese tradition, treating the suffering of soldiers on the march rather than glory [modern-scholarly-inference; web: https://www.cn-poetry.com/caocao-poems/song-of-bitter-cold.html].

**Other attributed poems include** 對酒 *Dui jiu* ("To My Wine" — a utopian vision of peaceful rule), 陌上桑 *Mo shang sang* ("Mulberry by the Path" — Daoist-transcendence imagery), the 氣出唱 *Qi chu chang* trio and 秋胡行 *Qiu hu xing* (Daoist/xian themes), and 卻東西門行 *Que dong xi men xing* ("Closing the East-West Gate"). Authorship on some of these Daoist-themed pieces is debated but they are collected in all modern editions [attested; web: https://kongming.net/poetry/caocao/].

### Recurring poetic images and themes

- Mortality and life's brevity (人生幾何; 烈士暮年) — appears in *Duan ge xing*, *Gui sui shou*, *Qiu hu xing*
- Bleached bones / depopulated landscape — *Haoli xing*, *Kuhan xing*
- Unrealized ambition and the longing for talent — *Duan ge xing*, and structurally linked to the 求賢令 edicts
- Cosmic scale / sea and stars — *Guan canghai*
- The aging warrior's stubborn will — *Gui sui shou*, *Kuhan xing*

### Style — the 建安風骨 Jian'an style

Cao Cao, Cao Pi 曹丕, and Cao Zhi 曹植 ("the Three Caos" 三曹) anchor the 建安 Jian'an era (196–220 CE, a reign-name of Emperor Xian 獻帝) [documented; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jian%27an_poetry]. Jian'an style is characterized by: (1) a shift from the elaborate catalog-*fu* 賦 toward *yuefu*-derived *shi* 詩; (2) personal, direct voice replacing Han allegorical decorum; (3) *fengyu* 風雨 of lament for life's brevity combined with heroic ambition. Cao Cao's specific innovation, per Burton Watson, is re-vitalizing the archaic four-character *Shijing* meter with contemporary diction [attested]. Cao Zhi, not Cao Cao, is generally credited as "the first major poet of the new *shi* style" of the era [attested; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jian%27an_poetry].

## 3. Military Writings

### Commentary on Sunzi — 孫子略解 / 魏武注孫子

- Cao Cao authored the **earliest extant commentary** on the *Sunzi bingfa* 孫子兵法 [documented]. The title as transmitted in the *Shiyi jia zhu Sunzi* 十一家注孫子 tradition is **魏武注孫子** *Wei Wu zhu Sunzi* ("Emperor Wu of Wei's Commentary on Sunzi"); it is also referenced as **孫子略解** *Sunzi lüejie* ("A Brief Exegesis of Sunzi") [attested; web: https://www.loc.gov/item/2021666390; http://chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Diverse/sunzishijiazhu.html].
- His **preface** is preserved and famously declares that previous annotations had not focused on the essential ideas, and announces his intent to edit and clarify the text [documented; web: https://www.asianstudies.org/publications/eaa/archives/new-perspectives-on-the-sunzi-sun-tzu-from-contemporary-chinese-military-writings/]. Scholars note he "altered and modified the original text" as well as exegeting it [attested].
- Style: the glosses are terse — "most of Cao Cao's commentaries on the manual are short and to the point, unlike other scholars" like Du You 杜佑 or the later Song annotators [attested; web: https://the-scholars.com/viewtopic.php?t=10230].
- The commentary is transmitted as part of the Song-dynasty compilation 十一家注孫子 *Shiyi jia zhu Sunzi* ("Eleven Masters' Annotated Sunzi" — earlier ten-master variant 十家注); the earliest surviving printed exemplar is a three-*juan* manuscript from the Shaoxi 紹熙 reign (1190–1194) of Song Guangzong, digitized by the Library of Congress [documented; web: https://www.loc.gov/item/2021666390]. The eleven commentators: Cao Cao (Three Kingdoms); Meng Shi 孟氏 (Liang 502–557); Li Quan 李筌, Du Mu 杜牧, Chen Hao 陳皞, Jia Lin 賈林 (Tang); Mei Yaochen 梅堯臣, Wang Xi 王晳, He Yanxi 何延錫, Zhang Yu 張預 (Song); plus Du You's 杜佑 commentary from the *Tongdian* 通典 [documented].
- **What his commentary reveals about his military thought**: a close scholar-general relationship to terrain, logistics, timing, and deception (*ji* 詭). An *Early Medieval China* 2022 article by Meow Hui Goh argues deception is both a war tactic and a mode of writing in third-century China, with Cao Cao central to this [attested; web: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/15299104.2022.2101766 — Goh, "Genuine Words: Deception as a War Tactic and a Mode of Writing in Third-Century China," *Early Medieval China* 28 (2022)].
- A disputed irony: one academic paper argues Cao Cao "failed to integrate [Sunzi's] teaching into his endeavor" at the Red Cliffs campaign, showing theory did not guarantee practice [attested; web: https://www.academia.edu/18055467/The_Flames_over_Chi_Bi_Cao_Cao_and_the_Art_of_War_of_Sun_Zi].
- A recent peer-reviewed article — "A Choice between Su 速 and Jiu 久," *T'oung Pao* / *Monumenta Serica* (2024) — revisits Cao Cao's exegetical choices on Sunzi's "speed vs. endurance" debate [attested; web: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/02549948.2024.2335811].

### *Bingshu jieyao* 兵書接要 and *Bingfa* 兵法

- "Cao Cao wrote two major works on warfare. The first was a fully original work called 'Summary of the Essentials' (*jieyao* 接要) and was a general survey of Chinese military literature. His second work was an extensive commentary on Sunzi's Art of War. His Summary of the Essentials is no longer extant, but his commentary on Sunzi's work has survived" [documented; attested to anonymous answer on The Scholars of Shen Zhou forum, but the factual claim is repeated in encyclopedic sources; web: https://the-scholars.com/viewtopic.php?t=10230].
- Fragments of the *Bingshu jieyao* are preserved in *Taiping yulan* citations [attested].

## 4. Edicts, Memorials, and Public Writings

### 讓縣自明本志令 — "Let Order Relinquishing Fiefs to Clarify My Basic Ambition" (December 210 CE / early 211)

- Foundational autobiographical document [documented]. Written when Emperor Xian offered Cao Cao three additional counties; Cao Cao declined, retaining one enfeoffment of 10,000 households and returning 20,000 [documented; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao].
- Source text cited: *Cao Cao ji* 曹操集, Beijing: Zhonghua Shuju, 1959, 2.41–43 [documented; web: https://uw.manifoldapp.org/read/698726ee-3a7b-45b8-916d-c76c40bf0081/section/6337064e-3bb6-41f7-8e7d-450777043593].
- Core claims in the essay (translator unnamed on the UW Manifold digital edition): (a) "If it had not been for me, who knows how many people would have proclaimed themselves emperors or kings" — self-justification as stabilizer rather than usurper; (b) loyalty analogies to Lord Huan of Qi 齊桓公 and Lord Wen of Jin 晉文公 who, despite hegemonic power, remained loyal to the Zhou; (c) the claim of originally modest ambition — "I just wanted to have a mediocre plot of land, retreat from the world, and cut off all human contact"; (d) voluntary fief reduction as evidentiary proof of restraint [documented].
- Rafe de Crespigny characterizes this document as "one of the earliest forms of autobiography in China" offering "a rich vein of discourse concerning the moral justification for hegemonic domination at a time of dynastic breakdown" (de Crespigny, *Imperial Warlord: A Biography of Cao Cao 155–220 AD*, Leiden: Brill, Sinica Leidensia 99, 2010) [attested; web: https://brill.com/display/title/18666].

### 求賢令 — The three "Edicts Seeking Talent" (210, 214, 217 CE)

- A sequence of three edicts issuing the slogan **唯才是舉** "raise talent and talent only" [documented; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao].
- 210 CE edict: the first, issued two years after the Red Cliffs defeat; cites the metaphor of men "wearing ragged clothes but carrying precious jade" (poor but talented) and invokes Jiang Ziya 姜子牙 fishing the Weishui 渭水 as a model of talent found outside the aristocracy [documented; web: https://chinesestories.net/cao-cao-issued-the-three-orders-for-talent-seeking-to-start-the-prosperity-of-talents-in-the-wei-and-jin-dynasties/].
- 214 CE edict: continues the argument.
- 217 CE edict = 舉賢勿拘品行令 "Edict to Raise the Worthy Without Regard to Moral Conduct" — the most provocative of the three, arguing that men of dubious character (even those accused of crimes, disloyalty, or bribe-taking) should still be employed if their ability is needed [documented]. Frequently cited as the starkest Legalist-vs-Confucian breach in third-century rhetoric [modern-scholarly-inference; web: https://daydaynews.cc/en/history/452919.html].
- Historical significance: they formalize the break with Han-era moral-cultivation-as-qualification recruitment norms, and presage the 九品中正 nine-rank system established under Cao Pi [attested].

### 整齊風俗令, 抑兼并令, and other administrative orders

- 整齊風俗令 "Edict to Regulate Customs" and 抑兼并令 "Edict Restraining [Land] Consolidation" — attested in de Crespigny and Chinese scholarship as part of Cao Cao's Legalist reform package to curb great-family estates and standardize administrative ritual [attested]. English-language web sources are sparse; mainly accessible via de Crespigny 2010 and the *Sanguozhi* original text [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao].
- 屯田令 "Edict on *Tuntian* [Agricultural Colonies]" — the 196 CE policy that settled landless refugees and soldiers on state land in exchange for half their harvest; a defining economic measure of his regime [documented; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuntian].

### 遺令 — "Last Testament" (Feb 220 CE)

- Issued shortly before his death in Luoyang, 15 March 220 [documented]. Famously **mundane** — instead of dynastic-succession bombast, directs the distribution of his perfume/incense (香) among his concubines, instructs them to make and sell silk shoes to support themselves, and prescribes simple burial without jade or gold [attested; web: https://chinesestories.net/uncover-which-woman-cao-cao-still-couldnt-forget-to-arrange-in-person-before-he-died/; https://daydaynews.cc/en/history/1123980.html].
- Textual caution: "the longest received version of his will is fraught with textual problems" [attested; web: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1179/1529910413Z.0000000008 — Meow Hui Goh, "Becoming Wen: The Rhetoric in the 'Final Edicts' of Han Emperor Wen and Wei Emperor Wen," *Early Medieval China* 19 (2013)].
- Reading tradition: The document's startling domesticity has been read both as (a) Daoist/Legalist anti-ritual austerity, (b) calculated humility staging his posthumous reputation, or (c) genuine late-life disillusionment [modern-scholarly-inference].

## 5. Letters

Cao Cao's letters survive in fragments preserved through the *Sanguozhi*, Pei Songzhi's commentary, and Sui-Tang-Song encyclopedias (*Beitang shuchao*, *Chuxue ji*, *Taiping yulan*) [documented; web: various Wikipedia/kongming sources]. None were preserved as an intact epistolary corpus.

- **Letter to Sun Quan 孫權, 208 CE, on the eve of Red Cliffs** — the notorious "hunting letter" claiming 800,000 (八十萬) troops descending by land and water, read aloud at Sun Quan's war council and dismissed as exaggeration by Zhou Yu 周瑜, who estimated actual strength at ~220,000 [attested; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Red_Cliffs]. Preserved in the *Sanguozhi* with Pei Songzhi's commentary citing the *Jiangbiao zhuan* 江表傳 [documented].
- **Letters to Xun Yu 荀彧** — correspondence with his chief strategist. Xun Yu's 212 death (possibly suicide by empty food-box signal, per the *Weishi chunqiu* 魏氏春秋 cited by Pei Songzhi) followed his opposition to Cao Cao's enfeoffment as Duke of Wei 魏公; the precipitating exchange of messages is preserved in the *Sanguozhi* and the *Hou Hanshu* [attested; web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xun_Yu].
- **Letters to Cao Pi and Cao Zhi** — instructive-paternal. A famous exhortation to Cao Zhi on the 215 Sun Quan campaign: "When I was Magistrate of Dunqiu 頓丘, I was 23 years old. Thinking back to what I did at that time, I have no regrets at all. Now you are also 23 years of age — would you not strive for excellence in your tasks?" [attested; web: https://kongming.net/novel/sgz/caocao-2.php].
- **Fragments in *Beitang shuchao*, *Chuxue ji*, *Taiping yulan*** contain short epistolary excerpts of uncertain address [attested].
- Scholarly note: Xiaofei Tian and Antje Richter have contributed to the scholarship on early medieval Chinese letters; see Antje Richter (ed.), *A History of Chinese Letters and Epistolary Culture* (Leiden: Brill, 2015) [attested; web: https://scholar.harvard.edu/files/xtian/files/letters_and_gifts_from_early_medieval_china_tian.pdf — Xiaofei Tian, "Material and Symbolic Economies: Letters and Gifts in Early Medieval China"].

## 6. Core Recurring Arguments (≥3 appearances across corpus)

[All modern-scholarly-inference unless noted; these are patterns one derives from reading the primary texts in the *Cao Cao ji* together.]

1. **Meritocracy over pedigree (唯才是舉)** — 求賢令 ×3 (210, 214, 217); implicit in *Duan ge xing* (yearning for worthy advisers, "山不厭高，海不厭深，周公吐哺，天下歸心"); 讓縣令 (presents himself as having risen by merit, not birth). **Four+ appearances.**
2. **Tension between ambition and self-justification** — 讓縣令 (explicit); *Duan ge xing* (literary sublimation); 遺令 (ostentatious austerity as final posture); *Haoli xing* / *Xielu xing* (positioning himself morally above fellow warlords). **Four+ appearances.** De Crespigny explicitly frames his writings as "moral justification for hegemonic domination" [attested].
3. **Discipline and law-based statecraft (Legalist vocabulary against Confucian orthodoxy)** — 求賢令 series; 抑兼并令; 整齊風俗令; reported application in his military administration (Pei Songzhi on strict discipline, including his legendary self-punishment when his horse trampled crops). **Three+ clusters.** [attested; web: https://inf.news/en/news/ad927192cf0d8d9f6f71f60d9995bdb8.html]
4. **Life's brevity vs. political urgency** — *Duan ge xing* (人生幾何); *Gui sui shou* (烈士暮年); *Qiu hu xing*. **Three poems.** Core Jian'an *feng-gu* 風骨 theme in his personal voice.
5. **Heaven/mandate skepticism and pragmatic ruthlessness** — 讓縣令 reframes mandate-rhetoric as slander; 舉賢勿拘品行令 explicitly deprioritizes moral character; 遺令 shows indifference to burial-ritual metaphysics. **Three+ appearances.**
6. **The worthy-adviser-as-scarce-resource topos** — *Duan ge xing* (周公吐哺 Duke of Zhou spitting out his food three times to receive visitors); 求賢令 (Jiang Ziya image; the "ragged-clothes jade-carrying" metaphor). **Two+ direct, plus structural echoes.**

## 7. Vocabulary Signature

- **唯才是舉** "raise talent and only talent" — his signature four-character policy slogan, first use attributed to the 210 edict [documented].
- **周公吐哺，天下歸心** "The Duke of Zhou spat out his meat; the world's hearts turned to him" — *Duan ge xing*'s closing couplet, invoking the Zhou paragon of talent-recruitment; this sharpened the Duke-of-Zhou / Cao Cao analogy he repeatedly used [documented].
- **本志** "basic/original ambition" — keyword of the 讓縣自明本志令; makes *zhi* 志 (aim, will, ambition) a central self-description term [documented].
- **烈士暮年，壯心不已** "the fierce-willed man in his twilight years, his heroic heart unstilled" — from *Gui sui shou*; iconic self-portrait of the aging determined leader, widely echoed in later Chinese poetry [documented].
- **Legalist register**: preference for *ling* 令 (command/edict) genre over Confucian *biao* 表 memorial style; heavy use of *fa* 法 (law), *xing* 刑 (punishment), *gong* 功 (merit) terms — consistent with Shang Yang 商鞅 / Han Feizi 韓非子 vocabulary [modern-scholarly-inference].
- His prose is noted for directness and absence of elaborate parallelism — the same "discarding of archaic diction" Watson noted in his poetry transferred to edicts [modern-scholarly-inference].

## 8. Works Cited / Echoed by Cao Cao

- **Sun Zi 孫子** — directly, through his commentary, with extensive engagement [documented].
- **Duke of Zhou 周公** — *Duan ge xing*'s climactic reference [documented].
- **Jiang Ziya 姜子牙 / Lü Shang 呂尚** — the 210 求賢令 [documented].
- **Lord Huan of Qi 齊桓公, Lord Wen of Jin 晉文公** — the 210 讓縣令 [documented].
- **Han Feizi 韓非子, Shang Yang 商鞅** — not cited by name in surviving works (no direct quotation), but the Legalist institutional vocabulary and the 舉賢勿拘品行令's anti-Confucian ethics align programmatically with their thought [modern-scholarly-inference]. Historians note Cao Cao "came into contact with a lot of legalist thought" and "gradually turned to forceful means" [attested; web: https://inf.news/en/news/ad927192cf0d8d9f6f71f60d9995bdb8.html].
- **Earlier yuefu tradition** — he reuses traditional titles (*Haoli*, *Xielu*, *Duan ge xing*) while filling them with personal political content [documented].
- **Shijing 詩經** — the old four-character meter that Watson credits him with revitalizing [attested].

## Contradictions and Open Questions (Preserved, Not Smoothed)

- **The Red Cliffs / Sun Tzu paradox**: his commentary on Sunzi is universally praised, yet his worst defeat (208 Chibi) arguably violated Sunzi's warnings on fighting on unfamiliar terrain and overreliance on numbers [attested vs. documented tension].
- **"Hunting letter" numbers (800,000)** [attested in *Sanguozhi*] vs. Zhou Yu's ~220,000 estimate [attested in *Sanguozhi*] — preserved as a contradiction in the source itself, not a later interpolation.
- **讓縣令 sincerity**: read by de Crespigny as strategic autobiography; read by some Chinese scholars as genuine self-disclosure; read by the traditional *Yanyi* 演義 romance tradition as hypocrisy. **Textual record is consistent; interpretations diverge.**
- **Zhonghua Shuju edition dating**: Western scholarship commonly cites "1974" (reissue); the underlying 1959 edition is the actual first publication. The 2013 revised critical edition exists but is less cited in Anglophone work [attested].
- **Attribution of Daoist-themed *Qiu hu xing* and *Qi chu chang*** — collected in modern editions but authorship occasionally questioned [modern-scholarly-inference].
- **遺令 textual problems** — Meow Hui Goh (EMC 2013) notes "the longest received version of his will is fraught with textual problems," implying the received text may conflate multiple late-life utterances [attested].
