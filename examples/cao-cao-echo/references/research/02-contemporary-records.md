# 02 — Contemporary and Near-Contemporary Records of Cao Cao 曹操

*Research Agent 2 (Contemporary Records) — scope: how contemporaries and near-contemporaries recorded him. Companion document to other agents' outputs on works, deeds, and reception.*

## Introduction — why this file exists

Cao Cao 曹操 (155–220 CE) is one of the most source-contested figures in Chinese history *already within his own era's written record* — not merely because later folk culture (esp. Luo Guanzhong's 14th-c. *Sanguo Yanyi*) blackened him, but because the near-contemporary textual layer itself is structurally adversarial: a pro-Wei official history, a rival-state hostile biography, a Jin-dynasty synthesis, and a 5th-century critical-apparatus annotation all survive. Pei Songzhi's 429 CE commentary to Chen Shou's *Sanguo Zhi* is unusual in world historiography because it *preserves* the disagreements side by side rather than harmonising them. This file reconstructs what contemporaries and near-contemporaries actually wrote and flags the bias-vector of each source.

**Labelling convention used below:**
- `[documented]` — direct primary-source attestation, text extant.
- `[attested]` — reported in a near-contemporary source (Sanguo Zhi, its cited sources, Hou Han Shu) but with some transmission uncertainty.
- `[attributed]` — quoted speech or act credited to Cao Cao by a single late/hostile source; credibility varies.
- `[legendary]` — embellishment not in pre-5th-century records, or demonstrably novelistic.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` — reconstruction by historians (de Crespigny, etc.) from the patchwork of sources.

## Dimension 1 — *Sanguo Zhi* 三國志 · *Wei Shu · Wudi ji* 魏書·武帝紀 by Chen Shou 陳壽

### 1.1 The author and his position

Chen Shou 陳壽 (233–297 CE, courtesy name Chengzuo 承祚) was born ~13 years after Cao Cao's death. He served Shu-Han as an official before its fall in 263, then transferred to Western Jin (which had just succeeded Wei). His *Sanguo Zhi* was compiled under the Jin dynasty; it was considered a private work during his lifetime and only elevated to official-history status after his death. `[documented: en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Shou]`

**Bias vector:** Because Jin inherited legitimacy from Wei (via Sima Yi → Sima Shi → Sima Zhao → Sima Yan), Chen Shou had political-structural reasons to legitimise Wei as the Mandate-bearing successor to Han. He places *Wei Shu* (30 juan) before *Shu Shu* (15 juan) and *Wu Shu* (20 juan); uses Wei reign-eras as the dating backbone; and reserves imperial nomenclature ("帝") for Wei rulers (including Cao Cao posthumously as Wei Wudi 魏武帝 "Emperor Wu of Wei") while calling Shu and Wu rulers "先主" / "主" (Former Ruler / Ruler). `[documented] [web: http://www.chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Historiography/sanguozhi.html]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms]`

However, Chen Shou's reputation in Chinese historiography is that of a relatively restrained and honest compiler. He does preserve materially unflattering events (Xu Province massacre, the Lü Boshe killing in one laundered version, the Nine Bestowals) — he just frames them with the minimum commentary that political survival under Jin allowed. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

### 1.2 Structure of the *Wudi ji*

The *Annals of Emperor Wu* is Vol. 1 of the *Wei Shu* portion of *Sanguo Zhi*. It is written in the *benji* 本紀 (imperial-annals) form — year-by-year, edict-by-edict, campaign-by-campaign — the same form reserved in Chinese tradition for reigning emperors. This is the formal device through which Chen Shou asserts Cao Cao's imperial legitimacy, even though Cao Cao himself never assumed the throne. `[documented]`

The annals cover:
- origins in Qiao 譙 (Pei 沛 commandery), descent from eunuch family through his father Cao Song 曹嵩 (adopted son of the eunuch Cao Teng 曹騰) — a fact Chen Shou records without euphemism;
- early career: 174 CE official ranks (Northern Commandant of Luoyang 洛陽北部尉), 184 anti-Yellow-Turban campaigns, 189 flight from Dong Zhuo 董卓;
- consolidation 190–200: defeat of Yuan Shu, Lü Bu, reception of Emperor Xian at Xu 許 in 196;
- Guandu 200, Northern campaigns 204–207 (Ye 鄴, Wuhuan), Red Cliffs 208, consolidation of the north 211–215;
- Duke of Wei 213, King of Wei 216, Hanzhong campaign 219, death at Luoyang 220. `[documented]`

### 1.3 Chen Shou's closing appraisal 評曰

The end of the *Wudi ji* carries Chen Shou's famously tight *pingyue* summary. The Chinese text:

> 評曰：漢末，天下大亂，雄豪並起，而袁紹虎摉四州，強盛莫敵。太祖運籌演謀，鞭撻宇內，攬申、商之法術，該韓、白之奇策，官方授材，各因其器，矯情任算，不念舊惡，終能總御皇機，克成洪業者，惟其明略最優也。抑可謂非常之人，超世之傑矣。

Working translation: *"At the end of Han, the realm fell into great disorder and heroes arose in every direction; Yuan Shao alone straddled four provinces, his strength unmatched. Taizu [Cao Cao] laid his schemes and marshalled his plans, whipped the realm into order, drew on the legalist craft of Shen [Buhai] and Shang [Yang] and the military surprise of Han [Xin] and Bai [Qi]. He appointed officials according to talent, suited each to his vessel; he mastered feelings by calculation (矯情任算) and did not dwell on old grievances. Thus in the end he grasped the imperial machinery and founded the great enterprise — this was owing to the superiority of his strategic intelligence alone. He may indeed be called an extraordinary man (非常之人), a hero surpassing his age (超世之傑)."* `[documented] [web: https://m.gushiwen.cn/guwen/bookv_0138593aee5d.aspx]`

Three things to notice about this appraisal:
1. **It is unambiguously favourable** — Chen Shou uses "超世之傑" (hero surpassing his age) as the capstone.
2. **It is politically careful**: no word about usurpation or the deaths of Kong Rong, Xun Yu, or the Xu Province civilians. Those are *recorded in the narrative* but absent from the summary.
3. **The phrase 矯情任算 ("mastering/manipulating feelings by calculation")** is double-edged — it can be read as either self-discipline or as the instrumental-emotional approach later hostile tradition would seize on as "奸" (cunning/treacherous). Chen Shou is likely trying to split the difference. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

## Dimension 2 — Pei Songzhi's 裴松之 commentary (429 CE)

Pei Songzhi 裴松之 (372–451), under commission from Emperor Wen of Liu-Song (r. 424–453), completed his annotation in 429 CE. His method was *not* to emend the text but to quote rival and supplementary sources at length — preserving contradictions Chen Shou had resolved or omitted. The result is roughly 2× the original's length. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annotated_Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pei_Songzhi]`

### 2.2 Pei's method — what it means for reconstruction

Pei does two things unusually well for a pre-modern Chinese commentator: (1) when sources disagree, he gives both and sometimes adds his own judgement ("臣松之以為…"), and (2) he is explicit when he thinks a cited source is unreliable. His commentary on the Lü Boshe material, for instance, gives all three variants without deciding among them. This makes the Pei layer indispensable for any serious reconstruction of Cao Cao. `[documented]` `[modern-scholarly-inference — de Crespigny, *Imperial Warlord*, 2010]` `[web: https://brill.com/display/title/18666?language=en]`

## Dimension 3 — *Hou Han Shu* 後漢書 by Fan Ye 范曄

Fan Ye 范曄 (398–445) compiled the *Hou Han Shu* (*Book of the Later Han*) in the Liu-Song period, drawing on earlier now-lost Han histories (esp. Hua Qiao 華嶠's version). It appeared after Pei's annotation (~429) but uses mostly independent source material. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao]`

**Bias vector:** Fan Ye writes from the *Han court's* point of view. Where Chen Shou treats Cao Cao as a proto-emperor building a new legitimate order, Fan Ye treats him as a **controller of the sovereign** — the man who held Emperor Xian hostage at Xu Chang and whose son finally took the throne by a forced abdication. Yuan Hong 袁宏's *Hou Han Ji* (another Later-Han chronicle also consulted by Fan Ye) is even more explicit about this framing. Fan Ye's *Hou Han Shu* "describes a ruthless overthrow of the Han court by Cao Cao and his son Cao Pi." `[documented] [web: https://brill.com/view/journals/tpao/104/5-6/article-p511_3.xml]`

Key sections relevant to Cao Cao appear not in a dedicated biography (since he was "Han Chancellor" not "Han subject") but in:
- the annals of Emperor Xian (漢獻帝紀);
- the biographies of Kong Rong (孔融傳), Xun Yu (荀彧傳), Yang Biao (楊彪傳, father of Yang Xiu), Dong Cheng (董承傳, leader of the 200 CE anti-Cao plot);
- the "Dong Zhuo Biography" collective.

Fan Ye's unique contribution: his accounts of Han-loyalist reactions to Cao Cao's rise (the Yi Tong conspiracy 衣帶詔 of 200, Dong Cheng's execution, Empress Fu 伏皇后's attempted letter of appeal in 214 which led Cao Cao to have her and her sons killed) are the most complete. `[attested]`

## Dimension 4 — Specific Episodes and Source-Triangulation

### 4.1 The Xu Shao 許劭 evaluation

**The line:** "治世之能臣，亂世之奸雄" — "*In an age of good governance, a capable minister; in an age of chaos, a cunning hero / crafty villain.*" The character 奸 (*jian*) is ambiguous between "treacherous/wicked" and "cunning/crafty" — both readings were already available in Han usage.

**Earliest attested source:** Sun Sheng's *Yi Tong Za Yu* 異同雜語, cited in Pei Songzhi's commentary to Sanguo Zhi 1 (Wudi ji). The evaluator is Xu Shao 許劭 (150–195), a Runan 汝南 scholar who ran with his cousin Xu Jing 許靖 the famous *Yuedan ping* 月旦評 ("First-of-the-month assessments") of notable persons. `[attested] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xu_Shao]`

**Variants:**
- *Yi Tong Za Yu* (Sun Sheng, 4th c., cited by Pei): Xu Shao initially refuses; Cao Cao pressures; Xu relents with "君清平之奸賊，亂世之英雄" (or in another transmission "治世之能臣，亂世之姦雄"). Cao Cao is said to have laughed and departed pleased.
- *Hou Han Shu* (Fan Ye, 5th c.), Biography of Xu Shao: gives a harsher framing — Cao Cao *coerced* Xu Shao into the assessment; the line is rendered as more damning ("清平之姦賊，亂世之英雄" — "a treacherous villain in an orderly age, a hero in a chaotic one"). Fan Ye's framing makes Cao Cao menacing.
- *Sanguo Zhi* proper (Chen Shou, 3rd c.): does **not** contain the line in the main text. The line reaches us only via Pei's citations and via the Hou Han Shu. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao]`

**Bias read:** The line is almost certainly authentic in some form (multiple independent attestations, contemporary ambient knowledge of the *Yuedan ping*), but the *exact phrasing* most people now quote is a composite that favours the sharper reading. The bare fact that Xu Shao said something ambivalent about Cao Cao is secure; the elegant "治世之能臣，亂世之奸雄" formulation is a stabilised literary version.

### 4.2 The Lü Boshe 呂伯奢 household killing — three variants

This is the *locus classicus* for preserved disagreement in the Pei layer. Pei quotes three sources to the Sanguo Zhi passage about Cao Cao's flight eastward from Dong Zhuo (189–190 CE). All three agree that Cao Cao, an acquaintance of Lü Boshe, stopped at his home during the flight and that deaths occurred; they disagree sharply on motive and on whether Cao Cao said anything afterward. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%BC_Boshe]`

**Variant A — *Wei Shu* 魏書 (Wang Chen, pro-Wei official):**
> 太祖以卓終必覆敗，遂不就拜，逃歸鄉里。從數騎過故人成皋呂伯奢；伯奢不在，其子與賓客共劫太祖，取馬及物，太祖手刃擊殺數人。

*Cao Cao judged Dong Zhuo would fall, declined the appointment, and fled home. With a few riders he passed his old friend Lü Boshe's home at Chenggao. Lü was out. His sons and guests tried to rob Cao Cao of his horse and goods. Cao killed several of them.* **No remorse line.** Pure self-defence. `[attested — pro-Wei sanitisation]`

**Variant B — *Shi Yu* 世語 (Guo Ban, Jin anecdotal):**
> 太祖過伯奢。伯奢出行，五子皆在，備賓主禮。太祖自以背卓命，疑其圖己，手劍夜殺八人而去。

*Cao Cao visited Lü Boshe. Boshe was out; his five sons were home and received him with full guest-host rites. Cao Cao, because he had defied Dong Zhuo, suspected they were plotting against him. He took his sword and in the night killed eight people and fled.* The killings are **preemptive on suspicion** — the hosts were innocent. **Still no remorse line.** `[attested]`

**Variant C — *Za Ji* 雜記 (Sun Sheng, Jin critical-moralist):**
> 太祖聞其食器聲，以為圖己，遂夜殺之。既而悽愴曰：「寧我負人，毋人負我！」遂行。

*Cao Cao heard the sound of cookware and, thinking they were plotting against him, killed them by night. Afterwards, mournful, he said: "Better that I wrong others than that others wrong me!" — and departed.* `[attested — in the bitterest / most philosophically weighted form]`

**Critical observation on 4.2:** The phrase most associated with Cao Cao in popular memory — "寧我負人，毋人負我" — comes from *only one source*, Sun Sheng's *Za Ji*, preserved only because Pei cited it. It is absent from both Chen Shou's main text and the pro-Wei *Wei Shu*. The *Sanguo Yanyi* novelisation changes "人" (a person / others) to "天下人" (the people of the realm) to produce the far more cosmic "寧教我負天下人，休教天下人負我" — that escalation is **Luo Guanzhong's**, not contemporary. `[documented]` `[web: https://www.tumblr.com/siumerghe/653067858778144768/an-examination-of-cao-caos-i-would-rather-betray]`

### 4.3 The Xu Province 徐州 massacres, 193–194 CE

**Sources and their numbers:**
- *Sanguo Zhi* Wudi ji (Chen Shou): Cao Cao attacks Tao Qian 陶謙 to avenge his father Cao Song 曹嵩 (murdered en route from Langya to Cao Cao in 193); Chen Shou records the campaign took cities including Pengcheng 彭城 but gives minimal civilian-casualty language.
- *Hou Han Shu* (Fan Ye): Tao Qian's garrison at Yinping murdered Cao Song for his wealth; Cao Cao's retaliation killed "hundreds of thousands" of men and women, bodies stoppered up the Si River (泗水為之不流). `[attested]`
- *Wu Shu* (Wei Zhao 韋昭, the Wu-state official history, cited by Pei): blames Tao Qian directly — says Tao dispatched the commandant Zhang Kai 張闓 with 200 guards who then killed Cao Song. This attribution matters because Chen Shou, writing under a successor-of-Wei regime, has motive to indict Tao Qian; Wu historians had less.
- *Hou Han Shu* biography of Tao Qian and the *Zizhi Tongjian* (Sima Guang, Song) consolidate to: the 泗水不流 ("Si River ceased to flow" from corpses) description.

**Modern scholarly assessment (de Crespigny, *Imperial Warlord*, 2010):** the massacres are historical but the figure "hundreds of thousands" is almost certainly rhetorical. The point of the sources preserving this material is that *even Chen Shou, who was otherwise protective of Cao Cao's memory, did not expunge the record*. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao's_invasion_of_Xu_Province]`

### 4.4 Battle of Guandu 官渡 (200 CE)

Cao Cao's decisive defeat of Yuan Shao 袁紹's numerically superior army. `[documented]`

**Source treatment:**
- *Sanguo Zhi* Wudi ji: the core narrative — Yuan Shao ~100,000 men vs. Cao Cao ~30,000–40,000; Cao Cao's night raid on Wuchao 烏巢 grain depot (Oct. 200) decisive; Yuan Shao's army collapses.
- *Sanguo Zhi* biography of Yuan Shao (Wei Shu 6): preserves detail on Xu You 許攸's defection, which delivered the Wuchao intelligence.
- **Unflattering fact preserved in the main text:** Chen Shou records that Cao Cao buried alive the surrendered remainder of Yuan Shao's army — figures of 70,000–80,000 executed prisoners appear in the sources. This is in the **primary text**, not an annotation — showing Chen Shou did not expunge mass killings where they were strategically material. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Guandu]`
- Pei's annotations add Guo Jia 郭嘉's "ten victories vs. ten defeats" memorandum (*十勝十敗論*) — the classic analytic set-piece — from the *Fu Zi* 傅子 by Fu Xuan.

### 4.5 Kong Rong 孔融 execution, 208 CE

**Formal charges (per Hou Han Shu biography of Kong Rong and Pei's citations):** plotting rebellion (謀反), slandering the court (謗訕朝廷), and impiety / disrespect of the ruler (大不敬 / 不孝言論). The "impiety" charge was built on Kong Rong's provocations — e.g. suggesting that children owe no special loyalty to parents who merely "stored them in a vessel" (a satirical thrust at Cao Cao's filial rhetoric) — a line Cao Cao had collected as ammunition. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kong_Rong]`

**Sources:** Hou Han Shu (the main, critical narrative); *Wei Shi Chunqiu* of Sun Sheng cited by Pei; *Hou Han Ji* of Yuan Hong. Chen Shou's *Sanguo Zhi* has no standalone Kong Rong biography — his treatment is oblique. Fan Ye devotes full attention. Kong Rong, as a Han loyalist *par excellence* (descendant of Confucius, 20th generation), had become dangerous; the execution of his household (including famously two young children) is fully recorded. `[documented]`

**Kong Rong was one of the original "Seven Masters of Jian'an" 建安七子** — so his execution is the literal intersection of Cao Cao's literary patronage and his political intolerance. `[documented]`

### 4.6 Xun Yu 荀彧 death, 212 CE

Xun Yu was Cao Cao's most consequential civilian adviser — the architect of the "welcoming the emperor" strategy of 196 and Cao Cao's senior administrator for sixteen years. In 212 he is said to have **opposed Dong Zhao's 董昭 proposal** that Cao Cao be advanced to Duke of Wei with the Nine Bestowals (九錫) — a proposal everyone in the court understood as the penultimate step before the Cao family's eventual usurpation. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xun_Yu]` `[web: https://threestatesrecords.com/wp-content/uploads/2018/09/the-two-xun.pdf]`

**Source variants on his death:**
- *Sanguo Zhi* Xun Yu biography (Chen Shou): Xun Yu was sent to the Qiao army on the Sun Quan campaign; fell ill at Shouchun 壽春; died of illness. **Neutral, natural death.** `[attested — Chen Shou's softest framing]`
- *Wei Shi Chunqiu* 魏氏春秋 of Sun Sheng, cited by Pei: Cao Cao sent Xun Yu a food container; Xun Yu opened it, found it **empty**, understood the message, and took poison. The empty-box version. `[attested — critical source]`
- *Hou Han Shu* (Fan Ye): frames Xun Yu's death as politically motivated suicide; notes the closeness to his opposition to the Nine Bestowals. `[documented]`
- *Zizhi Tongjian* (Sima Guang, Song): settles on poisoning by his own hand in the 10th month of Jian'an 17.

**What is at stake:** whether Cao Cao drove his closest adviser to suicide for political disloyalty. Chen Shou's version exonerates him; Sun Sheng's implicates him. Both are within the contemporary-record envelope. Modern consensus (de Crespigny and others) is that *the closeness of the death to the Dong Zhao proposal is too striking to be coincidence*, but that the "empty box" is probably a literary concretisation of a more complex cold-shouldering. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

### 4.7 Yang Xiu 楊修 execution, 219 CE

Yang Xiu (175–219), son of Yang Biao 楊彪 (the Han loyalist minister), was a celebrated wit and low-level Cao Cao secretariat official.

**Sources:**
- *Sanguo Zhi*, Chen Si Wang (Cao Zhi) biography: notes Yang Xiu was killed in Cao Cao's final year; brief mention.
- Pei's citations from *Dian Lüe* 典略 and *Jiu Zhou Chunqiu* 九州春秋 add the famous "chicken ribs" 雞肋 anecdote (during the 219 Hanzhong retreat, Cao Cao's night-watch codeword was "chicken ribs" — "not worth much but a pity to discard" — and Yang Xiu instructed the camp to pack up, having correctly divined Cao's mind; Cao Cao had him executed for insolence). `[attested]`
- The formal reasons Chen Shou records are (a) connection with Yuan Shu (Yang Xiu's mother was Yuan Shu's sister), and (b) involvement on Cao Zhi's 曹植 side in the succession struggle against Cao Pi. `[documented]`

**Bias caveat:** the "chicken ribs" line is widely treated as **literary** — the *Romance* popularised it but Pei's citation is the earliest surviving attestation. The political motives (Yuan connection, succession) are the likely actual cause. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Xiu_(Han_dynasty)]`

**Jian'an connection:** Yang Xiu was a regular participant in the Ye city literary salon — his execution, like Kong Rong's, marks a point where the Cao court's literary culture and its political ruthlessness meet in the same body.

### 4.8 Hua Tuo 華佗 killing

**Sources:** *Sanguo Zhi* has a standalone biography of Hua Tuo in the *Fangshi* (方技) section; *Hou Han Shu* also carries a biography.

**Account (Chen Shou):** Hua Tuo, summoned repeatedly to treat Cao Cao's chronic headaches, made excuses (his wife's illness) to avoid permanent service. Cao Cao discovered the deception, had Hua Tuo imprisoned and executed. Cao Cao is recorded to have *later regretted* the decision — specifically when his prodigy son Cao Chong 曹沖 died of illness in 208 and no physician of comparable skill remained. `[attested] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hua_Tuo]`

**Scholarly note:** this episode is one of the few where Chen Shou records Cao Cao's regret explicitly in the main text. The *Sanguo Yanyi* later transforms this into the elaborate skull-opening plot (Hua Tuo proposing trepanation, Cao Cao suspecting assassination) — **that is novelistic**, not in the primary sources. `[legendary — for the trepanation story; the bare execution and regret are documented]`

### 4.9 Battle of Red Cliffs 赤壁 (208 CE)

Cao Cao's greatest defeat. The textual handling is a classic case of perspective-dependent narration. `[documented]`

**Numbers:**
- Cao Cao's letter to Sun Quan (quoted in *Sanguo Zhi* Wu Shu / Sun Quan biography, per Pei's citation of Jiang Biao Zhuan 江表傳): claims "800,000 water forces" coming to "hunt with the General in Wu" — an explicit psychological-warfare threat, not a literal count.
- Zhou Yu 周瑜's briefing to Sun Quan (*Sanguo Zhi* Wu Shu, Zhou Yu biography): estimates Cao Cao has perhaps 150,000–160,000 northern troops plus 70,000–80,000 newly-impressed former Jing Province 荊州 troops under Liu Biao's son Liu Cong (low morale, unfamiliar with water warfare) — so ~220,000–240,000 real effectives at most. `[documented]`
- Modern scholarly consensus (de Crespigny): Cao Cao's actual deployable force at Red Cliffs probably 80,000–100,000, vs. allied Sun-Liu force of ~50,000. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

**Causes of defeat (multi-source):**
- **Disease / plague** in Cao Cao's army — mentioned in Cao Cao's *own* subsequent letter ("因燒船自退" / "it was only because of sickness that I burned my own ships and retreated") — this is Cao Cao's face-saving framing; preserved in Pei's citation of Jiang Biao Zhuan. `[attested]`
- **Huang Gai 黃蓋's fire attack** — preserved in the Wu Shu biographies of Zhou Yu and Huang Gai. Wu sources emphasise the fire; Wei sources emphasise the plague. `[documented]`
- **Sun-Liu alliance** formed through Lu Su 魯肅 and Zhuge Liang 諸葛亮's diplomacy — documented in the Wu Shu and Shu Shu.

**The interesting thing historiographically:** Chen Shou's *Wudi ji* treats Red Cliffs briefly and euphemistically — "meeting unfavourable conditions, [Cao Cao] returned troops." The **full** account emerges only when the Wu-side biographies are read alongside. This is a clear instance of Chen Shou's pro-Wei compositional choices. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Red_Cliffs]`

### 4.10 Duke of Wei (213) → King of Wei (216)

**Chronology from *Sanguo Zhi* Wudi ji:**
- **213 CE (Jian'an 18):** Cao Cao declines, then accepts, the title 魏公 Duke of Wei with a fief of ten commanderies (parts of present Hebei, Henan) and the **Nine Bestowals** 九錫 — a ritual package (chariot, clothing, musical instruments, crimson doors, raised thresholds, imperial guard, battle-axe, bow-and-arrows, black millet wine) that had been conferred only on Wang Mang before his usurpation. The symbolic parallel was unmistakable to all Han officials — this is what Xun Yu had died opposing the year before. `[documented]`
- **216 CE (Jian'an 21):** elevated from Duke to **King** 魏王 — a rank in principle reserved for imperial relatives. Cao Cao, a non-Liu, holding the title of King was already a post-Han-dynasty concession in all but name.
- **217 CE:** named Cao Pi heir-apparent (in the Wei state), formalising the succession that would produce the 220 abdication.
- **220 CE:** Cao Cao dies at Luoyang in the 1st month; Cao Pi accepts Emperor Xian's "abdication" in the 10th month; Cao Cao is posthumously honoured as Wei Wudi 魏武帝 "Martial Emperor of Wei". `[documented]`

**Source gradient:** Chen Shou's *Sanguo Zhi* records these steps in dignified imperial-annals form (the form is itself the claim of legitimacy). Fan Ye's *Hou Han Shu* records the same facts as the progressive destruction of the Han constitutional order. Both are right about what happened; they differ on what to call it. `[documented]`

### 4.11 Jian'an 建安 literary salon at Ye 鄴

After Cao Cao's consolidation of Ye (Yuan Shao's former capital) in 204, his court there became the major literary center of the age. The core group:
- The **Three Caos** 三曹 — Cao Cao 曹操 (the father); Cao Pi 曹丕 (future Wei Wendi, author of the *Dian Lun* 典論, the earliest major Chinese work of literary criticism); Cao Zhi 曹植 (the poet-prodigy).
- The **Seven Masters of Jian'an** 建安七子: Kong Rong 孔融, Chen Lin 陳琳, Wang Can 王粲, Xu Gan 徐幹, Ruan Yu 阮瑀, Ying Yang 應瑒, Liu Zhen 劉楨. The grouping is codified in Cao Pi's *Dian Lun · Lun Wen* 典論·論文. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jian'an_poetry]` `[web: http://www.chinaknowledge.de/History/Division/personsjiananqizi.html]`

**Significance for the contemporary-record layer:** four of these seven served Cao Cao directly, and Wang Can authored the *Ying Xiong Ji* — one of Pei Songzhi's cited sources. Kong Rong was executed by Cao Cao. Five of the seven died in the **Jian'an 22 (217 CE) plague** that swept the Ye area — preserved in a famously moving letter from Cao Pi to Wu Zhi 吳質. The same literary world that produced the most intimate testimonies of Cao Cao is the world whose members Cao Cao could also kill. `[documented]`

## Dimension 5 — Public acts, self-representation, and peer characterisations

### 5.1 Cao Cao's own voice

Unusually for a Chinese warlord, Cao Cao left a substantial first-person corpus preserved in the *Sanguo Zhi* Wudi ji (and more fully in later compilations like the Ming-dynasty *Cao Cao Ji* 曹操集):
- **Edicts** — especially the **求賢令** ("Edicts for Seeking Talent") of 210, 214, 217, declaring "唯才是舉" ("only ability counts"), explicitly overriding Confucian moral-character requirements for office;
- The **讓縣自明本志令** ("Edict Returning Counties and Clarifying My Original Intent"), 210 CE — Cao Cao's apologia pro vita sua, in which he denies imperial ambition and claims only to have "wished to be a good Han general" `[documented]`;
- Military orders preserved by the Wei Shu;
- Poetry — the *yuefu* 樂府 poems "Hao Li Xing" 蒿里行 (on the civilian suffering of civil war), "Duan Ge Xing" 短歌行 (the "Short Song"), "Guan Cang Hai" 觀滄海, "Gui Sui Shou" 龜雖壽, etc. These are preserved in the *Wen Xuan* 文選 (6th c., Xiao Tong) and the *Yue Fu Shi Ji* 樂府詩集. The poetry is **not** filtered through Chen Shou and provides the most unmediated contemporary voice we have. `[documented]`

### 5.2 How allies characterised him

- **Xun Yu** 荀彧 (adviser 192–212): reportedly called him "曠世之才" (a talent of an age) in early correspondence preserved via Pei. Treated Cao Cao as Han's protector until 212. `[attested]`
- **Xun You** 荀攸 (nephew of Xun Yu, strategist): served loyally; the Xun You biography is one of the cleanest pro-Cao testimonies in *Sanguo Zhi*.
- **Guo Jia** 郭嘉 (strategist, d. 207): authored the "Ten Victories / Ten Defeats" memorandum (*十勝十敗論*, preserved in *Fu Zi* via Pei) that articulated why Cao Cao would beat Yuan Shao. Cao Cao is recorded to have wept at Guo Jia's death and attributed part of the Red Cliffs disaster to his absence. `[attested]`
- **Cheng Yu** 程昱 (adviser): preserved as ruthlessly pragmatic; the *Sanguo Zhi* biography notes (with some sources claiming) that Cheng Yu supplied Cao Cao's army with human-flesh rations during a famine (this is in the Pei commentary, not the main text, and the attribution is contested). `[attributed — Cao Man Zhuan–adjacent]`

### 5.3 How enemies characterised him

- **Yuan Shao**: Chen Lin 陳琳's famous anti-Cao proclamation (檄文) of 200, preserved in the *Wen Xuan*, attacks Cao Cao's eunuch-descended lineage in the most brutal terms — calling him a "descendant of foulness" via his grandfather Cao Teng. After Guandu Chen Lin was captured, pardoned, and absorbed into the Cao court as one of the Jian'an Seven Masters — one of the more remarkable career reversals of the age. `[documented]`
- **Liu Bei**: treated Cao Cao as the defining enemy of Han legitimacy; the *Shu Shu* biographies show this was a long-standing rhetorical stance.
- **Sun Quan**: pragmatic opposition; Sun Quan's letter to Cao Cao after Red Cliffs reportedly urged him "to take the throne" — a famously double-edged compliment that Cao Cao is said (per Pei's citation of *Wei Lüe*) to have dismissed with the retort "this fellow wants to set me over the fire!" (是兒欲踞吾著爐火上邪). `[attested]`
- **Kong Rong**: most persistent rhetorical opponent within the Cao court itself, until his 208 execution.

## Dimension 6 — The Cao Cao Tomb discovery (2009, Xigaoxue 西高穴)

### 6.1 What was found

In December 2008, local workers digging for kiln mud in Xigaoxue village, Anfeng township, Anyang county, Henan, uncovered a large Eastern Han–period tomb (designated *M2*). The discovery was announced publicly on 27 December 2009 by the Henan Provincial Cultural Heritage Bureau. `[documented] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao_Mausoleum]`

**Finds:**
- 250+ relics including stone reliefs of daily life;
- **Eight stone tablets** (圭形石牌) inscribed "魏武王" (*Wei Wu Wang*, "King Wu of Wei" — Cao Cao's final self-bestowed title);
- Weapons, stone pillows labelled as personal belongings of "Wei Wu Wang";
- **Three skeletons**: one male aged ~60s, one female ~50s, one female ~20s.

### 6.2 Match with documented record

Cao Cao in 218 issued the **Terminal Edict** (終令) ordering a "thin burial" (薄葬) — no gold or jade, no grave goods of value, no tumulus, a location chosen for "barren high ground." The Xigaoxue tomb matches this specification *unusually well*: modest for an imperial-class tomb, no tumulus, high ground west of Ye. `[documented]`

### 6.3 Authenticity debate

**For authenticity** (Henan provincial archaeological authorities, 2010 State Administration of Cultural Heritage endorsement):
- Location and period match;
- The "Wei Wu Wang" inscriptions correlate with Cao Cao's exact final title;
- The "thin burial" architecture matches his recorded edict;
- Lu Qian 陸潛's 4th-century epitaph (preserved via the *Yuan He Jun Xian Tu Zhi* 元和郡縣圖志) places Cao Cao's tomb in this area.

**Against authenticity** (23-expert Suzhou forum, August 2010):
- Grave robbers had disturbed the site — provenance of inscribed tablets questionable;
- "魏武王" title-usage is anachronistic in form — the question is whether Cao Cao would have been so titled contemporaneously on burial goods;
- Henan provincial government has tourism-revenue motive;
- An alternative proposal (Sept 2010, *Kaogu* 考古 journal article) holds that M2 and its partner tomb belong instead to **Cao Huan 曹奐** (the last Wei emperor) and his father Cao Yu 曹宇 (a son of Cao Cao). `[documented] [web: https://www.globaltimes.cn/content/496527.shtml]`

### 6.4 DNA work (Fudan University, 2009–2016)

Li Hui 李輝's team at Fudan attempted to identify Cao Cao's Y-chromosome haplotype via comparison with self-identified descendant clans and with ancient DNA from the tooth of Cao Cao's **granduncle Cao Ding 曹鼎** (from a verified separate tomb). The published finding (*Journal of Human Genetics*, 2013 and follow-ups through 2016) identified the Cao family paternal lineage as **Y-haplogroup O2-M268+ (F1462+, PK4−)**, and noted a significant enrichment of this haplotype in claimed-descendant clans. `[documented] [web: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22189622/]`

**Caveats** (as flagged by scholars like Zhu Ziyan 朱子彥):
- The Cao Ding DNA match confirms the *Cao clan* haplotype, not directly Cao Cao personally;
- No DNA was (or plausibly could be) extracted from the Xigaoxue M2 skeletons to match against — so the DNA work cannot independently confirm the tomb identification;
- The haplogroup finding is genuinely informative about the Cao lineage but does not close the Xigaoxue debate.

### 6.5 Current (post-2016) consensus

The mainstream Chinese archaeological establishment has moved toward accepting Xigaoxue M2 as Cao Cao's mausoleum, and in 2018 the site was re-developed as a protected archaeological park. A substantial minority of specialists (the Suzhou-forum tradition) continue to dissent. The best we can say for the Echo project is: **the tomb is probably Cao Cao's; the "thin burial" architecture confirms his documented austere-burial edict; the inscribed tablets are the strongest positive evidence but remain the most disputed.** `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

## Synthesis — the shape of the contemporary record

Three features of Cao Cao's contemporary record make him unusual:

1. **Structural adversariality of the sources.** Most major historical figures of the early medieval Chinese world are remembered through a single dominant source tradition. Cao Cao has at least four competing ones (pro-Wei *Wei Shu*, pro-Wu *Cao Man Zhuan*, Han-loyalist *Hou Han Shu*, and Jin-moralist *Za Ji* / *Wei Shi Chunqiu*), and Pei Songzhi preserved all of them in view of each other.

2. **His own voice survives at exceptional length.** Between edicts, letters preserved in the *Wei Shu* and *Wei Lüe*, and the poetic corpus transmitted through the *Wen Xuan*, we have more first-person Cao Cao than we have first-person material from most of his peer warlords combined. This is why de Crespigny can title a book *Imperial Warlord* and actually quote Cao Cao in it.

3. **The archaeological layer now constrains the textual.** Post-2009, any account of Cao Cao's burial practice and material culture must also match Xigaoxue M2 (on the mainstream identification). The "thin burial" edict (documented in Chen Shou) and the "thin burial" tomb (found in Anyang) corroborate each other — and, incidentally, refute the legend of the "72 decoy tombs" that had entered folk memory via *Sanguo Yanyi*–era sources. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

**Implication for the Echo distillation:** Cao Cao should be modelled not as a single canonical figure but as a *resolution* of the adversarial record — a man whose documented actions are relatively stable across the sources (he did take the Nine Bestowals; he did massacre in Xu Province; he did kill Kong Rong, drive Xun Yu to silence, execute Yang Xiu; he did lose Red Cliffs; he did write the poems) and whose inner disposition varies sharply depending on which near-contemporary source is granted primacy. The Echo should acknowledge and work within that variance.
