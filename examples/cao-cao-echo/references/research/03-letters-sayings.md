# Cao Cao 曹操 — Letters, Edicts, and Documented Sayings

**Scope**: The personal voice of Cao Cao (155–220 CE) as preserved in his own surviving prose, his attested speech, and the sayings that circulated about him. This file attempts to separate what is genuinely his voice (edicts, letters, poems collected in 《曹操集》) from later dramatizations (Sanguo Yanyi, 14th c.).

**Source labels used below:**
- `[documented]` = extant in standard critical edition (Zhonghua Shuju 《曹操集》, 1959/1974) with a pre-Tang textual witness
- `[attested]` = recorded in Chen Shou's 《三国志》 (3rd c.) or in Pei Songzhi's 裴松之 commentary (429 CE) citing a named earlier source
- `[attributed]` = later source (Tang through Ming), or a text where authenticity is contested
- `[legendary]` = originates in 《三国演义》 (14th c., >1100 years after Cao Cao) or post-Ming folk tradition; NOT Cao Cao's historical voice

**Hierarchy of translations used:** de Crespigny (scholarly biography and paraphrase); Stephen Owen (*An Anthology of Chinese Literature*, 1996); Burton Watson (where available); Jean Yuan/Vickie Fang (Classical Chinese Poetry project); ad-hoc scholarly translations; my own literal renderings flagged `[tr. RA3 working]`.

## I. KEY POLICY / PERSONAL PROSE DOCUMENTS

### 1. 《让县自明本志令》 Rang xian zi ming ben zhi ling — "The Edict Declining Fiefs and Clarifying My True Ambition" (also called 《述志令》 Shuzhi ling, "Edict Stating My Will"). Promulgated December 210 / January 211 CE.

**Status**: `[documented]` — preserved in 《三国志·魏书·武帝纪》 Pei Songzhi commentary citing 《魏武故事》 Wei Wu gushi. Collected in 《曹操集》 (Zhonghua Shuju, 1959), juan 2.

**Historical setting**: Emperor Xian had offered Cao Cao enlarged fiefs (three additional counties, 20,000 households) following the consolidation of the north. Cao Cao declined publicly in a long autobiographical manifesto — the earliest substantial Chinese autobiography outside historiographical tradition. De Crespigny (2010) calls it the "Apologia" and treats it as his most important self-presentation.

**Key passage 1 — the core provocation** (the whole text's signature line):

> 设使国家无有孤，不知当几人称帝，几人称王。
>
> "Were it not for me, no one knows how many there would be calling themselves emperor, how many calling themselves king." `[tr. RA3 working]`
>
> De Crespigny renders the spirit: Cao Cao asserts that "without him the empire would have fragmented into many more claimants." `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao]`

This line is quoted in virtually every modern Chinese biographical treatment (Gushiwen, Zhihu, Sohu cite the exact text). `[web: https://m.gushiwen.cn/mingju/juv_60f5e0a63a9a.aspx]`

**Key passage 2 — arc of ambition, from modest officer to marquis**:

> 孤始举孝廉，年少，自以本非岩穴知名之士…欲为一郡守，好作政教，以建立名誉，使世士明知之。
>
> "When I was first recommended as Filial and Incorrupt, I was young, and did not at all regard myself as a famous recluse of cliff or cave.… My wish was to be a commandery-governor, to administer well, to establish a name, and so make myself known to the men of the age." `[tr. RA3 working, following de Crespigny's summary]`

**Key passage 3 — self-defense against accusations of usurpation**:

> 或者人见孤强盛，又性不信天命之事，恐私心相评，言有不逊之志，妄相忖度，每用耿耿。
>
> "Perhaps, seeing my power grown great — and knowing that by temperament I do not hold with talk of Heaven's Mandate — men privately appraise me and whisper that I have disloyal ambitions; they measure me with falsehoods, and this weighs on me continually." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Key passage 4 — the iron refusal of the throne, using the Duke of Zhou model**:

> 然欲孤便尔委捐所典兵众以还执事，归就武平侯国，实不可也。何者？诚恐己离兵为人所祸也。既为子孙计，又己败则国家倾危，是以不得慕虚名而处实祸。
>
> "Yet were I to hand over my troops to the court and retire to my marquisate of Wuping — this in truth I cannot do. Why? I genuinely fear that once parted from my army, harm will come to me from others. For the sake of my descendants, also: were I ruined, the dynasty would totter. Therefore I cannot crave a hollow name and walk into real disaster." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Analysis of voice:**

- Cao Cao uses **孤** gū ("the solitary one") throughout, the formal first-person of a feudal lord — a claim of status short of the imperial 朕 zhèn.
- He mixes **flat statement of power** ("were it not for me…") with **self-minimizing narrative** ("I was young, I did not think myself a famous recluse") — a rhetorical move that de Crespigny calls "the iron hand in the iron glove — no velvet." The self-deprecation is tactical: he makes the reader concede he had no initial ambition, so that his current position is argued as response to circumstance.
- The logic of the refusal to disarm is brutally frank: **I would be killed if I surrendered my army.** This is not pious rhetoric; it is realpolitik written openly. Lu Xun 鲁迅 in his 1927 Guangzhou lecture 《魏晋风度及文章与药及酒之关系》 ("Wei-Jin Demeanor and the Relationship of Literature to Drugs and Wine") praised precisely this quality — he said Cao Cao was a very honest man (**"一个很有本事的人，至少是一个英雄"**, "a man of great ability, at the very least a hero") and that the directness of Cao Cao's official writings was itself a literary innovation against the "parallel prose" ornamentation of his age. `[web: https://www.atlantis-press.com/proceedings/ichess-25/126022456]`
- **Rhetorical structure**: (a) biographical narrative of modest origins → (b) escalating list of services rendered the Han → (c) admission that power is now irreversible → (d) refusal of the fief-expansion, not of the power → (e) the Duke of Zhou parallel: he will NOT take the throne, but he WILL not relinquish the army.

### 2. 《求贤令》 Qiu xian ling — The Three Edicts Seeking Talent (210 CE, 214 CE, 217 CE)

**Status**: `[documented]` — all three preserved in 《三国志·魏书·武帝纪》. Collected in 《曹操集》.

#### 2a. First Edict, Spring 210 CE — 《求贤令》

**Signature phrase — "recommend on merit alone":**

> 唯才是举，吾得而用之。
>
> "Recommend on the sole criterion of talent — those I get, I will use." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Key passage — the famous rhetorical question**:

> 若必廉士而后可用，则齐桓其何以霸世！今天下得无有被褐怀玉而钓于渭滨者乎？又得无盗嫂受金而未遇无知者乎？二三子其佐我明扬仄陋，唯才是举，吾得而用之。
>
> "If one must have only men of unblemished integrity before they can be used — then how did Duke Huan of Qi ever become hegemon of the age! Is there not somewhere under Heaven today a man in rough cloth, holding jade in his breast, fishing on the banks of the Wei? Is there not a man [like Chen Ping] who slept with his sister-in-law and took bribes, but whom no one has yet recognized? You, my two or three sons, assist me: illuminate and raise up the humble and obscure — recommend on talent alone, and I will use them." `[tr. RA3 working]`

The first example is Lü Shang (Jiang Ziya), fisherman-minister of King Wen of Zhou; the second is Chen Ping, Han founding-era minister repeatedly accused of adultery and corruption. Cao Cao is explicit: **the model ministers of the ancients would fail a Confucian character check.**

#### 2b. Second Edict, December 214 CE — 《敕有司取士毋废偏短令》

> 夫有行之士未必能进取，进取之士未必能有行也。陈平岂笃行，苏秦岂守信邪？而陈平定汉业，苏秦济弱燕。由此言之，士有偏短，庸可废乎！
>
> "Men of [conventional] virtuous conduct are not always able to move the age forward; men who move the age forward are not always of [conventional] virtuous conduct. Was Chen Ping a man of solid conduct? Was Su Qin faithful to his word? Yet Chen Ping settled the Han's great work, and Su Qin rescued weak Yan. Thus: if a man has a fault, how can that void his use?" `[tr. RA3 working]`

#### 2c. Third Edict, August 217 CE — 《举贤勿拘品行令》 "Promote the Worthy Without Regard to Their Character"

**The most shocking of the three**:

> 昔伊挚、傅说出于贱人，管仲，桓公贼也，皆用之以兴。萧何、曹参，县吏也，韩信、陈平负污辱之名，有见笑之耻，卒能成就王业，声著千载。吴起贪将，杀妻自信，散金求官，母死不归，然在魏，秦人不敢东向；在楚，则三晋不敢南谋。今天下得无有至德之人放在民间，及果勇不顾，临敌力战；若文俗之吏，高才异质，或堪为将守；负污辱之名，见笑之行，或不仁不孝而有治国用兵之术：其各举所知，勿有所遗。
>
> "In antiquity, Yi Yin and Fu Yue rose from the base orders; Guan Zhong was an enemy of Duke Huan, yet Duke Huan used them and flourished. Xiao He and Cao Shen were mere county clerks; Han Xin and Chen Ping bore names of defilement and shame — and yet they accomplished the kingly work, their fame resounds a thousand years. Wu Qi was a greedy general — he killed his wife to show his loyalty, scattered gold to chase office, did not return home when his mother died; yet while he served Wei, the men of Qin dared not look east; while he served Chu, the three Jin states dared not plan southward. Now — is there nowhere under Heaven, cast out among the common folk, a man of ultimate virtue? A man bold and heedless who will fight the enemy to the last? A clerk among literary officials of some rare gift, fit to be a general or governor? A man bearing a shameful name, whose conduct invites laughter — **or a man un-benevolent and un-filial, who yet possesses the arts of governing the state and managing armies** — let each of you recommend what you know, and leave nothing out." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Analysis of voice (all three edicts):**

- De Crespigny's paraphrase captures Cao Cao's insistence: "So long as a man has ability, I can use him" and "In times of peace, we may admire fine virtue, but in time of trouble it is achievement and ability which obtain rewards." `[web: https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/bitstream/1885/41887/1/morrison51.html]`
- The phrase **不仁不孝** bù rén bù xiào — "lacking benevolence, lacking filiality" — directly negates the two supreme Confucian virtues. To ask for such men in an official edict, in 217 CE, was legally and socially incendiary. It amounted to telling the Han literati class that their criteria of worth had been overridden.
- The rhetorical strategy is **argument from historical precedent** — Cao Cao never says "I reject Confucian ethics"; he says "the founders of every golden age used men you would have rejected." He makes the reformer's case through tradition itself.
- Voice marker: Cao Cao in edicts is terse, interrogative ("**岂**... **邪**?"), fond of listing worthies as rhetorical hammer-blows. He is already writing for the eye as well as the ear; these are prose edicts, not oral proclamations.

### 3. 《遗令》 Yi ling — The Last Testament, promulgated early 220 CE, days before his death on 15 March 220.

**Status**: `[documented]` — preserved in Lu Ji's 陆机 《吊魏武帝文·序》 (which reproduces the testament in its preface) and 《三国志》 commentary. Lu Ji's transcription is early 4th century; Cao Cao's original is fragmentary but the textual tradition is solid. Stephen Owen translates it in *An Anthology of Chinese Literature: Beginnings to 1911* (Norton, 1996).

**Key passage (most-translated section):**

> 吾婢妾与伎人皆勤苦，使著铜雀台，善待之。于台堂上施六尺床，下繐帐，朝晡上脯糒之属。月旦十五日，自朝至午，辄向帐中作伎。汝等时时登铜雀台，望吾西陵墓田。余香可分与诸夫人，不命祭。诸舍中无所为，可学作履组卖也。吾历官所得绶，皆著藏中。吾余衣裘，可别为一藏。不能者，兄弟可共分之。
>
> "My maidservants and female musicians have all labored hard; let them live on the Bronze Sparrow Terrace, and treat them well. On the hall of the Terrace set out a six-foot bed with a fine gauze curtain; at morning and at dusk set out dried meat and grain-porridge and such things. On the first and fifteenth of the month, from dawn till noon, they are to perform music before the curtain. You [my sons] should from time to time climb the Bronze Sparrow Terrace and gaze toward my tomb-field at West Mound. The leftover perfume may be divided among my ladies; do not appoint sacrifices [to me]. Those in the side-rooms with nothing to do — they may learn to weave silk shoe-straps and sell them. The seals and ribbons I have received from my various offices, all store them away. My leftover robes and furs — make of them a separate store. Those who cannot [bear to divide them], my brothers may share among themselves." `[tr. Stephen Owen 1996 (partial, augmented here)]`
>
> `[web: https://humanusliteratus.blogspot.com/2013/06/bronze-sparrow-terrace-unsung-songs.html]`

**The opening clause (as rendered by Owen 1996, pp. 279ff. of the Anthology)**:

> "My consorts and concubines are all to remain on the Terrace of the Bronze Sparrow, and on that terrace set a six-foot couch surrounded by lace hangings. In the morning and late afternoon let them set out wine, meat, and grain and such things. At dawn on the fifteenth of every month they are to perform before the screen…" `[tr. Stephen Owen, *An Anthology of Chinese Literature*, 1996]`

**Lu Xun's 1927 reading** (from 《魏晋风度及文章与药及酒之关系》 "Wei-Jin Demeanor and the Relationship of Literature to Drugs and Wine," Guangzhou lecture, 23 July 1927):

Lu Xun argued — against the Ming-and-later tradition of seeing the testament as either crocodile-tears deceit or pathetic mundanity — that the **un-imperial intimacy** of the testament is precisely what redeems Cao Cao as a *real* human figure. The man who was building an empire spent his last breaths arranging the re-employment of his entertainers as shoemakers. Lu Xun treats the testament as evidence that Cao Cao was **honest** in a way the later Confucian hagiographies could never be honest: "He didn't play the sage on his deathbed." Lu Xun positions this as continuous with Jian'an-period directness, and locates the origin of later Wei-Jin literary candor here. `[web: https://www.atlantis-press.com/proceedings/ichess-25/126022456]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lu_Xun]`

**Analysis of voice:**

- **Scale register**: a dying emperor-in-all-but-name writes about shoe-straps. The testament contains no reference to Heaven's Mandate, to the dynasty's fate, to his political legacy. The instruction regarding the tomb is also comparatively modest: 西陵 (West Mound), minimal grave goods (elsewhere in the same testament Cao Cao prescribes burial in everyday clothes with no jade or precious objects).
- **The perfume line — 余香可分与诸夫人** "the leftover perfume may be divided among my ladies" — has been much commented on. This is a man who in his bedroom had imported aromatics and who thought to distribute them after his death. It is domestic in a register almost unprecedented in the prose of Chinese rulers.
- **The shoemaking instruction** — "those in the side-rooms with nothing to do may learn to weave silk shoe-straps and sell them" — is extraordinary: he is planning a petty trade to keep his retained women self-supporting after he is gone. This is the voice of a practical household-head, not a sovereign.
- The clause "do not appoint sacrifices [to me]" — 不命祭 — is understated but pointed: Cao Cao does not demand the full ancestral cult. Contrast this with the elaborate state sacrifices his son Cao Pi will establish for himself as Emperor Wen of Wei.

### 4. Shorter policy edicts — what survives

**《军谯令》 Jun Qiao ling — Edict at Qiao, 208 CE** — on the burial and provisioning of families of soldiers who had died in his campaigns. `[documented]` in 《曹操集》.

> 吾起义兵，为天下除暴乱。旧土人民，死丧略尽，国中终日行，不见所识，使吾凄怆伤怀。其举义兵已来，将士绝无后者，求其亲戚以后之，授土田，官给耕牛，置学师以教之。为存者立庙，使祀其先人。
>
> "When I raised the righteous army, it was to rid the empire of cruelty and disorder. Of the old homeland and its people, the dead are almost all; I walk a whole day through my own country and see no face I know — this grieves me to my core. For every officer or soldier who since the raising of the army has died without descendant, let his kin be sought out and made his heir; grant them land, supply them oxen for plowing, appoint teachers to instruct their sons; and for the living, establish shrines where they may offer to their forebears." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Analysis**: This is the private-grief register — **I walk all day through my own country and see no face I know** — made public and made policy. Note the continuity with 《蒿里行》: the same man who wrote of bones bleaching in the fields now legislates for the families of the dead.

**《整齐风俗令》 Zheng qi feng su ling — Edict Rectifying Customs (date uncertain, between 204–213)** — against factional slander and the private naming-and-ranking practices of the Later Han. Fragments in 《曹操集》. Attacks the 月旦评 "first-of-the-month assessments" culture of Xu Shao and his cousins, which Cao Cao had himself once sought approval from (see Section III.6 below).

**《抑兼并令》 Yi jian bing ling — Edict Restraining Engrossment, 213** — against large landholders buying up peasant holdings. Fragments survive.

**《修学令》 Xiu xue ling — Edict Establishing Schools, 203** — orders every prefecture of 500 households to establish a school and appoint officials to teach classics. `[documented]` in the annals.

## II. LETTERS AND DOCUMENTED EXCHANGES

### 5. Letter-exchange with Sun Quan 孙权 before and after Red Cliffs

**The pre-battle letter (late 208 CE)** — summarized in 《三国志·吴书·周瑜传》:

> 近者奉辞伐罪，旄麾南指，刘琮束手。今治水军八十万众，方与将军会猎于吴。
>
> "Lately, receiving the imperial mandate to chastise the guilty, my standards swung southward, and Liu Cong submitted with folded hands. Now I am training a naval force of 800,000 men; I shall shortly join you, General, in a hunting-party at Wu." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Status**: `[attested]` — preserved in Chen Shou (280s CE), just 70 years after the event. The "hunting-party" is a euphemism for military encounter. The 800,000 figure is understood by all later commentators to be grossly inflated — actual force perhaps 220,000.

**Voice features**: Cao Cao's diplomatic voice is **menacing politesse** — the elegant euphemism "hunting" paired with an invented army-size. This is a letter to intimidate, not to negotiate. Sun Quan's advisor Zhou Yu saw through it.

### 6. Cao Cao's post-defeat remark on Red Cliffs

**To Sun Quan, in a letter dated shortly after the retreat (early 209 CE), paraphrased in Pei Songzhi commentary to 《三国志·吴书·周瑜传》 citing the 《江表传》**:

> 赤壁之役，值有疾病，孤烧船自退，横使周瑜虚获此名。
>
> "At the Red Cliffs campaign, it happened that illness was on us; I burned the ships and withdrew of my own accord — and on top of that, allowed Zhou Yu to take the hollow credit for it." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Status**: `[attested]` — Pei Songzhi citing Yu Pu's 《江表传》 (3rd c.). `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battle_of_Red_Cliffs]`

**Analysis of voice**: This is extraordinary in two registers. (a) **Damage control**: Cao Cao rewrites the narrative — he burned his own ships, not Huang Gai. (b) **Wounded vanity**: the contempt in "allowed Zhou Yu to take the hollow credit" is palpable. He cannot stand that the loss has given a southerner a name. This is one of the clearest surviving windows onto Cao Cao's pettier self.

### 7. On Chen Lin 陈琳 — after capturing the author who had libeled his ancestry

**Chen Lin in 200 CE, writing Yuan Shao's war-declaration, called Cao Cao's eunuch grandfather "a prodigy of wickedness" and said Cao Cao's father had bought office "with wagons of gold and cartloads of jewels."** After Guandu, Chen Lin was captured. Cao Cao's recorded rebuke:

> 卿昔为本初移书，但可罪状孤而已，恶恶止其身，何乃上及父祖邪？
>
> "When you wrote the circular for Benchu [Yuan Shao's style-name], you had only to list my crimes. But hatred should stop at the man himself — why did you have to reach up to my father and grandfather?" `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Chen Lin's reply:** 矢在弦上，不得不发 "The arrow was on the string — it had to fly."

**Status**: `[attested]` — 《三国志·魏书·王粲传》 commentary. Cao Cao then pardoned Chen Lin and made him a secretary. `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Lin_(Three_Kingdoms)]`

**Analysis**: Two voice-features. (a) Cao Cao's **family-honor register** — "hatred should stop at the man himself" is a principle, not a tantrum; he is explaining, not just complaining. (b) The pardon after the rebuke is characteristic: **he forgives because he wants the man's pen.** Utility overrides grievance.

### 8. On Guo Jia 郭嘉 after Red Cliffs (209 CE)

**Paraphrased in Pei Songzhi commentary to 《三国志·魏书·郭嘉传》 citing 《傅子》**:

> 郭奉孝在，不使孤至此。
>
> "If Guo Fengxiao had been here, he would not have let me come to this." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Status**: `[attested]`. `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guo_Jia]`

**Also on Guo Jia's death** (207 CE), said to Xun You:

> 诸君年皆孤辈也，唯奉孝最少。天下事竟，欲以后事属之，何意卒尔失之，悲夫！
>
> "All of you are my generation — only Fengxiao was the youngest. Once the affairs of the empire were settled, I meant to entrust the work-after-me to him. How could I have imagined losing him so suddenly? Bitter, bitter!" `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Analysis**: The **悲夫 "bitter, bitter"** register appears in Cao Cao's surviving voice only around a handful of deaths — Guo Jia, Dian Wei 典韦, Cao Ang 曹昂 (his eldest son, killed at Wancheng). The intimate grief is reserved. This is consistent with his poetry (see section IV).

### 9. On Xun Yu 荀彧 and his death (212 CE)

The primary extant sources are frustratingly opaque. The "empty food-box" story (Cao Cao sends Xun Yu an empty container; Xun Yu understands and takes poison) appears in Pei Songzhi commentary citing 《魏氏春秋》 `[attested]` but de Crespigny (2010) and most modern historians treat it skeptically; the *Sanguo Yanyi* dramatization of Cao Cao raging at Xun Yu is `[legendary]`.

**What Cao Cao wrote** regarding Xun Yu before the rift is preserved in his 《请增封荀彧表》 Memorial Requesting Additional Fief for Xun Yu (207 CE):

> 与彧共事已来，发言授策，无施不效。彧之功业，臣由以济，用披浮云，显光日月。
>
> "Since Xun Yu and I began serving together, every word he spoke, every strategy he offered — none failed. It is by his work that I myself was rescued, that the drifting clouds were parted and the sun and moon shone forth." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Status**: `[documented]` in 《曹操集》.

**Analysis**: Before the break, Cao Cao's voice on Xun Yu is **unguardedly reverent** — "his work parted the drifting clouds, made sun and moon shine." After the break in 212, Cao Cao's voice about Xun Yu is a recorded silence. No poem, no letter, no edict in *Cao Cao ji* addresses the death. This silence is itself data.

### 10. Letters / edicts to his sons — what survives

Direct private letters from Cao Cao to Cao Pi 曹丕 or Cao Zhi 曹植 **do not survive as coherent texts**. What does survive:

**《戒子植》 Jie zi Zhi — "Admonition to My Son Zhi" (c. 214 CE)**, fragment cited in 《三国志·魏书·陈思王传》 commentary:

> 吾昔为顿邱令，年二十三，思此时所行，无悔于今。今汝年亦二十三矣，可不勉与！
>
> "When I was Prefect of Dunqiu, I was twenty-three years old. Thinking back on what I did then, I have no regrets now. You too are now twenty-three. Can you not strive?" `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Status**: `[attested]`.

**Analysis of voice**: Cao Cao the father is **uncomplicated and direct** — a command to his poet-son not to waste his age. No ornament. The self-reference ("I have no regrets") is striking: the author of the Rang xian ling's extended self-defense against accusations of usurpation, here, with his son, declares he has no regrets. Private register, compressed.

## III. FAMOUS DOCUMENTED & ATTRIBUTED SAYINGS

### 11. "Better that I wrong others than others wrong me" — three versions of the Lü Boshe incident

This is the single most culturally weighted Cao Cao quotation. The three surviving variants are crucial because they show how the story hardened over three centuries.

**Version A — Wang Chen 王沈 《魏书》 (pro-Wei official history, mid-3rd c.)** — `[attested]`:

> 伯奢不在，其子与宾客共劫太祖，取马及物，太祖手刃击杀数人。
>
> "Lü Boshe was not at home. His sons and some guests conspired to rob the Grand Ancestor [Cao Cao] — taking his horses and belongings. The Grand Ancestor, blade in hand, struck down several of them." `[tr. RA3 working]` `[web: https://www.tumblr.com/siumerghe/653067858778144768]`

No quote is attributed to Cao Cao. This is the **softened** version: Cao Cao kills in self-defense against robbers. This was the Wei state's preferred account.

**Version B — Guo Ban 郭颁 《世语》 Shi yu (Western Jin, c. 280–300 CE)** — `[attested]`:

> 太祖过伯奢。伯奢出行，五子皆在，备宾主礼。太祖自以背卓命，疑其图己，手剑夜杀八人而去。
>
> "The Grand Ancestor passed by Lü Boshe's. Lü Boshe was out; his five sons were all there and prepared the ritual of host and guest. The Grand Ancestor, knowing he had defied Dong Zhuo's orders, suspected them of plotting against him — and, sword in hand, by night, killed eight people and left." `[tr. RA3 working]`

Still no quote; but the framing has **hardened** — the killing is preemptive and paranoid, not self-defense.

**Version C — Sun Sheng 孙盛 《杂记》 (Eastern Jin, mid-4th c.)** — `[attributed]` (the quote itself; the killing is `[attested]`):

> 太祖闻其食器声，以为图己，遂夜杀之。既而凄怆曰：「宁我负人，毋人负我！」遂行。
>
> "The Grand Ancestor heard the sound of their cooking-vessels, took it for a plot against himself, and so killed them in the night. Afterwards, in bleak sorrow, he said: 'Better I wrong another than another wrong me.' And he departed." `[tr. RA3 working]`

This is the first appearance of the famous line — **~140 years after Cao Cao's death**, in an Eastern Jin source whose author Sun Sheng is elsewhere known to dramatize. The **凄怆** "in bleak sorrow" qualifier is important: Sun Sheng's Cao Cao *regrets* the killing.

**Sanguo Yanyi (14th c.) version** — `[legendary]`:

> 宁教我负天下人，休教天下人负我。
>
> "Let me wrong all the men of the empire; never let the men of the empire wrong me."

This is the **most extreme** rendering: (a) it globalizes the injury from "a man" to "all men under Heaven"; (b) it removes the "bleak sorrow" qualifier that softened Sun Sheng; (c) it appears ~1150 years after Cao Cao's death. This is the Cao Cao of Chinese popular memory — but it is NOT Cao Cao's documented voice.

**Summary of hardening**:
| Source | Date | Quote form | Tone |
|---|---|---|---|
| Wang Chen 魏书 | mid-3rd c. | (none) | Self-defense against robbers |
| Guo Ban 世语 | c. 290 | (none) | Preemptive paranoia |
| Sun Sheng 杂记 | mid-4th c. | 宁我负人，毋人负我 + "bleak sorrow" | Regretful self-justification |
| Sanguo Yanyi | 14th c. | 宁教我负天下人，休教天下人负我 | Global, unrepentant |

`[web: https://www.tumblr.com/siumerghe/653067858778144768/an-examination-of-cao-caos-i-would-rather-betray]`

### 12. Xu Shao's 许劭 appraisal — "A capable minister in an orderly age, a treacherous hero in a disordered age"

This is Cao Cao's most famous self-description — but it was not said BY him, and its form is unstable.

**Version A — 《后汉书·许劭传》** (Fan Ye, 5th c.) `[attested]`:

> 君清平之奸贼，乱世之英雄。
>
> "You are a treacherous villain in times of peace, a hero of the age in times of chaos." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Version B — 孙盛 《异同杂语》 cited in Pei Songzhi commentary to 《三国志·魏书·武帝纪》** `[attested]`:

> 子治世之能臣，乱世之奸雄。
>
> "You will be a capable minister in an ordered age, and a treacherous hero (奸雄 jiānxióng) in a disordered age." `[tr. RA3 working]`

**Cao Cao's reaction** (per Sun Sheng): 太祖大笑 — "The Grand Ancestor laughed out loud." `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xu_Shao]`

**Analysis**: Notice that **奸雄 jiānxióng** (the word that became Cao Cao's permanent epithet) and **英雄 yīngxióng** ("hero") are switched between the Houhanshu and Sun Sheng versions. The more famous version — in which he is the villain-in-peace and the *jianxiong* (not *yingxiong*) in chaos — is Sun Sheng's, and it is the formulation that stuck because it gives both positive roles to Cao Cao but with a moral sting. **Cao Cao's laugh** at being called a *jianxiong* is also attested — he is reported to have embraced, not rejected, the description.

**Crucially**: this is a saying ABOUT Cao Cao, not BY him. Sanguo Yanyi faithfully reproduces Sun Sheng's version, which is why it is widely known.

### 13. "Chicken ribs" 鸡肋 — Hanzhong campaign, spring 219 CE

**Source**: 《三国志·魏书·武帝纪》 Pei Songzhi commentary citing 《九州春秋》. `[attested]`.

> 夏侯惇入帐，问夜间口号。操随口答曰：「鸡肋！鸡肋！」… 杨修便教随行军士各收拾行装，准备归程。… 人问其故，修曰：「夫鸡肋，食之无肉，弃之可惜。公归计决矣。」
>
> "Xiahou Dun entered the tent and asked for the night's password. Cao [Cao] answered offhand: 'Chicken ribs! Chicken ribs!' ... Yang Xiu [hearing this] at once told the accompanying soldiers to pack their baggage and prepare to return. ... Asked why, Yang Xiu said: 'Chicken ribs — eat them, there is no meat; throw them out, it seems a pity. His Lordship's decision to withdraw is made.'" `[tr. RA3 working]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yang_Xiu_(Han_dynasty)]`

**Status of the utterance**: `[attested]` — earliest in Pei Songzhi's commentary (429 CE) citing earlier 《九州春秋》 (4th c.).

**Analysis of voice**: Cao Cao's **private idiom** — the campaign-weariness expressed through a throwaway culinary metaphor. The fact that Yang Xiu could decode it and was executed for decoding it is the reason we have the quote at all. It shows Cao Cao (a) thought in metaphor even offhand; (b) resented having his unspoken calculations read by subordinates.

## IV. PERSONAL VOICE IN THE POEMS — The register that matches none of the above

### 14. 《短歌行》 Duan ge xing — "Short Song Style" (c. 208 CE, possibly composed before Red Cliffs)

**Status**: `[documented]` — 《曹操集》; translations by Burton Watson, Stephen Owen, Jean Yuan & Vickie Fang.

Full Chinese and parallel English (translation following Jean Yuan/Vickie Fang, Classic Chinese Poetry project, with adjustments):

> 对酒当歌，人生几何？譬如朝露，去日苦多。
> 慨当以慷，忧思难忘。何以解忧？唯有杜康。
> 青青子衿，悠悠我心。但为君故，沉吟至今。
> 呦呦鹿鸣，食野之苹。我有嘉宾，鼓瑟吹笙。
> 明明如月，何时可掇？忧从中来，不可断绝。
> 越陌度阡，枉用相存。契阔谈𡙇，心念旧恩。
> 月明星稀，乌鹊南飞。绕树三匝，何枝可依？
> 山不厌高，海不厌深。周公吐哺，天下归心。
>
> "With wine before me, let song sound out — how long is a man's life?
> Like the morning dew, the bitter days gone by are many.
> Though feeling runs high and generous, sorrow is hard to forget.
> **What can dissolve sorrow? Only Du Kang's [wine].**
> Green, green is your collar; long, long is my heart —
> For your sake alone I have been murmuring until now.
> Yu-yu cry the deer, feeding on the wormwood of the field;
> I have worthy guests — strike the zither, blow the reeds!
> Bright, bright as the moon — when can I pluck it down?
> Sorrow rises from within, and cannot be cut off.
> They cross the paths and lanes, and stoop to visit me;
> Long parted, now we talk and feast — my heart recalls old kindness.
> The moon is bright, the stars are sparse, the crow and magpie fly south;
> Three times around the tree — on which branch can they rest?
> The mountain does not weary of height, the sea does not weary of depth.
> **The Duke of Zhou spat out his food — and all under Heaven turned their hearts to him.**" `[tr. RA3 working, after Yuan & Fang]` `[web: https://www.classicchinesepoetry.com/tag/cao-cao/]`

Burton Watson: Cao Cao is "the only writer of the period who succeeded in infusing the old four-character metre with any vitality, mainly because he discarded the archaic diction." `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry_of_Cao_Cao]`

**Analysis of voice**:
- **何以解忧？唯有杜康。** is Cao Cao's most quoted line in Chinese drinking culture. "Du Kang" is the legendary inventor of wine — here metonymy for wine itself. The register is **adult melancholy cut with a half-laugh**.
- **周公吐哺，天下归心** is the signature self-comparison. The Duke of Zhou — regent for the young King Cheng, who famously spat out his food three times in a single meal to greet visiting worthies — is the template Cao Cao wants applied to himself. Crucial: he is *not* claiming to be the Son of Heaven, he is claiming to be the regent. This is consistent with his refusal of the throne in the Rang xian ling.
- The **crow-on-the-wrong-branch image** (绕树三匝，何枝可依) is one of the haunting images of Chinese poetry: dispossessed talent unable to find a patron. This is Cao Cao writing as the patron-who-recognizes-this, recruiting by mirror.

### 15. 《龟虽寿》 Gui sui shou — "Though the Tortoise Lives Long" (207 CE, after White Wolf Mountain campaign; from the four-poem set 《步出夏门行》 "Stepping Forth from the Xia Gate")

**Status**: `[documented]`.

> 神龟虽寿，犹有竟时。腾蛇乘雾，终为土灰。
> **老骥伏枥，志在千里。烈士暮年，壮心不已。**
> 盈缩之期，不但在天；养怡之福，可得永年。
> 幸甚至哉，歌以咏志。
>
> "The divine tortoise, though long-lived, has its end;
> The soaring serpent rides the mist — yet becomes dust and ash at last.
> **The old warhorse bent at the trough still dreams of a thousand li;
> The man of ambition in his twilight years — his strong heart is not yet still.**
> The span of our growing and shrinking is not only in Heaven's keeping;
> The blessing of cultivated ease can grant us a long year.
> How fortunate we are — I sing to voice my will." `[tr. RA3 working, following YuanYuan Yu's version]` `[web: https://authorfrancomarincola.wordpress.com/2015/02/21/although-the-turtle-is-long-lived-poem-by-cao-cao-translated-by-yuanyuan-yu/]`

**Analysis of voice**:
- **老骥伏枥，志在千里；烈士暮年，壮心不已** is Cao Cao's second most famous line (after 宁我负人). It is the self-portrait at age 52 — having just completed the pacification of the north at White Wolf Mountain, with Jing Province and Wu still ahead of him.
- The **theological register is stark**: "the divine tortoise dies, the soaring serpent becomes ash." Cao Cao rejects the immortality cults of the Later Han. He accepts mortality — and then asserts will against it. This is the signature Cao Cao move: **clear-eyed about the fact, defiant about the stance**.
- Chairman Mao repeatedly quoted this poem; it is on the wall of many contemporary Chinese businesses. The image of the old warhorse-at-trough has become the Chinese emblem of late-career aspiration.

### 16. 《蒿里行》 Haoli xing — "Song of Haoli" (early 190s CE, after the failed anti-Dong Zhuo coalition)

**Status**: `[documented]`.

> 关东有义士，兴兵讨群凶。初期会盟津，乃心在咸阳。
> 军合力不齐，踌躇而雁行。势利使人争，嗣还自相戕。
> 淮南弟称号，刻玺于北方。铠甲生虮虱，万姓以死亡。
> **白骨露于野，千里无鸡鸣。生民百遗一，念之断人肠。**
>
> "East of the passes were men of righteousness who raised armies against the pack of villains.
> At first we were to meet at the Ford of Meng, our hearts set on Xianyang.
> But when the armies massed, our strength would not align — we hung back, flew in the straggling-goose line.
> Power and profit set men against each other; soon they were cutting each other down.
> In Huainan, a brother proclaimed his title; in the north, another carved his seal.
> Armor grew lice and nits; the ten-thousand common folk died away.
> **White bones lie exposed in the wilderness — for a thousand li no cock crows.
> Of a hundred of the living, barely one remains — to think of it breaks a man's heart.**" `[tr. RA3 working, after Jean Yuan]` `[web: https://chinesepoetry.substack.com/p/unburied-white-bones-cao-cao-and]`

**Analysis of voice**:
- This is Cao Cao's **war-poetry register** — not glorification, but devastation-witness. The poem explicitly blames the failure of the 190 CE coalition (the anti-Dong Zhuo alliance, in which he himself participated and which he watched collapse from mutual suspicion).
- The **concrete noun register** — 虮虱 *jīshī* "lice and nits" on armor — is a Cao Cao signature. His poetry does not use the stylized diction of the later shī tradition; it names the lice on the armor.
- **生民百遗一，念之断人肠** — "of a hundred, barely one survives; thinking of it breaks a man's heart" — is frequently cited in Chinese historiography of the Three Kingdoms era as if it were a census. It is, strictly, a poetic line; but demographic studies of the Han-Wei transition (~40% population loss by some estimates) suggest Cao Cao was not exaggerating by much.
- Note the poem is NOT about the Yellow Turban rebellion (which started in 184 CE); it is about the coalition's failure to capture Luoyang in 190 CE and the subsequent warlord period. Many secondary sources misattribute its occasion; the original text's references (Meng Ford, Xianyang, Huainan's Yuan Shu, "the north" = Yuan Shao stamping his own seal) point to 190–197 CE events.

### 17. 《观沧海》 Guan canghai — "Viewing the Deep-Blue Sea" (206/207 CE, from Jieshi Mountain on the Liaodong coast, returning from White Wolf Mountain)

**Status**: `[documented]`.

> 东临碣石，以观沧海。水何澹澹，山岛竦峙。
> 树木丛生，百草丰茂。秋风萧瑟，洪波涌起。
> 日月之行，若出其中；星汉灿烂，若出其里。
> 幸甚至哉，歌以咏志。
>
> "Eastward I climb Jieshi to view the deep-blue sea.
> How the waters swell! The mountain-islands stand sheer.
> Trees grow in dense thickets, the hundred grasses run wild.
> The autumn wind rustles cold; the great waves rise and surge.
> Sun and moon on their courses — as though they issued from within.
> The shining Milky Way — as though it rose from within.
> How fortunate I am — I sing to voice my will." `[tr. RA3 working, after Vickie Fang]` `[web: https://www.classicchinesepoetry.com/tag/cao-cao/]`

**Analysis of voice**: Cao Cao is often credited as the originator of the *shanshui* (landscape) mode in Chinese poetry — this is the founding example. The striking move: **sun, moon, Milky Way "as though from within" the sea** — the physical universe seen as contained in a single view. The "voice" here is calmly grandiose: the poet stands where the whole cosmos can be seen rising.

## V. VOICE FEATURES — SYNTHETIC NOTES

### A. The two registers

**Administrative voice** (edicts, letters, Rang xian ling): terse, rhetorical-question-heavy, fond of **historical precedent** (Yi Yin, Guan Zhong, Chen Ping, Lü Shang) as argument-anchors. Uses 孤 gū consistently. Mixes grandiose self-assertion ("were it not for me") with tactical self-minimization ("I was young, I did not think myself a famous recluse"). Voice of a man who wrote his own edicts — not a committee.

**Poetic voice** (the dozen-plus surviving poems): elegiac, haunted, concrete, classical-allusive but not archaic. The four-character meter he revived was dead literary form; he reanimated it by talking in it. Themes: brevity of life, bones of the dead, the drifting talent looking for a branch, the old horse still dreaming, the sea containing the cosmos.

**The gap between the two registers is the man.** His edicts are a statesman's; his poems are a mourner's and a drinker's. De Crespigny notes the puzzle: the man who wrote 《观沧海》 is also the man who massacred civilians at Xuzhou in 193. Neither register cancels the other.

### B. Self-deprecation paired with grandiose claim

Consistent verbal habit: he will say "I was young and unimpressive" and in the next breath say "were it not for me the empire would have a dozen emperors." The effect is neither false-modesty nor boasting — it is a **rhetorical closing of the bid**: "I had no initial claim, therefore my current position is earned, not usurped."

### C. Humor — dark, sardonic, utilitarian

The recorded humor is bleak. The Chen Lin exchange — "hatred should stop at the man himself, why reach up to my father?" — is a complaint delivered deadpan. The Yang Xiu chicken-ribs incident is not Cao Cao being witty; it is him being caught in a thought by a clever courtier. Cao Cao's laugh at Xu Shao's "treacherous hero" appraisal is the closest thing to unguarded humor in the record — he enjoys the label.

### D. Moments of melancholy around death

Three real moments:
1. After Guo Jia's death — "only Fengxiao was the youngest… bitter!"
2. The reflected grief of 《军谯令》 — "I walk all day through my own country and see no face I know"
3. The perfume clause of the Last Testament — the practical kindness that does the work of emotion he cannot state.

### E. Private-voice vs. public-voice gap with his sons

In the 《戒子植》 "Admonition to Cao Zhi" — just three sentences, utterly plain, no rhetoric — we get perhaps the cleanest window onto Cao Cao without an audience beyond his son. The public edicts lose their rhetorical elaboration when he is talking to his kids.

### F. The silence around Xun Yu

Cao Cao's surviving corpus contains no elegy, no explanation, no meditation on Xun Yu's death. Before the 212 break, Xun Yu is the man whose work parted "the drifting clouds and made sun and moon shine." After, total silence. Given Cao Cao's documented tendency to memorialize associates in verse (Dian Wei) and in edicts (his slain soldiers' families), the silence is weighted.

## VI. WHAT IS NOT CAO CAO'S VOICE — important exclusions

The following are commonly circulated as Cao Cao's words but should NOT be used as his voice:

- **"宁教我负天下人，休教天下人负我"** ("Let me wrong all the world, never let the world wrong me") — this global form is **Sanguo Yanyi, 14th c.** `[legendary]`. Sun Sheng's 4th-c. version is narrower and qualified with "bleak sorrow."
- **"治世之能臣，乱世之奸雄"** — said ABOUT Cao Cao by Xu Shao, not BY him. Cao Cao reportedly laughed at it, but did not author it.
- The extended "Cao Cao dialogue" in *Sanguo Yanyi* chapters 1–120 is 14th-century historical fiction. Some episodes (Guandu, Red Cliffs) are grounded in *Sanguozhi*, but the DIALOGUE is novelistic. Treat it as reception-history only.
- **"周公吐哺，天下归心"** is documented and from 《短歌行》; use it. But note that Cao Cao is quoting/alluding to the Duke of Zhou legend (from 《史记·鲁周公世家》), not inventing the image.
- The "**empty food box**" episode used to compel Xun Yu's suicide is `[attested]` (Pei Songzhi citing 《魏氏春秋》) but weakly — de Crespigny treats it skeptically. The *Yanyi* version is `[legendary]`.
