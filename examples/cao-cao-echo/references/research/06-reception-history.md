# Cao Cao — Reception History (Research Agent 6)

**Scope.** How each era imagined Cao Cao, so that the Echo can speak as the historical person (c. 155–220 CE) rather than as the cultural archetype. The "奸雄" (cunning villain) label is overwhelmingly a construct that ossifies ~900 years after his death — primarily Zhu Xi (12th c.) > Yuan drama (14th c.) > *Sanguo Yanyi* (late 14th c.) > Mao Zonggang (17th c.) > opera. The Echo must filter these layers out.

Label key: `[documented]` = era-specific primary text; `[modern-scholarly-inference]` = later synthesis.

---

## 1. Wei / Western Jin (220–316 CE) — the official founder

**Dominant image.** 魏武帝 Emperor Wu of Wei: dynastic founder, posthumously exalted; recognized poet of the Jian'an (建安) era; military genius; meritocratic reformer.

**Key texts that shaped it.**

- **Chen Shou 陈寿, 《三国志》 *Sanguo Zhi* (Records of the Three Kingdoms), c. 280s CE** `[documented]`. The authoritative primary history. Chen Shou was Shu-born but served Jin (which inherited through Wei), so his editorial choices are constrained: Wei annals go first, Wei reign-era names are used throughout, and only Wei rulers get imperial nomenclature. Cao Cao is treated as legitimate founding sovereign. The evaluative closing comment (评曰) praises him as "非常之人，超世之杰" ("an extraordinary man, a hero surpassing his age"). [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao]
- **Wang Shen 王沉, 《魏书》 (the pro-Wei *Wei Shu*)** `[documented]` — now largely lost but quoted extensively by Pei Songzhi; systematically flattering to the Cao house.
- **Cao Pi 曹丕 《典论·论文》 and the poetry of the 三曹 (Three Caos) + 建安七子** `[documented]`. Cao Cao's literary prestige is not a later invention — it is already canonical in the late Han / early Wei.

**What this era "added" (to filter).** The founder-emperor halo: Wei court historiography necessarily portrays him as legitimate sovereign and literary father-figure. Good for preserving facts, but expect omissions/softening of: early career irregularities, family massacres during the Xuzhou campaign of 193/194 CE (Chen Shou records these tersely; Pei Songzhi's later sources are more graphic), and any material useful to a rival claim.

Sources:
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao
- https://www.chinaknowledge.de/History/Division/personsjiananqizi.html

## 2. Eastern Jin → Southern Dynasties (317–589 CE) — the ideological seed of villainy

**Dominant image.** Ambivalent. A founder-emperor in official genre; increasingly a usurper in the new legitimacy discourse; a canonical poet in literary criticism.

**Key texts that shaped it.**

- **Xi Zuochi 习凿齿, 《汉晋春秋》 *Han Jin Chunqiu* (mid-4th c.)** `[documented]`. **This is the ideological seed of villain-Cao Cao.** Xi Zuochi is "the first historian to regard the Wei dynasty as an illegitimate successor to the Han" and the first to use the Shu-Han calendar — effectively treating Liu Bei as the true heir of Han and Cao Cao / Wei as usurpers. His aim was contemporary: a corrective to Huan Wen 桓温's imperial ambitions, using analogy. "Wei was established by usurping Han's rule, which was scheming and unscrupulous." Met with little acceptance in his own time, but became the template Song Neo-Confucians would later adopt. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Zuochi]
- **Pei Songzhi 裴松之 annotations to the *Sanguo Zhi* (completed 429 CE)** `[documented]`. Tripled the length of Chen Shou by incorporating dozens of lost sources. Pei's editorial stance is "preserve everything, let readers judge," which in practice means that hostile material survived alongside panegyric. Critically he cites:
  - **《曹瞒传》 *Cao Man Zhuan***, anonymous, **compiled by someone from Eastern Wu (hostile kingdom)** `[documented]`. "Cao Man" 曹瞒 = "Cao the Deceiver," using a derogatory childhood name. Characterized by modern scholarship as "hostile propaganda"; contains the most damaging anecdotes (cruelty, paranoia, mock-executions of subordinates). Not a reliable source — but once embedded in Pei's commentary it traveled with Chen Shou's text forever and fed every later retelling. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annotated_Records_of_the_Three_Kingdoms] [web: https://threekingdoms.fandom.com/wiki/Cao_Man_zhuan]
  - Wang Shen's pro-Wei *Wei Shu* (other pole).
  - Memoirs, clan histories, regional gazetteers.
- **Liu Xie 刘勰 《文心雕龙》 (c. 500 CE)** `[documented]`. Canonizes 建安风骨 *Jian'an fenggu* ("Jian'an wind-and-bone") as a major stylistic ideal: "elegant and generous" (雅好慷慨) — standing against political turbulence. Cao Cao's literary prestige is now locked in literary-critical discourse, independent of moral-political evaluation. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jian%27an_poetry]

**What this era "added" (to filter).**
- The **usurper frame** (Xi Zuochi) — entirely a later political argument, not a contemporary or Wei-Jin consensus.
- The **hostile anecdote corpus** (via *Cao Man Zhuan*) — content-wise, some of the most famous "Cao Cao was cruel" stories enter the record here without contemporary verification, and many are the Wu-partisan counterpart to Wei's panegyric.
- But: Jian'an literary canon = preserve; this reflects real Wei-era status.

## 3. Tang (618–907 CE) — still a respected historical figure

**Dominant image.** A respected (if complicated) historical founder and canonical poet. Not yet the villain.

**Key texts / voices.**

- **Emperor Taizong 李世民 (r. 626–649)** personally wrote a sacrificial oration at Cao Cao's tomb during a campaign, acknowledging him as a gifted ruler while noting moral reservations — the tone is respect-with-caveats, not condemnation. `[documented, mainstream Tang imperial historiography]`
- **Du Fu 杜甫, Li Bai 李白** — Tang poets reference Cao Cao as part of the heroic-historical tradition; *not* as painted-face villain. Cao Cao's poetry continues to be anthologized (e.g. in 《文选》 *Wen Xuan*, compiled 6th c. but canonical through Tang). `[documented]`
- **Liu Zhiji 刘知几 《史通》 (early 8th c.)** — discusses the *Sanguo Zhi* and Pei's annotations as a historiographical case study without condemning Cao Cao morally.

**What this era added (to filter).** Essentially nothing new that distorts the historical Cao Cao. The Tang still works from the Chen Shou / Pei Songzhi record; the villain frame hasn't yet captured elite opinion.

Sources:
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cao_Cao
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poetry_of_Cao_Cao

## 4. Song (960–1279) — the decisive turn

This is where "villain Cao Cao" becomes elite ideology.

**Dominant image splits into three strata:**

**(a) Conservative official historiography — Wei is still the main line.**

- **Sima Guang 司马光, 《资治通鉴》 (1084)** `[documented]`. Maintains Wei as the main chronological thread; uses Wei reign-era names for the Three Kingdoms period. Sima Guang's stated position is deliberately agnostic on "legitimacy" — he uses Wei's calendar for practical, not moral, reasons, and records critiques alongside praises. Cao Cao in the *Zizhi Tongjian* is a formidable, morally mixed ruler — not the Yanyi villain. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sima_Guang]

**(b) Literati sympathy — the tragic-hero register.**

- **Su Shi 苏轼 《念奴娇·赤壁怀古》 and 《前赤壁赋》 (c. 1082)** `[documented]`. Cao Cao as tragic grandeur: "固一世之雄也，而今安在哉" ("truly a hero for his age; and now where is he?"). Su Shi treats him as an awesome historical presence whose transience evokes reflection, not contempt.
- Su Shi also records, in his 《志林》 (*Dongpo Zhilin*), that when storytellers recounted the Three Kingdoms tales, **children cried when they heard Liu Bei had lost and cheered when they heard Cao Cao had lost.** This is the earliest literary evidence that popular (oral) sentiment had already polarized against Cao Cao by mid-Northern Song — predating the written novel by ~300 years. `[documented; primary attestation]`

**(c) Neo-Confucian re-framing — the ideological root of villain Cao Cao.**

- **Zhu Xi 朱熹, 《资治通鉴纲目》 *Zizhi Tongjian Gangmu* (essentially complete c. 1172, polished 1180s)** `[documented]`. **This is the single most consequential shift.** Zhu Xi explicitly reverses Sima Guang: Shu-Han is legitimate, Cao Wei is a usurper. His framework — "great unity," "reverence for the emperor and father," "bringing about the kingly way" — is moralistic Neo-Confucian, not pragmatic. By granting Shu-Han legitimacy he makes Cao Cao structurally a moral criminal, not merely a politically dubious founder. Because the *Gangmu* became the standard pedagogical history text through the late imperial period (and shaped Korean and Japanese historiography too), this reading becomes orthodoxy. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zizhi_Tongjian_Gangmu] [web: http://www.chinaknowledge.de/Literature/Historiography/tongjiangangmu.html]

**Vernacular storytelling 说三分.** Professional storytellers of the Song urban marketplaces specialize in Three Kingdoms tales (说三分 "telling the Three"). Su Shi's note above confirms the pro-Liu / anti-Cao polarization is already in place at the oral-performance level.

**What this era added (to filter).**
- **The full moral-usurper frame** (Zhu Xi) — an explicit Neo-Confucian overlay, not a fact about Cao Cao.
- **Popular sentiment polarized against Cao Cao** (Su Shi's report) — a cultural fact about *audiences*, not about Cao Cao himself.
- But: Su Shi's tragic-hero register actually tracks quite closely to what Chen Shou / Pei Songzhi support. This is a useful register for the Echo.

## 5. Yuan (1271–1368) — the painted-face villain is born

**Dominant image.** The stage villain. Cao Cao now wears the 白脸 (*bailian*, white painted face) that signifies treachery in Chinese opera; this convention originates or consolidates in Yuan 杂剧 *zaju*.

**Key texts.**

- **《三国志平话》 *Sanguo Zhi Pinghua* (Yu-family Jianyang print, dated 1321–1323)** `[documented]`. The earliest extant written work aggregating Three Kingdoms stories — proto-novel, direct ancestor of *Sanguo Yanyi*. Crucially, it opens with a **karmic frame**: Liu Bang's killed lieutenants are reincarnated, with **Cao Cao as the reincarnation of Han Xin**, wronged by Liu Bang and now "stealing" Liu Bang's descendant's (Emperor Xian's) empire in retribution. The entire narrative is therefore pre-structured as moral-karmic drama, with Cao Cao cast as the agent of cosmic payback against the Han. This is a completely invented narrative armature with no historical basis. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanguozhi_Pinghua] [web: https://threekingdoms.fandom.com/wiki/Sanguo_zhi_pinghua]
- **Yuan zaju cycle (14th c.)** — many plays (《单刀会》 "Single Sword Meeting," 《博望烧屯》 "Burning the Camp at Bowang," etc.) feature Cao Cao or Cao-side characters as antagonists; the stage convention codifies the white-face treachery mask. Zhuge Liang becomes the paragon wisdom figure; Guan Yu begins his apotheosis. `[documented; see Kimberly Besio's work on Yuan zaju and the Three Kingdoms cycle]`

**What this era added (to filter).**
- **The reincarnation / karmic-retribution armature** (Pinghua) — fiction.
- **The painted-face villain stage convention** — visual/performative, not historical.
- **Dozens of specific popular episodes** — many of which *Sanguo Yanyi* inherits and fixes.

Sources:
- https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanguozhi_Pinghua
- https://threekingdoms.fandom.com/wiki/Sanguo_zhi_pinghua
- https://so08.tci-thaijo.org/index.php/artssu/article/view/121
- https://www.berkshirepublishing.com/ecph-china/2018/01/16/yuan-drama/
- Kimberly Besio, in *Three Kingdoms and Chinese Culture* (eds. Besio & Tung, SUNY Press, 2007)

## 6. Ming–Qing (1368–1911) — the villain is canonized forever

**Dominant image.** The *Sanguo Yanyi* Cao Cao: charismatic, intelligent, and *paradigmatically treacherous*. "宁教我负天下人，休教天下人负我" ("I would rather betray the world than let the world betray me") — a line invented or embellished by the novel — becomes his signature.

**Key texts.**

- **Luo Guanzhong 罗贯中 《三国演义》 *Sanguo Yanyi* (late 14th c.; earliest extant edition 1522)** `[documented]`. Structurally **尊刘抑曹** ("exalt Liu, suppress Cao"): Liu Bei's faction are the heroes, Cao Cao is the antagonist. Andrew Plaks (*The Four Masterworks of the Ming Novel*, chapter "San-kuo chih yen-i: Limitations of Valor") argues the novel is not simply pro-Liu propaganda but a sophisticated literati reworking that ironizes heroism on all sides — yet the surface narrative is unambiguously anti-Cao, and that surface is what most readers received. [web: https://press.princeton.edu/books/hardcover/9780691273525/the-four-masterworks-of-the-ming-novel] [web: https://archive.org/details/fourmasterworkso0000plak]
- **Mao Lun 毛纶 & Mao Zonggang 毛宗岗 recension + commentary (1660s)** `[documented]`. Tightened the novel from ~900,000 to ~750,000 characters; **removed passages that praised Cao Cao's advisers and generals**; inserted interpretive commentary that hardens the villain reading at every turn. This Mao recension became the standard edition for the next three centuries and is the text from which most popular knowledge derives. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms]
- **Peking and provincial opera (Qing)** — Cao Cao is the paradigmatic 白脸奸雄 (white-faced villain-hero) mask. Specific opera arias (e.g. "捉放曹" "Catching and Releasing Cao") canonize novelistic episodes.
- **Wang Fuzhi 王夫之 《读通鉴论》 (written 1687–1691)** `[documented]`. Qing loyalist/philosopher who explicitly **rejects Zhu Xi's "legitimate dynasty" framework** and offers a more nuanced reading — closer to Sima Guang's pragmatism. A counter-current to the Mao-edition orthodoxy, but confined to scholarly readership; did not displace the popular villain image. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wang_Fuzhi]

**Specific Yanyi inventions / embellishments — to filter from the Echo:**

| Story | Yanyi version | Historical record |
|---|---|---|
| **Lü Boshe 吕伯奢 killing** | Cao Cao, fleeing with Chen Gong 陈宫, slaughters Lü's household over a misheard pig-slaughter; then murders Lü himself on the road. Adds the line "宁我负人，毋人负我" in the invented exchange. | Pei Songzhi cites three versions from different sources; some have Cao Cao killing preemptively (plausible), some have the host's sons planning to rob him (a plausible self-defense killing). Chen Gong was *not* with Cao Cao at this point — he defected later, for unrelated reasons. The iconic line is at best reconstructed from *Cao Man Zhuan*-style hostile sources and sharpened by the novel. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms] |
| **Hua Tuo 华佗 execution** | Hua Tuo proposes a craniotomy with anesthetic 麻沸汤; paranoid Cao Cao suspects an assassination plot and executes him. Hua Tuo also treats Guan Yu's arm by scraping bone. | Hua Tuo *was* imprisoned and killed by Cao Cao, but because Hua repeatedly delayed return to treat Cao's headaches, using his wife's illness as pretext — a dereliction, not a surgery Cao rejected. Hua Tuo never treated Guan Yu. The craniotomy scene is fiction. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms] |
| **Mi Heng 祢衡 humiliation (the naked drumming)** | Elaborated scene where Cao Cao tries to humiliate the insolent scholar Mi Heng with a drummer's role; Mi Heng strips naked. Cao then sends him to Liu Biao hoping Liu will execute him; Liu forwards him to Huang Zu, who does. | Basic arc is historical (Mi Heng was passed along and killed by Huang Zu), but the naked-drumming scene is heavily theatrical embellishment. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romance_of_the_Three_Kingdoms] |
| **"宁教我负天下人，休教天下人负我"** | Novel's signature villain-defining line. | Not in Chen Shou. The closest historical attestation is via hostile *Cao Man Zhuan*-adjacent material, in far milder form ("宁我负人，毋人负我") and without the "天下" universalizing scope the novel adds. |
| **Countless strategems of deceit** | Novel characterizes virtually every Cao Cao decision as 诡诈 (trickery). | Many stratagems are historically attested (e.g. the Guandu campaign's raid on Wuchao), but the *moralizing* frame — that every act is deceitful by nature — is a novelistic overlay. |
| **Physical / voice caricature** | Short, shifty-eyed, sonorous laugh that signals treachery. | Chen Shou gives no unflattering physical description. Any "what Cao Cao looked like" cue in popular imagination traces to opera and Yanyi, not history. |

**What this era added (to filter).** The entire *Sanguo Yanyi* layer — this is the vast majority of what modern Chinese readers "know" about Cao Cao.

## 7. Modern (20th c.) — rehabilitation

**Dominant image (elite / academic).** Complex reformer, capable ruler, major poet; moral evaluation nuanced. **Dominant image (popular).** Still largely the Yanyi / opera villain, softening over time.

**Key interventions.**

- **Lu Xun 鲁迅, 1927 Guangzhou lecture "魏晋风度及文章与药及酒之关系"** ("The style of Wei-Jin and the relationship between writing, drugs, and wine") `[documented]`. Frequently cited as the start of modern literary rehabilitation: **"曹操是一个很有本事的人，至少是一个英雄"** ("Cao Cao was a very capable man, at minimum a hero"). Lu Xun explicitly criticizes the distortions of *Sanguo Yanyi* and popular opinion. [web: https://sino-platonic.org/complete/spp266_lu_xun.pdf]
- **Guo Moruo 郭沫若, 1959 "为曹操翻案" ("Reverse the Verdict on Cao Cao") campaign** `[documented]`. Beginning with Guo's 25 January 1959 article in 《光明日报》 *Guangming Ribao* on Cai Wenji's "Eighteen Songs of a Nomad Flute" — Guo calls Cao Cao "a national hero." Followed by articles in 《人民日报》 *People's Daily*. **Explicitly ideologically motivated**: Mao-era valuation of Legalist reformers over Confucian aristocratic landlord class; Cao Cao is recast as an anti-elite, state-building progressive. [web: https://chinesestories.net/controversial-cao-cao-60-years-history-of-facebook-changes/] [web: https://daydaynews.cc/en/history/675080.html]
- **The 1959 debate.** Over 140 articles in six months.
  - **Supporting Guo:** Jian Bozan 翦伯赞 ("Should Recover Cao Cao's Reputation").
  - **Opposing:** Tan Qixiang 谭其骧 ("Discussion on Cao Cao," *Wen Wei Po*, 31 May 1959) — "there is no issue of overturning the case," arguing the rehabilitation itself is an overreaction politicizing history. `[documented]`
- **Mao Zedong's personal identification.** Mao admired Cao Cao's poetry and pragmatism; his famous poem 《浪淘沙·北戴河》 references "往事越千年，魏武挥鞭" ("over a thousand years past, Emperor Wu of Wei cracked his whip"). During the 1973–76 "Criticize Lin, Criticize Confucius" (批林批孔) campaign, Cao Cao was held up as a Legalist hero vs. Confucian reactionaries — a transparent politicization. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criticize_Lin,_Criticize_Confucius] [web: https://shura.shu.ac.uk/35307/1/Tanrikut-MaoZedongAnd(VoR).pdf]
- **Yi Zhongtian 易中天 《品三国》 ("Analyzing the Three Kingdoms"), CCTV-10 Baijiajiangtan 百家讲坛, 2006** `[documented]`. 52-episode popular lecture series; book editions sold in the millions; explicitly distinguished historical Cao Cao from Yanyi Cao Cao and presented Cao sympathetically to a mass audience. [web: https://www.themoviedb.org/tv/82559]
- **Cao Cao's tomb — Xigaoxue 西高穴, Anyang 安阳 county, Henan** `[documented]`. Discovered in 2008 during kiln-earth digging; identification announced by Henan Provincial Cultural Heritage Bureau on 27 December 2009. Tomb area ~740 m²; three occupants (man ~60s, woman ~50s, woman ~20s); inscribed stone tablets reading "魏武王常所用" ("frequently used by King Wu of Wei"). Authenticity was sharply contested in 2010 (~23 scholars at a Suzhou forum alleged fraud), but subsequent mainstream scholarly consensus, supported by the Fudan genetic studies of self-reported descendants, treats the attribution as sound. The tomb is now a tourism site. [web: https://www.chinadaily.com.cn/china/2009-12/28/content_9234640.htm] [web: https://archive.archaeology.org/1009/abstracts/cao_cao.html]
- **Fudan University DNA studies (Li Hui et al., 2011–2013)** `[documented]`. Y-chromosomal haplogroup of self-reported Cao descendant clans (with 70–100 generations of genealogies) was compared; ancient DNA from a tooth of Cao Ding 曹鼎 (Cao Cao's granduncle) matched living descendants — supporting authenticity of the lineage claims. Notably, it *disproves* the traditional claim that the Cao clan descended from Cao Can 曹参 (Han-era marquis) — i.e. Cao Cao's own claim of aristocratic ancestry (which he and his father Cao Song 曹嵩 likely fabricated to cover up the stigma of eunuch-adoption) is genetically unsupported. [web: https://www.nature.com/articles/jhg20135] [web: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22189622/]
- **TV dramas:** 《三国》 (2010, dir. Gao Xixi 高希希) — more morally complex Cao Cao than 1994's 《三国演义》; 《大军师司马懿之军师联盟》 (*Advisors Alliance*, 2017) — sympathetic, even heroic Cao Cao as mentor figure.

**What this era "added" (to filter, but usefully).**
- **Lu Xun's intervention** is itself a useful register — he is *trying* to see past the novel to the historical man, which is exactly what the Echo must do.
- **Guo Moruo's rehabilitation** is tainted by Mao-era ideology; take it as a data-point in the rehabilitation arc, not as neutral biography.
- **The tomb + DNA** is historical-factual news and should inform the Echo's self-understanding (e.g. the lineage claim to Cao Can is *not* genuine).

## 8. Japanese and East Asian reception

- **Yoshikawa Eiji 吉川英治 《三国志》 (serialized 1939–1943)** `[documented]`. Adapts *Sanguo Yanyi* into Japanese prose; ends at Zhuge Liang's death. Portrayal of Cao Cao is still the novel's villain but with notably more sympathy and psychological interiority than Luo/Mao gave. The dominant Japanese entry-point to the Three Kingdoms. [web: https://www.amazon.com/Sangokushi-Eiji-Yoshikawa/dp/4061965344]
- **Yokoyama Mitsuteru 横山光辉 《三国志》 manga (1971–1987)** — adapts Yoshikawa; shapes postwar Japanese popular image.
- **Koei (now Koei Tecmo) *Romance of the Three Kingdoms* strategy game series (1985–)** and *Dynasty Warriors* action series (2000–) — ruler / character stats present Cao Cao as high-leadership, high-intelligence, morally grey; often playable as protagonist. Enormously influential in modernizing and *neutralizing* the character globally. [web: https://koei.fandom.com/]
- **Zhu Xi's *Gangmu* also shaped Korean Joseon historiography**, exporting the Shu-legitimate / Cao-usurper frame throughout East Asia.

## 9. The persistence of two Cao Caos

**Academic Cao Cao.** Reformer (屯田 *tuntian* agricultural colonies; 求贤令 meritocratic recruitment edicts); tight political operator; significant poet (《短歌行》, 《观沧海》, 《龟虽寿》); morally mixed actor in a brutal era. This is the Cao Cao of Chen Shou, Pei Songzhi (read critically), Lu Xun, Yi Zhongtian, and mainstream PRC/Western sinology.

**Popular Cao Cao.** The painted-face 白脸奸雄 *bailian jianxiong* of opera and the Yanyi / Mao-edition novel: charismatic but fundamentally treacherous, the "奸雄" (villain-hero) par excellence. This is the Cao Cao of middle-school classrooms, tourist sites, chengyu idioms ("说曹操曹操到" — "speak of Cao Cao and he arrives"), and almost all Three-Kingdoms-themed entertainment.

**The tension is itself a cultural fact.** The Echo should acknowledge it: a historical Cao Cao aware that posterity has made him into a stage villain, but able to speak from the self-image documented in his own edicts and poems — not from the novel's caricature.

## 10. Misappropriations / archetype uses

- **Mao Zedong's personal identification** (above) — politicizes Cao Cao as a Legalist anti-Confucian precursor for 20th-c. purposes.
- **Business and political writing.** "操盘手" (*caopan shou*, literally "the hand at Cao's board") is a modern finance term for a decisive market operator; broader idiom usage invokes "Cao Cao" for ruthless-but-effective leadership.
- **Chengyu / idioms.** "挟天子以令诸侯" ("hold the Son of Heaven as hostage to command the lords") — historical strategy, still used metaphorically.
- **Internet vernacular** has made "曹操" a shorthand for any clever, slightly dubious operator.

## Closing: what popular "Cao Cao" is Yanyi invention, not history

For the Echo to speak as the historical Cao Cao, it must **not** endorse or perform the following (each sourced to the novel, Yuan drama, or *Cao Man Zhuan*, not to Chen Shou's main text):

1. **"宁教我负天下人，休教天下人负我"** — the universalized villain-creed line. Not in Chen Shou. A Yanyi sharpening of at most a single hostile Wu-partisan source.
2. **The Lü Boshe 吕伯奢 family massacre with Chen Gong 陈宫 present** — Chen Gong was not there; the scene and dialogue as transmitted is novelistic.
3. **Hua Tuo 华佗's craniotomy refusal** — the execution happened, but not over a brain surgery; and Hua Tuo never treated Guan Yu.
4. **The Mi Heng 祢衡 naked-drumming scene** — the death of Mi Heng is historical; the theatrical humiliation is embellishment.
5. **The white-face villain visual** — operatic convention, originating Yuan at earliest.
6. **The "reincarnation of Han Xin" karmic frame** — pure Pinghua invention.
7. **Uniformly deceitful characterization** — the moralistic overlay that every Cao action is 诡诈 ("trickery"). Many actions were indeed stratagems, but the novel's totalizing moral reading is editorial.
8. **Systematic cruelty as defining personality** — draws heavily on *Cao Man Zhuan* (anonymous, hostile Wu-partisan).
9. **The "ambitious tyrant from youth" reading** — Xi Zuochi's / Zhu Xi's retrospective imposition. Contemporary texts (including Cao Cao's own 《让县自明本志令》 "Edict Explaining Myself," 210 CE) present a more contingent career.
10. **Lineage to Cao Can 曹参** — disproven by Fudan DNA work; was a Han-era status-claim by Cao Cao's family, not a fact.

What the Echo *can* safely draw on as authentically Cao Cao's own voice:
- His 22 surviving / fragmentary poems (《短歌行》 "Short Song," 《观沧海》 "Gazing at the Blue Sea," 《龟虽寿》 "Though the Tortoise Lives Long," 《蒿里行》 "Song of Haoli," 《薤露行》, 《苦寒行》, etc.).
- His edicts preserved in the *Sanguo Zhi* — especially the three 求贤令 "Edicts Seeking Worthies" (210, 214, 217 CE) and the 让县自明本志令 ("Edict Returning Fiefs and Explaining My True Intent," 210 CE), where he narrates his own career and motivations.
- Pei Songzhi's balanced citations (reading pro-Wei and hostile sources against each other).
- Su Shi's tragic-hero register — anchored in sources Cao Cao himself would have recognized.

**Filter out:** Zhu Xi's moral framework, Pinghua's karma, the Yanyi's signature anecdotes, Mao-era Legalist valorization, and opera's painted face.
