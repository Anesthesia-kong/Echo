# Chongzhen 崇禎 / Zhu Youjian 朱由檢 (1611-1644) — Contemporary Records

**Scope.** Sources produced by people who lived through his reign, or interviewed those who did, within roughly thirty years of his death on the *jiashen* day 1644/3/19 (Gregorian 25 April 1644) on Coal Hill (煤山 / 萬歲山). The 1739 Qing-commissioned 《明史》 is deliberately *excluded* — it is reception, not testimony, handled by Agent 6. This document is feed-material for an Echo distillation: the aim is how the man *appeared* to eyewitnesses — how he moved, spoke, decided, collapsed.

Every claim is origin-tagged. Labels used:
- **[documented]** — appears in a near-contemporary written source, multiply or securely attested.
- **[attested]** — appears in one or two near-contemporary sources, plausibly accurate.
- **[attributed]** — reported in a contemporary source but flagged internally as hearsay, or appearing only in a single later loyalist text drawing on oral tradition.
- **[legendary]** — hagiographic or folkloric, likely shaped by post-1644 loyalist sentiment; preserved for the record but unreliable as literal biography.

---

## 1. Source landscape

The defining fact of Chongzhen historiography is an absence: **the official 《崇禎實錄》 (Chongzhen Veritable Records) was never properly compiled.** The dynasty fell before its own historiographers could close the reign, and the Qing state had political reasons to keep the ledger open. What exists instead is a proliferation of private and semi-official records, nearly all written between 1644 and the 1680s, by men who had served Chongzhen, or had watched him fall, or had in some cases stood in the ruined palace days after his body was cut down.

This matters for the Echo project in a specific way: the loyalist chronicles — 《國榷》, 《明季北略》, 《烈皇小識》, 《三垣筆記》, 《石匱書後集》 — are **not** derivatives of the Ming Shi. They are its *sources*. The 1739 compilation is downstream of them. So where Ming Shi and a loyalist text diverge, the loyalist text usually has the earlier witness behind it, and Ming Shi is the editorial smoothing. [documented]

The semi-official spine that does survive is 《崇禎長編》, compiled by Hanlin historiographer Wang Ji 汪楫 (d. 1636) in the style of a *shilu* (veritable record). It survives in 68 juan, of which 66 are dense year-by-year annals for 1627-1632, plus two late juan — by another hand — covering fragments of 1643-1644. So the beginning and the very end are documented in *shilu*-style rhythm; the desperate middle is not. [documented]

Around this skeleton cluster three bands of testimony:

1. **Loyalist private chronicles** — Ming officials who survived 1644, some serving the Southern Ming courts, writing while memory was fresh and witnesses alive.
2. **Eyewitness jiashen (1644) accounts** — short, dated, written within a year or two, often by men who were physically inside Beijing when it fell.
3. **Foreign observers** — Jesuits in Beijing (Schall) and Hangzhou (Martini); Joseon envoys to the Ming capital who filed reports preserved in the 朝鮮王朝實錄.

## 2. Official / semi-official records

### 2.1 《崇禎長編》 (Chongzhen changbian) — Wang Ji 汪楫

- **Dates of coverage:** 1627 (accession)-1632 dense; fragments for 1643-1644.
- **Author position:** Hanlin historiographer compiling in the style of the missing *shilu*; died 1636, so personally observed only the first nine years.
- **Value for Echo:** the single most procedurally accurate source for the early reign — the 1627 Wei Zhongxian purge, the 1629 Jisi Incident (Manchu raid on the capital), the January 1630 arrest of Yuan Chonghuan at audience, the late-1630 lingchi execution. Records audience order, who spoke, who was present, what was decreed. [documented]
- **Bias:** institutional; softens the emperor's outbursts into formulaic 震怒 ("flew into a rage") where private sources preserve the specific invective. But the procedural record is trustworthy.

### 2.2 崇禎邸報 (Di Bao, the court gazette)

- The Chongzhen-era *dibao* (government bulletin) survived in fragments that Tan Qian 談遷 was able to borrow and collate in Beijing in the 1650s. [documented]
- Significance: it is the base layer under most loyalist chronicles. When Tan Qian's 《國榷》, Ji Liuqi's 《明季北略》, and Wen Bing's 《烈皇小識》 agree on a decree, audience date, or promotion/dismissal, they usually agree because all three had access to the same gazette entries.

## 3. Loyalist chroniclers (private, pre-1680)

### 3.1 《國榷》 Guoque — Tan Qian 談遷 (1594-1658)

- **Method:** began in 1621; manuscript stolen 1647 and rewritten from memory; then in the **tenth year of Shunzhi (1653)** Tan Qian went north with the reconstructed manuscript and spent **two and a half years in Beijing**, interviewing surviving Ming officials, palace eunuchs, and imperial clansmen, and borrowing the surviving *dibao*. The final Chongzhen-reign juan reflect this trip. [documented]
- **Value for Echo:** Guoque is the closest thing to a cross-examined private record. Where a detail is in Guoque, it has typically been checked against multiple 1650s informants.
- **Temperament toward Chongzhen:** tragic-betrayed. Tan Qian writes a ruler diligent to the point of exhaustion, surrounded by small men, destroyed by circumstance as much as by his own temper. [documented]

### 3.2 《明季北略》 and 《明季南略》 — Ji Liuqi 計六奇 (1622-after 1687)

- Ji Liuqi was 22 when Beijing fell. He was from Wuxi, not in the capital, but compiled 《北略》 (24 juan, northern events, ending in 1644) and 《南略》 (Southern Ming sequel) from the testimony of refugees and surviving officials who passed through Jiangnan. [documented]
- **Value:** dense anecdotal detail on specific audiences, specific outbursts, specific dismissals — often traceable to a named informant.
- **Weakness:** "quite reliable because of the short time distance between the events and the compilation but it suffers from historiographic shortcomings by including popular tales." Ji Liuqi does not always filter marketplace rumor. [documented]
- **Temperament:** tragic-betrayed, like Tan Qian, but with sharper attention to the emperor's personality faults.

### 3.3 《烈皇小識》 Liehuang xiaoshi — Wen Bing 文秉 (1609-1669)

- Son of Grand Secretary **Wen Zhenmeng 文震孟** (1574-1636), who served Chongzhen briefly in 1635 and was pushed out by factional enemies. Wen Bing grew up inside the late Ming official class; after 1644 he withdrew to the mountains of Wuxian. [documented]
- **Stance — important:** Wen Bing is the most **pro-Chongzhen** of the loyalist chroniclers. His father's generation is the frame of reference: the emperor as upright and overwhelmed, the ministers as the disaster. Many of the paranoid-ruler anecdotes in later sources are *absent* from 《烈皇小識》, replaced with scenes of ministers lying, shirking, or politicking. Wen Bing preserves the memorials verbatim and often indicts the courtier rather than the ruler. [documented]
- **Value for Echo:** the surviving voice of the loyalist faction that, at the time, blamed Grand Secretaries Wen Tiren 溫體仁, Zhou Yanru 周延儒, and the military hierarchy rather than the throne.

### 3.4 《三垣筆記》 Sanyuan biji — Li Qing 李清 (1602-1683)

- Li Qing served as *gei shi zhong* 給事中 (supervising secretary) at the Ming court through the 1630s and into the Hongguang Southern Ming in 1645. "Three Enclosures" (三垣) refers to the three censorial offices where he held posts; he was inside the memorial-traffic of the reign.
- **Temperament — the most balanced loyalist.** Li Qing writes in cool notebook register, episode by episode, without the editorial sweep of Tan Qian or the exoneration project of Wen Bing. He records the emperor's diligence and his cruelty in the same paragraph. [documented]
- **Value for Echo:** the texture of palace life — specific audiences, specific memorials, specific exchanges — preserved in a calm voice.

### 3.5 《石匱書後集》 Shigui shu houji — Zhang Dai 張岱 (1597-c. 1684)

- Zhang Dai, the great memoirist of late-Ming Jiangnan leisure, devoted his surviving post-1644 decades to a private Ming history. [documented]
- **Unusually harsh assessment.** Where Tan Qian and Wen Bing write tragedy, Zhang Dai writes indictment. His Chongzhen is diligent but fundamentally paranoid, rash in reward-and-punishment, unable to trust any minister long enough to let policy succeed, destroying his own generals, turning intelligence into cruelty. The signature judgment — the emperor *had* the form of a sage-ruler but not the substance, and his diligence accelerated the fall rather than slowing it — is Zhang Dai's. [documented]

## 4. Eyewitness 甲申 (1644) accounts

### 4.1 《甲申傳信錄》 Jiashen chuanxin lu (1653)

- Compiled 1653 from multiple 1644 eyewitness fragments. [documented]
- Covers the final two months: rebel advance, 南遷 debate, fall on 3/19, aftermath under Li Zicheng's Shun regime, Qing entry.
- Especially valuable for *who was in the palace in what position on which day.*

### 4.2 《甲申紀事》 Jiashen jishi — Zhao Shijin 趙士錦

- Serving as minor official in Ministry of Works when Beijing fell. Captured by Li Zicheng, held, interrogated, released. First-person diary-style. [documented]
- **Suicide-note divergence:** Zhao Shijin's version of what was on the imperial robe is minimal — essentially the two characters **天子** — against the longer moral confession in later loyalist chronicles.

### 4.3 《甲申核真略》 Jiashen hezhen lüe — Yang Shicong 楊士聰

- Censor in Beijing in 1644. The title means "Brief Verification of the Truth of the *jiashen* Year" — explicitly skeptical. [documented]
- **Value for Echo:** a contemporary doing active source-criticism on his own peers. Competing versions of the fall were *already* in circulation — not an artifact of later Qing ideology.

### 4.4 Qian Qianyi 錢謙益 (1582-1664)

- Senior late-Ming official, defected to Qing 1645, later repented. His occasional prose and poetry preserve scenes and rumors from the fall.
- **Bias:** extreme — reputation-management across Shun-Qing-Southern-Ming transitions makes him unreliable as self-narrator, but reliable first-hand witness on others.

## 5. Foreign observers

### 5.1 Johann Adam Schall von Bell 湯若望 (1592-1666)

- Jesuit astronomer in Beijing from 1630 on the calendar-reform commission. Correspondence 1634+ includes the telescope and astronomical instruments he built for the emperor. [documented]
- **Direct contact with Chongzhen:** documented, though mediated — Schall submitted instruments and memorials, was received, exchanged through eunuch channels.
- **The clavichord episode (1640).** A clavichord originally presented by Matteo Ricci to the Wanli emperor was rediscovered. Chongzhen ordered Schall to repair it and to translate the Latin psalm inscribed on it — *Cantate Domino canticum novum* (Psalm 96/98). The instrument and accompanying gifts were brought to the palace on **8 September 1640**. [documented]
- **《進呈書像》 Jincheng shu xiang (1640).** A Chinese-language illustrated life of Christ, 48 woodblock prints with captions, adapted from a 1617 Munich engraved *Life of Christ*. Presented to Chongzhen in 1640. [documented]
- **Historica Narratio (Vienna 1665).** Published under Schall's name, actually compiled in Vienna from his letters.
- **Schall's portrait of Chongzhen:** **curious, personally decent, intellectually alert, but boxed in by officials and incapable of sustained trust** — a ruler hemmed in by his own court. [documented]
- **Bias flag:** Schall later served the new Qing regime; the 1665 Vienna printing is a Jesuit PR document. Expect a softened Chongzhen.

### 5.2 Martino Martini 衛匡國 (1614-1661)

- Italian Jesuit, arrived in China 1642, in Jiangnan / Hangzhou area during the fall. His 《De Bello Tartarico Historia》 (Antwerp 1654) was the first detailed European narrative of the Manchu conquest. [documented]
- Not in Beijing when it fell — his account of the emperor's death is second-hand.
- **Portrait:** brief, sympathetic, a virtuous emperor betrayed.

### 5.3 Joseon 朝鮮王朝實錄 — Korean envoy reports

- Annual embassies to Beijing filed reports preserved in the 實錄.
- **Value:** outside-eye detail on court ceremony, emperor's appearance at formal receptions, capital state, grain prices, morale. Report increasing visible strain year over year. [attested]
- **Bias:** Joseon had reasons to recognize Ming legitimacy even after 1644; Chongzhen is treated as the last proper Son of Heaven.

## 6. Eyewitness descriptions of the person

### 6.1 Appearance

- Idealized court portraits. [attributed]
- **"Haggard and careworn" in later years** — Li Qing, Wen Bing, Tan Qian via 1650s informants. By 1642-43, officials who had not seen him for a year report being shocked. [documented]
- **At the end, 1644/3/19:** hair disordered / hanging loose (披髮), blue silk robe (藍袍), red trousers (紅褲), one foot bare. [documented — multiply attested]

### 6.2 Speech

- **Interrupts ministers.** An official begins a formal memorial at audience; the emperor cuts in before the stylized opening is complete, demanding the point. [documented]
- **Demands numerical answers.** How many troops, how much grain, how many days, how many taels. Vague answers → reddens, rises, accuses of 推諉 (shirking). [documented]
- **Cites 書 (Shangshu) and 詩 (Shijing) mid-argument** — moral shaming, not erudition display. [documented]
- **震怒 — "flew into a rage."** The recurring verb across sources. [documented]

### 6.3 Emotional range

- **Weeping:** attested at Wen Zhenmeng's 1635 dismissal; at Consort Tian's 1642 death; at audience over military defeats. [documented]
- **Drinking wine alone** on 1644/3/18, weeping, cursing ministers. [attested]
- **Laughter — essentially unattested.** The silence is itself data. [documented by absence]
- **Rage — routine.** Default affective register in audience.

### 6.4 Work habits

- **雞鳴即起 — rose at cockcrow.** Constant habit. 14-18 hour working days. Edicts issued at midnight. Stacks of memorials on desk. [documented]
- **No banquets, no entertainments.** Contrast Wanli and Tianqi. [documented]

### 6.5 Frugality

- **Mended clothes.** Empress Zhou personally weaving cloth. Probably **[legendary]** in strong form; [attested] in weak form (court genuinely reduced personal expenditure).
- **But:** in 1644, refused to open the privy purse to pay the Beijing garrison. When Shun regime seized the treasury, silver was found (37 million taels figure is disputed hearsay per Yang Shicong). [documented — refusal; attested/disputed — magnitude]

### 6.6 Household

- **Empress Zhou 周皇后** — ordered to suicide 1644/3/19, hanged herself in Kunning Palace. [documented]
- **Consort Tian 田貴妃** — d. 1642. His grief best-documented instance of affection. [documented]
- **Princess Changping 長平公主** — teenage daughter, struck with sword, severed left arm, survived briefly. [documented]
- **Consort Yuan 袁貴妃 + Princess Zhaoren 昭仁** — killed by emperor the final day. [documented]
- **Crown Prince Zhu Cilang 朱慈烺 + two princes** — sent out disguised, eventually lost. [documented]
- **Wang Cheng'en 王承恩** — chief eunuch; accompanied to Coal Hill; hanged himself beside him. [documented]

### 6.7 Personality

- **Paranoid distrust — uniform across sources.** Every chronicler (Wen Bing exonerating, Zhang Dai condemning, Li Qing balanced) records the same pattern: minister raised → suspected → dismissed → often executed. [documented]
- **Rapid favor→disgrace cycle** (see §7 counts).
- **Assassination fear 1627** — entered the palace with his own food in his sleeve, suspecting Wei Zhongxian's poison. [attested]

## 7. Key decisions with dates

### 7.1 Accession and Wei Zhongxian purge
- **1627/8:** accession at 16 sui.
- **1627/11:** Wei Zhongxian demoted, hanged himself en route. [documented]
- **1628:** 逆案 purge — ~161 associates punished. [documented]

### 7.2 Postal-relay reform (1629) → Li Zicheng
- **1629:** postal-relay system cut for budget. **Li Zicheng 李自成**, courier at 銀川 station in Shaanxi, lost his job. [documented]

### 7.3 Jisi Incident + Yuan Chonghuan fall
- **1629/10 (Nov 1629):** Manchu raid to Beijing suburbs. [documented]
- **1630/1/13:** Yuan Chonghuan arrested at audience. [documented]
- **1630/9/22:** Executed by slicing 凌遲 at Ganshiqiao 甘石橋. Crowds reportedly ate his flesh. [documented]

### 7.4 Purge cycle (total reign counts)
- **~7 Supreme Military Commanders (總督)** executed/suicided/dismissed in disgrace. [documented]
- **~11 Regional Commanders (巡撫)** killed or disgraced. [documented]
- **~14 Ministers of War (兵部尚書)**, or ~17 counting acting/interim. [documented]
- **~50 Grand Secretaries (閣臣)** cycled through in 17 years. [documented]

### 7.5 Specific generals lost
- **1639:** Lu Xiangsheng 盧象昇 killed in battle at Jiazhuang, abandoned by eunuch supervisor Gao Qiqian 高起潛. [documented]
- **1640:** Xiong Wencan 熊文燦 executed (Zhang Xianzhong pacification failure). [documented]
- **1642:** Songshan 松山 disaster. Hong Chengchou 洪承疇 captured, eventually surrendered. Chongzhen held memorial services believing him martyred. [documented]
- **1642:** Chen Xinjia 陳新甲 executed (scapegoat for secretly authorized peace talks). [documented]
- **1643:** Sun Chuanting 孫傳庭 killed at Tongguan 潼關 after Chongzhen overrode his judgment. [documented]

### 7.6 The 1644 南遷 debate
- Early 1644: Li Mingrui 李明睿 formally proposed moving court to Nanjing. [documented]
- Emperor privately attracted; wouldn't own the decision publicly, waited for ministers to propose. Ministers, wary of being blamed later, refused. Debate deadlocked. [documented]

### 7.7 Final day (1644/3/18-19, Gregorian 24-25 April 1644)
- **3/18 evening:** drinks alone, weeps, curses ministers.
- **3/19 predawn:** strikes the palace bell; none come.
- Goes to Empress Zhou — already hanged. Kills Consort Yuan and Princess Zhaoren. Slashes at Princess Changping (cuts off left arm; survives).
- Dispatches three princes disguised.
- Changes clothing — hair loose, blue silk robe, red trousers — walks with Wang Cheng'en to Coal Hill.
- Hangs himself from a tree. Wang Cheng'en hangs beside.
- Note left on hem/lapel of robe. (See §8.)
[documented — multiply attested]

## 8. Contradictions to preserve

### 8.1 Yuan Chonghuan — traitor or framed?
- **Prosecution-contemporary:** Yuan had privately negotiated with Manchus, killed Mao Wenlong 毛文龍 without authorization, brought his army under Beijing's walls. [attested]
- **Loyalist (Tan Qian):** Hong Taiji's 反間 counter-intelligence plant via captured eunuchs; emperor believed the rumor too fast. [documented]
- Both readings near-contemporary. **Preserve both.**

### 8.2 The suicide note
- **Long version (loyalist consensus):** full moral confession. [documented but already stabilized by 1650s as literary object]
- **Minimal version (Zhao Shijin):** essentially **天子** — two characters. [documented from most physically proximate witness]
- Not easily reconcilable. The famous note may be partly a loyalist composition.

### 8.3 1644 南遷 — genuine desire or performed refusal?
- **Loyalist reading (Li Qing, Tan Qian):** privately wanted move; ministers failed him.
- **Harsh reading (Zhang Dai):** performance of indecision *was* the decision; he refused because he could not bear political cost.
- Both contemporary. Both defensible.

### 8.4 Four-character verdicts — irreconcilable near-contemporary judgments
- **Tan Qian + Ji Liuqi:** tragic-betrayed.
- **Zhang Dai:** harsh / paranoid.
- **Wen Bing:** ministers-at-fault / emperor-exonerated.
- **Schall:** curious, decent, hemmed in.

All four by men who lived the reign or interviewed survivors within a decade. **Echo must carry all four registers.**
