# Emerson — Contemporary Records

Emerson is unusually well-witnessed: the Concord circle kept journals, wrote letters, and annotated each other constantly. He was also a major public figure on the lyceum circuit, so newspaper coverage is extensive from about 1838 onward.

## 1. Within the Family

**Mary Moody Emerson (1774–1863)** — Emerson's paternal aunt; his most formative early influence. Her journals (the "Almanacks") are a Calvinist-mystical-romantic document. She raised the orphaned Emerson boys (their father William died 1811) as much as their mother did. Emerson's 1869 memorial essay on her is the most emotionally intimate portrait he ever wrote of anyone. [documented — Cole 1998]

**William Emerson (1769–1811)** — father; Unitarian minister at First Church Boston; died when Waldo was 8. Set the professional template that Waldo followed into ministry and later broke.

**Ruth Haskins Emerson (1768–1853)** — mother; kept the large household running in genteel poverty; lived with Waldo in Concord in her later years.

**Edward Bliss Emerson (1805–1834)** and **Charles Chauncy Emerson (1808–1836)** — Waldo's gifted younger brothers, both died of tuberculosis in their late 20s. Their deaths devastated him; Charles in particular was thought by the family to surpass Waldo intellectually. Waldo's early optimism is shadowed by these losses.

**Ellen Tucker Emerson (1811–1831)** — first wife; died of tuberculosis after less than two years of marriage. He visited her tomb daily for a long period; her death shaped the 1832 resignation from the ministry and the subsequent European trip. In 1832 he opened her coffin (documented in his journal, 29 March 1832). [documented — JMN; Richardson 1995]

**Lidian Jackson Emerson (1802–1892)** — second wife, m. 1835. Four children: **Waldo** (1836–1842, died of scarlet fever at age 5 — devastation recorded in the essay "Experience" and the poem "Threnody"), **Ellen** (1839–1909), **Edith** (1841–1929), **Edward Waldo** (1844–1930). Lidian's letters and Ellen's later compilations preserve the domestic Emerson.

**Ellen Tucker Emerson (daughter, 1839–1909)** — Waldo's literary-domestic assistant in later years; her *Life of Lidian Jackson Emerson* (1980, ed. Delores Bird Carpenter) is a primary source for family life.

## 2. The Concord Circle (Daily Witnesses)

**Henry David Thoreau (1817–1862)** — lived in the Emerson household for extended periods (1841–1843 as handyman-companion; again 1847–1848 during Emerson's English lecture tour). Thoreau's journal records constant exchanges with Emerson. Walden (1854) is inseparable from the Emerson circle. Their friendship cooled somewhat in the 1850s (tensions over Thoreau's independence and Emerson's paternalism) but never fully broke. Emerson's funeral eulogy for Thoreau (1862) is a major document — though Thoreau's sister Sophia felt it reduced him. [documented — Thoreau's journal; Harding 1965 *The Days of Henry Thoreau*]

**Amos Bronson Alcott (1799–1888)** — fellow Transcendentalist, founder of Fruitlands (the failed utopian community, 1843). Emerson supported him financially and defended him publicly despite Alcott's eccentricities. Alcott's *Journals* record many conversations.

**Louisa May Alcott (1832–1888)** — Bronson's daughter; grew up in the Emerson orbit; later wrote of him with great warmth in *Eminent Women of the Age* (1869).

**Margaret Fuller (1810–1850)** — brilliant, intense; co-edited *The Dial* with Emerson 1840–1842; their relationship was emotionally charged. She wanted a deeper intimacy than Emerson could offer; his essay "Friendship" and "Love" partly address the tension. Her letters to him are among the most important documents of the period. After her death in a shipwreck off Fire Island (1850), Emerson co-edited her *Memoirs* (1852). [documented — Fuller *Letters*, Hudspeth ed.]

**Nathaniel Hawthorne (1804–1864)** — neighbor in Concord. Lived in the Old Manse (owned by Emerson's grandfather) 1842–1845. Hawthorne's *American Notebooks* and his preface to *Mosses from an Old Manse* give a skeptical-friendly portrait. Hawthorne never quite bought Transcendentalism but respected Emerson personally.

**Ellery Channing (the younger, 1817–1901)** — poet, biographer of Thoreau, Emerson's walking companion for decades.

**Elizabeth Palmer Peabody (1804–1894)** — bookstore owner, founder of the Transcendentalist publishing circle, midwife of the movement.

**Sarah Alden Ripley** (1793–1867) — remarkable classicist, Emerson's cousin-in-law, one of his intellectual benchmarks.

**George Ripley (1802–1880)** — founder of the Brook Farm commune (1841–1847). Emerson declined to join, though he supported the experiment in principle; the declination is recorded in his journal.

## 3. The Broader American Literary Scene

**Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr. (1809–1894)** — friend and later biographer. His *Ralph Waldo Emerson* (American Men of Letters series, 1884) is the first scholarly biography, by a close contemporary. Holmes famously called "The American Scholar" "our intellectual Declaration of Independence." [documented]

**James Russell Lowell (1819–1891)** — critic, poet; his satirical *A Fable for Critics* (1848) contains a famous portrait:
> "There comes Emerson first, whose rich words, every one,
> Are like gold nails in temples to hang trophies on, [...]
> He is Plotinus-Montaigne..." [documented]

**Walt Whitman (1819–1892)** — Emerson sent his famous 1855 letter greeting Whitman "at the beginning of a great career" after receiving a copy of *Leaves of Grass*. Whitman used the letter in promoting the second edition without Emerson's permission — a mild embarrassment. Emerson continued to praise Whitman's genius while distancing himself from some of the content. [documented — Whitman's *Correspondence*, Miller ed.]

**Frederick Douglass (c. 1818–1895)** — met Emerson in antislavery contexts. Douglass lectured with Emerson's associates and appreciated Emerson's (eventual) public abolitionism. [documented — Douglass correspondence; Gougeon 1990]

**Herman Melville (1819–1891)** — heard Emerson lecture and mocked him as "Mr. Plinlimmon" in *Pierre* (1852); *The Confidence-Man* (1857) may contain Emerson-parody. Yet Melville annotated his Emerson volumes heavily — the relationship was ambivalent-obsessive rather than hostile.

**Henry James Sr. (1811–1882)** — father of William and Henry James; Swedenborgian and Emerson-admirer. Young William and Henry grew up in the Emerson orbit.

**William James (1842–1910)** — Emerson's godson; later acknowledged Emerson as a central influence on his own pragmatism. His Emerson Centenary Address (1903) is a key document.

## 4. European Witnesses

**Thomas Carlyle (1795–1881)** — lifelong correspondent. Emerson met Carlyle at Craigenputtock, Scotland in August 1833, on Emerson's first European trip; they corresponded until 1872. Emerson arranged American publication of *Sartor Resartus* (1836) and edited Carlyle for Boston. Their correspondence (ed. Slater 1964) is one of the great 19th-century exchanges. They diverged politically — Carlyle grew reactionary, Emerson moved toward abolition — but the affection held.

**John Sterling (1806–1844)** — English cleric-skeptic; died young; Emerson's *Letters to Sterling* are minor but revealing.

**Arthur Hugh Clough (1819–1861)** — English poet; befriended Emerson on the 1847–1848 lecture tour; stayed in Concord.

**Herman Grimm (1828–1901)** — German art historian and cultural critic; major European Emerson-advocate; facilitated Nietzsche's exposure.

**Matthew Arnold (1822–1888)** — his lecture "Emerson" (1883, delivered in Boston after Emerson's death) is a famously backhanded appreciation: Arnold denies Emerson is a "great writer" or "great philosopher" but grants him the best of Americans at "the art of living." [documented — Arnold *Discourses in America*]

**Friedrich Nietzsche (1844–1900)** — encountered Emerson at 17 and read him obsessively for the rest of his life; underlined nearly every page of his German edition; took the title *Die fröhliche Wissenschaft* (The Gay Science) from Emerson's phrase "the gay science"; the concept of the *Übermensch* bears Emersonian DNA. *This is a deeply formative intellectual debt, not a casual citation.* [documented — Stack 1992 *Nietzsche and Emerson*; Cavell on Emerson-Nietzsche]

## 5. Public Life: The Lyceum, the Trial, the Politics

**The Lyceum Circuit.** Emerson lectured ~1,500 times between 1833 and 1875 — from Cincinnati to California. He was the highest-paid lecturer of his generation. Each lecture was delivered multiple times, revised, and later harvested for essays. This is his *actual professional life*, more than the book publishing. [documented — Eric Cheyfitz; Von Frank, *An Emerson Chronology*]

**The Divinity School Address crisis (1838).** On 15 July 1838 Emerson delivered the commencement address to six graduating Harvard Divinity School seniors. He rejected miracles as evidence, rejected historical Christianity's claim to unique revelation, and called for preachers to trust their own souls. The Unitarian establishment — Andrews Norton above all — responded with *The Latest Form of Infidelity* (1839). Emerson was effectively banned from Harvard until his Phi Beta Kappa address in 1867. [documented]

**Lecture reception.** Contemporary newspaper accounts (preserved in Cabot 1887, later in Von Frank's *Chronology*) record mixed responses: rapt audiences; befuddled audiences; hostile clergy. He was renowned for a slightly awkward but magnetic stage presence — tall, lean, with a voice Emily Dickinson called "the voice of God."

**Emily Dickinson (1830–1886)** — never met Emerson but read him deeply; her compression owes something to his aphoristic essays.

## 6. Key Biographical Episodes

**1832** — Resigned pulpit at Second Church Boston over inability in good conscience to administer communion. Public act; published sermon explaining.

**1832–1833** — European trip: met Coleridge (Highgate), Wordsworth (Rydal Mount), Carlyle (Craigenputtock).

**1836** — Published *Nature*. Helped found the informal "Transcendental Club" (Hedge's Club).

**1837** — "The American Scholar" address, Phi Beta Kappa, Harvard.

**1838** — Divinity School Address.

**1840–1844** — Co-edited *The Dial* magazine with Margaret Fuller.

**1842 — Death of son Waldo** (age 5) on 27 January. The central wound of Emerson's life. *"Threnody"* (1847) and *"Experience"* (1844) both responses. Lidian never fully recovered. The grief is unmistakably different from his earlier oracular assurance.

**1845** — Refused to lecture at the New Bedford Lyceum after it excluded Black members.

**1847–1848** — Second European trip; extensive lecture tour in England and Scotland; basis for *English Traits*.

**1850 — The Fugitive Slave Law.** Emerson's public turn against the Compromise of 1850 is the decisive moment in his political evolution. His journal records the outrage; his 1851 lecture on the Fugitive Slave Law, his 1854 address on the same, and his 1855 speech in support of Sumner mark the conversion of the private objector into a public abolitionist. [documented — Gougeon 1990]

**1859 — Defense of John Brown.** Emerson publicly called Brown "that new saint, than whom none purer or more brave was ever led by love of men into conflict and death — the new saint awaiting his martyrdom, and who, if he shall suffer, will make the gallows glorious like the cross." [documented — journal and 1859 lecture]

**1862 — Met Lincoln** at the White House.

**1865** — Harvard finally invited him back to give the commemoration oration.

**1872 — House fire.** His Concord house burned; for months afterward his mind showed signs of decline.

**1872–1873 — World tour** (Egypt, Mediterranean) to recuperate. Returned in diminished condition.

**1875–1882 — Mental decline.** Aphasia, memory loss. James Elliot Cabot assembled his late books with Ellen's help.

**27 April 1882 — Death** in Concord. Buried at Sleepy Hollow Cemetery, near Thoreau and Hawthorne.

## 7. Preserved Contradictions Among Witnesses

- **Private man vs platform persona.** The man who hesitated, revised, and doubted in his journals was not the man who thundered on the lyceum platform. Carlyle noted the difference after their 1833 meeting.
- **Warm or cold?** Thoreau sometimes experienced Emerson as a withholding friend; Louisa May Alcott remembered him as kindly and attentive; Lidian on some days felt he was too abstract; his children found him warm.
- **On reformers.** He supported, skeptically, the Brook Farm and Fruitlands experiments but did not join them. He criticized reformers for being one-eyed; yet he himself became one on slavery.
- **On race.** Early journals contain racial theorizing that is explicitly racist in modern terms (e.g., phrenological hierarchy of races). By 1850s he was a leading abolitionist in New England. The evolution is documented; neither phase can be erased.
- **Whitman.** Emerson's 1855 letter to Whitman is unreserved ("I greet you at the beginning of a great career"). His 1860s–70s comments are more qualified. He never withdrew the praise but distanced from the sexual explicitness.
