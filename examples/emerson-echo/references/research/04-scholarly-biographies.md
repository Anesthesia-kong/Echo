# Emerson — Modern Critical Scholarship

Emerson scholarship has gone through three major phases: (1) the early hagiographic era (Cabot, Holmes, late 19th century) that canonized him; (2) the mid-20th-century critical/chastened era (Whicher's *Freedom and Fate* 1953, Matthiessen's *American Renaissance* 1941) that complicated the cheerful surface and foregrounded the shadow; (3) the post-1980 pluralization — Cavell's philosophical recovery, Richardson's literary biography, Gougeon's abolitionism studies, Buell's synthesis, Packer on darkness. Current scholarship treats Emerson as the central American philosophical and literary figure of the 19th century, whose reputation had been damaged by New Criticism's hostility in the 1940s–60s.

## 1. The Three Phases

### (a) Hagiographic (1884–c.1930)
- **Oliver Wendell Holmes Sr.**, *Ralph Waldo Emerson* (1884) — American Men of Letters series; first scholarly biography; by a contemporary friend.
- **James Elliot Cabot**, *A Memoir of Ralph Waldo Emerson* (1887, 2 vols.) — by his literary executor; detailed, respectful, close to the family.
- **Van Wyck Brooks**, *The Flowering of New England* (1936) — situated Emerson in the Concord cultural efflorescence.

### (b) Critical / Chastened (c.1941–1980)
- **F. O. Matthiessen**, *American Renaissance* (1941) — landmark; linked Emerson to Whitman, Thoreau, Hawthorne, Melville as the 1850–1855 peak of American literature; reversed the earlier view that Emerson was merely a sage, recovered him as a writer.
- **Stephen E. Whicher**, *Freedom and Fate: An Inner Life of Ralph Waldo Emerson* (1953) — pivotal; read Emerson's career as a move from early optimistic self-reliance to chastened acknowledgment of fate and limit in "Experience" (1844) and *The Conduct of Life* (1860). Key frame still referenced in debates.
- **Jonathan Bishop**, *Emerson on the Soul* (1964) — reconstruction of the metaphysical spine.
- **Joel Porte**, *Emerson and Thoreau: Transcendentalists in Conflict* (1965); *Representative Man: Ralph Waldo Emerson in His Time* (1979). Porte is one of the great Emerson readers of his generation.
- Meanwhile, the **New Critics** (Yvor Winters, F. R. Leavis) were hostile — finding Emerson sentimental, undisciplined, insufficiently tragic. Emerson's stock fell in mid-century syllabi.

### (c) Pluralization and recovery (1980–present)
- **Harold Bloom**, *Ralph Waldo Emerson* (Modern Critical Views, 1985); *The American Religion* (1992); *Figures of Capable Imagination*, various essays. Bloom rescued Emerson as "the mind of America" and situated him as the father of a distinct American tradition (Dickinson, Whitman, Stevens, Ashbery).
- **Stanley Cavell**, *The Senses of Walden* (1972); *Conditions Handsome and Unhandsome* (1990); *This New Yet Unapproachable America* (1989); *Emerson's Transcendental Etudes* (2003). The major philosophical recovery — Cavell read Emerson as a serious philosopher in ordinary-language and perfectionist traditions, aligned with Wittgenstein, Austin, and Thoreau.
- **Robert D. Richardson Jr.**, *Emerson: The Mind on Fire* (1995). The landmark modern biography: ~600 pages, deeply researched, literary in texture. Companion to his *Thoreau: A Life of the Mind* (1986).
- **Barbara L. Packer**, *Emerson's Fall* (1982); *The Transcendentalists* (2007). Foregrounded the dark side, the post-Waldo grief, the theological crises.
- **Lawrence Buell**, *Emerson* (2003); *Literary Transcendentalism* (1973); *The Environmental Imagination* (1995). Synthetic; includes reception-history.
- **Len Gougeon**, *Virtue's Hero: Emerson, Antislavery, and Reform* (1990); *Emerson and the Conduct of Life* with Wes Mott; *Emerson & Eros* (2007). Definitive treatment of Emerson's political evolution.
- **Phyllis Cole**, *Mary Moody Emerson and the Origins of Transcendentalism* (1998). Recovered Emerson's formative aunt.
- **David M. Robinson**, *Emerson and the Conduct of Life* (1993); *Apostle of Culture* (1982). The religious-preacherly Emerson.
- **Wesley T. Mott**, *The Strains of Eloquence: Emerson and His Sermons* (1989).
- **Sarah Ann Wider**, *The Critical Reception of Emerson* (2000).
- **Albert von Frank**, *An Emerson Chronology* (2 vols., 1994).
- **Saundra Morris** and **Joel Porte** (eds.), *The Cambridge Companion to Ralph Waldo Emerson* (1999).

## 2. Key Findings (labeled)

1. **Emerson is a significant philosopher, not just a belletrist** [Cavell 1989, 2003; scholarly-consensus post-1990]. Cavell's argument — that Emerson's "self-reliance" names a distinctive moral-epistemic perfectionism continuous with Wittgenstein — broke the New Critical prejudice that Emerson was unrigorous.

2. **The turning point of the corpus is "Experience" (1844)** [Whicher 1953; Packer 1982; Cavell]. After his son Waldo's death in 1842, the oracular-optimistic voice of *Nature* and "Self-Reliance" could no longer be sustained without chastening. "Experience" enacts the chastening. Whether this is collapse (Whicher) or deepening (Packer, Richardson) is a live disagreement.

3. **Emerson evolved politically on race and slavery** [Gougeon 1990 — definitive]. Early journals contain racial theorizing modern readers find repugnant. His public turn against the Fugitive Slave Law (1850), his 1859 defense of John Brown, and his Emancipation advocacy mark a genuine moral evolution — not a late conversion of convenience, but years of moral work. The phases must both be acknowledged.

4. **Transcendentalism is not a unified doctrine** [Packer 2007; Buell 1973]. The term names a loose association (the "Hedge Club" / "Transcendental Club") of New England intellectuals in the 1830s–40s, drawing on German Idealism via Coleridge and Carlyle, Unitarian dissent, and Neoplatonism. Emerson is its best-known figure but not its theologian. Alcott, Fuller, Thoreau, Parker, Ripley, Peabody each carried the movement differently.

5. **Emerson's relationship to religion is specific, not generic "spirituality"** [Robinson 1982; Mott 1989; McAleer 1984]. He resigned the Unitarian pulpit (1832) because he could not in conscience administer communion as a commemorative rite. His later "natural supernaturalism" (M. H. Abrams's term) is a specific post-Unitarian religion — immanent, non-dogmatic, nature-as-sacrament, individual-soul-as-temple. Emerson continued to speak in religious registers his whole life.

6. **The journal is the laboratory** [Porte 1982; Richardson 1995]. Emerson's essays are assembled from journal entries, revised for lectures, polished for publication. This is not plagiarism of oneself; it is a compositional method. The journal-to-essay path is traceable and scholarly edition-maintained (JMN).

7. **Emerson's influence on American philosophy is underappreciated** [Cavell; Goodman 1990 *American Philosophy and the Romantic Tradition*]. William James, John Dewey, and in a different way C. S. Peirce all descend from Emerson. Pragmatism without Emerson is historically impossible. James's Emerson Centenary Address (1903) explicitly acknowledged the debt.

8. **The global reception is real and significant** [Stack 1992; Buell 2003]. Nietzsche was obsessively Emerson-influenced. Modern Indian thought (Tagore, Gandhi indirectly) encountered Emerson. Maeterlinck, Proust, and Woolf all read him.

9. **"Self-reliance" is not individualism in the libertarian sense** [Cavell; Kateb 1995]. Emerson's self-reliance is the command to trust the soul *because* the soul is not merely individual — it is the local expression of the Over-Soul. A pure individualism (the pop-culture Emerson) misses the metaphysical core. The self you are commanded to rely on is the self that is in contact with the universal.

10. **Emerson had structural doubts about the essay form itself** [Packer 1982; Cavell 2003]. He often despaired of "the want of a theory" in his own writing. The aphoristic-orbital structure is not merely stylistic choice; it is a philosophical commitment (no system for the unsettled soul) AND a limitation (he could not sustain argument in the way of Hegel or Mill).

11. **The Harvard Divinity School Address (1838) was a public scandal** [Richardson 1995]. Andrews Norton's response (*Discourse on the Latest Form of Infidelity*, 1839) effectively barred Emerson from Harvard for three decades. Emerson himself underestimated how inflammatory the Address would be. This episode shaped the rest of his career — he became a lyceum lecturer, not a Unitarian clergyman.

12. **Emerson's marriage was intellectually unequal but loving** [Richardson 1995; E. T. Emerson 1980]. Lidian Emerson was devout in a Calvinist-inflected way her husband had left behind. Their marriage was affectionate, practical, and tested by Waldo's death (which Lidian never fully recovered from). Emerson's writing life depended on her management of the household.

13. **His friendships were emotionally demanding and often disappointing** [Porte 1979; Fuller letters]. Margaret Fuller, Thoreau, Bronson Alcott — each felt at some point that Emerson withheld the intimacy they sought. The essays "Friendship" and "Love" address this — he theorized a distance-preserving friendship that some friends felt as coldness.

14. **The poetry is better than mid-20th-century criticism allowed** [Bloom 1976 *Figures of Capable Imagination*; Pack 1997 *Emerson's Poetry*]. "Each and All," "Days," "Brahma," "Uriel," "Hamatreya," and "The Sphinx" are major American lyrics. The journals contain dozens of unfinished lyrics of high quality.

15. **"Experience" is perhaps the greatest American essay** [Cavell; Bloom; Richardson]. A consensus view post-1980. The combination of grief, metaphysical range, stylistic audacity, and unresolved tension makes it the touchstone of his achievement.

16. **Nietzsche-Emerson is an intellectual filiation, not a surface similarity** [Stack 1992 *Nietzsche and Emerson: An Elective Affinity*; Cavell]. Nietzsche obtained a German Emerson edition at 17; annotated it throughout his life; quoted Emerson openly and silently; the *Übermensch*, the amor fati, the critique of the herd, the genealogical method — each has Emersonian roots. Kaufmann's early Nietzsche scholarship understated this; corrected since 1990.

## 3. Active Scholarly Controversies

**A. Continuity vs rupture in Emerson's career**
- **Rupture (Whicher 1953)**: Early optimism → late chastening. Two Emersons, separated by Waldo's death.
- **Continuity (Packer 1982; Richardson 1995; Cavell)**: One Emerson, with progressive depth. The chastening was always available in the early work (read "Compensation").

**B. Political Emerson: conservative or radical?**
- **Conservative self-reliance (Kateb 1995)**: Individualism as bulwark against collectivism.
- **Radical abolitionist (Gougeon 1990)**: Evolved into a public champion of abolition. Signed petitions, hosted fugitives.
- **Both (Buell 2003)**: Private radicalism, public restraint, with a breaking point in 1850–1854.

**C. The race question**
- **Sanitized reading (older biographies)**: Emerson-as-abolitionist only.
- **Hard reading (Nicoloff 1961; Newfield 1996)**: Early racialism cannot be excised; it shaped his category of "Anglo-Saxon" genius in *English Traits*.
- **Evolutionary reading (Gougeon 1990; scholarly-consensus)**: Both are true. The early views are documented; the evolution is documented.

**D. Cavell's perfectionism reading**
- **Affirmed (Conant, Hammer, Laugier)**: Emerson as serious ethical philosopher in an Austin-Wittgenstein tradition.
- **Contested (Eldridge, Goodman)**: Cavell reads too much of himself into Emerson; Emerson was not a proto-ordinary-language philosopher.

**E. Emerson and democracy**
- **Democratic (Kateb 1995; Lysaker 2008)**: Self-reliance is the democratic virtue par excellence — everyone has a soul.
- **Anti-democratic (Arvin 1959; partly Bloom)**: He was elitist; "great men" framework; impatience with mass politics.

**F. The "Emerson was unoriginal" charge (Santayana 1911)**
- Santayana's "The Genteel Tradition in American Philosophy" dismissed Emerson as a sage whose gospel was a "philosophy of moods."
- Rebuttal from Cavell, Bloom, Richardson: Santayana's dismissal reflects his own Platonic-Catholic aesthetic, not a fair reading. The aphoristic-orbital method is not a failure of rigor but a distinct philosophical commitment.

## 4. Myth-Debunking (popular Emerson vs. documented Emerson)

1. **Myth: Emerson preached rugged individualism.** — Self-reliance rests on the Over-Soul: trust the soul *because* it is in contact with the universal. Pure individualism is a misreading.

2. **Myth: Emerson was always against slavery.** — Early journals contain racial hierarchies. His public abolitionism begins c. 1844 (West Indies Emancipation address) and deepens decisively after 1850. [Gougeon 1990]

3. **Myth: Emerson was a cheerful optimist.** — His son Waldo's death (1842) produced "Experience" (1844), one of the darkest essays in American literature. He lived with tuberculosis in the family (brothers, first wife).

4. **Myth: "Self-Reliance" tells you to ignore criticism.** — The essay is more subtle: trust what is constant in you against what is fashionable in the culture. It is compatible with deep self-suspicion (as the journals show).

5. **Myth: Emerson broke cleanly from religion.** — He remained deeply religious in a post-Christian sense. Preached into the 1840s occasionally; referenced Scripture constantly; was a friend and correspondent of clergy.

6. **Myth: The Transcendentalists were a tight-knit movement.** — Loose association of individualists who disagreed on most specifics. Brook Farm (Ripley) and Fruitlands (Alcott) failed; Emerson and Thoreau quarreled; Fuller went to Italy.

7. **Myth: Emerson invented the American lyceum.** — He was its most famous lecturer but did not invent it (Josiah Holbrook, 1826).

8. **Myth: "To be great is to be misunderstood" excuses any contrarian pose.** — In context, he lists six figures (Socrates, Jesus, Luther, Copernicus, Galileo, Newton) to argue that *specific kinds* of great originality were misunderstood. The aphorism does not license every crank.

9. **Myth: Emerson was cold and withdrawn.** — Thoreau described silences; Louisa May Alcott described warmth; letters to children show real tenderness. The public lecturer's reserve was different from the domestic man.

10. **Myth: He died still himself.** — The last decade (1872 house fire onward) was marked by aphasia, memory loss, and diminished capacity. Cabot and Ellen assembled his late books. This is not shameful; it is documented and affects how to read *Letters and Social Aims* (1876).
