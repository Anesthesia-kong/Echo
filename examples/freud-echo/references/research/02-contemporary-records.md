# Freud — Contemporary Records (Research Agent 2 of 6)

**Dimension:** Same-generation accounts of Freud during his own lifetime (1856–1939), plus near-contemporary memoirs written by people who actually met him.

**Label key:** [documented] = direct primary text or contemporaneous record; [attested] = witness account written later by a participant; [attributed] = claim transmitted by a contemporary but not independently corroborated; [legendary] = circulated story without clear primary anchor; [modern-scholarly-inference] = historians' reconstruction beyond the sources.

---

## 1. How Freud appeared in clinical and intellectual settings

### 1.1 The Wednesday Psychological Society / Vienna Psychoanalytic Society (1902–1938)

- **[documented]** From autumn 1902, Freud held weekly Wednesday-evening meetings at Berggasse 19, initially with four founders (Wilhelm Stekel, Alfred Adler, Max Kahane, Rudolf Reitler). By 1906 it had grown to ~17 members; that year Otto Rank, aged 22, was appointed paid secretary and began writing the minutes in hand from 16 October 1906 through 27 April 1910. [web: https://www.encyclopedia.com/psychology/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/minutes-vienna-psychoanalytic-society]
- **[documented]** The minutes were bequeathed in Rank's will (d. 1939, will executed 1950) to Hermann Nunberg and Ernst Federn, who edited four volumes published by International Universities Press: Vol. I 1906–1908 (1962, tr. Margarete Nunberg), Vol. II 1908–1910 (1967), Vol. III 1910–1911 (1974), Vol. IV 1912–1918 (1975). [web: https://www.amazon.com/Minutes-Vienna-Psychoanalytic-Society-Vol/dp/0823633802]
- **[documented]** Structure of meetings: paper presented; coffee, cakes, cigars; discussion; **Freud always had the last word**. The waiting-room table sat outside Freud's consulting room, door open, couch visible, antiquities on walls. [web: https://www.freud.org.uk/2020/05/14/freud-at-home-the-wednesday-psychological-society/]
- **[attested]** The atmosphere was reported as quasi-religious. Fritz Wittels (member, later biographer) described it in terms of "the foundation of a religion in that room. Freud himself was its new prophet who made the heretofore prevailing methods of psychological investigation appear superficial." (Wittels, *Sigmund Freud: His Personality, His Teaching and His School*, 1924). [web: https://www.freud.org.uk/2020/05/14/freud-at-home-the-wednesday-psychological-society/]
- **Bias flag:** Wittels broke with Freud around 1910, reconciled later; his book is both admiring and score-settling.

### 1.2 Clark University, Worcester MA, September 1909

- **[documented]** Freud arrived New York Harbor 29 August 1909 aboard the *George Washington*, accompanied by Jung and Ferenczi, at invitation of G. Stanley Hall. The Clark conference ran 6–19 September 1909; Freud delivered **Five Lectures on Psycho-Analysis** in German across five consecutive days (7–11 Sept), with only preparation being walks with Jung. He received an honorary LL.D. — the only honorary degree he ever received. [web: https://commons.clarku.edu/freudhall/ ; https://www.chronicle.com/article/when-freud-came-to-america/]
- **[attested]** Freud later (in *An Autobiographical Study*, 1925) wrote of Clark: "As I stepped on to the platform at Worcester to deliver my Five Lectures upon Psycho-Analysis it seemed like the realization of some incredible day-dream: psycho-analysis was no longer a product of delusion, it had become a valuable part of reality." Koelsch's Clark history essay ("Incredible day-dream: Freud and Jung at Clark, 1909") takes its title from this line. [web: https://commons.clarku.edu/clarkuhistory/2/]

### 1.3 Walking tours with Ferenczi

- **[documented]** Freud took summer holidays with Ferenczi repeatedly — Sicily 1910 (where the Schreber case was discussed); later walks in Dolomites during the 1910s. Correspondence in *Freud–Ferenczi Correspondence* (3 vols, ed. Brabant/Falzeder/Giampieri-Deutsch, Harvard/Belknap 1993–2000) records the daily texture. **Under-sourced** in this agent's web-only pass — the Brabant volumes are the primary source and not available via open web.

## 2. Specific quotes about Freud from contemporaries

### 2.1 From patients

- **[attested; retrospective distance ~10–20 years]** **H.D. (Hilda Doolittle)**, *Tribute to Freud* (New York: Pantheon, 1956; 2nd ed. New Directions with foreword by Kenneth Fields, 1974; 3rd ed. 2012 foreword by Adam Phillips). Analysis: 1 March 1933 for ~3 months, resumed autumn 1934. H.D. was 46; Freud 77. "Writing on the Wall" composed 1944, a decade after; "Advent" is the contemporaneous journal.
  - On meeting Freud's chow: "A little lion-like creature came padding towards me — a lioness, as it happened. The Professor said, 'Do not touch her — she snaps — she is very difficult with strangers.'" (ND 1974 ed., p. 98 range; check specific printing). [web: https://psycheandmuse.wordpress.com/h-d-freud/]
  - Famous line of authorial self-quotation: "My bat-like thought-wings would beat painfully in that sudden searchlight." [web: https://www.ndbooks.com/book/tribute-to-freud/]
  - H.D. calls him throughout "the Professor," not by name — a deliberate distancing/elevating device.
  - **Dissent flag:** she records privately resisting the penis-envy doctrine, substituting "woman is perfect." [web: https://www.freud.org.uk/2024/04/10/hilda-and-freud/]
  - **Bias flag:** written during Freud's late life/posthumously; tone is elegiac and reverential, filtered through H.D.'s poetic sensibility and her grief for her dead brother/father that the analysis reopened.

- **[attested; short retrospective distance]** **Joseph Wortis**, *Fragments of an Analysis with Freud* (New York: Simon & Schuster, 1954). Analysis: winter 1934–35 (4 months, didactic). Wortis kept a diary of every session — one of the tightest contemporaneous records of Freud's technique.
  - Wortis's preface: "My main purpose is to give a picture of Freud as I saw him, and an account of some of his methods and views." [web: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/341833837]
  - Wortis judged the analysis itself clinically unimpressive, remarking he was "as healthy as when I took it up." This is a rare contemporary dissenting data point about outcome.
  - **Bias flag:** Wortis later became a critic of psychoanalysis (Pavlovian/biological psychiatrist); his framing of the diary in the 1954 publication is shaped by that later turn.

- **[attested; long retrospective distance]** **Smiley Blanton**, *Diary of My Analysis with Sigmund Freud* (New York: Hawthorn, 1971). Analysis 1929–30 plus summers 1935–37. Published posthumously by his wife Margaret Blanton.
  - Blanton records Freud joking: "For an analyst not to relate his dreams, now that's a sign of serious resistance!" (exchange when Freud asked him not to record dreams). [web: https://www.acsu.buffalo.edu/~duchan/history_subpages/smileyblanton.html]
  - Freud wrote a letter of reference to Ernest Jones on 30 May 1930 describing Blanton as "a pleasant man, especially interested in the orientation of children (Vassar College)."
  - **Bias flag:** Blanton went on to co-found the Peale Institute with Norman Vincent Peale; the diary as edited by his wife may smooth the rougher edges.

- **[attested; long retrospective distance]** **Abram Kardiner**, *My Analysis with Freud: Reminiscences* (New York: Norton, 1977). Analysis: 1921–22, six months. Written 55+ years later.
  - Kardiner's book documents Freud admitting limitations of his technique and reporting Freud saying (reconstructed): "I am no great analyst. I was too much occupied with theoretical problems" — a line widely cited from Kardiner but filtered through half-a-century recall. Treat as [attributed], not direct transcript.
  - **Bias flag:** Kardiner became a major figure in American culture-and-personality school; his rereading of Freud's technique is partly ex post polemic.

- **[attested; very long retrospective distance]** **Sergei Pankejeff ("Wolf Man", 1886–1979)**: Gardiner ed., *The Wolf-Man and Sigmund Freud* (1971) — Pankejeff's own 1970 memoir; Muriel Gardiner's framing essays; plus Karin Obholzer, *The Wolf-Man: Conversations with Freud's Patient — Sixty Years Later* (interviews 1974–76, German 1980, English 1982, Pankejeff died 1979). [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Pankejeff]
  - Pankejeff to Obholzer on the primal-scene interpretation: "terribly far-fetched"; "the whole thing is improbable, because in Russia, children sleep in their nanny's bedroom, not their parents'." [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Pankejeff]
  - Pankejeff remained in quasi-analytic contact with Freudians until death — was never really "cured," contra Freud's 1918 case history.
  - **Bias flag (severe):** Pankejeff spoke across 55+ years, under financial dependence on the analytic community (Gardiner's stipend). Obholzer was not an analyst; Freudian loyalists have contested her edits.

- **Freud's own reconstructions — flag as such, not contemporary testimony:** Dora (*Bruchstück einer Hysterie-Analyse*, 1905, treatment 1900), Little Hans (1909), Rat Man (1909), Schreber (1911, based on Schreber's own memoir — Freud never met him), Wolf Man (1918). These are **Freud-authored** case histories, not patient testimony.

### 2.2 From colleagues

- **[attested]** **C. G. Jung**, *Memories, Dreams, Reflections* (rec. Aniela Jaffé, ed. 1962, Eng. 1963) — chapter "Sigmund Freud." Jung recorded two Freud fainting spells: **Bremen 1909** (pre-voyage dinner, triggered by Jung's talk of Northern European peat-bog corpses; Freud afterwards said the topic implied Jung's "death-wishes" toward him); and **Munich 1912** (Park Hotel lunch, IV Psychoanalytic Congress context, during discussion of Karl Abraham's Akhenaten paper — Jung defended Akhenaten as creative, not father-hostile; Freud slid from his chair). Jung: "I was alarmed by the intensity of his fantasies — so strong that, obviously, they could cause him to faint." [web: https://www.theparisreview.org/blog/2017/07/10/freud-fainting/ ; https://mossdreams.blogspot.com/2014/02/when-jung-made-freud-swoon.html]
  - **Bias flag (severe):** MDR compiled 1957–1961 during Jung's last years; post-break, post-mythologization. Chapter edited by Jaffé; Jung family later restricted/revised material. The "death-wish" exchange is a Jung reconstruction.

- **[documented]** **Sándor Ferenczi**, *Clinical Diary* (1932; first published German 1985, Eng. Harvard/Dupont ed. 1988). Written in final year of life; crucial **inside dissenting view**.
  - Records Ferenczi's estrangement from Freud over "Confusion of Tongues" paper (Wiesbaden Congress, Sept 1932). Ferenczi stopped in Vienna en route to read the paper aloud; the encounter was painful. Freud denied having injured him and "viewing Ferenczi's sense of injury as further evidence that Ferenczi was in the wrong." [web: https://www.freud.org.uk/collections/archives/ferenczi-hub/]
  - Diary contains sharp critiques of classical technique as re-traumatizing; develops "mutual analysis" experiments.
  - **Bias flag:** Ferenczi was terminally ill (pernicious anaemia). Jones in his 1957 Vol. III implied Ferenczi was psychotic at the end — a claim now discredited. Treat Jones's framing with severe skepticism. [web: https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1989/08/17/freud-ferenczi-2/]

- **[documented]** **Otto Rank**, post-break writings: *Das Trauma der Geburt* (1924), *Technik der Psychoanalyse* (1926, 1929, 1931). Rank's letters to Ferenczi (1924–25) show him initially seeking rapprochement, then rejecting Freud's paternal authority. Later Rank (e.g., *Truth and Reality*, 1929) moves toward existential/will psychology. [web: https://thephilosophicalsalon.com/no-more-insight-than-a-small-boy-rank-on-freud/]

- **[attested]** **Wilhelm Stekel**, *Autobiography of Wilhelm Stekel: The Life Story of a Pioneer Psychoanalyst* (ed. Emil Gutheil, pub. Liveright NY 1950, posth.; Stekel suicide 1940 London). Records the Nov 1912 *Zentralblatt für Psychoanalyse* rupture — Stekel refused Freud's choice of Viktor Tausk as book-review editor; Freud withdrew, Stekel kept the journal. Freud's verdict (quoted by contemporaries): "Stekel is going his own way." Freud then founded the *Internationale Zeitschrift für Psychoanalyse* (1913). [web: https://www.encyclopedia.com/psychology/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/zentralblatt-fur-psychoanalyse]
  - **Bias flag:** Stekel is heavily self-justifying; edited posthumously.

### 2.3 From witnesses / public intellectuals

- **[documented]** **Thomas Mann**, "Freud und die Zukunft," address at Vienna Akademischer Verein für medizinische Psychologie on Freud's 80th birthday, **delivered 8 May 1936** at the Wiener Konzerthaus (published Bermann-Fischer 1936; *Imago* 22, 1936). Mann visited Berggasse 19 that morning to present the printed text to Freud. The speech places Freud with Schopenhauer and Nietzsche as a figure of "the future." [web: https://salmagundi.skidmore.edu/articles/247-thomas-mann-and-sigmund-freud]
  - Repeated in abridged form at the New School for Social Research, NYC, 19 April 1937.

- **[documented]** **Stefan Zweig**, correspondence with Freud 1908–1939 (published ed. Fischer 1987, Eng. selections). Zweig delivered eulogy at Freud's cremation at Golders Green Crematorium, **26 September 1939**, alongside Ernest Jones's address. Zweig had been classified "enemy alien" in Britain; required documents just to travel to the funeral. [web: https://jewishcurrents.org/the-friendship-between-sigmund-freud-and-stefan-zweig ; https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-030-51787-8_4]
  - Zweig's last letter to Freud: 14 Sept 1939 (nine days before Freud's death 23 Sept).
  - Zweig's *Die Welt von Gestern* (*The World of Yesterday*, completed Feb 1942 the day before Zweig's suicide in Petrópolis; pub. 1942 Stockholm / 1943 English) contains extended portraits of Freud as an embodiment of old Viennese humanistic Europe.

- **[documented]** **Albert Einstein – Freud correspondence**, *Warum Krieg? / Why War?* Commissioned 1931 by the International Institute of Intellectual Cooperation (League of Nations); Einstein's letter dated 30 July 1932; Freud's reply September 1932; published by League of Nations March 1933 in trilingual (En/Fr/De) edition. Suppressed circulation after Hitler came to power January 1933. Einstein's choice of Freud as interlocutor was itself a contemporary judgment on Freud's status as "representative intellectual." [web: https://www.themarginalian.org/2013/05/06/why-war-einstein-freud/]

## 3. Public reputation trajectory

- **[documented]** **Viennese medical rejection**, 1895–c.1906. *Studien über Hysterie* (with Breuer 1895) and *Die Traumdeutung* (1899, dated 1900) drew small, mostly hostile medical reception. Freud himself dated his period of "splendid isolation" from 1895.
- **[documented]** **Amsterdam 1907 — First International Congress of Psychiatry, Neurology, Psychology, and Assistance of the Insane** (2–7 Sept 1907). Freud did not attend in person; Jung spoke defending Freud. Aschaffenburg and others attacked psychoanalysis. Jung to Freud (letter, 11 Sept 1907): described speakers at Amsterdam as "pouring unadulterated sewage" on Freud's theories, expressing "contempt bordering on nausea for the genus Homo sapiens." [web: https://www.asa3.org/ASA/PSCF/2007/PSCF12-07Jones.pdf]
  - This letter (from *The Freud/Jung Letters*, ed. McGuire, Princeton/Hogarth 1974) is the best contemporary snapshot of where academic-medical opinion stood in 1907.
- **[documented]** **Salzburg 1908** — First International Psychoanalytic Congress, 27 April 1908, Hotel Bristol. Freud read the Rat Man case for ~4 hours. 42 attendees.
- **[documented]** **Nuremberg 1910** — Second Congress, 30–31 March 1910. International Psychoanalytical Association (IPA) **formally founded**, at Ferenczi's proposal with Freud's backing. Jung elected first president; Zurich chosen as HQ (Viennese members protested — this was one seed of the Adler break). [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Psychoanalytical_Association]
- **[documented]** **Clark 1909** — first breakthrough into American fame (see 1.2 above).
- **[documented]** Interwar period — Freud becomes an international intellectual celebrity: *Introductory Lectures* (1916–17) widely translated; Goethe Prize, Frankfurt am Main, **28 August 1930** (highest German cultural honor; Anna Freud read his acceptance because of his jaw cancer).
- **[documented]** **1938 exile** — see §6.

## 4. The great breaks

- **[documented]** **Fliess (c.1900–1906, final rupture 1906).** Correspondence ran 1887–1904 (peak 1899). Cooling began c.1900. Final rupture triggered by Weininger/Swoboda plagiarism-of-bisexuality accusation (*Geschlecht und Charakter* 1903; Fliess's pamphlet *In eigener Sache* 1906). Freud destroyed his side of the correspondence post-1904; Fliess's side survived because Marie Bonaparte bought the letters in 1936 from a bookseller and refused Freud's request to destroy them. First pub. Anfänge der Psychoanalyse (1950); full ed. Masson 1985. [web: https://www.encyclopedia.com/psychology/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/fliess-wilhelm-1858-1928]
- **[documented]** **Adler (1911).** Adler served as first president of the Vienna Psychoanalytic Society 1910 after Freud handed over. Conflict crystallized in three fraught Wednesday meetings Jan–Feb 1911 (Minutes Vol. III records these in near-verbatim detail — a key primary source). Adler resigned presidency and left **22–23 February 1911**; nine members left with him; he founded the Society for Free Psychoanalytic Research (later Society for Individual Psychology). Freud issued ultimatum forcing members to choose. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Adler]
- **[documented]** **Jung (1912–1914).** Tensions 1911–12 over libido (*Wandlungen und Symbole der Libido*, 1912). The **"Kreuzlingen gesture"** (May 1912): Freud visited Binswanger in Kreuzlingen without detouring to Jung in Zurich. Two fainting spells (1909, 1912) bracket the decay. Final exchange: Freud's letter 3 January 1913 proposing "that we abandon our personal relations entirely" (full text reproduced in Open Culture repro from McGuire ed., Princeton 1974). Last personal meeting: IV Congress Munich, September 1913. Jung resigned IPA presidency April 1914; resigned from IPA altogether July 1914. [web: https://www.openculture.com/2014/06/the-famous-letter-where-freud-breaks-his-relationship-with-jung-1913.html]
- **[documented]** **Rank (1924–26).** Trigger: *Das Trauma der Geburt* (Vienna: Internationaler Psychoanalytischer Verlag, December 1923, dated 1924), dedicated to Freud on his 68th birthday. Initially Freud tolerated; Abraham and Jones pushed back hard within the Committee. Rank and Freud exchanged many conferences 1924–25; reconciliation failed; Rank to the US late 1924; formal break 1926. Freud's *Hemmung, Symptom und Angst* (1926) is partly a rebuttal. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Trauma_of_Birth]
- **[documented]** **Ferenczi (late 1920s–1933).** Slow estrangement over "active technique" and counter-transference experiments. Flashpoint: "Confusion of Tongues" paper read to Freud privately in Vienna en route to Wiesbaden Congress, September 1932. Ferenczi died 22 May 1933. Jones's Vol. III (1957) propagated a claim that Ferenczi died psychotic — rejected by subsequent scholarship (Grosskurth, Dupont). [web: https://www.nybooks.com/articles/1989/08/17/freud-ferenczi-2/]

### 4.1 The "Secret Committee" (1912–mid-1920s)

- **[documented]** Proposed by **Ernest Jones** to Freud in a letter 30 July 1912, after the Jung troubles surfaced. Formally constituted autumn 1912 with Jones, Ferenczi, Rank, Hanns Sachs, Karl Abraham; Max Eitingon added May 1919. Freud distributed **six Greek-intaglio rings** set in gold as tokens of membership (a seventh for himself). Committee dissolved 1924 during Rank crisis; reconstituted 1925 with Anna Freud in Rank's place; ceased functioning effectively after Abraham's death Dec 1925 and Ferenczi's 1933. Primary source: Phyllis Grosskurth, *The Secret Ring* (1991) based on committee circular letters. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secret_committee_(psychoanalysis)]
- **Bias flag:** Committee had an agenda — to defend Freud's priority and expel heretics. Its internal circular letters (Abraham, Jones, Ferenczi et al. to each other) are the most revealing contemporary document of how Freud's movement managed itself, but the writers were all loyalists.

## 5. Documented incidents

- **[documented]** **Bremen, 20 August 1909:** Freud's first fainting spell in Jung's presence, pre-voyage to America. Witnesses: Jung, Ferenczi. Recorded in Jung MDR and in Freud's letters. [web: https://www.theparisreview.org/blog/2017/07/10/freud-fainting/]
- **[documented]** **Munich, 24 November 1912:** Second fainting spell, Park Hotel lunch after editorial meeting. Witnesses: Jung, Ferenczi, Jones, others. Jung carried Freud to a couch. Freud reportedly said upon coming to: "How sweet it must be to die." Treat the dying-line as [attributed] via Jung's later narration.
- **[documented]** **22 March 1938 — Gestapo raid on Berggasse 19.** 6,000 schillings seized; Anna Freud taken to former Hotel Metropol (Morzinplatz) for ~12 hours' interrogation. Max Schur had given Anna a lethal dose of Veronal as a precaution against torture. This incident is what finally decided Freud to emigrate. [web: https://www.freud.org.uk/exhibitions/leaving-today-the-freuds-in-exile-1938/ ; https://www.amny.com/news/twelve-hours-of-anna-freud-under-a-nazi-interrogation-lamp/]
- **[documented]** **4 June 1938:** Freuds board the Orient Express, Vienna → Paris (one day with Marie Bonaparte) → London (4 June arr. Victoria Station). Rescue network: Marie Bonaparte (funds), William C. Bullitt (US ambassador to France, diplomatic pressure), Ernest Jones (UK entry permits), Anton Sauerwald (Nazi commissar, privately sympathetic). Freud was made to sign a statement that he had been well-treated; his (probably apocryphal) sarcastic addendum "I can heartily recommend the Gestapo to anyone" is **[attributed/legendary]** — it appears first in Jones Vol. III (1957) and should be flagged as possibly embellished. [web: https://blogs.loc.gov/loc/2019/09/freuds-last-days-in-vienna-as-nazis-approached/]
- **[documented]** **23 September 1939:** Freud dies at 20 Maresfield Gardens, London, from morphine overdose administered by Max Schur at Freud's longstanding request, to end suffering from jaw-cancer metastases. Schur's account: *Freud: Living and Dying* (1972).

## 6. Bias flags — synthesis

1. **Ernest Jones** 3-vol biography (Hogarth 1953, 1955, 1957) — **hagiographic, inside-circle**. Characterized Adler/Jung/Rank as "heretics" driven by psychopathology; portrayed loyalists (especially himself) as vanguard. Lisa Appignanesi, Hans Eysenck, Roy Porter all flag it as mythology. Paskauskas showed Jones silently altered Freud's English in quotations. Still a trove of primary anecdote because Jones was there, but requires cross-check. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Life_and_Work_of_Sigmund_Freud]
2. **The Secret Committee** had agendas — its members' memoirs (Jones, Sachs *Freud: Master and Friend* 1944, Eitingon) tilt loyalist.
3. **Patient memoirs written decades later** (Kardiner 55 yrs, Blanton 40+ yrs posthumous, Pankejeff/Obholzer 55+ yrs): subject to reconstructive memory, later theoretical commitments, and (in Pankejeff's case) financial dependence on the movement.
4. **Dissenter memoirs** (Stekel 1940/50, Jung MDR 1962) tilt the other way — settling scores.
5. **Jung MDR** was compiled by Jaffé during Jung's last years and edited by the Jung family; sections were removed or altered.
6. **Freud's own case histories** (Dora, Hans, Rat Man, Schreber, Wolf Man) are **reconstructions by Freud**, not contemporary testimony *about* Freud. Treat as evidence of Freud's self-presentation, not as neutral record.

## 7. Contradictions between contemporaries (preserved)

- **On Freud's clinical manner:** Blanton warm and engaged; Wortis found him doctrinaire and technically loose; H.D. reverential; Kardiner (retrospectively) judged him distracted, saying Freud told him he was "not a great analyst." These are irreconcilable on the face, though partially explained by period (Freud in his 70s was different from Freud at 66) and by patient transference.
- **On Ferenczi's end:** Jones in Vol. III (1957) said Ferenczi died "mentally deranged"; Balint, Dupont, Grosskurth, and the Clinical Diary itself show a lucid dissenter with a terminal somatic illness.
- **On the Kreuzlingen gesture:** Freud in letters said he had little time; Jung felt it was a calculated snub. Both readings are in the extant correspondence (McGuire 1974 ed.).
- **On the Gestapo-recommendation quip:** multiple versions — no source earlier than Jones.

## 8. Under-sourced in this pass

- **Freud–Ferenczi Correspondence** (Brabant/Falzeder/Giampieri-Deutsch, 3 vols Harvard 1993–2000) — closed-stack primary source, critical for the 1916 walking tours and the slow Ferenczi decay. Not captured by this web-pass.
- **Minutes Vol. III (1910–11)** — the verbatim record of the Adler break meetings (Jan–Feb 1911) is the single best primary source for Freud managing a schism; only encyclopedia-level summaries reached this pass.
- **Lou Andreas-Salomé**, *Freud Journal* (1912–13, pub. 1958) — major participant-observer not fully covered here.
- **Hanns Sachs**, *Freud: Master and Friend* (Harvard 1944) — inside-loyalist but closer contemporary than Jones.
- **Fritz Wittels**, *Sigmund Freud: Der Mann, die Lehre, die Schule* (1924) — first biography, published in Freud's lifetime with Freud's (partially annoyed) knowledge.
- **Peter Gay**, *Freud: A Life for Our Time* (Norton 1988) — the consensus modern-scholarly synthesis; not directly quoted in this document but should be the anchor for any finished echo.
- **Contemporary Viennese press coverage** (*Neue Freie Presse*, *Die Fackel* Karl Kraus's hostile pieces) — an important corrective to inside-circle memoirs; not captured here.
