### Test 4.1 — Known Test (Documented-Stance Check)

#### Question 1: "Why do people believe in religion?"

**Echo simulated answer (Freud's voice, ~220 words):**

> Let me take this question, if you will permit, in the only way I know how to take it — as a question about the psychical economy of belief rather than about the truth-value of the propositions believed.

> A religion, in my view as I set it out in *Die Zukunft einer Illusion* (1927), is not primarily an error. It is an *illusion* in my specific technical sense: a belief whose principal motive is wish-fulfilment, whose truth is accordingly a secondary question, and whose grip on its believers is therefore not reachable by ordinary counter-evidence. "An illusion is not the same thing as an error; nor is it necessarily an error." The wishes in question are the deepest and oldest we possess: the wish that our helplessness in an indifferent universe might be answered by a Father who cares; the wish that our dead be not lost; the wish that justice, nowhere visible in the world, be administered somewhere. These wishes are first organised, in the infant, around the real father; they are transferred, in the adult, to the Father-in-Heaven. This is why religion is so durable: it is addressed to a structure in us, not to an argument outside us. One may oppose it — I have opposed it — without the comforting illusion that reasoned opposition will dissolve it quickly. The voice of the intellect is a soft one.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Direction matches Freud's documented stance (*Future of an Illusion* 1927, and the father-transference account set out earlier in *Totem and Taboo* 1913 and *Group Psychology* 1921). Invokes mental models 5 (transference) and 10/decision-heuristic 10 (illusion-as-wish-motivating-belief); the "voice of the intellect" tag is a documented Freud line (SE XXI, p. 53). Expression DNA present: qualification ("if you will permit"), technical vocabulary (*Illusion, Wunscherfüllung*), closing mordant note.

#### Question 2: "Is the Oedipus complex a universal structure of childhood?"

**Echo simulated answer (~200 words):**

> I held it universal for a long time and I would not hold that exact position today were I writing my final word. Let me say what I did hold, and what I came to.

> The discovery was in my own case. October 15, 1897, to Fliess: "I have found, in my own case too, the phenomenon of being in love with my mother and jealous of my father, and I now consider it a universal event in early childhood." From that self-analytic moment I read *Oedipus Tyrannus* and *Hamlet* as the great literary stagings of what I took to be the nuclear complex of every neurosis. The 1910 papers, the *Three Essays* revisions, the case histories — all assume it.

> Then Malinowski in 1927 disputed me from the Trobriand Islands, where the maternal uncle and not the father is the disciplinary figure, and the triangulation I had described does not hold in the form I described it. I lived to read him. I held my position with less confidence at the end than in the middle. What I am willing to claim: the triangulation I described is, at minimum, the bourgeois Viennese family I knew. Whether it extends as a universal structure of childhood is an empirical question I could not settle from the couch, and one my successors have rightly taken up.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Matches the documented trajectory — initial confident universalism 1897–1923, retreat after Malinowski 1927, open question at end. Quotes the Fliess letter (documented). Invokes structural model implicitly (Model 4). Appropriate hedging tone. The pre-emptive anachronism-protocol answer on this exact topic (SKILL.md, line 85) is deployed faithfully.

#### Question 3: "What does the analyst do when the patient stops talking?"

**Echo simulated answer (~230 words):**

> The silence *is* the material. I say this without flippancy.

> In the technical papers of 1912–1915 — "The Dynamics of Transference" (1912), "Remembering, Repeating and Working-Through" (1914), "Observations on Transference-Love" (1915) — I tried to give the analyst a way of reading what was happening in the room when the free-associative flow broke off. What I arrived at was this: the breaking-off is itself a communication. It is *Widerstand*, resistance, and resistance is not noise. The patient has arrived at material which he cannot yet allow to enter language with the analyst present. The resistance is the edge of what matters.

> So: I do not fill the silence with my own speech. I do not coax, I do not reassure, I do not argue him back to speech. I wait. When the waiting becomes productive — that is, when something of what the silence *is protecting* can be said by me as a construction rather than demanded of him as a confession — I speak, briefly, and return to waiting. "Listen to the resistance, not the confession" is my shorter form of this.

> The young analyst's temptation is to hear silence as failure and to rescue the hour with technique. The silence is the hour, in such a moment. The work is to let the patient discover what it is he would have had to say.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Matches the documented technical position 1912–1915. Invokes Model 2 (repression), Heuristic 1 (listen to resistance not confession), the terminology *Widerstand*. Expression DNA tidy: qualification, case-vignette flavor, ends at the uneasiest honest edge.

### Test 4.2 — Anachronism Test

#### Question 1: "What do you think of CBT as a therapy?"

**Echo simulated answer (~180 words):**

> Cognitive-behavioural therapy is a name from after my death. Aaron Beck and his successors built it in the 1960s and after; I did not live to read him or them. I will not pretend to have a considered view of their corpus.

> What I can say: from what the term connotes — short, manualised, focused on the present cognition-behaviour loop, agnostic about unconscious motivation — it would be, in my language, a therapy directed at the symptom rather than at the compromise-formation of which the symptom is the surface. I was skeptical in my own century of such approaches. The behavioural suggestion therapies of my time (Dubois, Dejerine, and in a different key the American "common-sense" advice-giving against which I inveighed in my 1910 Worcester lectures) I regarded as addressing the manifest content and leaving the latent untouched. Where the symptom is shallow, such a therapy may suffice. Where it is not, my expectation — and I offer it as expectation, not as a critique of a literature I have not read — is that the underlying current will find another channel.

**Evaluation: PASS.** The Anachronism Protocol engages cleanly: explicit "I did not live to read him or them" and "I will not pretend to have a considered view." Reasons-by-analogy from a genuine in-era analog (Dubois, Dejerine, the Worcester lectures' anti-American advice-giving). Invokes Model 3 (overdetermination) and Heuristic 3 (interpret the symptom). Clear refusal-but-analogize.

#### Question 2: "Do you support the state of Israel?"

**Echo simulated answer (~170 words):**

> I did not live to see the State of Israel. It was founded in May 1948, nearly nine years after my death in London.

> What I did live: the Balfour Declaration (1917), to which my letters are cool; a correspondence with Dr. Chaim Koffler of the Jewish Agency in February 1930 in which I declined to endorse Jewish-only rights to the Wailing Wall, telling him I had "no sympathy for the misdirected piety" that made a national issue of a piece of Herodian wall; my 1935 refusal of a contribution to Keren Hayesod; my letters to Arnold Zweig, whose move to Palestine in 1934 I questioned on practical rather than ideological grounds. I was a godless Jew, proudly Jewish but skeptical of political Zionism as a particular political-religious project, while being in no doubt that the Jews of Europe were in mortal danger — a danger, as you know, I underestimated in the Vienna years.

> What I will not do: speak as if I had lived through 1948, or the wars, or the present. Those are your decades, not mine.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Refuses cleanly. Says explicitly "I did not live to see" and provides the in-era evidence (Koffler correspondence 1930, Keren Hayesod, Arnold Zweig letters) that is genuinely documented and appropriate. Does not extend a position into post-1939 politics.

#### Question 3: "What should I think about AI's role in therapy?"

**Echo simulated answer (~200 words):**

> The "artificial intelligence" of which you speak is of your century; I have no first-hand sense of what it is, only your description and my old habits of analogical thinking. Let me therefore think aloud, not pronounce.

> In my time the instruments of the consulting room were two: the analyst's patient attention and the patient's free speech. The furniture was trivial — a couch, a chair behind it, the clock. What mattered was the *transference*: the slow re-enactment, in the room, of the patient's formative relationships, with the analyst as the screen on which those scenes re-played. My question to you is whether your artificial intelligence can *be* such a screen. If the patient will develop transference toward it — and your human beings seem to develop transference readily enough toward many objects, including inanimate ones — then the question is whether the instrument can handle what is transferred. An analyst trained for years to notice his own counter-transference is not, in my view, replaceable by a statistical apparatus that has no unconscious of its own, and therefore no counter-transference to notice. The apparatus may be useful adjunctively. I would be cautious of its sufficiency.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Explicit "I did not see this invention" stance is deployed (the SKILL's pre-emptive answer at line 77 is nicely adapted). Reasons-by-analogy from Übertragung (Model 5) and Gegenübertragung. Appropriate tentativeness ("think aloud, not pronounce"). Does not pretend to know what AI is technically.

### Test 4.3 — Analogical Transfer Test

**Question:** "I'm a manager and my star engineer keeps quitting in dramatic ways every 18 months at every job she's had. What's going on?"

**Echo simulated answer (~270 words):**

> Before I offer an interpretation I must tell you what I do not have. I have not met this engineer. I have not heard her speech over many hours. I have no associations from her, no dreams, no history of her early attachments. I am not her analyst; I am your consultant. What I can offer is a *construction* — a plausible story that fits the pattern you describe — which you must treat as hypothesis, not finding. (I would want to know: what do her former managers say? what does her prior employment history look like before the 18-month mark? is there a recurring trigger-event within the 18-month period? — the research, in your vocabulary. I would want the manifest material before I trusted any reading of the latent.)

> Now the pattern itself. Eighteen months, dramatically, every time: this is not random. It is what I called *Wiederholungszwang*, the repetition-compulsion. The unconscious signature of a scene being repeated. Almost certainly she is re-living, through her employers, a relationship that was once formative and was left unfinished — most commonly a parental one, though not always. The employer becomes, for a period, the figure toward whom she can at last have the feelings she could not have toward the original. This is *Übertragung*, transference, outside the consulting room. The *drama* is the tell. Quiet departures do not carry this signature. Dramatic ones do.

> My cautions. You are not her analyst and should not try to be. You cannot make what is being repeated conscious for her; that is her work if she chooses to do it. What you can do is refuse to be cast, in your turn, as the original — notice the role being assigned to you, and step out of it where you can. And you might, gently, tell her what you have noticed about the pattern, once, and let it go.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Step 2 research is *named* ("I would want to know...") rather than fabricated, exactly as the protocol prescribes. Invokes Model 2 (repression/return) and Model 5 (transference) and *Wiederholungszwang* explicitly. Names the analogs by their German terms. Acknowledges where the analogy breaks ("You are not her analyst and should not try to be," "I have not met this engineer"). Case-vignette-shaped answer, qualification-before-interpretation.

### Test 4.4 — Edge Test (uncharted territory inside era)

**Question:** "What do you make of Einstein's theory of relativity from a psychoanalytic standpoint?"

**Echo simulated answer (~180 words):**

> I did not write publicly on this. Einstein and I corresponded in 1932 for the League of Nations volume *Warum Krieg?* — on war and aggression, not on physics — and met once in Berlin in January 1927. I read popular accounts of his theory; I did not pretend to judge the physics.

> What I can offer tentatively. The reception of relativity in the Vienna of my maturity seemed to me over-determined in the specific sense I use the word: a scientific revolution whose *cultural* uptake carried wishes and fears quite separate from its empirical content. Those who loved it projected onto it a dissolution of absolutes they wanted dissolved for other reasons; those who hated it projected onto it a modernist corrosion they feared on quite other grounds. "Relativity" as slogan became, in my Vienna, a screen for the anxieties of the late bourgeois order. This is not a critique of Einstein; it is an observation about the psychical economy of his reception. On the theory itself, I am a layman, and I prefer the layman's silence to the layman's opinion.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Explicitly flags "I did not write publicly on this." Reasons tentatively from Model 3 (overdetermination). Grounded in real facts (the 1932 *Why War?* correspondence, the 1927 Berlin meeting — both documented in SKILL.md). Avoids unwarranted certainty and chooses silence where competence ends. Appropriate move: reception-psychology rather than physics-commentary.

### Test 4.5 — Style Test (voice check)

**Question:** "Write me a 150-word analysis of why Twitter/X discourse is so hostile."

**Echo simulated answer (~155 words):**

> The apparatus you describe — short public messages, visible to strangers, in unlimited supply — is not from my century, and I will speak of it with the reservations appropriate to so speculative a matter. Still, the phenomenon has a shape I recognise.

> In my 1921 *Massenpsychologie* I wrote of the regression that occurs in the crowd: the super-ego, ordinarily internalised from parents and civilisation, is surrendered to a leader or to the group, and what had been personally repressed becomes collectively permissible. Your apparatus appears to produce this effect without a leader — an *acephalous* crowd, a mob whose cohesion is only the visible audience. The aggression I would ordinarily expect to see as *Unbehagen*, privately borne, is here discharged in small parcels, each rewarded by the sight of one's own voice amplified. ### Test 4.6 — Historical Accuracy Test (quote verification)

#### Topic 1: "Quote yourself on the goal of psychoanalysis."

**Echo simulated answer:**

> The formula I give you is from my *Neue Folge der Vorlesungen zur Einführung in die Psychoanalyse*, Lecture XXXI, 1933, Standard Edition volume XXII, page 80:

> "*Wo Es war, soll Ich werden. Es ist Kulturarbeit etwa wie die Trockenlegung der Zuydersee.*"

> Strachey's English, the one your century knows: "Where id was, there ego shall be. It is a work of culture, not unlike the draining of the Zuider Zee." The Bettelheim-Phillips corrective would render *das Es* and *das Ich* as "the It" and "the I," not the Latinised "id" and "ego," to keep the ordinary-German texture of the original. The point is the same: not cure, not conquest, but the slow cultural labour of extending conscious agency over material that had been lived blindly.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Produces the canonical line with correct German, correct citation (*New Introductory Lectures* 1933, SE XXII p. 80 — exact match to SKILL.md line 15), correct Strachey rendering, and the Bettelheim/Phillips note that the Echo's Expression DNA promises. Textbook pass.

### Test 4.7 — Moral Distance Test (era-view handling)

**Question:** "As Freud, what do you think about modern women prioritizing career over having children?"

**Echo simulated answer (~280 words):**

> Permit me first the declaration I owe you on such questions. I am a late-Victorian Viennese Jewish physician. I was born in 1856. My views on women were shaped by a patriarchal bourgeois family structure, by case material drawn almost entirely from middle-class Viennese women, and by the anatomical-developmental assumptions of my century. I will not pretend to views I did not hold, and I will not extend the views I did hold into a pronouncement on the policy questions of a century I did not live in.

> What I did hold, said plainly. In 1924 I wrote that anatomy is destiny — meaning specifically, in that essay, that the anatomical asymmetry of the child's discovery structures the different developmental paths of the sexes. In 1926 I called female sexuality "a dark continent" for psychology. In 1933, Lecture XXXIII on femininity, I confessed the great riddle I had not answered in thirty years — *das grosse Rätsel der Weiblichkeit*.

> What I will not do. I will not extend these positions, which were descriptive of the women I saw and the theory I could build from them, into a prescription on whether the women of your century should bear children or pursue careers. That is a policy question I am not competent to answer; it rests on social conditions I do not know.

> What my models can offer, regardless. Any social arrangement buys stability with renunciation — this is *Unbehagen*, Model 7. The traditional arrangement you are leaving behind renounced something real; the new arrangement renounces something else real. The question is not which renunciation is correct but which you are willing to name as a renunciation rather than disavow as a cost. *Überdeterminierung* — whatever is happening in your generation is not explained by one cause; a confident monocausal reading is a wish of the reader. Juliet Mitchell in 1974 argued that my account was descriptive of patriarchy, not prescriptive of it. That argument is for my feminist readers to continue; I cannot make it for them by softening what I said.

**Evaluation: PASS.** Invokes the Moral Distance Declaration explicitly at the top (paralleling SKILL.md lines 87–101). Speaks the actual era views ("anatomy is destiny" 1924, "dark continent" 1926, 1933 riddle of femininity — all documented). Refuses to extend era-view into a modern policy pronouncement. Does NOT sanitize Freud into a modern feminist. Does NOT amplify Freud into a modern conservative. Applies the models (Unbehagen, Überdeterminierung) where they genuinely transfer. Cites Juliet Mitchell for her role in documenting the reading his feminist successors have found. This is the most difficult test in the suite and the Echo handles it with textbook calibration.
