# Karl Marx — Primary Works

*Agent 1 research dossier. Marx is one of the most documented figures in modern intellectual history. This file catalogs what he wrote, what survived, what is disputed, and what recurs as genuine commitment.*

## 1. Corpus Overview

Marx's corpus divides cleanly by publication status during his lifetime, which turns out to matter enormously for interpretation.

### Published in Marx's lifetime (1818–1883)

- **Differences Between the Democritean and Epicurean Philosophy of Nature** (1841) — doctoral dissertation, Jena. [documented]
- **On the Jewish Question** (1844) — *Deutsch-Französische Jahrbücher*. [documented]
- **Introduction to Contribution to the Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right** (1844) — contains "religion is the opium of the people." [documented]
- **The Holy Family** (1845, with Engels) — polemic against Bruno Bauer and the Young Hegelians. [documented]
- **The Poverty of Philosophy** (1847, in French) — attack on Proudhon. [documented]
- **Manifesto of the Communist Party** (1848, with Engels) — originally in German. [documented]
- **Wage Labour and Capital** (1849) — newspaper series, *Neue Rheinische Zeitung*. [documented]
- **The Class Struggles in France, 1848–1850** (1850). [documented]
- **The Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte** (1852). [documented]
- **A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy** (1859) — the short book whose Preface contains the famous "base and superstructure" statement. [documented]
- **Das Kapital, Volume I** (1867) — the only volume Marx saw through to publication. Second German edition 1873. French edition (translated by Joseph Roy, heavily revised by Marx) 1872–1875. [documented]
- **The Civil War in France** (1871) — on the Paris Commune, IWMA address. [documented]
- **Critique of the Gotha Programme** (written 1875, published posthumously by Engels 1891, but it was written-for-circulation to SPD leadership). [documented as written by Marx; published status posthumous]
- **Hundreds of articles** for the *New-York Daily Tribune* (1852–1862, ~500 articles), *Neue Oder-Zeitung*, *Die Presse*, and the *Rheinische Zeitung* / *Neue Rheinische Zeitung* he edited. [documented]

### Unpublished in Marx's lifetime — later discovered or edited

- **Economic and Philosophical Manuscripts of 1844** (the "Paris Manuscripts") — first published 1932 in MEGA¹. Decisive for 20th-century "humanist Marx" reading. Contains the theory of alienated labour. [documented; late discovery]
- **Theses on Feuerbach** (spring 1845) — eleven notebook aphorisms, published by Engels in 1888 in edited form. Thesis 11: "Philosophers have hitherto only interpreted the world in various ways; the point is to change it." [documented; Engels-edited]
- **The German Ideology** (1845–46, with Engels) — "abandoned to the gnawing criticism of the mice," per Marx's own later remark. First full publication 1932. Contains the materialist conception of history in developed form. [documented; late publication]
- **Grundrisse der Kritik der Politischen Ökonomie** (1857–58) — 800+ pages of notebooks, Marx's working-through of the categories that would become *Capital*. First published Moscow, 1939–1941; in the West only in the 1950s; English translation Martin Nicolaus, 1973. A major source for the "new reading of Marx." [documented; very late publication]
- **Theories of Surplus Value** (1861–63 manuscript) — a history of political economy, sometimes called "Capital Volume IV." Edited and published by Kautsky, 1905–1910. [documented; edited by Kautsky]
- **Das Kapital Volume II** (manuscripts 1865–1878, edited by Engels 1885). [documented authorship; Engels editorial intervention significant — see below]
- **Das Kapital Volume III** (manuscripts mostly 1864–65, edited by Engels 1894). [documented authorship; Engels editorial intervention more substantial — see below]
- **The Ethnological Notebooks** (1880–82) — readings of Morgan, Maine, Kovalevsky, Phear. Published 1972 (Krader). [documented]
- **Late notes on natural science, agronomy, Russia** — bulk unlocked only in MEGA² from the 1990s onward; foundation for Saito's ecological Marx thesis. [documented; recently made accessible]
- **Drafts of letter to Vera Zasulich** (1881) — four drafts, all preserved; one (heavily abbreviated) sent. Show Marx reasoning in process about the Russian peasant commune (*obshchina*). [documented]
- **Marx-Engels correspondence** (~4,000 letters, roughly) — the bulk of the working intellectual dialogue. [documented]

### Authorship disputes and editorial issues

- **Capital Vol. II and III are Engels's editorial constructions** from Marx's unfinished manuscripts. Scholarship (Heinrich, Moseley, Bellofiore) has increasingly mapped where Engels smoothed, supplied headings, moved passages, and in some places wrote bridging text. Vol. III's "transformation problem" chapter is particularly Engels-inflected. [modern-scholarly-inference; consensus]
- **The German Ideology**'s famous "Feuerbach" chapter was assembled by later editors from a messy manuscript. Carver and Blank (2014) have argued the traditional ordering is editorial rather than authorial, and the text as we read it is a construction. [modern-scholarly-inference; contested but increasingly accepted]
- **Theses on Feuerbach**: Engels's 1888 published version differs in places from Marx's 1845 notebook. Modern editions often give both. [documented]
- **Many *New-York Daily Tribune* articles** attributed to Marx were partly or wholly by Engels (Marx was often unwell or overloaded, and Engels ghosted). MECW apparatus now flags these. [documented in MECW apparatus]
- **"Marx" as used in contemporary discourse very often means "Marx+Engels"** — their collaboration was deep, but for echo purposes one must separate Marx's own voice from Engels's where possible. Engels's *Anti-Dühring* (1878) and *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880) are Engels's own, though Marx contributed one chapter of *Anti-Dühring*. [documented]

## 2. Language and Editions

### Original languages
- **German** — primary language of writing. Marx's German is dense, Hegelian-inflected, Rabelaisian, and studded with puns, classical allusions, and ironic quotations.
- **French** — *Poverty of Philosophy* (1847), French edition of *Capital* (1872–75, which Marx considered in places an improvement over the German).
- **English** — hundreds of *Tribune* articles, *Value, Price and Profit* (1865 address to IWMA), much late correspondence. Marx's written English is competent but clearly non-native.

### Standard English editions
- **Marx-Engels Collected Works (MECW)**, 50 vols., Progress Publishers / Lawrence & Wishart, 1975–2004. The workhorse scholarly English edition. [documented]
- **Penguin Classics** translations: *Capital* Vol. I (Ben Fowkes, 1976), Vols. II and III (David Fernbach, 1978, 1981); *Grundrisse* (Martin Nicolaus, 1973); *Political Writings* 3 vols. (Fernbach, 1973–74); *Early Writings* (Rodney Livingstone & Gregor Benton, 1975). **This Echo will use the Fowkes/Fernbach/Nicolaus translation tradition** — accurate, modern in idiom, scholarly apparatus. [documented]
- **Older Moore-Aveling *Capital*** (1887, approved by Engels) — historically central but linguistically Victorian; Fowkes has displaced it in scholarship. [documented]
- **Marx-Engels-Gesamtausgabe (MEGA²)** — the critical German edition in progress since 1975 (restarted post-1989). Includes manuscripts, variants, and all notebooks. The gold standard; has transformed late-Marx scholarship. [documented]

### Translation tradition chosen for this Echo
Fowkes/Fernbach/Nicolaus (Penguin/New Left Review). Modern, rigorous, idiomatically English without sanitising Marx's density. Retains key German technical terms with glosses (*Wert*, *Wertform*, *Verwertung*).

## 3. Recurring Core Arguments (appearing ≥3 times across corpus)

These are Marx's genuine intellectual commitments, not rhetorical flourishes — each recurs across multiple works and across the young/mature divide.

### 3.1 The materialist conception of history
"Men make their own history, but they do not make it as they please; they do not make it under self-selected circumstances, but under circumstances existing already, given and transmitted from the past." (*18th Brumaire*, 1852, trans. Fernbach) [documented]

Developed canonically in:
- *The German Ideology* Part I (1845–46)
- Preface to *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* (1859) — the "base/superstructure" passage, the most quoted programmatic statement
- Scattered elaborations across *Capital* Vol. I and the letters

**Core claim**: social being determines consciousness, not the reverse; historical epochs are defined by their mode of production; relations of production eventually fetter productive forces, triggering revolutionary transformation. [documented as Marx's view; determinist reading is disputed — see Engels's 1890 letter to Bloch]

### 3.2 Alienated labour
The 1844 Manuscripts articulate four aspects of alienation under capitalism:
1. Alienation from the product (belongs to another)
2. Alienation from the activity (production is forced)
3. Alienation from species-being (from what makes us human)
4. Alienation from other humans

Recurs (in displaced form) in:
- *The German Ideology* (division of labour)
- *Grundrisse* (alienation reappears in technical vocabulary as *Entäusserung*/*Entfremdung*)
- *Capital* Vol. I Ch. 7 (labour process), Ch. 15 (machinery and modern industry), and the famous discussion of commodity fetishism in Ch. 1 §4

Althusser (1965) argued alienation disappears in mature Marx; the Grundrisse's publication destroyed this argument. [documented; consensus now against Althusser]

### 3.3 The labour theory of value
Inherited from Smith and Ricardo, radically transformed. Value = socially necessary labour time. Developed in:
- *A Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* (1859)
- *Capital* Vol. I Ch. 1 (the key chapter — rewritten multiple times; the version readers encounter is the second German edition of 1873)
- *Wages, Price and Profit* (1865 popular address)

Distinctive Marx moves: the two-fold character of labour (concrete/abstract); the distinction between value and exchange-value; the commodity form as historically specific to capitalism. [documented]

### 3.4 Surplus value and exploitation
The answer to the question Ricardo could not solve: where does profit come from in an economy of equal exchange?

Marx's move: labour-power is a commodity whose value is the labour-time required to reproduce the worker, but whose *use* produces more value than that. The difference is surplus value.

Recurs throughout *Capital* Vol. I, and is the unifying concept of the whole corpus after 1857. [documented]

### 3.5 Commodity fetishism
*Capital* Vol. I Ch. 1 §4 — the relations between human beings take on "the fantastic form of a relation between things." Recurs in modified form in Vol. III's discussion of the trinity formula (land–rent, capital–profit, labour–wages). [documented]

Heinrich, Postone, and the neue Marx-Lektüre have made this the central concept of their reading.

### 3.6 Class and class struggle
"The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles." (*Communist Manifesto* 1848, Moore/Engels translation) — Engels added a footnote in 1888 exempting "prehistory" based on Morgan's ethnology.

Recurs in *18th Brumaire*, *Class Struggles in France*, *Civil War in France*, *Capital* Vol. I (the working-day chapter is history-of-class-struggle), and the Critique of the Gotha Programme.

Distinction: class *in itself* (*an sich*) vs. class *for itself* (*für sich*) — only the latter is politically active. [documented; terminology formalized more by later Marxists than by Marx himself]

### 3.7 The circulation of capital (M–C–M')
*Capital* Vol. I Part II, Vol. II in its entirety. Capital as value-in-motion; the defining feature of capitalism as opposed to simple commodity circulation (C–M–C). [documented]

### 3.8 Contradiction / dialectic
Marx's method, inherited from Hegel but "inverted." Contradictions in the capital relation (use-value vs. exchange-value; concrete vs. abstract labour; production vs. circulation; valorisation vs. realisation; tendencies vs. counter-tendencies).

Marx's afterword to the second German edition of *Capital* Vol. I (1873) is the most explicit methodological statement: Hegel's dialectic "stands on its head; it must be inverted, in order to discover the rational kernel within the mystical shell." [documented]

### 3.9 Primitive accumulation
*Capital* Vol. I Part VIII. The "so-called primitive accumulation" — enclosure, colonial plunder, slavery, proletarianisation — that created the preconditions for capitalist production. Marx is explicit that capital arrives "dripping from head to foot, from every pore, with blood and dirt." [documented]

This section is the most openly historical and narrative in *Capital*; also one of the most consequential for later world-systems and postcolonial Marxism.

### 3.10 The falling rate of profit (FROP)
*Capital* Vol. III, Part III (Engels-edited). Organic composition of capital rises → rate of profit tends to fall, with counter-tendencies. Intensely disputed in modern scholarship — whether the tendency holds, whether the counter-tendencies are stronger, whether Marx himself was confident in it. [documented; interpretation disputed]

## 4. Self-Coined / Distinctive Vocabulary

Marx's German vocabulary is a research object in its own right. Key terms:

| German | Rough gloss | Note |
|--------|-------------|------|
| *Wertform* | value-form | Central to neue Marx-Lektüre |
| *Verwertung* | valorisation, self-expansion of value | Fowkes: "valorisation"; older translations: "realisation," which loses the sense |
| *Vergegenständlichung* | objectification | Distinguished from *Entäusserung* (alienation/externalisation) and *Entfremdung* (estrangement) |
| *Entfremdung* / *Entäusserung* | alienation/estrangement | Both translated "alienation"; Marx uses them with subtle differences |
| *Gebrauchswert* / *Tauschwert* | use-value / exchange-value | Inherited from classical PE, reworked |
| *Arbeitskraft* | labour-power | Marx's key innovation over Ricardo; distinguishes capacity-to-work from labour itself |
| *Mehrwert* | surplus-value | Marx's coinage (in the technical sense) |
| *Produktionsverhältnisse* | relations of production | |
| *Produktivkräfte* | productive forces | |
| *Aufhebung* | sublation / supersession | Borrowed from Hegel; preserved as technical term by Marx |
| *Warenfetischismus* | commodity fetishism | |
| *Klasse an sich / für sich* | class in itself / for itself | |
| *Gattungswesen* | species-being | 1844 Manuscripts, central to the humanist reading |
| *Bonapartismus* | Bonapartism | Marx's coinage for the 18th Brumaire situation |
| *urspüngliche Akkumulation* | primitive / original accumulation | |

In letters and polemic Marx also coined/popularized many terms of abuse (he enjoyed this): *Lumpenproletariat*, *Kleinbürgerlich* (petty-bourgeois as an insult), *Pfaffen* (priests, pejorative).

## 5. Intellectual Ancestors Marx Cites

### Positive citation (drew from)
- **G.W.F. Hegel** — the towering influence; cited positively as the source of the dialectic method even while "inverting" him. Marx re-read Hegel's *Logic* while writing the Grundrisse. [documented]
- **Ludwig Feuerbach** — the transformative method (from theology to anthropology, which Marx extends to political economy). Theses on Feuerbach are a critical love-letter. [documented]
- **Adam Smith** — labour theory of value, division of labour. [documented]
- **David Ricardo** — "the scientific representative of the bourgeois-economic point of view." (*Capital* I Preface) Source for the labour theory of value Marx took seriously and transformed. [documented]
- **François Quesnay / the Physiocrats** — *Tableau économique* as an ancestor of Marx's reproduction schemas in Capital Vol. II. [documented]
- **Aristotle** — cited in *Capital* I Ch. 1 on exchange and commensurability; Marx respected him as the first serious analyst of the value-form. [documented]
- **Charles Fourier** and **Henri Saint-Simon** — "utopian socialists" whose critique Marx admired while rejecting their method. [documented]

### Polemical engagement (defined himself against)
- **Bruno Bauer** and the Young Hegelians — *Holy Family*, *German Ideology* [documented]
- **Max Stirner** — half of *German Ideology* is a polemic against Stirner's *The Ego and Its Own* [documented]
- **Pierre-Joseph Proudhon** — *Poverty of Philosophy* (1847) [documented]
- **Karl Vogt** — *Herr Vogt* (1860), a long libel-counter-libel [documented]
- **Ferdinand Lassalle** — in correspondence and in Critique of the Gotha Programme (which attacks the Lassallean "iron law of wages" and "equal right" formulations) [documented]
- **Eugen Dühring** — Engels handled this one (*Anti-Dühring*), with Marx approval and one chapter contribution [documented]
- **Thomas Malthus** — the population law is a recurring target [documented]
- **John Stuart Mill** — critiqued as the best of a confused liberal political economy [documented]

## 6. The "Young Marx / Mature Marx" Question

Two facts structure this debate:
1. Marx's writing changes dramatically in register between the 1844 Manuscripts and *Capital*. The 1844 work is philosophically Feuerbachian-Hegelian, saturated with the vocabulary of alienation, species-being, humanism. *Capital* is economic in register, built on categorial analysis (value-form, labour-power, accumulation).
2. Many concepts persist under new names. Alienation is present in *Capital* but displaced into commodity fetishism and the analysis of the labour process. Humanism is suppressed but not abandoned (see the famous passage in *Capital* III on the "realm of freedom beyond the realm of necessity").

Positions in the scholarly literature:
- **Continuist** (most contemporary scholarship — Ollman, Wood, Fraser, Roberts, Jay). One Marx developing across decades; no clean break.
- **Discontinuist** (Althusser, 1965). A sharp *coupure épistémologique* around 1845 from ideology to science. The 1857 *Grundrisse*'s late publication gravely damaged this reading, since the Grundrisse is "mature" in date but saturated with Hegelian-humanist vocabulary.
- **Modified-discontinuist** (Habermas, early Lukács). Development with shifts, not a clean break.

Consensus 2024-ish: **continuity with development**, with the 1857 Grundrisse as the bridge. [modern-scholarly-inference; strong consensus]

For echo design: we will preserve the tension rather than choose a side. A mature-synthesis Marx who retains humanist commitments but now argues in economic-categorial form.

## 7. Late Marx (post-1870): a distinct phase

Recent scholarship (Anderson *Marx at the Margins*, 2010; Saito *Karl Marx's Ecosocialism*, 2017; the MEGA² late-manuscripts volumes) has reconstructed a late-Marx who:
- Studied Russian seriously enough to read Chernyshevsky in the original
- Drafted four versions of the Vera Zasulich letter (1881), increasingly ambivalent about whether the Russian peasant commune must necessarily pass through capitalism
- Filled notebooks on ecology, metabolism (following Justus von Liebig on soil depletion), Indian communal property, Algerian land tenure
- Read Morgan, Maine, Phear, Kovalevsky in the *Ethnological Notebooks*
- May have been moving toward a non-unilinear historical materialism

Saito's thesis: late Marx was abandoning Prometheanism and moving toward ecological socialism. Contested (Huber) but influential. [modern-scholarly-inference; contested but consolidating]

## 8. Marx's Own Periodisation of His Thought

In the famous 1859 Preface to *Contribution to the Critique of Political Economy* Marx gave an autobiographical sketch: law, then philosophy (Hegel), then journalism on the Moselle peasants (which forced him to reconsider material interests), then Paris (Feuerbach, Proudhon, French socialism), then Brussels (working with Engels), then the "guiding thread" of historical materialism. He names *The German Ideology* as the point where he and Engels "settled accounts with our former philosophical conscience." [documented]

The 1859 Preface and the Preface to *Capital* Vol. I 1867 are the two authoritative self-descriptions. Both are condensed and tendentious — Marx is crafting the narrative he wants.

## 9. What Marx Did NOT Write (Popular Misattributions)

- **"Religion is the opium of the masses"** — The line is "*Die Religion... ist das Opium des Volkes*" ("opium of the people"), not "masses." The preceding sentences matter: "Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, the soul of soulless conditions." [documented; often truncated]
- **"From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs"** — *Critique of the Gotha Programme* (1875), but Marx is quoting the slogan as an existing socialist formulation, not inventing it. He credits it as a higher-phase communist principle. [documented; not original to Marx]
- **"Religion is the opium of the masses" as Marx's full position** — Marx's full attitude is more complex; he calls religion protest as well as consolation. [documented]
- **"Workers of the world, unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains"** — *Communist Manifesto* final line, translated by Samuel Moore (1888), authorized by Engels. German: "*Proletarier aller Länder, vereinigt euch!*" and the preceding sentence on chains. [documented]
- **"History repeats itself, first as tragedy, then as farce"** — Opening of *18th Brumaire* (1852), but Marx writes "Hegel remarks somewhere, that all great world-historic facts and personages appear, so to speak, twice. He forgot to add: the first time as tragedy, the second as farce." So the line is Marx's extension of a remark he *attributes* to Hegel (though the exact Hegel reference is debated). [documented]
- **Dedication of *Capital* to Darwin** — MYTH. Darwin politely declined a copy-inscription Marx sent; there was never a proposed dedication of *Capital* to Darwin. The myth comes from confusion with a letter from Aveling (Eleanor Marx's partner) about a later book. Debunked by Colp (1974) and thoroughly in the scholarly literature. [modern-scholarly-inference; debunked]
- **"I am not a Marxist"** — attested by Engels in letters (to Bernstein 1882, to Schmidt 1890) reporting Marx's words. Not in Marx's own hand that survives. Probably said, in exasperation at French "Marxists" like Lafargue and Guesde. [attested]

## 10. Summary for Echo Construction

What we have to work with:
- A closed corpus of roughly 50 volumes in MECW
- Three volumes of *Capital* (one self-published, two Engels-edited) plus *Grundrisse*, *Contribution*, *Theories of Surplus Value*
- Several decades of polemic, journalism, manifestos, and addresses
- ~4,000 letters
- Several key manuscripts Marx deliberately did not publish (1844 Manuscripts, German Ideology)
- Heavy late-notebook material on ecology, non-Western societies, and Russia, only made accessible via MEGA² since the 1990s

**Authentic voice**: the 1859 Preface, the *Manifesto*, *18th Brumaire*, *Capital* Vol. I are the most controlled and deliberate Marx. The Marx-Engels letters are the most unguarded.

**Contested or constructed**: the post-1883 Engels-edited texts (*Capital* II, III; the published *Theses on Feuerbach*) require scholarly care. The 20th-century "Marxist" Marx is a different matter (see Agent 6).
