## 1. Friedrich Engels (1820–1895) — collaborator, financial patron, literary executor

**Bias flag**: Pro-Marx to the maximum. Engels shaped Marx's reception more than anyone. His memoirs, eulogies, and post-1883 editorial interventions are inseparable from the "historical Marx."

**Key texts on Marx as a person**:
- **Eulogy at Highgate Cemetery, 17 March 1883** — the "two great discoveries" formulation (the materialist conception of history; the law of motion of capitalism). Pronounces Marx "the best-hated and most-calumniated man of his time." Famously: "His name will endure through the ages, and so also will his work." [documented]
  - Note: the eulogy has shaped every subsequent introduction to Marx. It is reception-history more than contemporary-record.
- **"On the History of the Communist League"** (1885) — Engels's retrospective account of the 1840s. Self-serving, but the best source on pre-1848 Marx in action.
- **Many letters after Marx's death** — to Bernstein, Kautsky, Sorge, Bebel — describing Marx's working habits, health, family life, and views. These are [attested] sources: Engels remembering what he observed.

**Specific observations preserved in Engels correspondence**:
- Marx's punishing working schedule, especially 1850s–1860s at the British Museum Reading Room
- Marx's chronic ill-health after c. 1860 — hidradenitis suppurativa (boils/carbuncles), liver trouble, insomnia; Engels pleads with him to stop working and see doctors
- The financial situation: Engels subsidised Marx for decades, partly from his share of his family's Manchester textile firm (Ermen & Engels). After 1869 Engels sold out and put Marx on a proper annual allowance — Marx's chronic poverty effectively ended then. [documented in correspondence]
- Marx's intellectual method: reading voraciously in several languages, taking massive notebooks, struggling to bring *Capital* to completion. Engels in 1867: "Without your self-sacrifice I could never have accomplished the immense work involved in Vol. I." [attested]

## 2. Wilhelm Liebknecht (1826–1900) — SPD co-founder, Marx's London friend

**Bias flag**: Devoted disciple; prone to hagiographic smoothing.

**Source**: *Karl Marx: Biographical Memoirs* (first published in German, 1896; English edition translated by Ernest Untermann, 1901). [attested]

**Preserved episodes**:
- Sunday walks on Hampstead Heath with the Marx family and the young Liebknecht, 1850s. Marx reciting long passages of Goethe, Dante, Shakespeare from memory. [attested]
- The famous "pub-crawl" evening — Liebknecht recounts a Sunday outing along the Tottenham Court Road where Marx and his companions got progressively drunker, broke gas-lamps, and fled the police. Probably embroidered but not fabricated. [attested, part-legendary]
- Marx's children — especially Jenny (Jennychen), Laura, and Eleanor — were central to his emotional life. Liebknecht stresses how the public image of "cold revolutionary theorist" misrepresents the tender paterfamilias.
- Marx loved reading novels aloud — Cervantes, Balzac, Fielding, Scott.
- The family nickname "Moor" (*der Mohr*) — from Marx's very dark complexion and hair — used even by his children and by Engels. [documented]
- Marx's irritation at being asked to produce bite-size political statements: "My entire doctrine is a protest against the habit of reducing science to catechisms." [attested via Liebknecht, not documented in Marx's own hand]

## 3. Paul Lafargue (1842–1911) — son-in-law (married Laura Marx 1868)

**Bias flag**: Loyal, admiring, French; his "Marxism" is one of the targets of the "I am not a Marxist" remark.

**Source**: *Reminiscences of Marx* (1890, memorial volume). [attested]

**Preserved observations**:
- **Marx's working habits at the British Museum**: arrived at 9 AM, stayed until 7 PM, then returned home to work through the night. The famous (probably partly embroidered) claim that Marx's pacing in his Maitland Park Road study wore a groove in the carpet. [attested, possibly legendary]
- **Marx's library habits**: underlined, annotated in margins, cross-referenced. Never wrote on first reading — re-read everything.
- **Conversational style**: dense, rapid, sarcastic, prone to classical and literary allusion. Could be charming to friends, devastating to adversaries.
- **Favourite writers**: Aeschylus, Shakespeare, Goethe, Balzac, Dante, Cervantes, Diderot. Re-read Aeschylus in Greek every year. [attested]
- **Cigar habit**: Marx's only real extravagance; he is supposed to have said the cheap cigars he could afford "cost him more than he earned," a joke he repeated. [attested]
- **Personal reserve**: "He was the tenderest of fathers... the most amiable of companions" — but only with intimates; with the outside world he was austere. [attested]

## 4. Jenny Marx, née von Westphalen (1814–1881) — wife

**Bias flag**: Loyal but honest; her memoir fragment is a poignant record.

**Source**: *A Short Sketch of an Eventful Life* (memoir fragment, c. 1865, published posthumously). [documented]

**Preserved content**:
- The grinding poverty in **Dean Street, Soho (1850–56)** — two rooms for five, then six, then four people after two babies died. The pawnshop cycle: silverware, shawls, coats in and out weekly.
- **Deaths of children**: Guido (1850, age 1), Franziska (1852, age 1), Edgar ("Musch," 1855, age 8). Edgar's death was the most devastating. Marx wrote to Engels (April 6 1855): "My heart is broken, my brain is in a whirl." [documented]
- Jenny's long labour secretarying Marx's dreadful handwriting; copying *Capital* manuscripts into fair copies.
- Her own ambivalence toward revolutionary life ("We got engaged in 1836; we waited seven years; we had every right to expect a quieter domestic life").

## 5. Eleanor Marx ("Tussy," 1855–1898) — youngest daughter, later socialist

**Bias flag**: Loving daughter, loyal to father's political legacy.

**Source**: Letters; introductions to editions of Marx's works she translated; recollections scattered through her own writings. [documented/attested]

**Preserved content**:
- **Hans Röckle stories** — the serialised fairy tale Marx invented for his daughters on walks. Marx would resume the story week after week, inventing cliffhangers. [attested]
- Marx as the principal parent for games, storytelling, literature.
- The 1865 **"Confessions"** parlor-game — an album Jennychen kept in which Marx filled in answers to Victorian questionnaire-style prompts. These are actually documented in his own hand. [documented]

## 6. Hostile contemporaries — adversaries and breakaways

### Mikhail Bakunin (1814–1876)

**Bias flag**: Became Marx's most serious adversary; Marx ejected Bakunin from the IWMA at The Hague (1872). Bakunin's descriptions of Marx are polemically charged.

**Representative passages** (from Bakunin's *Statism and Anarchy*, 1873, and correspondence):
- Marx as "from top to toe, a man of authority." [attested]
- "Marx is undoubtedly one of the most acute and erudite men of our day... but there is also a good deal of ambition and vanity." [attested]
- Bakunin's 1872 polemic charges Marx with seeking dictatorship within the International.
- Ugly ethnic remarks: Bakunin wrote in correspondence about Marx's Jewish heritage in antisemitic terms — this is part of the period's discourse. Preserve, do not sanitize.

### Pierre-Joseph Proudhon (1809–1865)

**Bias flag**: Personal and theoretical break after Marx's *Poverty of Philosophy* (1847).

**Representative**: Proudhon's 1846 letter to Marx — a remarkable statement of method-disagreement, refusing the dogmatism he sensed: "Let us not, because we are at the head of a movement, make ourselves the leaders of a new intolerance..." [documented]

### Ferdinand Lassalle (1825–1864)

**Bias flag**: Frenemy. Marx privately despised Lassalle while needing to cooperate.

**In Marx's own correspondence** (not Lassalle's voice — but important for how Marx saw a contemporary):
- Repeated ugly characterizations in private Marx-Engels letters, including antisemitic nicknames and remarks about Lassalle's appearance. These letters contain some of the ugliest material in the whole Marx corpus. [documented — preserve in research with Moral Distance Declaration for runtime handling]

### Eugen Dühring (1833–1921)

Attacked by Engels (with Marx's approval) in *Anti-Dühring*. Dühring himself was virulently antisemitic and nationalist; his "response" tradition shaped reactionary German thought.

## 7. State surveillance — the hostile-state record

### Prussian surveillance
Marx was expelled from Prussia (1849), France (twice — 1845, 1849), and Belgium (1848). Each state filed him.

The famous **1852 Prussian spy's report** on Marx in London (reproduced in McLellan, *Karl Marx: Interviews and Recollections*, 1981) described:
- Marx's "Bohemian intellectual" household
- Two-room Dean Street flat, "everything broken, tattered, torn, with a thick dust over all"
- Marx himself: "disorderly, cynical host... in torn dressing-gown, covered in ash from his pipe"
- But: "an impression of rare intellectual superiority"

[documented in Prussian police archives; the bias is hostile-state, but the physical description corroborates Liebknecht and Lafargue]

### British Home Office
Much thinner file — Britain did not surveil Marx seriously. Scotland Yard concluded he was a harmless if noisy refugee. [documented]

## 8. Press work as contemporary record

### *New-York Daily Tribune* (1852–1862)
~500 articles under Marx's byline (some by Engels). Editor Charles Dana was Marx's entry point; correspondence with Dana preserves how Marx covered the Crimean War, the American Civil War (for which he was strongly pro-Union), the Indian Rebellion of 1857, Chinese affairs, continental European politics. [documented]

This journalism — almost none of it about "Marxism" — is the most sustained picture of Marx as a working observer of contemporary events.

### *Neue Rheinische Zeitung* (1848–49)
Marx's own paper in Cologne during the 1848–49 revolution. Trial for inciting insurrection, June 1849; Marx's self-defence speech is preserved and is a notable primary source. [documented]

## 10. Famous sayings reported by contemporaries — provenance

- **"I am not a Marxist"** — reported by Engels in letters to Bernstein (November 1882) and to Conrad Schmidt (August 5, 1890). [attested via Engels]
- **"De omnibus dubitandum"** ("everything must be doubted") — motto Marx wrote in the 1865 Confessions. [documented]
- **"Nihil humani a me alienum puto"** ("nothing human is alien to me") — from Terence; Marx's favourite maxim per 1865 Confessions. [documented — Marx quoting Terence]
- **"Moor"** — family nickname, in correspondence. [documented]
- **Engels's graveside "two great discoveries"** — reception-history-inflected eulogy, not Marx's self-description. [documented as Engels's formulation]

## 11. What Marx Looked Like in Public

Composite from contemporaries:
- Short-to-medium stature, strongly built, very dark hair and complexion, piercing dark eyes
- Hair went white early (by 50s) but heavy black beard remained
- Often dishevelled in private; made effort at public meetings
- Voice reportedly thick with Rhineland accent when speaking German in English exile
- In speaking, intense, rapid, sarcastic; not a great public orator (Lassalle was the great orator of German socialism; Marx was not)
- Chronic carbuncles — probably hidradenitis suppurativa (modern diagnosis, Shuster 2007). Caused him constant pain from the 1860s on. [documented; modern diagnosis is [modern-scholarly-inference]]

## 12. Key Conflicts Preserved (Not Harmonized)

1. **Marx the "authoritarian" (Bakunin) vs. Marx the "democrat" (Liebknecht)** — The Hague 1872 split produces two rival contemporary narratives that have never been fully reconciled. Bakuninists and Marxists each had witnesses; preserve both.

2. **Marx the "lazy dilettante" (hostile press, later Sperber's mildly revisionist reading) vs. Marx the "superhuman worker" (Engels, Lafargue)** — Marx's output is enormous AND he failed to finish *Capital*. Both are true; the tension is real.

3. **The tender family man (family sources) vs. the vicious polemicist (adversary-sources, and Marx's own published polemic)** — both documented. Marx could be both.

4. **Marx's Jewishness** — ethnic-heritage yes; baptized 1824 at age six; raised Prussian-Lutheran culturally. His own correspondence contains antisemitic language about other Jews (notably Lassalle). His "On the Jewish Question" (1844) has been read as both antisemitic and as a critique of the "Jewish question" as such. This is unresolved in scholarship and in Marx's own attitudes. [documented; interpretively contested]
