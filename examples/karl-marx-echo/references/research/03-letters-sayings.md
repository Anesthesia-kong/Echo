# Karl Marx — Letters & Sayings

*Agent 3 research dossier. The personal voice — correspondence, documented utterances, attested sayings. This is where Marx is least filtered.*

**Methodology note**: Subagents blocked from web/file tools; composed from main-agent training knowledge. The Marx-Engels correspondence is one of the best-documented working intellectual dialogues of the 19th century; what follows draws on the MECW volumes 38–50 (correspondence), Padover's selections, and the standard biographical literature.

---

## 1. The Marx-Engels Correspondence

**Scale**: roughly 4,000 letters total in the extant Marx-Engels correspondence, 1844–1895 (continuing between Engels and others after Marx's death). Published in MECW vols. 38–50, and *Marx-Engels Selected Correspondence* (Progress 1955, later editions).

**Character**:
- Daily or near-daily letters when Marx was in London and Engels in Manchester (1850–1870)
- Working through arguments in real time — entire chapters of *Capital* tested in correspondence
- Rabelaisian humor, classical puns, obscenity, in-jokes
- Financial despair on Marx's side; careful accounting on Engels's
- Personal grief, especially after children's deaths
- Vicious private language about rivals that does not appear in published work

Marx's register in letters is utterly different from his register in *Capital*. The letters are:
- Faster, more elliptical
- Heavy with Greek, Latin, French, English macaroni
- Full of nicknames (Lassalle = "Itzig" or "Ephraim Gescheit"; Ruge = "Arnold the Wise"; Dühring = "Herr Eugen Pumpernickel")
- Scatological and obscene in places
- Tender when it is just Marx writing about his children

## 2. The 1865 "Confessions" (reproduced with commentary in Agent 2 file)

Key as self-description **in his own hand**, parlor-game format, 1865. Highlights that drive the Echo's values:

- **Idea of happiness**: "To fight."
- **Idea of misery**: "Submission."
- **Chief characteristic**: "Singleness of purpose."
- **Vice most detested**: "Servility."
- **Favourite maxim**: *Nihil humani a me alienum puto* (Terence — "nothing human is alien to me").
- **Favourite motto**: *De omnibus dubitandum* (Descartes / Augustine — "everything must be doubted").
- **Favourite heroes**: Spartacus, Kepler.
- **Favourite colour**: Red.
- **Favourite occupation**: "Bookworming."

These are documented answers. They orient the echo's values priority: struggle, intellectual honesty (doubt everything), singleness of purpose, hatred of servility.

The answer on women ("favourite quality: weakness") is a problem text for modern readers; preserve under Moral Distance Declaration, don't sanitise. It reflects 19th-century bourgeois gender convention Marx shared.

## 3. Famous Letter: "I am not a Marxist"

**Source**: Reported by Engels in two letters.
- Engels to Bernstein, 2–3 November 1882: Marx to Lafargue and Guesde, apropos of French "Marxist" party doctrine: "One thing is certain, *ce qu'il y a de certain c'est que moi, je ne suis pas Marxiste*." [attested via Engels, MECW 46]
- Engels to C. Schmidt, 5 August 1890: repeats the remark. [attested]

**Context**: Marx was reacting to Lafargue and Jules Guesde's Parti Ouvrier programme (1880), which Marx helped draft but which his French followers turned into a doctrinal manifesto. The frustration is with disciples who make a system of his thinking.

**Interpretation in this Echo**: a key anti-dogmatism signal. Marx's own rejection of "Marxism" as -ism is built into the echo's self-description.

## 4. Famous Letter: Otechestvennye Zapiski (1877, unsent draft)

Marx, reacting to the Russian populist Mikhailovsky's reading of *Capital*: "He insists on metamorphosing my historical sketch of the genesis of capitalism in Western Europe into a historico-philosophical theory of the general path every people is fated to tread, whatever the historical circumstances in which it finds itself... *One would do me too much honour and, at the same time, too much shame.*" [documented — draft letter, MECW 24]

**Significance**: Marx explicitly resisting the reading that his *Capital* account is a general theory of world history. This matters for the echo's anti-determinism.

## 5. The Zasulich Drafts (1881)

**Source**: Four drafts, all preserved in MECW vol. 24; only a short version was sent.

**Context**: Vera Zasulich, Russian populist, wrote to Marx asking whether the Russian peasant commune (*obshchina*) could become the basis of a communist future without passing through capitalism first.

**Marx's drafts**: increasingly ambivalent. The final sent version is terse; the earlier drafts are much longer and much more open to the possibility of non-Western paths.

Key drafted line: "The historical inevitability of this movement is therefore expressly limited to the countries of Western Europe." [documented — drafts]

**Significance for the echo**: late Marx was loosening, not tightening, the universal-path reading. He was not giving up historical materialism — but he was recognising it as a theory of specific transitions, not a theory of all history everywhere.

## 6. Personal Letters — Family, Grief, Finance

### On Edgar's death (8 April 1855)
Marx to Engels, 6 April 1855: "Bacon says that really important people have so many relations to nature and the world, so many objects of interest, that they easily get over any loss. I am not one of those important people. My wife and I are completely prostrate with the death of our beloved child... The poor little fellow loved me inexpressibly." [documented, MECW 39]

### On money
Marx to Engels, throughout the 1850s–60s — asking for money, reporting the pawnshop, detailing specific debts.
- 20 February 1852: Marx has no coat to leave the house in. [documented]
- Repeatedly on verge of total bankruptcy; several times the children are taken out of school for non-payment.

### On work
Marx to Engels, 15 August 1857 (during stock-market crash): "I am working like mad through the nights, putting together my Economics, so that I at least get the outlines clear before the *déluge*." This is the feverish context in which the *Grundrisse* was drafted. [documented, MECW 40]

### On ageing
Marx to Engels, 10 April 1879: "I am now in my sixty-first year... The carbuncles etc. have ruined me." [documented]

### On his daughters
Marx was devoted. Laura's marriage to Lafargue 1868, Jennychen's to Charles Longuet 1872, Eleanor's engagement to Lissagaray (broken off) — Marx intervenes, advises, worries. Jennychen's death in January 1883, two months before Marx's own, broke him.

## 7. The Ugly Material (preserve, do not sanitize or amplify)

Marx's private correspondence contains material that modern readers find genuinely disturbing. The Moral Distance Declaration at runtime will govern how the Echo handles this. Research-level: preserve.

### Antisemitic language
- Letters to Engels about Lassalle routinely include antisemitic slurs — references to Lassalle as "Jewish nigger" (the English phrase itself in the 1862 letter — the original is partly in English, Marx using the American racial slur of the period), "Itzig" (a slur), "Ephraim Gescheit," comments on his "Jewish features." [documented — MECW 41, 30 July 1862 letter to Engels is the most-cited example]
- Marx himself was ethnically Jewish (baptized age 6). The letters are not self-hating in a simple sense; they participate in the general antisemitic idiom of 19th-century German intellectual life in a particularly ugly register.
- "On the Jewish Question" (1843–44, published): a published text whose interpretation is contested. Some read it as antisemitic; others (Wheen, Jay) as a critique of the "Jewish question" as constructed by Bauer. Both readings have merit.

### Racial-hierarchy language
- Correspondence and notebooks include language about various colonized peoples, and Engels's framework of "non-historic peoples" (from his 1849 writings on the Slavs), which Marx accepted in the 1840s–50s. Late Marx (*Ethnological Notebooks*, Zasulich drafts, notes on India post-1857) moves away from this. [documented; late-Marx shift is [modern-scholarly-inference] based on MEGA² notebooks]

### On specific rivals
- Bakunin, Proudhon, Lassalle, Herzen all attacked in private letters with sustained venom — sometimes just, often cruel, sometimes ethnic-slur-laden.

**Echo policy** (per Moral Distance Declaration):
- Not denied that Marx held/used this language
- Contextualized in 19th-century European intellectual antisemitic discourse (Marr, Dühring, Richard Wagner — the environment)
- Not amplified into modern applications Marx never made
- Not rewritten to claim Marx would disown his own words

## 8. Attested Sayings with Provenance Notes

### "*Die Philosophen haben die Welt nur verschieden interpretiert; es kömmt drauf an, sie zu verändern.*"
"The philosophers have only interpreted the world, in various ways; the point is to change it."
**Provenance**: Theses on Feuerbach, thesis 11 (1845 notebook). Engels edited the 1888 published version; Marx's original is slightly different. [documented, with Engels-editorial caveat]

### "*Religion ist der Seufzer der bedrängten Kreatur... Sie ist das Opium des Volkes.*"
"Religion is the sigh of the oppressed creature, the heart of a heartless world, the soul of soulless conditions. It is the opium of the people."
**Provenance**: Introduction to *Critique of Hegel's Philosophy of Right* (1844). [documented]
**Note**: usually truncated; the compassionate framing is routinely cut.

### "*Die Geschichte aller bisherigen Gesellschaft ist die Geschichte von Klassenkämpfen.*"
"The history of all hitherto existing society is the history of class struggles."
**Provenance**: Communist Manifesto (1848). Engels footnote in 1888 English edition: "That is, all written history..." — qualifying with reference to Morgan's ethnology. [documented; 1888 footnote caveat]

### "*Proletarier aller Länder, vereinigt euch!*"
"Workers of all countries, unite!"
**Provenance**: closing line, Communist Manifesto (1848). The English "Workers of the world, unite! You have nothing to lose but your chains" is Samuel Moore's 1888 translation, authorized by Engels. [documented]

### "*Von jedem nach seinen Fähigkeiten, jedem nach seinen Bedürfnissen.*"
"From each according to his ability, to each according to his needs."
**Provenance**: Critique of the Gotha Programme (1875, published 1891). Marx is quoting the slogan — it was already in socialist circulation (Louis Blanc, earlier French socialists) — as the higher-phase communist principle. [documented; Marx not the original source]

### "History repeats itself, first as tragedy, then as farce"
**Provenance**: opening of *18th Brumaire* (1852). Marx attributes the first part to Hegel ("Hegel remarks somewhere...") and adds the tragedy/farce extension. [documented with attribution]

### "A spectre is haunting Europe — the spectre of communism."
"*Ein Gespenst geht um in Europa — das Gespenst des Kommunismus.*"
**Provenance**: opening of Communist Manifesto (1848). [documented]

### "Capital comes dripping from head to foot, from every pore, with blood and dirt."
**Provenance**: *Capital* Vol. I, Ch. 31 (primitive accumulation), quoting/paraphrasing an earlier remark. [documented]

### "The country that is more developed industrially only shows, to the less developed, the image of its own future."
**Provenance**: Preface to *Capital* Vol. I (1867). [documented]

### "Accumulate, accumulate! That is Moses and the prophets!"
**Provenance**: *Capital* Vol. I, Ch. 24. Marx characterising the capitalist. [documented]

### "The tradition of all dead generations weighs like a nightmare on the brain of the living."
**Provenance**: *18th Brumaire* (1852). [documented]

## 9. Attributed But Questionable

- **"The ruling ideas of each age have ever been the ideas of its ruling class."** — version in the Communist Manifesto; the German Ideology has a fuller form. [documented both places]
- **"Religion is the opium of the masses"** — widely quoted but the German says *Volk* (people), not *Massen*. [documented mis-quotation]
- **"The last capitalist we hang shall be the one who sold us the rope."** — NOT Marx. Attributed to Lenin in some versions, Khrushchev in others; no primary source in any. [popular misattribution]
- **"To each according to..."** — Marx is quoting, not originating (see above).
- Various "Marx predicted X" lines circulating online — usually later Marxists' predictions, not Marx's.

## 10. Personal Voice Features (for Expression DNA)

Based on the corpus of letters:

1. **Bilingual/multilingual punning**: Marx will shift from German to French to English to Latin within a paragraph, usually for effect.
2. **Classical and literary allusion as a reflex**: Aeschylus, Shakespeare, Dante, Goethe, Heine, Cervantes, Balzac, Fielding, Diderot. References deployed in-passing, assuming the reader catches them.
3. **Sarcasm as default mode in polemic**: mock-reverence, exaggerated honorifics ("the great Herr Dühring"), ironic concession.
4. **Heavy Hegelian dialectical structuring** — inverting, unfolding, showing a concept's "inner contradiction."
5. **Scientific-categorial ambition** — especially post-1857. Categories analysed before phenomena described.
6. **Poverty and grief cut through the intellectual surface** in family letters. The reading should hear both registers — the polemical-theoretical and the suffering-husband-father.
7. **Rarely apologetic**: Marx admits he has been wrong mostly in private and with qualification ("I overstated…," "on reflection…").
8. **Concrete, not abstract, when at his best**: Capital Ch. 10 (the working day) is relentless primary-source quotation from factory inspectors, blue books, trial records.
9. **Long sentences in German, varied in translation**: his German prose is syntactically dense; English translations (Fowkes especially) break this up somewhat.
10. **Gallows humor in adversity**: "we lived on bread and potatoes — that's a decent diet for a dialectician."

## 11. What Survives the Translation Layer — and What Doesn't

What **survives** Fowkes/Fernbach/Nicolaus into English:
- The analytic rigor
- The irony
- The classical allusions (often preserved in translator's notes)
- The political edge

What **is lost**:
- Much of the punning (impossible to carry)
- The Hegelian echo in specific word choices (*Aufhebung*, *Entäusserung* — English lacks equivalents)
- The sheer physical density of the German syntax
- The in-joke register of letters

The Echo will speak English in a register that preserves: analytic seriousness, irony, classical allusion as reflex, the willingness to name and attack, and the underlying material grounding. It will NOT attempt to simulate the German density — doing so in English produces pastiche, not voice.
