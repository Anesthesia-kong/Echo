# Karl Marx — Reception History

*Agent 6 research dossier. The distinction between Marx the 19th-century thinker and "Marx" as reconstructed by later movements. This is especially important for Marx: the gap between his corpus and 20th-century "Marxism" is among the largest such gaps in intellectual history, and the Echo must not collapse it.*

**Methodology note**: composed from main-agent training knowledge. Citations as `[ref: <work>]`.

---

## 1. Late 19th Century: Engels, Kautsky, the Second International (1883–1914)

### Engels as systematiser (1883–1895)
- After Marx's death, Engels edits *Capital* Vols. II (1885) and III (1894) from Marx's unfinished manuscripts — an editorial construction with real interpretive consequences
- Popularises Marx via *Anti-Dühring* (1878) and *Socialism: Utopian and Scientific* (1880)
- *Origin of the Family, Private Property, and the State* (1884) — Engels's own extension, drawing on Morgan
- *Ludwig Feuerbach and the End of Classical German Philosophy* (1886) — retrospective framing
- Engels in correspondence continually correcting crude economic-determinist readings ("if someone here asserts that the economic factor is the *only* determining one, he transforms that proposition into a meaningless, abstract, senseless phrase") — 1890 letter to Bloch, MECW 49 [documented]

### Karl Kautsky (1854–1938) — "Pope of Marxism"
- Main theorist of the SPD and the Second International
- Writes authoritative expositions through which most 19th-century socialists encountered Marx
- Systematic but somewhat flattening — the living intellectual dialectic of Marx-the-thinker becomes a doctrine-Marxism in Kautsky's hands. This is the first major reception-transformation. [modern-scholarly-inference]

### Bernsteinian Revisionism (1899)
- Eduard Bernstein, *The Preconditions of Socialism* (1899) — challenges Marxist orthodoxy: capitalism is not collapsing, the middle class is not disappearing, crises are becoming less severe. Gradualist path to socialism.
- Bernstein's "the movement is everything, the final goal nothing."
- Fierce Second International controversy; Kautsky and Luxemburg oppose; ultimately shapes SPD's de facto reformism

### Rosa Luxemburg (1871–1919)
- *The Accumulation of Capital* (1913) — extension of Marx's Vol. II reproduction schemas into a theory of imperialism
- Revolutionary Marxist opposed to Bernsteinian revisionism, also critical of Leninist vanguardism
- Murdered January 1919, Berlin

### Georgi Plekhanov (1856–1918)
- "Father of Russian Marxism"; teacher of Lenin
- Expounds historical materialism in strongly-determinist, philosophical-materialist key
- Significant for setting the terms in which Russian and Soviet Marxism encountered Marx

## 2. Russian Revolution and Marxism-Leninism (1903–1953)

### Lenin (1870–1924)
- *What Is to Be Done?* (1902) — vanguard party of professional revolutionaries. A major innovation beyond Marx; Marx had no equivalent theory.
- *Imperialism, the Highest Stage of Capitalism* (1917) — extension to globalised capital (drawing on Hilferding, Hobson)
- *State and Revolution* (1917) — radical-democratic moment, smashing the bourgeois state
- Departs from Marx's baseline expectation (revolution in advanced capitalist countries) by leading one in backward capitalist Russia
- The Russian Revolution (October 1917) is the decisive event that re-codes "Marx" for the 20th century

### Stalin (1878–1953) and DIAMAT
- *Dialectical and Historical Materialism* (1938) as chapter 4 of the *History of the CPSU(B): Short Course* — the Stalinist catechism
- Reduces Marx's dialectic to four "features" of matter; Engels's *Dialectics of Nature* selectively canonised
- Becomes state doctrine across the Soviet bloc 1945–89
- The mass violence of Stalinism — collectivisation (millions dead 1929–33), Terror (1936–38), Gulag — conducted in Marx's name
- Scholarly consensus: DIAMAT is a 20th-century political-ideological construction using Marx's vocabulary; the causal relation to Marx's own thought is contested but no serious scholar thinks Marx's texts require Stalinism. [modern-scholarly-inference]

### The "official" Marx of the Soviet bloc
- Marxism-Leninism as state ideology
- Marx-Engels Institute (Moscow) — curates the MEGA¹ (interrupted by Stalin's purges, never completed)
- Academies of Sciences produce codified textbooks
- Marxism becomes a legitimating discourse for state socialism; the live critical power of Marx's categories is dampened

### Trotsky (1879–1940)
- *The Revolution Betrayed* (1937) — Soviet Union as degenerated workers' state
- *Their Morals and Ours* (1938)
- The Trotskyist tradition preserves anti-Stalinist Marxism through the 20th century

## 3. Western Marxism (anti-Stalinist Marxist thought, 1920s–1970s)

The response of Marxist intellectuals outside Soviet orbit, or in the parties opposing Moscow.

### Georg Lukács (1885–1971)
- *History and Class Consciousness* (1923) — reintroduces Hegelian reading of Marx; reification as the central category
- Marxism as method, not doctrine
- Lukács is forced to recant many positions later but the 1923 book remains seminal

### Karl Korsch (1886–1961)
- *Marxism and Philosophy* (1923) — parallel move to Lukács

### Antonio Gramsci (1891–1937)
- *Prison Notebooks* (written 1929–35, published posthumously)
- Hegemony, organic intellectuals, war of position vs. war of manoeuvre
- Huge influence on late-20th-century Western Left

### The Frankfurt School
- Max Horkheimer, Theodor Adorno, Herbert Marcuse, Walter Benjamin, later Jürgen Habermas
- *Dialectic of Enlightenment* (1944, Horkheimer & Adorno)
- Critical theory; reads Marx through Freud, Weber, and a heightened focus on culture
- Exile in the US during Nazism shapes their pessimism about the proletariat as revolutionary subject

### Louis Althusser (1918–1990)
- *For Marx* (1965), *Reading Capital* (1965, with Balibar, Macherey)
- Structural Marxism; epistemological break; rejection of humanism
- Massively influential 1960s–70s, especially in France, Latin America; largely superseded in the 2000s but remnants in Rancière, Badiou

### Jean-Paul Sartre
- *Critique of Dialectical Reason* (1960) — existential-Marxist synthesis

### The New Left (1956–1968)
- 1956 — Hungary crushed by USSR; Khrushchev's secret speech; many leave Communist parties
- Anti-Stalinist, humanist reading of Marx; publication of the 1844 Manuscripts crucial here
- E.P. Thompson, *The Making of the English Working Class* (1963) — culturalist-humanist English Marxism
- *New Left Review* founded 1960

## 4. Maoism and Third-World Marxism

### Mao Zedong (1893–1976)
- Adaptation of Marxism to an overwhelmingly peasant society
- "New Democracy" and peasant-based revolution — significant departure from Marx's industrial-proletariat baseline
- Cultural Revolution (1966–76) — class struggle reconceived as cultural-ideological
- Mass famine of the Great Leap Forward (1958–62, tens of millions dead) — conducted in Marx's name
- Maoism's status as Marxism: contested. Soviet Marxism rejected it; Western Marxism of 1960s–70s was attracted to it; modern scholarship generally reads it as a substantial sinicising transformation. [modern-scholarly-inference]

### Third-World / Anti-Colonial Marxism
- Lenin's *Imperialism* as the opening
- Cabral, Fanon (*Wretched of the Earth* 1961), CLR James (*The Black Jacobins* 1938)
- Che Guevara, Mariátegui
- Post-colonial critiques of Marx's own Eurocentrism emerging (Dipesh Chakrabarty, Gayatri Spivak)

## 5. Analytical Marxism (1970s–1990s)

- G.A. Cohen, *Karl Marx's Theory of History: A Defence* (1978)
- John Roemer, *A General Theory of Exploitation and Class* (1982)
- Jon Elster, *Making Sense of Marx* (1985)
- The "September Group" — rational-choice Marxism; philosophical clarity; stripping out Hegel
- By the 1990s most members conclude that much of Marx's apparatus cannot be formally salvaged; Cohen eventually shifts to egalitarian political philosophy (*If You're an Egalitarian, How Come You're So Rich?* 2000)

---

## 6. 1968 and After

- May 1968 Paris; anti-Vietnam War movement in the US; Prague Spring crushed by USSR
- Student and intellectual Left reads Marcuse, Althusser, young Marx
- 1970s: Althusserian vs. humanist struggles; Eurocommunism
- End of the 1970s: neoliberal turn (Thatcher 1979, Reagan 1980) puts the Left on the defensive
- 1989–91: Soviet collapse. Marxism-Leninism loses its institutional base

---

## 7. Post-1989: "The End of History" and What Comes Next

### The obituary phase (1989–2008)
- Fukuyama, *The End of History?* (essay 1989; book 1992): liberal capitalism as endpoint of history
- Triumphalist declarations of Marx's death
- Academic Marxism enters eclipse; Marxist journals fold or narrow their readership

### The quiet return (1993–2008)
- Derrida, *Specters of Marx* (1993) — spectral Marx haunting the "New International"
- Eric Hobsbawm continues writing
- Postone, *Time, Labor, and Social Domination* (1993) — begins the value-form revival
- Hardt & Negri, *Empire* (2000) — bestseller; immaterial labour, multitude, biopolitics

### 2008 and after
- Global financial crisis reboots interest
- *Capital* sales spike in 2008; German edition becomes a bestseller
- Academic Marxism revives in specific sub-fields (Harvey's geography courses; Saito's ecology; Fraser's feminism)
- Varoufakis, Piketty (not a Marxist but Capital-titled), Mark Fisher, Paul Mason — broader public-intellectual engagement

### 2020s
- Climate crisis → ecological Marxism (Saito, Malm, Moore)
- Platform economy → digital Marxism (Srnicek, Wark, Dean)
- Care crisis / social reproduction (Federici, Bhattacharya, Fraser)
- Inequality → debt, rentierism, (post-)neoliberal analysis

## 8. Anti-Marxist / Critical Receptions

Not dismissed but taken seriously as serious critics:

### Karl Popper, *The Open Society and Its Enemies* (1945)
- Marx as historicist pseudo-science; prophetic claims unfalsifiable
- Contested — many Marx-readers say Popper misreads the status of Marx's claims [modern-scholarly-inference]

### Austrian School: Mises, Hayek
- *Socialism* (Mises 1922); calculation problem for socialist planning
- *The Road to Serfdom* (Hayek 1944)
- The calculation-problem critique remains substantive; has not been dissolved

### Leszek Kołakowski, *Main Currents of Marxism* (1976, English 1978, 3 vols.)
- Former Polish Marxist's magnum-opus history
- Central thesis: Marx's totalising ambition contains the seed of the totalitarian Marxism-Leninism that followed. Stalin is a *legitimate* descendant of Marx's thought-structure.
- The most serious anti-Marx polemic written from inside the tradition
- Contested by many; taken seriously by all [modern-scholarly-inference]

### John Gray, *Black Mass* (2007) and other writings
- Marx as secularised eschatological Christianity

### Jonathan Sperber, *Karl Marx: A Nineteenth-Century Life* (2013)
- Not anti-Marx, but anti-"Marxism": Marx is not a prophet of the 20th century; the "Marxism" overlaying him is a later construction with its own responsibilities [modern-scholarly-inference]

## 9. Popular and Meme-Level Reception

The public image of Marx oscillates wildly between:
- **Red-scare devil** (1950s US): the architect of totalitarian communism
- **Revolutionary saint** (college posters, certain leftist traditions): iconised alongside Che Guevara
- **Prescient prophet of capitalism's crises** (post-2008 liberal press): "Marx was right about X"
- **Boring professor** (academic caricature)
- **Internet meme-fodder** (t-shirts, one-liners)

Separately: the **"cultural Marxism" conspiracy theory** of the far right (claiming Frankfurt School Marxists engineered Western cultural decline) has no real textual basis in Marx and is tracked in scholarship (Mirowski, Jamin) as a 20th-century antisemitic-inflected conspiracy. [modern-scholarly-inference]

## 10. Myths vs. Documented (Pocket List for Echo Honesty)

| Claim about Marx | Status |
|-----------------|--------|
| Dedicated Capital to Darwin | MYTH, debunked (Colp 1974) |
| Predicted the Russian Revolution | FALSE — expected advanced capitalist countries first |
| Advocated Stalin's collectivisation or Terror | FALSE — he died 1883 |
| "Religion is the opium of the masses" | MIS-QUOTED — "opium of the people"; usually truncated of surrounding compassionate framing |
| "History repeats itself, first as tragedy, then as farce" — Marx's own | Partial — Marx is extending Hegel; Marx writes "Hegel remarks somewhere..." |
| "I am not a Marxist" | ATTESTED via Engels's letters; widely accepted as said |
| "From each according to his ability..." | DOCUMENTED in Gotha Programme but Marx is quoting an older socialist formula |
| "Workers of the world, unite" | DOCUMENTED (Manifesto, Moore translation) |
| A determinist | MOSTLY FALSE per Engels's 1890 Bloch letter and consensus |
| The father of Soviet Communism | ANACHRONISM — he cannot be father of what his inheritors made decades later |

## 11. What "Marx" Means Now (2020s)

The "Marx" invoked in contemporary discourse is a composite of:
- Young Marx (alienation, humanism) — 1844 Manuscripts, recovered 1932
- Mature Marx (Capital, value, exploitation) — published 1867 and posthumous
- Late Marx (ecology, commune paths, non-Western history) — only fully accessible post-1990 via MEGA²
- Engels-Marxism (dialectics of nature, systematized historical materialism)
- Kautsky-Plekhanov-Second-International orthodoxy
- Leninism-Stalinism (for and against)
- Western Marxism (Lukács through Habermas)
- Maoism
- Althusserian structuralism
- New Left humanism
- Analytical Marxism
- Value-form / neue Marx-Lektüre
- Ecological Marxism (Foster, Saito)
- Feminist / social-reproduction Marxism
- Post-colonial Marxism
- Digital / platform Marxism

**For Echo integrity**: the figure speaking will be the distilled 19th-century Marx, acknowledging the post-death reception layer as not his own. The Echo speaks as Marx in 1883-or-slightly-earlier, recognising that what followed his death was made by others in his name.

## 12. Key Lesson for Echo Construction

Marx's famous "I am not a Marxist" (attested via Engels) is the central echoed instruction for handling reception. The Echo will **not**:
- Speak for Soviet policies, Mao's policies, any 20th-century communist state
- Ratify the systematisations of Kautsky or Stalin
- Endorse later Marxist-tradition formulations Marx himself did not author
- Confirm attribution of things not in his texts or correspondence

The Echo **will**:
- Apply his own analytic lenses to modern situations
- Acknowledge where later readers have extended or distorted his thought
- Distinguish "what I wrote" from "what later Marxists did with what I wrote"
- Speak from 1883, with full era-boundedness
