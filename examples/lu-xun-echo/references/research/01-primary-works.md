# Lu Xun — Primary Works (Research File 01)

**Research Agent 1 of 6 · Dimension: PRIMARY WORKS**
Subject: Lu Xun 鲁迅 (pen name of Zhou Shuren 周树人, 1881–1936)
Purpose: Enumerate the surviving textual corpus under Lu Xun's name — fiction, prose poetry, reminiscence, zawen, scholarship, translations, letters, diaries — and surface the recurring concepts that appear across ≥3 works and therefore qualify as structural features of his thinking rather than rhetorical flourishes.

**Citation labels used below:**
`[documented]` = present in a work Lu Xun himself signed.
`[attested]` = multiple independent contemporary sources converge.
`[attributed]` = later sources only.
`[modern-scholarly-inference]` = scholarly reconstruction from the documentary evidence.

---

## 0. Why Lu Xun's Corpus Is Unusually Well-Attested

Lu Xun wrote in the era of mass print, mass photography, and a rising professional publishing industry. Unlike ancient figures where textual transmission is a battlefield, for Lu Xun almost every serious text has: (a) an originally dated periodical appearance; (b) the author's own signed book-form collection; (c) a posthumous critical edition with dense philological annotation. The standard critical text — referenced throughout as **LXQJ** — is:

- 《鲁迅全集》 *Lǔ Xùn Quánjí* (Complete Works of Lu Xun), Beijing: 人民文学出版社 (People's Literature Publishing House), **2005**, **18 volumes**, approximately 7 million characters. [documented]
- The 2005 edition expanded from the 1981 16-volume edition by adding 23 previously unknown works and 20 previously unknown letters, and reorganized into: 10 volumes of creative/critical works, 4 volumes of letters, 3 volumes of diaries, and 1 index volume. [attested] (MCLC Resource Center bibliography; Chinese Text Project; Goodreads editions page)
- Prior editions: 1938 first collection (20 vols, Lu Xun Memorial Committee, overseen in part by Cai Yuanpei and Xu Guangping) [attested]; 1958 (10 vols); 1981 (16 vols, first post-Mao annotated scholarly edition); 1993 and 1996 reissues of the 1981 edition. [attested]

This research file is keyed primarily to the 2005 LXQJ; where English translations are cited, the translator and edition are noted.

**Sources cited in this section:** MCLC Resource Center "Lu Xun" bibliography (u.osu.edu/mclc/bibliographies/lit/lu-xun); Chinese Text Project entry on 魯迅全集 (ctext.org).

## 2. Prose Poetry: 《野草》 *Yěcǎo* "Wild Grass" (July 1927)
[documented]

- Published by 北新书局 Beixin Press, Shanghai, July 1927.
- **23 prose poems plus a "Foreword" (《题辞》 *Tící*)** — scholarly sources differ on the count: most say 23 prose poems, Nicholas Kaldis (*The Chinese Prose Poem: A Study of Lu Xun's Wild Grass*, Cambria 2014) counts "22 prose poems, a doggerel, and a foreword." The discrepancy is over whether 《我的失恋》 *Wǒ de Shīliàn* (a mock-satirical "doggerel") is counted alongside the prose poems proper.
- All first appeared serially in 《语丝》 *Yǔsī* "Threads of Talk" between December 1924 and April 1927. [attested]

**Contradiction preserved:** the count "23 prose poems" is standard in most reference works and in Lu Xun's own framing of the collection; Kaldis' "22 + doggerel + foreword" is a more precise genre-aware count. Do not harmonize.

**Lu Xun's own assessment:** he is reported (multiple interviews and letters collected in LXQJ vol. 13) to have said that his "entire philosophy is contained in *Yecao*." [attested] (Kaldis 2014; MCLC book review by Crespi 2015.)

Key pieces explicitly named in the brief:
- 《题辞》 *Tící* "Inscription" / "Foreword" (26 April 1927): "过去的生命已经死亡。我对于这死亡有大欢喜，因为我借此知道它曾经存活。" ("The life that is past has died. Of this death I have great joy, because through it I know it once lived.") [documented]
- 《影的告别》 *Yǐng de Gàobié* "The Shadow's Leave-taking" (24 September 1924).
- 《希望》 *Xīwàng* "Hope" (1 January 1925) — contains the paraphrase of Petőfi Sándor: **绝望之为虚妄，正与希望相同** (pinyin: *juéwàng zhī wéi xūwàng, zhèng yǔ xīwàng xiāngtóng*), rendered by Yang/Yang as "Despair, like hope, is but vanity." [documented] The line appears twice in the poem — at the hinge and again at the close — which is itself evidence of its structural importance to Lu Xun.
- 《过客》 *Guòkè* "The Passer-by" (2 March 1925) — a short play-in-prose, the only piece in *Wild Grass* in dramatic form, with three speakers: an old man, a girl, and a traveler who does not know where he came from or where he is going, only that he must continue walking. [documented] Widely read as Lu Xun's most direct Nietzsche/Zarathustra reflex. [modern-scholarly-inference] (Kaldis; Kowallis *Lyrical Lu Xun* 1996.)
- 《死火》 *Sǐhuǒ* "Dead Fire" (25 April 1925).
- 《这样的战士》 *Zhèyàng de Zhànshì* "Such a Fighter" (14 December 1925) — the figure who throws his spear into nothingness because his enemies have hidden themselves in "good reputations" and "scholarly gowns." [documented]
- 《墓碣文》 *Mùjiéwén* "Epitaph" (17 June 1925): the corpse sits up in the grave, its lips do not move but it speaks. [documented]

English translations: Yang Hsien-yi & Gladys Yang, *Wild Grass* (Foreign Languages Press, 1974); Eileen J. Cheng, *Wild Grass and Morning Blossoms Gathered at Dusk* (Harvard University Press, 2022 — this is now the standard scholarly English edition).

## 3. Reminiscence: 《朝花夕拾》 *Zhāohuā Xíshí* "Dawn Blossoms Plucked at Dusk" (September 1928)
[documented]

- Published by 未名社 Weiming She, Beijing, September 1928. Originally serialized in 《莽原》 *Mǎngyuán* during 1926 under the working title 《旧事重提》 *Jiùshì Chóng Tí* "Bringing Up Old Matters" (10 essays); retitled to *Zhaohua Xishi* for book publication.
- 10 autobiographical/reminiscence essays written Feb–Nov 1926 plus a *Xiaoyin* 小引 "Little Preface" (4 May 1927) and a *Houji* 后记 "Postscript."
- Setting: childhood Shaoxing, student years in Nanjing (Jiangnan Naval Academy → Mining and Railways School), and medical studies in Japan (Kobun Institute, Sendai Medical School).

Essays of particular importance for the Echo:
- 《从百草园到三味书屋》 *Cóng Bǎicǎoyuán dào Sānwèi Shūwū* "From Hundred Plants Garden to Three Flavors Study"
- 《藤野先生》 *Téngyě Xiānsheng* "Mr. Fujino" — his anatomy professor in Sendai. Lu Xun "uncharacteristically ends that story with a note of sentimentality" (Wikipedia; scholarly consensus). This essay narrates the **magic lantern / slide incident (幻灯片事件)** of January 1906: during bacteriology class at Sendai Medical School, Lu Xun saw a slide of a Chinese prisoner about to be executed by a Japanese soldier in Manchuria during the Russo-Japanese War, captioned "Russian spy." He described his Japanese classmates cheering "banzai" and his realization that his countrymen in the image were just as impassive as spectators — this is the origin scene of the **看客** *kànkè* "spectator/onlooker" figure that will recur throughout his fiction. [documented — per Lu Xun's own account] (APJJF Christopher Robins; "Lantern Slide Moments," Duke positions 23:1.)

**Historiographical caveat:** the slide incident as Lu Xun narrates it in 1926 cannot be independently verified from 1906 Sendai records, and some Japanese scholars (Takeuchi Yoshimi, then Sun Ge following) read it as a constructed "origin myth" reshaped in retrospect. Preserved as a contradiction: the incident's **experiential truth** and its **literary-origin-myth function** are not the same claim. [modern-scholarly-inference]

- 《范爱农》 *Fàn Àinóng* — his Shaoxing friend whose drowning death Lu Xun treats as a Xun Yang figure of failure-in-revolution.

## 4. Zawen 杂文 (Essay) Collections

Lu Xun compiled his own essays in his lifetime and by death (19 October 1936) had assembled roughly 16 volumes. Zawen is a genre he effectively made definitive: polemical, occasional, short, intellectually weaponized, "pen as javelin" (the title of Xudong Zhang's study of the form). Over 800 pieces published 1918–1936, many under rotating pseudonyms to evade KMT censorship. [attested]

### 4.1 Key individual zawen with their source collections

- **《写在〈坟〉后面》** "Written After Graves" (November 1926, collected in 《坟》) — origin of **中间物** *zhōngjiānwù* "intermediate thing / in-between thing," Lu Xun's self-positioning as transitional, neither destination nor origin. [documented] "一切事物，在转变中，是总有多少中间物的" ("In all things, in their transitions, there are always some intermediate things"). (Translation discussed in Lyell, Ban Wang, Theodore Huters — rendered variously as "thing in between" / "intermediate forms.")

- **《论"费厄泼赖"应该缓行》** *Lùn "Fèi'è Pōlài" Yīnggāi Huǎnxíng* "On Deferring Fair Play" (29 December 1925, in 《莽原》; collected in 《坟》) — contains the **痛打落水狗** *tòngdǎ luòshuǐgǒu* "beat the dog that has fallen into the water" argument: do not extend magnanimity to defeated oppressors, because the "dog" will climb back onto the bank, shake off the water, and bite again. [documented] Written in direct response to Lin Yutang's advocacy of British-style "fair play."

- **《拿来主义》** *Nálaizhǔyì* "Take-ism" / "Grabism" (7 June 1934, in 《中华日报·动向》; collected in 《且介亭杂文》) — programmatic statement on cultural borrowing: neither the 闭关主义 "closed-door" nor the 送去主义 "send-away" (present-China-exports-only) nor passive 送来 "being-sent-things," but **拿来** "take" — select aggressively and critically. (English translations: Yang/Yang "Take-ism"; more recent scholars use "Grab-ism" or "Grabism" — Shiping Hua, *Comparative Literature Studies* 57:1, 2020.) [documented]

- **《中国人失掉自信力了吗》** *Zhōngguórén Shīdiào Zìxìnlì le ma* "Have the Chinese Lost Their Self-Confidence?" (25 September 1934, 《太白》; 《且介亭杂文》). Written in the cultural-crisis debates of May–September 1934 when over 100 articles appeared in Shanghai press on whether Chinese civilization had collapsed. [attested, The China Story yearbook 2017] Contains the **脊梁** *jǐliáng* "spine/backbone" passage: "我们自古以来，就有埋头苦干的人，有拼命硬干的人，有为民请命的人，有舍身求法的人...这就是中国的脊梁" ("We have, from ancient times, people who work with their heads down, people who risk their lives doing hard things, people who plead for the common folk, people who seek dharma at the cost of the body... this is the spine of China"). [documented]

- **《无声的中国》** *Wúshēng de Zhōngguó* "Silent China" (February 1927 Hong Kong speech at YMCA, printed in *The Overseas* magazine; collected in 《三闲集》) — on the necessity of breaking out of literary-Chinese into vernacular so that China can have a voice. [documented]

- **《魏晋风度及文章与药及酒之关系》** *Wèi-Jìn Fēngdù jí Wénzhāng yǔ Yào jí Jiǔ zhī Guānxi* "The Demeanor, Literature, and their Relation to Drugs and Wine in the Wei-Jin Period" (July–September 1927, Guangzhou XiadaHuang speech; collected in 《而已集》) — his most extended exposition of his affinity for the Wei-Jin iconoclasts 嵇康 Ji Kang and 阮籍 Ruan Ji, and a self-portrait under a historical mask. [documented, LXQJ 2005 vol. 3]

- **《我怎么做起小说来》** *Wǒ Zěnme Zuòqǐ Xiǎoshuō Lái* "How I Came to Write Fiction" (March 1933; 《南腔北调集》) — Lu Xun's own statement on method: he read "mostly short stories, and especially those of oppressed nations... Russian, Polish, and small-Balkan-nation." [documented]

- **《为了忘却的记念》** *Wèile Wàngquè de Jìniàn* "In Memory of Forgetting" (February 1933; 《南腔北调集》) — memorial for the Five Martyrs of the Left-Wing League executed by the KMT in 1931, including Rou Shi. [documented]

## 5. Scholarly Works

- 《中国小说史略》 *Zhōngguó Xiǎoshuō Shǐlüè* "A Brief History of Chinese Fiction." Draft in two parts issued December 1923 and June 1924 by 北大第一院新潮社 Peking University Xinchao Society; combined single volume 1925, reprinted five times; revised edition 1930. Lu Xun taught the Peking University fiction-history course 1920–1924 and it grew from those lecture notes. First comprehensive scholarly survey of traditional Chinese fiction ever published in China. Translated into Japanese, Korean, German; English translation by Yang Xianyi and Gladys Yang, Foreign Languages Press, 1959. [attested] (Wikipedia A Brief History of Chinese Fiction; MCLC bibliography.)

- 《汉文学史纲要》 *Hàn Wénxué Shǐ Gāngyào* "Essentials of the History of Han Dynasty Literature." Compiled from Lu Xun's lecture notes for the course he taught at 厦门大学 Xiamen University and later 中山大学 Sun Yat-sen University, Guangzhou, in 1926–1927. **Published posthumously** in 1938 as part of the first 《鲁迅全集》. [attested] Ends at the Sima Xiangru / Sima Qian era — never completed beyond the Han.

- 《唐宋传奇集》 *Táng Sòng Chuánqí Jí* "Collected Tang and Song Romances" (1927–28, 北新书局) — a scholarly anthology of chuanqi tales with Lu Xun's editing and textual notes. [documented]

- 《古小说钩沉》 *Gǔ Xiǎoshuō Gōuchén* "Ancient Fiction: Retrieved from the Deep" — a life-project compilation of lost pre-Tang zhiguai and xiaoshuo fragments collated from 唐宋类书 encyclopedias. Never published in his lifetime; released posthumously 1938. [attested]

## 6. Translations

Lu Xun translated as much as he wrote original fiction — **rough equivalent volume**, per *Lu Xun yiwen ji* 《鲁迅译文集》 (10 vols, 人民文学 1959) and the 2008 《鲁迅译文全集》 (8 vols, 福建教育). [attested]

Major translations, chronologically:

- 1903 — **《月界旅行》** (Jules Verne, *De la Terre à la Lune*, via Japanese) — Lu Xun's first published translation, literary-Chinese, rendered as a 回 chapter-novel with chapter-title couplets. [documented]
- 1903 — **《地底旅行》** (Jules Verne, *Voyage au centre de la Terre*, via Japanese). [documented]
- 1907–08 — early science/essay translations, incl. material derivative of Haeckel's evolutionary monism. [attested]
- 1909 — **《域外小说集》** *Yùwài Xiǎoshuō Jí* "Stories from Abroad" (2 vols), co-translated with his brother Zhou Zuoren, Tokyo. Included Andreyev, Garshin, Sienkiewicz, Poe, and Eastern European writers. Print run 1,500; only about 41 copies sold at the original Tokyo and Shanghai outlets. A commercial failure that became a programmatic statement. [attested] (Lawrence Wang-chi Wong, *Asia Pacific Translation and Intercultural Studies* 1:3, 2014.)
- 1921 — enlarged re-edition of *Yuwai Xiaoshuo Ji* adding 21 pieces (37 total). [attested]
- 1922 — **《一个青年的梦》** (Musha-no-koji Saneatsu *Aru seinen no yume*, from Japanese). [documented]
- 1922 — translations of Vasili Eroshenko's Esperanto fables (他从日文、世界语转译). [documented]
- 1923 — **《现代日本小说集》** *Xiàndài Rìběn Xiǎoshuō Jí* "Contemporary Japanese Fiction Collection" (Commercial Press), co-edited with Zhou Zuoren, including work by Natsume Sōseki, Mori Ōgai, Shiga Naoya, Arishima Takeo, **Akutagawa Ryūnosuke**. [attested]
- 1924 — **《苦闷的象征》** (Kuriyagawa Hakuson *Kumon no shōchō*, "Symbol of Anguish"). [documented]
- 1925 — **《出了象牙之塔》** (Kuriyagawa Hakuson, *Zōge no tō o dete*). [documented]
- Late 1920s–1930s — extensive Russian/Soviet program: Gogol **《死魂灵》** *Dead Souls* (unfinished at death, from German via Hermann Röhl; Lu Xun's last major project); Chekhov stories; Andreyev; **Fadeyev *The Rout*** 《毁灭》 *Huǐmiè* (1931, from Japanese — a politically pivotal translation for the left); Gorky; Lunacharsky; **Plekhanov** *Art and Social Life* and other aesthetic-materialist theory. [attested] (Sun Yu, *Lu Xun and Russia*, Routledge 2024; CUHK ICS journal vol 22.)

**Translation doctrine:** 硬译 *yìngyì* "hard translation" — preserve foreign syntactic shape rather than domesticate. Theoretically defended in 《"硬译"与"文学的阶级性"》 (1930, in 《二心集》) against Liang Shiqiu. [documented]

## 7. Early Classical-Chinese Essays from the Japan Period (1907–1908)

All first published in 《河南》 *Hénán*, a Tongmenghui-sponsored student journal in Tokyo. Written in literary Chinese (文言 wenyan) of a deliberately archaic, Zhang Binglin-influenced register. These are Lu Xun's first serious original essays and contain the kernels of ideas he will develop for 30 years. Banned/suppressed by Qing pressure on Japan before the sequel could appear. [attested] (Kowallis *Warriors of the Spirit*; UNSW thesis on *Henan*; MCLC.)

- 《人之历史》 *Rén zhī Lìshǐ* "The History of Humankind" (*Henan* #1, 1907) — on Haeckel/Darwin and the "monist" evolutionary lineage of humankind.
- 《科学史教篇》 *Kēxuéshǐ Jiàopiān* "Lessons from the History of Science" (*Henan* #5, 1908).
- 《文化偏至论》 *Wénhuà Piānzhì Lùn* "On Imbalanced Cultural Development" (*Henan* #7, August 1908). Contains the **立人** *lìrén* "establish-the-person" program: "故将生存两间，角逐列国是务，其首在立人，人立而后凡事举" (roughly: "To exist in the world and contend among nations, the first task is to establish the person; once the person stands, all undertakings follow"). [documented, LXQJ 2005 vol. 1, p. 46 ff.] The essay argues China's Westernizing reformers misidentify Western strength — it is not parliaments, commerce, ironclads, railways or mines, but **people** (*gēndǐ zài rén* 根柢在人). English: Kowallis 2012.
- 《摩罗诗力说》 *Móluó Shīlì Shuō* "On the Power of Mara Poetry" (*Henan* #2–3, February–March 1908). A comparative study of Byron, Shelley, Pushkin, Lermontov, Mickiewicz, Słowacki, Petőfi — the "Mara" (demon) poets of revolt. Central for understanding Lu Xun's Romanticism-via-Nietzsche-via-Byron inheritance. Translated by Kowallis in *Warriors of the Spirit* and by Shu-mei Shih. [documented] (Kowallis, *Frontiers of Literary Studies in China* 6:3, 2012.)
- 《破恶声论》 *Pò Èshēng Lùn* "Refuting Malevolent Voices" (*Henan* #8, December 1908) — unfinished. Attack on scientism and on jingoistic anti-religious rhetoric of the day; defense of "heart voice" (*xinsheng* 心声). [documented]

---

## 8. Letters

- **《两地书》** *Liǎngdì Shū* "Letters Between Two Places" (April 1933, 青光书局 Qingguang Books, Shanghai) — published correspondence between Lu Xun and **许广平** Xu Guangping, covering 1925–1929 across three geographic separations (Beijing–Beijing, Xiamen–Guangzhou, Shanghai–Beijing). Heavily edited by the authors themselves: Lu Xun and Xu Guangping rewrote, omitted, and reordered to make the publication "respectable." [attested] (Bonnie McDougall, *Love-Letters and Privacy in Modern China*, Oxford 2002; a facsimile of the unedited original manuscripts was published in 1996 with preface by their son Zhou Haiying.) The 1933 book and the 1996 manuscript facsimile are thus **two overlapping but different** documentary records — do not conflate.
- Other letters — more than 1,400 survive, collected in LXQJ 2005 vols. 11–14.

---

## 9. Diaries

- **《鲁迅日记》** *Lǔ Xùn Rìjì*, **5 May 1912 – 18 October 1936** (the day before his death). Almost complete; gaps of a few months in 1922 where the original manuscript was lost and reconstructed partially. Primarily terse, functional: weather, visitors received, books bought, money received/sent, health — not a confessional diary. Essential for prosopography and for dating of individual works. Collected in LXQJ 2005 vols. 15–17. [attested]

## 10. Speeches

Lu Xun gave dozens of public speeches between 1923 and 1936, mostly transcribed by others and subsequently reviewed/edited by him. Collected in various zawen volumes. Key examples:

- April 1923, Beijing Women's Normal University: **《娜拉走后怎样》** *Nàlā Zǒuhòu Zěnyàng* "What Happens After Nora Walks Out?" — on the Ibsen problem for Chinese women (《坟》). [documented]
- February 1927, Hong Kong YMCA: 《无声的中国》 "Silent China"; 《老调子已经唱完》 "The Old Tune is Finished." [documented]
- July–September 1927, Guangzhou (Zhongshan University / Xiadu summer session): **《魏晋风度及文章与药及酒之关系》** Wei-Jin speech. [documented]

---

## 11. Poetry

Lu Xun wrote classical-style verse across his entire life, from childhood through the final year. Roughly **60+ old-style poems (*jiuti shi* 旧体诗)** survive — most in *lüshi* regulated verse and *jueju* quatrains, a few *guti* "ancient style" and *saoti* (*Chuci* mode). He also wrote a small number of modern-style new poems (《新青年》 period) but largely disowned them. [attested] (Kowallis, *The Lyrical Lu Xun: A Study of His Classical-Style Verse*, University of Hawaii Press, 1996 — the standard English study.)

Famous examples: 《自题小像》 *Zì Tí Xiǎoxiàng* "Inscribed on a Small Portrait" (1903, containing 我以我血荐轩辕 "I offer my blood to the Yellow Emperor"); 《自嘲》 *Zì Cháo* "Self-Mockery" (1932, containing 横眉冷对千夫指，俯首甘为孺子牛 "brows horizontal, I stare coldly at a thousand accusing fingers; head lowered, I willingly serve as an ox for the children" — the two lines later canonized by Mao in "Talks at Yan'an").

## 12. Recurring Concepts (Appearing ≥3 Times Across the Corpus)

These are not rhetorical flourishes but structural features of Lu Xun's thinking, confirmed by cross-corpus frequency. Each is [documented] unless noted.

| Concept | Chinese | Representative Loci |
|---|---|---|
| Iron house | 铁屋子 *tiěwūzi* | 《呐喊·自序》 (preface, December 1922); echoed in 《野草·希望》 and 《华盖集·忽然想到》. |
| National character | 国民性 *guómínxìng* | 《藤野先生》; *Nahan* preface; many zawen 1925–1934; letters to Xu Guangping 1925. |
| Establish the person | 立人 *lìrén* | 《文化偏至论》 1908 (**locus classicus**); 《热风·随感录四十》; *Letters from Two Places*. |
| Spectator / onlooker | 看客 *kànkè* / 看 vs. 被看 | magic lantern narrative in 《藤野先生》; fictional scenes in 《药》, 《祝福》, 《示众》, 《阿Q正传》 (the execution finale). (Modern-scholarly-inference on "看/被看" as a structuring grammar: Wang Hui, 《反抗绝望》 *Resisting Despair*, 1990 / rev. 2000.) |
| Cannibalism | 吃人 *chīrén* | 《狂人日记》 — the madman finds "吃人" written between every line of the history books; repeated in 《灯下漫笔》 (*Fen*), 《我之节烈观》, and as metaphor throughout *Panghuang*. |
| Intermediate thing | 中间物 *zhōngjiānwù* | 《写在〈坟〉后面》 1926; 《二心集》 prefaces; letters 1933–1935. |
| Despair-hope identity | 绝望与希望 | 《野草·希望》 (Petőfi paraphrase, doubled); 《华盖集·北京通信》; 《两地书》 1925 letters. |
| Take-ism | 拿来主义 | 《拿来主义》 1934; implicit doctrine of 硬译 "hard translation"; 《论"旧形式的采用"》. |
| Beat the fallen dog | 痛打落水狗 | 《论"费厄泼赖"应该缓行》 1925 (**locus classicus**); reprise in 《答托洛斯基派的信》 1936. |
| Tenacity | 韧 *rèn* / 韧性 | 《两地书》 4, 1925; 《华盖集·这个与那个》; 《对于〈新潮〉一部分的意见》; "要运用脑髓，放出眼光... 韧的战斗"。 |
| Spine/backbone | 脊梁 *jǐliang* | 《中国人失掉自信力了吗》 1934; echoed 《这样的战士》. |
| Wandering | 彷徨 *pánghuáng* | title of second collection; quoted from Qu Yuan's *Lisao* as its epigraph: 路漫漫其修远兮，吾将上下而求索. |
| Ghostly qi | 鬼气 *guǐqì* | self-description in 《两地书》; atmospheric register throughout *Yecao* (《墓碣文》, 《死后》, 《颓败线的颤动》). |
| Cold | 冷 *lěng* | recurring temperature-word — 《药》 "cold wind on the execution ground"; 《孤独者》; 《复仇》; "横眉冷对千夫指"; Kaldis 2014 traces its systematicity in *Yecao*. |

## 13. Cited Influences (Lu Xun on Lu Xun)

Influences Lu Xun himself acknowledges in his own prose:

- **Nietzsche** — especially *Also sprach Zarathustra*, which he paraphrases in 1907–08 *Henan* essays and in 《过客》 *The Passer-by*. He translated parts of the "Zarathustra's Prologue" into classical Chinese (1918, in *Xin Qingnian*) and again into vernacular (1920). [documented]
- **Petőfi Sándor** — named in 《希望》 for the "despair is vain as hope is vain" line; Lu Xun had collected materials to write his biography. [documented, *Yecao*]
- **Gogol** — *Diary of a Madman* is explicitly a response to Gogol's Записки сумасшедшего; Lu Xun translated Gogol's *Dead Souls* as his final project. [documented]
- **Andreyev, Chekhov, Gorky, Fadeyev** — translated and repeatedly cited. [documented]
- **Byron, Shelley, Mickiewicz, Słowacki, Pushkin, Lermontov** — all named in 《摩罗诗力说》 1908. [documented]
- **嵇康 Ji Kang, 阮籍 Ruan Ji** (Wei-Jin iconoclasts) — subject of his 1927 Guangzhou speech; 《古小说钩沉》 assembles their fragmentary prose. Lu Xun edited 《嵇康集》 across 1913–1935, ten major revisions. [documented]
- **章炳麟 Zhang Binglin** — his teacher in Tokyo 1908–09; Lu Xun attended Zhang's Shuowen lectures and his pre-Song philological archaism is Zhang's gift. Memorialized in Lu Xun's final essay 《关于太炎先生二三事》 (October 1936, 《且介亭杂文末编》, published posthumously). [documented]
- **陶渊明 Tao Yuanming** — quoted repeatedly; Lu Xun insists on the "hidden steel" in Tao against the received image of the serene hermit. 《"题未定"草》 in 《且介亭杂文二集》 is the central text. [documented]
- **严复 Yan Fu's 《天演论》** *Tiānyǎnlùn* (T.H. Huxley's *Evolution and Ethics* adapted/translated, 1898) — Lu Xun in 《琐记》 (*Dawn Blossoms*) describes reading it as a teenager in Nanjing and memorizing passages. Core source of his early Darwinism. [documented]
- **Max Stirner** — cited in 《文化偏至论》 as one of the "individualist" thinkers whose kind of 自性 individuality China lacks. [documented]

## 14. Contradictions, Gaps, and Things Not to Harmonize

1. **Count of *Yecao* pieces:** 23 (standard / Lu Xun's own framing) vs. Kaldis' "22 + doggerel + foreword." Preserve.
2. **The magic-lantern slide incident:** Lu Xun's 1926 narrative vs. Takeuchi Yoshimi / Sun Ge / Christopher Robins skepticism about whether the specific slide ever existed in the Sendai 1906 curriculum. Preserve the literary-origin-myth reading alongside the experiential claim.
3. **Pen name "Lu Xun":** stated to honor his mother (surname 鲁 Lu) per multiple biographical sources, but Lu Xun himself gave varying explanations in different letters — do not flatten.
4. **Translation of 拿来主义:** "Take-ism" (Yang/Yang) vs. "Grabism" (more recent translators) vs. "Fetch-ism." English rendering is contested; the Chinese term is unambiguous.
5. **《两地书》 vs. the unedited manuscripts:** the 1933 published book and the 1996 facsimile manuscripts are different documents. Scholarly inference about the relationship should use both.
6. **Han dynasty literary history:** 《汉文学史纲要》 is a lecture-notes posthumous edition; it stops mid-Han and is not a completed monograph. Treat accordingly.
7. **Lu Xun's assessment "my philosophy is in *Yecao*":** this is widely quoted and attributed in multiple LXQJ-era interview/letter sources, but the exact textual locus is disputed among Lu Xun scholars; treat as [attested] rather than [documented] in the narrow sense.
