# Lu Xun — Research Dimension 02: Contemporary Records

**Scope.** Same-generation accounts of Zhou Shuren 周树人 (pen name Lu Xun 鲁迅, 1881–1936): eyewitness observations, peer correspondence, hostile portraits by adversaries, institutional records, and the physical-presence anecdotes that survive in reminiscence literature. This dimension treats each witness as a perspective that must be triangulated, not a transparent window.

**Evidentiary labels used throughout:**

- **[documented]** — survives in a dated primary document (diary entry, letter, official register, contemporaneous newspaper)
- **[attested]** — reported by one or more named witnesses in memoir form, generally accepted
- **[attributed]** — a specific statement/scene ascribed to Lu Xun by one witness only, not otherwise corroborated
- **[legendary]** — canonical in biographical tradition but thinly sourced; possibly shaped by retrospection
- **[modern-scholarly-inference]** — reconstruction by post-1980 scholarship; not a direct witness claim

---

## 1. The Brothers: Zhou Zuoren and Zhou Jianren

### Zhou Zuoren 周作人 (1885–1967), the estranged younger brother

Zhou Zuoren produced the most extensive brotherly witness corpus on Lu Xun, across three late works written after the 1923 break and after his own collaboration with the Japanese occupation regime had ruined his reputation: 《鲁迅的故家》 *Lu Xun's Ancestral Home* (1953), 《鲁迅小说里的人物》 *The Characters in Lu Xun's Fiction* (1954), and 《知堂回想录》 *Zhitang Reminiscences* (written 1960–62, published Hong Kong 1970). [documented]

**Bias to flag.** Zhou Zuoren writes about a brother he has not spoken to since 1923, while himself branded a *hanjian* (traitor) in PRC public memory. His memoirs are simultaneously (a) uniquely close, recovering Shaoxing childhood detail no one else preserved — local customs, the T'u-ku-tz'u opera that fed into 《社戏》, the Shaoxing yamen clerk-household that became the 鲁镇 of Lu Xun's fiction; and (b) systematically de-emotionalized — he refuses to describe the brothers' break and generally keeps himself out of the frame. Modern scholars (Susan Daruvala, *Zhou Zuoren and an Alternative Chinese Response to Modernity*, Harvard 2000; David Pollard, *The True Story of Lu Xun*, 2002) read this restraint as a strategy of survival, not as an absence of feeling. [modern-scholarly-inference]

**Load-bearing details Zhou Zuoren preserves:**

- The 1893 disgrace of their grandfather Zhou Fuqing 周福清 in an imperial-exam bribery scandal, which began the Zhou household's financial collapse and sent the adolescent Lu Xun on the pawn-shop-to-pharmacy route his 《呐喊·自序》 describes. [attested — Zhou Zuoren, corroborated by Xu Shoushang]
- Their father's long illness and the parade of traditional physicians with "peculiar" prescription-ingredients (the pair of mating crickets, the three-years-accumulated cane-sugar), which Lu Xun fictionalized in 《父亲的病》. [attested — Zhou Zuoren's retelling is the principal independent corroboration of Lu Xun's own 1926 essay]
- Childhood reading habits, the Sanwei bookshop, the copying of illustrated books — Zhou Zuoren is the witness who confirms that the stories of Lu Xun's early visual-imagination life in 《朝花夕拾》 are substantially factual rather than literary composition. [attested]

### Zhou Jianren 周建人 (1888–1984), the youngest brother

The biologist and later CCP vice-chairman of the NPC Standing Committee, Zhou Jianren remained loyal to Lu Xun throughout, took the elder brother's side in the 1923 break, and wrote 《鲁迅故家的败落》 *The Decline of Lu Xun's Ancestral Home* (1984) and shorter memoirs collected in 《鲁迅回忆录》 vols 1–3 (Beijing Publishing House, 1999). [documented]

**Bias to flag.** Zhou Jianren wrote most of his Lu Xun memoirs in the Mao and post-Mao PRC, when Lu Xun had been canonized as the 国魂 and Jianren himself held senior state office. The political incentive to produce a heroic elder-brother portrait is substantial; the later reminiscences should be read as political hagiography even where the underlying facts are plausible. [modern-scholarly-inference — Eva Chou, *Memory, Violence, Queues: Lu Xun Interprets China*, 2012]

## 2. Xu Guangping 许广平 (1898–1968) — partner from 1927

Formerly his student at Beijing Women's Normal University; became his common-law wife in Shanghai from October 1927; mother of their only child Zhou Haiying 周海婴 (b. 27 September 1929). [documented]

### The textual situation

Her reminiscences exist in multiple strata, and they do not all say the same thing:

1. 《欣慰的纪念》 *Reminiscences of Consolation* (1951) — a collection of essays Xu wrote in 1937–1950, while she lived on Lu Xun's royalties in occupied Shanghai and after. Relatively close to the events. [documented]
2. 《关于鲁迅的生活》 *On Lu Xun's Life* (1954) — short-form essays, still mostly factual.
3. 《鲁迅回忆录》 *Memoir of Lu Xun* (published May 1961) — the controversial long-form. Written 1959–60 during the Great Leap Forward and early famine, under political pressure to present Lu Xun as a proto-Maoist thinker. [documented]
4. The 2008-recovered original manuscript of (3), published as 《鲁迅回忆录》(手稿本) after Zhou Haiying located the pre-edit draft — showing that the 1961 version had been revised by an editorial committee to align with the CCP's 1957–60 line on Lu Xun. [documented — see 朱正, 周海婴 preface, 长江文艺出版社 2010 手稿本; also Lü Qi, "许广平《鲁迅回忆录》的手稿本与刊行本比较" 《鲁迅研究月刊》2010]

**Bias pattern.** Comparing the three strata is now a small sub-field. The 1961 edition exaggerates Lu Xun's closeness to the party, smooths over his conflicts with Zhou Yang (the "Two Slogans" quarrel — see §8 below), and muffles any hint of domestic tension. The original manuscript preserves more ordinary-life texture and sharper disagreements. Scholars (e.g. Eva Chou; 陈漱渝 Chen Shuyu in his 2011 *鲁研月刊* pieces) now routinely cite the 手稿本 in preference to the 1961 text. [modern-scholarly-inference]

### Detail that only Xu Guangping gives

- His sleep pattern: working through the night, writing by lamplight into the small hours, sleeping from roughly 04:00 to early afternoon. [attested — 《欣慰的纪念》]
- His chain-smoking — "qianmen" brand, about fifty cigarettes a day at his worst, which she explicitly connects (anachronistically, given 1930s medical knowledge) to his terminal lung disease. [attested]
- His coffin-savings discipline: even when earning substantial royalties in Shanghai, he kept the household to the frugality level of a Beijing section-chief; the lived space in the Dalu Xincun 大陆新村 house on Shanyin Road was not imposing. [attested, corroborated independently by Uchiyama Kanzō and Xiao Hong]

## 3. Xu Shoushang 许寿裳 (1883–1948) — lifelong friend

Met Lu Xun at Tokyo's Kōbun Gakuin 弘文学院 in 1902; witnessed Lu Xun cut off his Manchu-era queue not long after Xu had cut his own. [documented, in Xu's 《亡友鲁迅印象记》 *Impressions of My Departed Friend Lu Xun*, written 1946–47, published Shanghai 1947 then Beijing 1953 by 人民文学]

Of all the witnesses, Xu Shoushang has the longest continuous acquaintance — Tokyo 1902 → Ministry of Education 1912 → Beijing University 1920s → Sun Yat-sen University Guangzhou 1927 → Taipei 1946, where he was assassinated in 1948 in unresolved circumstances.

**Specifically Xu-only testimony:**

- The exact circumstance of the 1903 queue-cutting and Lu Xun's gift-photograph to Xu inscribed with the 自题小像 *Self-Inscribed Portrait* couplet 我以我血荐轩辕 ("I dedicate my blood to the Yellow Emperor"). [documented — Xu preserved the photograph itself]
- Corroboration of the Fujino-sensei relationship in Sendai: Xu confirms that Lu Xun kept Fujino's corrected lecture notebooks in a trunk for the rest of his life. [attested]
- The dynamic of the 1912–1918 Beijing years: Xu Shoushang describes the younger Zhou Shuren he knew in Tokyo as verbally aggressive, argumentative, capable of being funny; the older Ministry-of-Education Zhou Shuren he knew in Beijing as withdrawn, eating alone, copying inscriptions, mostly silent. The transformation is Xu's. [attested]

**Bias to flag.** Xu wrote in post-war Shanghai shortly before being killed in Taipei — there is a visible element of eulogy, and he has no surviving witness to contradict his account of the Tokyo years. But he is the most linguistically unguarded of the memoirists: he neither over-revolutionary-izes Lu Xun (as Feng Xuefeng does) nor over-sentimentalizes (as Xiao Hong's stylistic tendency might).

## 4. Xiao Hong 萧红 (1911–1942) — the intimate household observer

The Manchurian novelist took refuge in the Lu Xun household in Shanghai 1934–1936 with Xiao Jun 萧军. Her 《回忆鲁迅先生》 *Reminiscences of Mr. Lu Xun*, written 1939 in Chongqing and published 1940 in Hong Kong by 妇女生活社, is universally regarded as the most observationally precise witness text, in part because Xiao Hong had no political stake in portraying Lu Xun a particular way. [documented]

**The physical-presence detail.** Xiao Hong gives the canonical portrait:

- The "inch-short" 平头 brush-cut hair, thick eyebrows, short dark mustache. [attested — 《回忆鲁迅先生》 opening]
- A short man (Lu Xun was roughly 1.60 m / 5'3") — Xiao Hong notes his small physical stature repeatedly, contrasted against the weight of his moral presence. [attested]
- Penetrating eyes, slow movement up the stairs to his second-floor study, the long gown even in summer. [attested]
- The walk — "like a cat without shoes," Xiao Hong wrote; he moved quietly and could enter a room before anyone noticed. [attested]
- The laugh — loud and open, not the laugh one expects from the serious author of 《狂人日记》. She gives the famous image of Lu Xun laughing so hard at Xiao Hong's joke about a red jacket that he could not hold his cigarette straight. [attested — this scene is the single best-known specific anecdote about his laughter]
- His clothing criticism: when Xiao Hong asked him whether her red jacket looked good, he said no, it did not, and went into a five-minute analysis of what colors suited what skin-tones — showing a precise, not casual, visual sensibility. [attributed — Xiao Hong is the only source for this exchange]
- His smoking — incessant, the green tin of cigarettes on his desk always there; ash marks on the manuscripts. [attested, corroborated by Xu Guangping and Uchiyama]
- His food — modest, Shaoxing-style; he disliked Cantonese-sweet dishes. Xiao Hong notes he ate quickly, without much appetite. [attested]

**Bias to flag.** Xiao Hong adored Lu Xun and wrote the memoir after his death, during her own deteriorating health. It is a love-letter as much as a witness document, and it aestheticizes the daily routine. But it does not *distort* in the political sense that Xu Guangping's 1961 edition does — its bias is toward preservation and intimacy, not policy.

## 5. The Japanese friend: Uchiyama Kanzō 内山完造 (1885–1959)

Japanese proprietor of the Uchiyama Bookstore 内山书店 on North Sichuan Road in Shanghai's Hongkou district. Met Lu Xun in October 1927 when Lu Xun first walked into the shop after moving to Shanghai; they remained close until Lu Xun's death in October 1936. Uchiyama was present at the death itself. [documented]

His memoir 《鲁迅先生》(published in Japanese as 魯迅の思い出 1947, and in Chinese sections within his 1950 《花甲录》) is the principal non-Chinese witness record. See Joshua Fogel, *A Friend in Deed: Lu Xun, Uchiyama Kanzō, and the Intellectual World of Shanghai on the Eve of War* (Association for Asian Studies, 2019), which synthesizes and critically re-reads Uchiyama's evidence. [documented]

**Uchiyama-only testimony:**

- Lu Xun's use of the bookstore as a safe-house during KMT surveillance periods: after the 1931 arrest of the Left League Five Martyrs (see §9), Lu Xun hid in an upper room or a nearby flat that Uchiyama arranged, for roughly a month. [attested — Uchiyama memoir; Xu Guangping also confirms in 《欣慰的纪念》]
- Lu Xun's daily routine of visiting the bookshop in the afternoons, sitting on a bench by the door, drinking tea, talking with whoever came in — a kind of informal salon. Uchiyama's description of Lu Xun as a *kyaku-zuki* (客好き, fond of guests) softens the canonical image of the solitary polemicist. [attested]
- The role of Uchiyama in introducing Lu Xun to Japanese literature in translation during the last decade and in acquiring banned imported books (Marxist, leftist). [attested]
- The final hours, 18–19 October 1936: Uchiyama was called by Xu Guangping at roughly 04:00 on the 18th and was one of the first non-household people at the bedside. [attested]

**Bias to flag.** Uchiyama had an evident commercial interest in the Lu Xun connection (his bookshop became a pilgrimage site post-1949); he writes warmly, with post-war Japanese-Chinese reconciliation in mind. Fogel shows that Uchiyama occasionally condenses chronology and simplifies political complexities — e.g., making Lu Xun seem less tangled in leftist faction disputes than he actually was. [modern-scholarly-inference — Fogel 2019]

## 6. The writer-peers: Mao Dun, Yu Dafu, Lin Yutang, Ye Shengtao

### Mao Dun 茅盾 (Shen Yanbing 沈雁冰, 1896–1981)

Mao Dun wrote multiple reminiscences of Lu Xun (later collected in 《我走过的道路》 vol. 2, 1984). He was the fellow left-wing fiction writer closest in literary stature to Lu Xun and co-organizer of the October 1936 funeral. [documented]

**Load-bearing Mao Dun observations:**

- Lu Xun's writing discipline: Mao Dun reports being amazed at the pace with which Lu Xun delivered *zawen* 杂文 essays for serialization — draft to print within 24–48 hours, hand-corrected proofs returning within hours. [attested]
- The 1930 founding meeting of the League of Left-Wing Writers 左翼作家联盟 at the Chinese Arts Academy, 2 March 1930 — Mao Dun places Lu Xun's opening address, 《对于左翼作家联盟的意见》, as a dampening caution against factionalism, not a revolutionary manifesto. Lu Xun warned the young leftist writers against four tendencies — including the tendency to "mistake the revolution for romance." [documented — the speech text survives in the *Lu Xun Quanji*, vol. 4]

### Yu Dafu 郁达夫 (1896–1945)

Yu Dafu of the Creation Society moved from being an implicit adversary (as Creation Society member in the late 1920s) to a personal friend in the 1930s, unusually for that camp. His memoir essay 《回忆鲁迅》 (1939, written after Lu Xun's death and Yu's own exile to Sumatra) offers a sympathetic portrait of Lu Xun's melancholy humor — e.g., Lu Xun's habit of responding to literary flattery with a self-deprecating 一塌糊涂 ("a complete mess"). [attested]

### Lin Yutang 林语堂 (1895–1976)

Originally allied in the early 1920s Yusi 语丝 circle, Lin Yutang drifted away in the early 1930s over the humor/*youmo* 幽默 magazines (《论语》, 《人间世》). Lu Xun eventually attacked Lin's humor-essay aesthetic as "小摆设" (little bric-à-brac) — a politically urgent moment demanded sharper weapons. Lin's retrospective *My Country and My People* (1935) and later American-period essays describe Lu Xun with a mixture of respect for his craft and distaste for what Lin saw as a bitter, unsleeping hostility. [attested]

**Bias to flag.** Lin Yutang wrote for an English-reading American audience during and after the war; his Lu Xun is partly an argument against leftism for Cold-War readers.

### Ye Shengtao 叶圣陶 (1894–1988)

Editor and educator; wrote shorter reminiscences, including on Lu Xun's care in editing the Commercial Press and Kaiming typography — Lu Xun's meticulousness about font choice, margin, punctuation. Confirms the visual-craft obsession that also shows up in his woodcut promotion. [attested]

## 7. The Communist liaisons: Qu Qiubai and Feng Xuefeng

### Qu Qiubai 瞿秋白 (1899–1935)

Deposed CCP general secretary; hid at Lu Xun's Shanghai home on multiple occasions in 1931–1934 when under KMT surveillance. Drafted the 1933 preface 《〈鲁迅杂感选集〉序言》 *Preface to Lu Xun's Selected Zawen* — a landmark early leftist canonization of Lu Xun, which Mao Zedong later echoed in the 1940 《新民主主义论》 characterization of Lu Xun as the 文化新军的最伟大和最英勇的旗手. [documented]

Xu Guangping's memoirs, Feng Xuefeng's later writings, and Qu Qiubai's own 《多余的话》 (1935, written awaiting execution) confirm that Qu and Lu Xun had become unusually close in a short span — Lu Xun valued Qu's literary-theoretical Russian-to-Chinese translation work and considered him one of the rare peers in classical learning. After Qu's execution by KMT forces in Fujian on 18 June 1935, Lu Xun took on the editing of Qu's translations. [documented, attested]

**Bias pattern.** The Qu-Lu Xun pairing has been heavily instrumentalized in PRC historiography as the moment the "greatest writer of modern China" meets the "martyred Communist"; primary evidence (the surviving Lu Xun diary entries, Xu Guangping's pre-1961 essays) supports the closeness but not the more elaborate revolutionary-comrade framing later imposed. [modern-scholarly-inference]

### Feng Xuefeng 冯雪峰 (1903–1976)

Poet, CCP literary liaison to Lu Xun from 1929 onward; after Qu Qiubai left Shanghai in 1934, Feng became the principal channel between the underground CCP and Lu Xun. His 《回忆鲁迅》 (first edition 1951, revised 1957) is a key source for the 1930s politics. [documented]

**Bias to flag.** Feng was purged as a rightist in 1957 and died in the Cultural Revolution; his pre-1957 memoir aligns with the then-orthodox reading. The Two-Slogans dispute of 1936 (see §8) is described by Feng in ways that credit him (and, later, Hu Feng) with interpreting Lu Xun's wishes correctly against Zhou Yang. Zhou Yang's own later recollections contradict Feng on specifics. The historical question of what Lu Xun actually wanted in 1936 remains contested. [documented, modern-scholarly-inference]

### Hu Feng 胡风 (1902–1985)

Literary protégé and later (1955) target of Mao's "Hu Feng counter-revolutionary clique" campaign. Hu Feng's 《鲁迅先生》 recollections, written mostly in the 1980s after his rehabilitation, insist on Lu Xun's hostility toward Zhou Yang's faction. Given Hu Feng's catastrophic personal history, his recollections are a partisan witness, but they are also the most politically non-aligned of the "insider" sources because he had no incentive to please the 1980s establishment. [documented]

## 8. The adversaries: honest portraits through hostile eyes

### Chen Xiying 陈西滢 (Chen Yuan 陈源, 1896–1970)

Modern Review 现代评论 faction; main target of Lu Xun's 1925–1926 Women's Normal University polemics. Chen's weekly "Xianhua" 闲话 columns contain some of the sharpest contemporary characterizations of Lu Xun — as a 刀笔吏 (petty-brush ink-clerk), as a man who "never misses a chance to pick a fight," as a provincial intriguer dressed up as a champion of students. The Lu Xun-Chen Xiying exchange of 1925–26 is documented in both parties' collected works and is the best-preserved pair of adversarial contemporary voices. [documented]

### Gao Changhong 高长虹 (1898–1954)

Founder of the Kuangbiao 狂飙 (Storm) Society, a young disciple of Lu Xun in 1924–25; the friendship broke in 1926 in a way that has become central to Lu Xun biography. Gao published vicious satirical pieces in 狂飙 magazine in late 1926 accusing Lu Xun of suppressing younger writers and attempting to "steal the moon" — a coded reference that Xu Guangping read (and modern scholars confirm: Eva S. Chou, "The End of Fiction, the Start of Politics: Lu Xun in 1926–27," *Sino-Platonic Papers* 266, 2016) as targeting her relationship with Lu Xun. Lu Xun's reply, the short story 《奔月》 *Flight to the Moon* (December 1926) in 《故事新编》, is a coded counter-attack. [documented]

### Liang Shiqiu 梁实秋 (1903–1987)

New Crescent 新月 faction; target of Lu Xun's 1928–1930 polemics over whether literature has class character. Liang argued that literature expresses universal human nature (*renxing* 人性), Lu Xun argued that class-position conditions literature — the sharpest exchange was Lu Xun's 1930 essay 《"丧家的""资本家的乏走狗"》, "*Capitalists' Used-Up Running-Dog,' the Loss-of-Home Kind.*" Liang's later Taiwan-period essays treat Lu Xun as a talented but ungenerous polemicist. [documented]

### The Creation Society attack, 1928

Cheng Fangwu 成仿吾 and Qian Xingcun 钱杏邨 (penname for Ah Ying 阿英) launched a coordinated 1928 attack on Lu Xun as 封建余孽 ("feudal remnant"), politically reactionary, and aesthetically outdated. Guo Moruo 郭沫若, writing under the pseudonym Du Quan 杜荃, contributed an essay calling Lu Xun a "Don Quixote" and a "二重的反革命" (doubly counter-revolutionary). The irony that Guo Moruo later became the PRC's principal Lu Xun eulogist has been mined by generations of Chinese literary historians. [documented — both Guo's 1928 essay and his post-1949 reversal are printed in full in standard anthologies]

**Bias value.** The Creation Society's 1928 hostility captures something the adulatory memoirs tend to lose: Lu Xun in the late 1920s did not look, to contemporaries on the leftward-radical edge, like an obvious left-wing saint. He looked like an older, petty-bourgeois, ironic, classically-trained essayist — exactly the kind of figure the avant-garde expected to sweep aside.

### Zhou Yang 周扬 (1908–1989)

League of Left-Wing Writers CCP representative; after Qu Qiubai left and Feng Xuefeng went to the Soviet areas, Zhou Yang ran the League in Shanghai. The 1936 "Two Slogans" dispute — Zhou Yang's 国防文学 (literature of national defense) against Lu Xun/Hu Feng/Feng Xuefeng's 民族革命战争的大众文学 (mass literature of the national revolutionary war) — is the best-documented personal-political antagonism of Lu Xun's last year. [documented]

Zhou Yang went on to become PRC vice-minister of culture; his post-1949 authority meant that Lu Xun's biting private letters about him (preserved in the *Quanji* letter-volume) became dangerous evidence in later campaigns (notably 1966, when the Cultural Revolution left-radicals used them to attack Zhou Yang). [modern-scholarly-inference]

## 9. Canonical episodes and their witness-situation

Below, each episode is given a date and the principal witnesses.

### 1906 Sendai slide-incident 幻灯片事件

Lu Xun's own 1922 《呐喊·自序》 narrates that during a bacteriology lecture at Sendai Medical School, a slide of a Chinese prisoner being executed during the Russo-Japanese War — surrounded by gawking Chinese onlookers — prompted him to abandon medicine for literature. [documented as claim, 1922 source]

**Contested status.** Japanese scholar Tanaka Jun 田中寿, and others (summarized in Christopher Robins, "Japanese Visions of Lu Xun in the Light of the Magic Lantern Incident," *The Asia-Pacific Journal*, 2007), have questioned:

1. Whether the specific slide Lu Xun describes was actually shown in that course — the Sendai Medical School surviving records do not confirm the sequence exactly as he describes.
2. Whether his abandonment of medicine was actually precipitated by this single event, or was a longer drift: poor grades in anatomy, loneliness, an anonymous letter accusing him (and Fujino) of cheating, and a growing interest in literature.
3. Whether the retrospective framing of 1922 serves narrative/political needs at the moment of *Nahan*'s publication.

Modern consensus: *something* of this kind happened, but the clean causality Lu Xun himself supplied is suspect. [modern-scholarly-inference]

### 1912–1918 the "ten years of silence" at Shaoxing Hostel 绍兴会馆

Witnesses: Xu Shoushang (who visited); Zhou Zuoren (who lived in Beijing nearby and saw him often, though they were not yet estranged); Lu Xun's own 《呐喊·自序》. The hostel had three rooms; a woman had hanged herself on the locust tree in the courtyard before Lu Xun's arrival — he mentions this. He spent evenings copying rubbings of ancient stone inscriptions (抄古碑), compiled the 《古小说钩沉》 collection of lost ancient fiction, and took almost no literary action. [documented, attested]

### 1918 Qian Xuantong visit and the "iron house" dialogue

Qian Xuantong 钱玄同, a *New Youth* editor, called on Lu Xun at the hostel repeatedly in 1917–18 trying to recruit him. Lu Xun's own preface narrates the famous exchange: "Imagine an iron house without windows, absolutely indestructible, with many people fast asleep inside who will soon die of suffocation..." Qian replied that if a few awoke, one could not say there was no hope of destroying the iron house. Shortly afterwards Lu Xun wrote 《狂人日记》 for the April 1918 issue. [documented — Lu Xun's own 1922 preface]

Qian Xuantong himself never published a counter-account, dying in 1939; the iron-house dialogue is effectively single-witness (Lu Xun). [attributed]

### 19 July / 2 August 1923 — the brother-break

Witnesses: (a) Lu Xun's diary — 廿四日 (19 July): "上午启孟自持信来, 后邀欲问之, 不至..." (my brother Qimeng brought a letter in person; I asked him to come discuss, he did not come); (b) Zhou Zuoren's 1923 letter of 18 July (preserved), which opens "鲁迅先生: 我昨日才知道, — 但过去的事不必再说了" ("Mr. Lu Xun: I only learned yesterday — but there is no need to speak of what has passed"); (c) on 2 August, Lu Xun moved out of the shared Badaowan 八道湾 compound. [documented]

**The contested cause.** Surviving scholarship (Pollard 2002; Susan Daruvala 2000; 止庵 Zhi'an's reconstructions; Eileen Cheng *Literary Remains* 2013) lists four hypotheses, none of which is definitively proven:

1. **Financial.** Habuto Nobuko 羽太信子 (Zhou Zuoren's Japanese wife) ran the household and spent more than Lu Xun's Ministry salary could support; tension over money had been chronic. [modern-scholarly-inference — source-material: family-economic records reconstructed by Zhi'an]
2. **The bathroom incident.** Habuto Nobuko accused Lu Xun of watching her bathe, or otherwise making a sexual advance. This is the version she herself circulated; Lu Xun denied it; Zhou Zuoren never publicly specified. [attributed — Habuto Nobuko; Zhou Zuoren never corroborated or denied in print]
3. **A long-rooted Japan-era incident.** A theory that some old grievance from the 1909 Tokyo period resurfaced. [modern-scholarly-inference, weakest documentation]
4. **Accumulated sibling-household friction** now catalyzed by a single incident — a multifactor view favored by most recent scholarship. [modern-scholarly-inference]

Zhou Zuoren's 1960s reminiscences explicitly refuse to address the cause; Xu Guangping's accounts (who was not present in 1923) repeat whatever Lu Xun had told her. The event remains the most famous unresolved puzzle in Lu Xun biography. [documented — unresolved status is itself well-documented]

### 18 March 1926 — the Duan Qirui massacre

At the Tian'anmen student demonstration against the Duan Qirui warlord government, troops fired on petitioning students; 47 killed, 200+ wounded, including Liu Hezhen 刘和珍, a student at Beijing Women's Normal University who had taken part in the earlier student-rights movement Lu Xun had supported against principal Yang Yinyu 杨荫榆. Lu Xun's essay 《记念刘和珍君》 of 1 April 1926 — "真的猛士,敢于直面惨淡的人生" — is one of the most anthologized of his *zawen*. [documented]

Witness corroboration: multiple eyewitness accounts of the massacre survive in contemporary newspapers (《京报》, 《晨报》); Xu Guangping, who knew Liu Hezhen at the Normal University, confirms the biographical details of Liu. [documented]

### 1926 flight to Xiamen; January 1927 to Guangzhou; April 1927 resignation

After the 18 March massacre Lu Xun was briefly listed by the Duan government as a dangerous radical; he left Beijing in late August 1926 for Xiamen University on a lucrative contract, then moved to Sun Yat-sen University in Guangzhou January 1927 (partly to be with Xu Guangping). After the 12 April 1927 Chiang Kai-shek purge in Shanghai and the 15 April counterpart in Guangzhou — during which his students were arrested — he failed to secure their release in a faculty meeting and resigned in protest. This episode is treated as a political turning-point toward his 1930s leftward alignment. [documented — Sun Yat-sen University's faculty-meeting minutes preserved; Xu Guangping's letters confirm]

### 7 February 1931 — Longhua execution of the Five Martyrs

Rou Shi 柔石, Hu Yepin 胡也频, Li Weisen 李伟森, Yin Fu 殷夫, Feng Keng 冯铿 were executed by KMT authorities at the Longhua Garrison in Shanghai, after arrest at a British-Concession literary meeting on 17 January. Lu Xun was particularly close to Rou Shi, whom he had mentored; the 1933 memorial essay 《为了忘却的记念》 *For the Sake of Memorial, To Forget* is the principal literary monument. Xu Guangping and Uchiyama both confirm Lu Xun went into hiding at Uchiyama's bookshop for roughly a month after the execution. [documented, attested]

### 19 October 1936 — death

Primary witnesses at bedside: Xu Guangping, Zhou Haiying (aged 7, recorded his memory decades later), Dr. Sudō Gorō 須藤五百三, Uchiyama Kanzō (arrived shortly after). Death at 05:25 (some accounts: 05:11) on 19 October 1936 in the Shanyin Road house. [documented]

**The Sudō controversy.** Dr. Sudō, a retired Japanese army doctor, had been Lu Xun's primary physician since 1934. A 1949 piece by 周建人 raised suspicion that Sudō had misdiagnosed (treating tuberculosis when the actual condition was late-stage pneumothorax/emphysema) or possibly, in the most extreme form of the accusation, had actively accelerated Lu Xun's death under Japanese intelligence direction. Most modern scholars (see 泉彪之助 Izumi Hyōnosuke's medical re-examination; Joshua Fogel in *A Friend in Deed*) reject the espionage accusation as Cold-War-era suspicion projected backward. A genuine medical misdiagnosis is plausible but not clearly malicious. [attested — accusation; modern-scholarly-inference — rejection]

### 22 October 1936 — funeral

Organized by Song Qingling 宋庆龄, Cai Yuanpei 蔡元培, Mao Dun, Feng Xuefeng. Estimated tens of thousands along the route from the Wanguo Funeral Parlor (Bibliothèque Nationale / Jiaotong Road) to the Wanguo Public Cemetery in Hongqiao. A calligraphic banner reading 民族魂 *Minzuhun*, "National Spirit" — written by the jurist Shen Junru 沈钧儒 — was draped over the coffin. Song Qingling, Uchiyama, and others spoke; the KMT did not interfere. [documented — the funeral is possibly the best-photographed funeral in 1930s Chinese intellectual history; period photographs and newsreel fragments survive]

## 10. Physical presence, voice, lectures — the anecdote layer

Compiling across Xiao Hong, Xu Guangping, Xu Shoushang, Zhou Zuoren, Uchiyama, and the student-witnesses (李霁野 Li Jiye, 台静农 Tai Jingnong, 孙伏园 Sun Fuyuan, 荆有麟 Jing Youlin, whose short memoirs are anthologized in 《鲁迅回忆录》 vols 1999–2003):

- **Voice.** A strong Shaoxing accent throughout his life; he never acquired northern-Mandarin pronunciation. Beijing students recall that lectures on Chinese fiction history (which became the basis of 《中国小说史略》) required concentration at first to follow his accent, but the content was so densely argued — citing text after text from memory — that it "repaid the effort," per Sun Fuyuan and Jing Youlin. [attested — multiple students]
- **Lectures.** Delivered seated or standing behind a desk, speaking without notes, smoking throughout. He laughed mid-lecture at the absurdities of the texts he was analyzing. Students recall that 中国小说史略 lectures at Peking University 1920–1926 were packed — including auditors from other schools. [attested — 川岛 Chuandao, 孙伏园]
- **Eyes.** "Like a sword blade" (刀锋似的) — recurring metaphor across hostile and friendly witnesses. Chen Xiying mocked it; Xiao Hong revered it; same observation.
- **Walking.** Quiet. Xiao Hong's "like a cat without shoes." Xu Guangping independently notes he could be in the doorway of his study before she realized he had come downstairs. [attested, independent corroboration]
- **Smoking.** Continuous. The green tin of "Qianmen" cigarettes. Xu Guangping tried repeatedly to get him to cut down in the final two years of tuberculosis; failed. [attested, corroborated]
- **Laughter.** Loud, open, sometimes sustained. Contrary to the posthumous image of the perpetually-scowling polemicist. Xiao Hong gives this the most weight; Yu Dafu and Uchiyama also confirm.
- **Handwriting.** Small, neat, heavily corrected; Mao Dun notes the manuscripts went to the printer with layers of paste-over edits. Lu Xun's hand-copying of classical texts during the Beijing decade produced a calligraphic style he retained: a kind of pre-Tang regular-script with Han-stele flavor. [attested]
- **Clothing.** The long gown 长袍 — wool in winter, cotton in summer — almost never a Western suit. Photographs confirm. He wore leather shoes; his son Zhou Haiying later recalled the sound of the shoes on the wooden stairs as his father came down in the afternoon. [attested]

## 11. Institutional and surveillance records

- **Ministry of Education personnel file** (1912–1926): Lu Xun served as section chief (佥事) and division head (科长) in the Social Education department; salary records from roughly 300 silver dollars/month at peak. Dismissed by Minister Zhang Shizhao 章士钊 in August 1925 over the Women's Normal University dispute; successfully sued for reinstatement at the Pingzheng Yuan 平政院 (administrative court) in 1926 — one of the few Republican-era administrative-court cases won against a Minister. [documented — both the dismissal order and the reinstatement judgment survive]
- **Beijing University lecturer register** (1920–1926): part-time lecturer in Chinese fiction history.
- **Xiamen University** (1926): contract at roughly 400 silver dollars/month, a substantial figure.
- **Sun Yat-sen University** (1927): head of Chinese Literature Department from January; resigned April.
- **KMT surveillance** (1930–1936): Shanghai Municipal Police (French Concession and International Settlement files), plus KMT internal security records, mark him as a surveilled leftist; arrest was considered but not executed, apparently because of his international reputation and Song Qingling's protective umbrella. No direct arrest ever occurred. [documented — files partially declassified and studied by 陈漱渝 and others from the 1980s]
- **League of Left-Wing Writers** (1930–1936): founding meeting 2 March 1930, ~50 attendees; Lu Xun's opening address preserved. [documented]

## Summary of witness count, contradictions, and bias patterns

**Witness count.** This research note draws on 22 distinct contemporary witnesses (named and documented): Zhou Zuoren, Zhou Jianren, Xu Guangping, Xu Shoushang, Xiao Hong, Xiao Jun, Uchiyama Kanzō, Mao Dun, Yu Dafu, Lin Yutang, Ye Shengtao, Qu Qiubai, Feng Xuefeng, Hu Feng, Chen Xiying, Gao Changhong, Liang Shiqiu, Cheng Fangwu, Guo Moruo, Zhou Yang, Sun Fuyuan, Sudō Gorō; plus students Li Jiye, Tai Jingnong, Jing Youlin, Chuandao, and the son-witness Zhou Haiying. ~16 major documented episodes covered.

**Main contradictions.** (1) The 1923 brother-break cause — Zhou Zuoren's silence vs. Habuto Nobuko's accusation vs. financial-tension hypothesis; irreducibly unresolved. (2) Xu Guangping's 1961 *Memoir* vs. her recovered 2008 manuscript — two different Lu Xuns. (3) The 1906 Sendai slide incident's exact historicity (Tanaka, Robins) vs. Lu Xun's own 1922 account. (4) Dr. Sudō's role in Lu Xun's death — misdiagnosis, espionage, or neither. (5) The 1936 Two-Slogans dispute — Feng Xuefeng/Hu Feng vs. Zhou Yang each claim to represent Lu Xun's actual view.

**Bias patterns observed.** Post-1949 PRC memoirs (Xu Guangping 1961, Feng Xuefeng 1951/57, Zhou Jianren late-period) systematically smooth Lu Xun toward CCP orthodoxy; Taiwan- and diaspora-era memoirs (Lin Yutang, Hu Shi letters) tilt the other way. Adversarial witnesses (Chen Xiying, Gao Changhong, Creation Society 1928, Liang Shiqiu) are the best corrective — their Lu Xun is petty, combative, provincial, resentful; that portrait must be layered with the intimate witnesses' laughing, guest-loving, cat-walking figure to get the man.
