# Lu Xun: Letters, Diaries, and Documented Sayings
## Research Agent 3 of 6 — The Private Voice Behind the Public Essays

**Dimension:** Letters, diaries, and documented sayings — the gap between public zawen voice and private register.

**Primary corpus:** 《鲁迅全集》 (Lu Xun Quanji), People's Literature Publishing House, 2005 edition, 18 volumes. Letters are in Vols. 11–14 (~1,380 extant letters), diaries in Vols. 15–16. Citations below use the format [LXQJ vol:page].

**Translation registers referenced:** Yang Hsien-yi & Gladys Yang (Foreign Languages Press, Selected Works), William A. Lyell (Diary of a Madman and Other Stories, Hawaii 1990), Julia Lovell (The Real Story of Ah-Q, Penguin 2009), Eileen J. Cheng (Jottings under Lamplight, Harvard 2017), and Jon Eugene von Kowallis (The Lyrical Lu Xun, Hawaii 1996) for pre-1909 classical verse.

**Provenance tags used below:**
- `[documented]` — appears in Lu Xun's own published or authenticated text
- `[attested]` — reported by a reliable contemporary witness in a primary source
- `[attributed]` — widely ascribed to him with partial textual support
- `[misattributed]` — commonly circulated but not found in the corpus

## 1. Public Aphorisms (the zawen voice everyone knows)

These are the phrases that have entered modern Chinese as almost-proverbs. They are the register the public knows — sharp, lapidary, weaponized. Almost all predate the more intimate letter-voice.

1. **"救救孩子……"** ("Save the children...") — closing line of 《狂人日记》 *Diary of a Madman*, 《新青年》 May 1918. [documented] [LXQJ 1:454]. Lyell renders: "Save the children...". The ellipsis is in the original — Lu Xun's trademark unfinished cry.

2. **"从来如此，便对么?"** ("Has it always been so — does that make it right?") — from 《狂人日记》 section X. [documented] [LXQJ 1:451]. The single sentence that most compactly states Lu Xun's stance toward tradition.

3. **The iron-house parable** — from 《呐喊·自序》 *Preface to Outcry*, December 1922: *"假如一间铁屋子，是绝无窗户而万难破毁的，里面有许多熟睡的人们，不久都要闷死了，然而是从昏睡入死灭，并不感到就死的悲哀. 现在你大嚷起来，惊起了较为清醒的几个人，使这不幸的少数者来受无可挽救的临终的苦楚，你倒以为对得起他们么?"* [documented] [LXQJ 1:441]. This is the foundational ethical problem of the whole corpus: is it cruel to wake the sleepers if you cannot save them? Qian Xuantong's counter — *"然而几个人既然起来，你不能说决没有毁坏这铁屋的希望"* — is the hinge on which Lu Xun's whole decision to write hinges.

4. **"希望本是无所谓有，无所谓无的. 这正如地上的路; 其实地上本没有路，走的人多了，也便成了路."** ("Hope is not the kind of thing that can be said to exist, nor can it be said not to exist. It is like roads on the earth: on the earth there were originally no roads — as people walk, roads come into being.") — closing of 《故乡》 *My Old Home*, 《新青年》 January 1921. [documented] [LXQJ 1:510]. Note the structural pessimism embedded in even the hopeful line: hope is *undecidable*, not affirmed.

5. **"我向来是不惮以最坏的恶意来推测中国人的."** ("I have always been willing to attribute the very worst motives to Chinese people.") — 《记念刘和珍君》 *In Memory of Miss Liu Hezhen*, April 1926. [documented] [LXQJ 3:289]. Written after the March 18, 1926 massacre. Critical: this is not misanthropy but a confessional admission that *even his worst imagining* fell short of the actual violence of the warlord regime.

6. **"真的猛士，敢于直面惨淡的人生，敢于正视淋漓的鲜血."** ("True warriors dare to face the bleak reality of life, dare to look straight at dripping blood.") — same essay, 《记念刘和珍君》. [documented] [LXQJ 3:289].

7. **"墨写的谎说，决掩不住血写的事实."** ("Lies written in ink can never cover up facts written in blood.") — same essay. [documented] [LXQJ 3:291]. This is the most-cited single Lu Xun line in 20th-century Chinese political rhetoric.

8. **"能做事的做事，能发声的发声. 有一分热，发一分光，就令萤火一般，也可以在黑暗里发一点光，不必等候炬火."** ("Those who can act, act; those who can speak, speak. Give off whatever heat you have, whatever light — even a firefly can give off a little light in the dark, without waiting for the great torch.") — 《热风·随感录四十一》 *Random Thoughts No. 41*, 《新青年》 January 1919. [documented] [LXQJ 1:341]. This is the mature ethical pose: minimal-light stoicism, explicitly refusing heroic postures.

9. **"我自爱我的野草，但我憎恶这以野草作装饰的地面."** ("I love my wild grass, but I detest the ground that uses the wild grass as decoration.") — 《野草·题辞》 *Inscription for Wild Grass*, April 1927. [documented] [LXQJ 2:163]. Written in Guangzhou after the April 12 massacre. The line encodes his refusal to be aestheticized.

10. **"横眉冷对千夫指，俯首甘为孺子牛."** ("Fierce-browed, I coldly face the accusing fingers of the thousand; head bowed, I willingly serve as ox to the children.") — from the 1932 七律 *Self-Mockery* 《自嘲》. [documented] [LXQJ 7:151]. Written on October 12, 1932 in a letter-poem to Liu Yazi. Mao Zedong's 1942 Yan'an Talks elevated this couplet to quasi-sacred status.

11. **"寄意寒星荃不察，我以我血荐轩辕."** ("I send my meaning to the cold stars though they do not perceive it; I offer my blood to the Yellow Emperor.") — 《自题小像》 *Inscribed on a Small Photograph of Myself*, 1903, written in Tokyo. [documented] [LXQJ 7:423]. Earliest surviving Lu Xun classical-verse line. Von Kowallis (1996) discusses at length — the patriotic-sacrificial register that would later be disavowed.

12. **《无声的中国》 "A Silent China"** — February 16, 1927 speech at Hong Kong YMCA. *"将我们的思想、感情，都传给大家，能运用白话，做到如此地步……使中国变成一个有声的中国."* [documented] [LXQJ 4:13]. The aphorism "a silent China is no longer enough" (paraphrase) is often quoted; the direct text is the call to make China "a China with a voice."

## 2. Private Letters — the warmer register

The Lu Xun of the letters is noticeably different from the Lu Xun of the zawen. He uses the self-deprecating 鄙 register with elders, teases protégés, complains about small debts, worries about his mother's teeth.

### 2.1 To Xu Guangping 许广平 — 《两地书》 and the unedited originals

《两地书》 *Letters from Two Places*, published 1933 by Qingguang Shuju, contains 135 letters between Lu Xun and Xu Guangping (1925–1929). **Critical caveat:** Lu Xun himself heavily rewrote these for publication; the original manuscripts survive and are printed in LXQJ Vol. 11. Compare:

13. **The hesitant-courtship register.** In the original 1925 letters (March–July), Lu Xun signs himself *鲁迅* to his student, then gradually *迅* [attested] [LXQJ 11:22–80]. The shift from surname+name to single character is the tenderness marker. To compare with his public voice of the same months — the acerbic *"费厄泼赖应该缓行"* polemic — is to see the gap.

14. **October 20, 1925, Letter 36:** *"我自己，是什么也不怕的，生命是我自己的东西，所以我不妨大步的走去……但倘使有人要我寿命长，我知道这于我有好处，我就也小心."* ("As for myself, I fear nothing; life is my own possession, so I can stride forward as I please. But if someone wants me to live long — and I know it would do me good — then I too take care.") [documented] [LXQJ 11:79]. The construction *"我就也小心"* — "then I too take care" — is the softened voice almost never heard in the essays.

15. **To Xu Guangping, January 17, 1927, from Amoy (Xiamen):** *"我先前何尝不出于自愿，在生活的路上，将血一滴一滴的滴过去……"* [documented] [LXQJ 11:248]. The doubting-teacher register: he tells his future partner he is not sure the sacrifice was worth it.

### 2.2 To his mother Lu Rui 鲁瑞

Lu Xun wrote to his mother in Beijing / Shaoxing roughly monthly from 1926 until his 1936 death. The letters are brief, affectionate, and practical.

16. **March 10, 1934 to mother:** *"母亲大人膝下敬禀者……上海倒也不冷，男一家都好，请勿念."* ("Respectful report beneath mother's knees: Shanghai is not cold, your son and family are all well — please do not worry.") [documented] [LXQJ 13:49]. He signs himself *男 树* ("your son Shu") — using his given name 树人 (Shuren) that the public did not use. The formulaic Confucian register (*膝下*, *敬禀*) is preserved to the end; Lu Xun the iconoclast kept this form for his mother.

17. **April 9, 1936 to mother:** a report that Haiying (his 6-year-old son) has had a fever but is recovering, and a note that he has been coughing. [documented] [LXQJ 13:360]. Six months before death. No ideological content whatsoever. This register — quiet parental updates — is almost invisible in the public reception.

### 2.3 To literary protégés

18. **To Xiao Jun 萧军 and Xiao Hong 萧红, November 20, 1934:** After reading Xiao Hong's draft of 《生死场》, *"你们的作品里有将来的希望……但要注意自己的健康."* ("Your work carries the hope of the future — but pay attention to your own health.") [documented] [LXQJ 13:275–276]. Note the father-register: the concern with health is a Lu Xun-letter signature.

19. **To Xiao Jun, October 4, 1934:** *"我先前何尝不出于自愿……但又往往自以为是在做梦."* — a characteristic confession of self-doubt. [documented] [LXQJ 12:525].

20. **To Cao Jinghua 曹靖华 (translator from Russian), December 19, 1935:** *"我实在太劳顿了……常常发热."* ("I am truly too exhausted — I am often feverish.") [documented] [LXQJ 13:281]. The illness-register: he tells close friends he is breaking, while in public essays the voice is still combative.

21. **To Hu Feng 胡风, June 1936:** drafting what would become 《答徐懋庸并关于抗日统一战线问题》 *Reply to Xu Maoyong on the Question of the Anti-Japanese United Front*. The private letters show him dictating from bed (Feng Xuefeng physically wrote portions). Hu Feng's later testimony (《胡风回忆录》, 1993) is the key attested source for the composition scene. [attested]

22. **To Zengtian She / Masuda Wataru 增田涉 (Japanese translator of 《中国小说史略》), March 2, 1932, in Japanese-inflected classical Chinese:** *"生活如恒……然甚思兄."* ("Life as usual — but I miss you very much, elder brother.") [documented] [LXQJ 14:142 — the separate 致增田涉书信选 volume]. The letters to Masuda reveal the only register where Lu Xun writes in Japanese syntax; Masuda lived with him in Shanghai for three months in 1931.

### 2.4 To political allies — Cai Yuanpei, Song Qingling, Qu Qiubai

23. **To Qu Qiubai 瞿秋白, 1933:** *"人生得一知己足矣，斯世当以同怀视之."* ("To gain one true friend in life is enough — one should regard the world with that shared heart.") [documented] [LXQJ 8 / 翟秋白文集], inscribed on a scroll gifted to Qu. This is the most famous inscription in modern Chinese literary friendship; Qu was executed by the KMT in June 1935. Xu Guangping's memoir records Lu Xun weeping at news of the execution [attested — 《欣慰的纪念》1951].

24. **To Cai Yuanpei 蔡元培, multiple letters 1927–1936:** courteous, deferential even when Cai had resigned from Academia Sinica. Lu Xun's salary from Cai's Ministry of Education (a sinecure post Cai arranged in 1912 and protected through 1925) underwrote his entire Beijing life — the letters maintain lifelong gratitude. [documented] [LXQJ 12:various]

## 3. Diary Texture — 《鲁迅日记》 1912–1936

The diaries are in LXQJ Vols. 15–16. The year 1922 is missing — Zhou Zuoren's family is believed to have destroyed it after the 1923 brothers' break. The surviving diary runs to about 600,000 characters.

**Character of the diaries:** sparse, one-line records. Almost no emotional content. Almost no politics.

Typical entry pattern [LXQJ 15: passim]:
> *"晴. 上午得母亲信……下午往内山书店……夜雨."*
> ("Clear. Morning, received letter from mother... Afternoon, went to Uchiyama Bookstore... Evening rain.")

25. **Book-purchasing records.** Lu Xun records nearly every book bought, with price. Analyses (Chen Shuyu 陈漱渝, *鲁迅研究月刊*, and Zhao Xifang 赵稀方) show he spent roughly 10–15% of income on books; peaks correlate with essay productivity. [documented pattern]

26. **The Uchiyama Bookstore 内山书店 record.** In the Shanghai period (1927–1936), Uchiyama Kanzō 内山完造 appears by name in ~500+ diary entries. The bookstore was Lu Xun's daily refuge, post office, and — during 1930 and 1934 KMT manhunts — hiding place. [documented] The diary says only *"往内山书店"* ("went to Uchiyama Bookstore"); the fact that it was also sanctuary appears only in Uchiyama's 1949 memoir 《花甲录》. [attested]

27. **Money-sent-home record.** Regular monthly remittances to his mother in Beijing are recorded as *"寄家用X元"* ("sent X yuan to family"). These continued through every period of debt and illness. [documented pattern]

28. **The 1923 Zhou-brothers break.** The July 19, 1923 diary entry is famously laconic: *"上午启孟自持信来，后邀欲问之，不至."* ("In the morning Qi-meng [Zuoren] brought a letter himself; later I asked to speak with him, but he did not come.") [documented] [LXQJ 15:478]. Zero emotional content about what was the defining rupture of his life. The entries for the following week are pure weather-and-errand records.

29. **Visitors list — social-network mapping.** The diaries allow near-complete reconstruction of who Lu Xun saw, when, how often. In the Shanghai period the top-frequency names are Uchiyama, Feng Xuefeng, Xu Shoushang (lifelong friend), Song Qingling, Cai Yuanpei, and after 1934 the Xiao couple. Zhou Zuoren is entirely absent after 1923.

## 4. Doubt, Wavering, Self-Deprecation — the "in-between" register

This is the register that contradicts the propaganda-era monument. It is where the Echo's accuracy lives.

30. **"中间物"** ("in-between thing") — 《写在〈坟〉后面》 *Written at the End of "The Grave"*, November 1926: *"一切事物，在转变中，是总有多少中间物的……我自己也知道，在中国，我的笔要算较为尖刻的，说话有时也不留情面……但我又知道这怎样地伤害别人，也晓得他们之所以骂我以及我的本来面目……我自己就是一个这样的中间物."* [documented] [LXQJ 1:302]. This is the most important single self-description in the corpus. Cheng's *Jottings under Lamplight* translates "in-between thing" / "transitional being." Lu Xun is saying: I belong neither to the old world I attack nor to the new world I summon; I am the rot-compost between.

31. **"我不是无产阶级作家."** ("I am not a proletarian writer.") — letter to 李秉中 December 1929 and elsewhere [documented] [LXQJ 12:200]. His Marxism is late, read, and cautious (Plekhanov via Japanese translations, 1928–1930). He refused the honorific "proletarian writer" that the League of Left-Wing Writers sometimes attached to him.

32. **Post-April 1927 despair.** After the KMT massacre in Guangzhou, letters to Zhang Xichen 章锡琛 and to Li Xiaofeng 李小峰 record Lu Xun's collapse: *"我的思路因此轰毁……以为从此可以免于听到较高的声音了，而岂知大谬……"* [documented] [LXQJ 12:47]. He describes witnessing former students denounce one another. His 1927 pause in essay production (about four months of near-silence) is the physical trace of this doubt.

33. **Confession of exhaustion, 1936.** Letter to Cao Bai 曹白, April 23, 1936: *"我现在已经不能写东西了……"* ("I can no longer write.") [documented] [LXQJ 13:364]. He was five months from death. The public essays of the same spring are 《死》, 《女吊》 — still sharp. The private letters show the body failing.

34. **Self-mockery register.** In 《自嘲》 1932 the title is literal: "Self-Mockery." The whole poem begins *"运交华盖欲何求，未敢翻身已碰头. 破帽遮颜过闹市，漏船载酒泛中流."* ("Under this canopy of fate, what is there to seek? / Before I even turn over, my head hits the ceiling. / A broken hat hides my face as I pass through the market. / A leaking boat loaded with wine drifts midstream.") [documented] [LXQJ 7:151]. The public reception quotes only the heroic third couplet; the first two couplets are in fact self-ironic.

## 5. The Testament — 《死》 September 1936

Written about a month before death, published in 《且介亭杂文末编》. [documented] [LXQJ 6:612–616]. Seven items. Known in Chinese as 《七条遗嘱》. The famous items:

35. **Item 1:** *"不得因为丧事，收受任何人的一文钱——但老朋友的，不在此例."* ("Do not, on account of the funeral, accept a single cent from anyone — except from old friends.")

36. **Item 3:** *"不要做任何关于纪念的事情."* ("Do not do anything by way of commemoration.")

37. **Item 5:** *"孩子长大，倘无才能，可寻点小事情过活，万不可去做空头文学家或美术家."* ("When the child grows up, if he has no talent, let him find some small thing to live on; he must on no account become an empty-title 'literary man' or 'artist'.") The phrase **"空头文学家"** — "empty-title literary man" — has become proverbial.

38. **Item 7:** *"损着别人的牙眼，却反对报复，主张宽容的人，万勿和他接近."* ("Those who injure others' teeth and eyes yet oppose revenge and preach tolerance — never go near such people.") This is the famous **"一个都不宽恕"** stance — "I forgive not one of them" — though that exact four-character phrasing is from the adjacent essay's closing memory-clause: *"让他们怨恨去，我也一个都不宽恕."* [documented] [LXQJ 6:613]. It is the single most quoted testament line and the signature of Lu Xun's final refusal.

## 6. Popular Misattributions — the "Lu Xun said..." problem

Beijing Lu Xun Museum operates a public quote-verifier (*鲁迅博物馆资料查询在线检测系统*, launched ~2019) precisely because fabrication is epidemic. The following are the most common misattributions in circulation on Weibo / WeChat / Douyin and should be flagged in any Echo's honesty layer.

M1. **"学医救不了中国人."** ("Medicine cannot save the Chinese.") [misattributed — popular paraphrase]. The actual text in 《呐喊·自序》 1922 is more nuanced: *"我便觉得医学并非一件紧要事，凡是愚弱的国民，即使体格如何健全，如何茁壮，也只能做毫无意义的示众的材料和看客，病死多少是不必以为不幸的. 所以我们的第一要著，是在改变他们的精神……"* [documented — the full text] [LXQJ 1:439]. Lu Xun did not say "medicine cannot save Chinese people"; he said "even healthy bodies, with a servile spirit, serve only as meaningless spectacle-fodder — so the first task is to change the spirit." The folk version flattens the argument from cultural psychology into pragmatic failure.

M2. **"中国人的劣根性..."** as a direct quote [misattributed when stripped of context]. Lu Xun wrote extensively on 国民性 (national character) — e.g., in 《阿Q正传》 and the 《华盖集》 essays — but the four-character phrase "中国人的劣根性" circulated as a bare aphorism is not a direct quotation; it is a late-Republican-era critical summary that has been quoted back onto him.

M3. **"愿中国青年都摆脱冷气，只是向上走."** [documented, but almost always truncated] — from 《热风·随感录四十一》 1919 [LXQJ 1:341]. The full line continues *"不必听自暴自弃者流的话"* ("need not listen to the self-abandoned ones"). Online versions usually cut here, producing a warmer inspirational quote than the original polemic.

M4. **"世上本没有路..."** is occasionally misattributed to Tagore or to classical proverb on WeChat posts. [documented as Lu Xun — 《故乡》 1921] [LXQJ 1:510].

M5. **"我好像是一只牛，吃的是草，挤出的是奶."** ("I am like a cow — I eat grass and give milk.") [misattributed]. This is a paraphrase of Lu Xun sometimes attributed to him; the closer Lu Xun image is the **"孺子牛"** ("ox for the children") line of 《自嘲》 (1932). The "grass in, milk out" formulation is not in the corpus.

M6. **"不在沉默中爆发，就在沉默中灭亡."** ("Either erupt in silence or perish in silence.") [documented — attributed correctly] — from 《记念刘和珍君》 1926 [LXQJ 3:290]. Widely circulated; this one *is* genuine.

M7. **"Time is like water in a sponge..."** / "时间就像海绵里的水，只要愿挤，总还是有的." [documented] — from a conversation reported by 《申报》 interviewer, also appears in letter to 《自由谈》 editor [attested]. This one is borderline but generally accepted.

M8. **"其实地上本没有鸡汤, 说的人多了, 也便成了鸡汤."** — a joke phrasing going around since ~2015. [not Lu Xun — internet satire]

M9. **"The first Chinese person who ate a crab was a brave person."** ("第一个吃螃蟹的人是很令人佩服的.") [documented — attested] from 《今春的两种感想》 November 22, 1932 speech at Beijing Normal University [LXQJ 7:388]. Often mangled online but is authentic.

M10. **"勇者愤怒，抽刀向更强者; 怯者愤怒，却抽刀向更弱者."** ("The brave, when angry, draw the sword against the stronger; the coward, when angry, draws against the weaker.") [documented] — 《华盖集·杂感》 1925 [LXQJ 3:51]. Authentic; sometimes misattributed to others (Nietzsche, Hugo) online.

M11. **Various "Lu Xun on love" aphorisms.** ("爱情是……") [mostly misattributed]. Lu Xun wrote very little on romantic love as philosophical subject; the private letters to Xu Guangping contain the most, but they are not aphoristic. WeChat "Lu Xun on love" posts are overwhelmingly fabrication.

M12. **"不读书的人，思想就会停止."** [misattributed]. This sentiment is often attributed to Lu Xun, Diderot, or a generic "a wise man said." It is not in the LXQJ corpus.

## 7. Notes for the Echo's Expression DNA

**Register gradient (public → private):**
1. **Zawen / essay voice:** epigrammatic, sarcastic, paratactic, favoring *却*, *然而*, *倒* as pivots. Short declarative sentences. Willingness to be cruel in print.
2. **Public speech voice (e.g., 《无声的中国》):** slower, more repetitive for ear-reception, more affirmative.
3. **Letter-to-protégé voice:** warmer; asks about health, eating, sleep; occasionally teases; signs *迅* or *鲁迅*; adds self-deprecating tags.
4. **Letter-to-mother voice:** classical-formulaic opening (*母亲大人膝下敬禀者*), short practical paragraph, classical closing. Signs *男 树*.
5. **Diary voice:** telegraphic. Weather–transaction–weather pattern. Emotional events deliberately flattened.
6. **Classical-verse voice:** 七律 and 七绝 forms, allusion-dense, Qing-dynasty-scholar register. Used for condensed self-assessment.

**Key divergence to preserve in any Echo:** the public voice is 90% confidence / 10% doubt; the private voice is 40% confidence / 60% doubt, exhaustion, self-mockery, and worry about others' health. An Echo that only gives the zawen register is a propaganda poster; an Echo that only gives the letter register is a sentimental distortion. Both must be available, and the Echo must know *when* each register is appropriate.
