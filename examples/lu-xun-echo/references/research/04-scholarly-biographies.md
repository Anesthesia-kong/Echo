# Lu Xun 鲁迅: Scholarly Biographies and Modern Critical Studies

**Dimension:** Research Agent 4 of 6 — post-1950 analytic synthesis, myth-debunking, and ongoing scholarly disputes.

**Evidence standard:** Every claim is labeled `[modern-scholarly-inference]` and attributed to a named scholar with book title, year, and publisher where possible. Where scholars disagree, both positions are preserved. Popular anecdote, WeChat posts, and non-peer-reviewed web summaries are excluded.

---

## I. Major Interpretive Schools

### 1. The Maoist canonization template (1940–1978) and its legacy

The official reading of Lu Xun as revolutionary saint was set by a very small number of texts whose influence is hard to overstate.

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Qu Qiubai 瞿秋白's《〈鲁迅杂感选集〉序言》(1933, reprinted in 《瞿秋白文集》政治理论编 vol. 3, 人民出版社 1989) is the foundational canonization text. Qu recast Lu Xun as a thinker who moved "from evolution to class struggle" (从进化论到阶级论) and from "petty-bourgeois intellectual" to "proletarian warrior." Most Chinese scholars since 1980 (e.g., Wang Hui, Qian Liqun, Wang Xiaoming — see below) treat this as a periodization scheme that over-determines later readings.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Mao Zedong's 1940《新民主主义论》anointed Lu Xun as "文化新军最伟大和最英勇的旗手" and declared "鲁迅的骨头是最硬的." Leo Ou-fan Lee 李欧梵, in *Voices from the Iron House: A Study of Lu Xun* (Indiana University Press, 1987), pp. 3–10 and passim, argues that this four-sentence passage did more to shape PRC Lu Xun scholarship than any scholarly book; it produced the hagiographic apparatus of the 鲁迅博物馆 system, the 《鲁迅全集》 1958 and 1981 editions' annotation practice, and the secondary-school canon.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Feng Xuefeng 冯雪峰, the CCP liaison to Lu Xun in the 1930s, wrote the most influential first-generation memoirs (《回忆鲁迅》人民文学出版社 1952; 《雪峰文集》vol. 4, 1983). These are indispensable but, as Zhu Zheng 朱正 and Chen Shuyu 陈漱渝 have shown in later biographical corrections, frequently shape anecdotes toward the Qu Qiubai line.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Hu Feng 胡风's pre-1955 essays (collected in 《胡风评论集》人民文学出版社 1984–85) preserved a more literary, less doctrinal Lu Xun; his 1955 purge as head of a "counter-revolutionary clique" made this reading politically untouchable for a generation.

### 2. The post-1978 "New Enlightenment" turn 新启蒙

The 1978–1989 decade produced the decisive break with the Mao-era reading. Scholars returned to Lu Xun's darkness, ambiguity, and individualist-Nietzschean roots.

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Li Zehou 李泽厚's《中国现代思想史论》(东方出版社 1987), in the chapter "胡适、陈独秀、鲁迅," frames Lu Xun within the "救亡压倒启蒙" ("national salvation overwhelmed enlightenment") thesis. Li argues Lu Xun's early Nietzschean-humanist position (the 1907–1908 essays in 《河南》月刊) was structurally incompatible with the 1930s League of Left-Wing Writers project into which he was pulled; the pull itself is what the thesis names. This is still the frame against which later readings define themselves.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Wang Yao 王瑶's《鲁迅作品论集》(人民文学出版社 1984) and《鲁迅的〈故事新编〉散论》re-read the late experimental fiction as literary rather than allegorical-political, opening the door for treating Lu Xun as a modernist.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Tang Tao 唐弢's《鲁迅的美学思想》(人民文学出版社 1984) restored aesthetic categories to a scholarship that had been dominated by political ones.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Wang Hui 汪晖's《反抗绝望：鲁迅及其文学世界》(*Resistance to Despair*, PhD dissertation 1988 under Tang Tao; first book edition 上海人民出版社 1991; revised 河北教育出版社 2000; further revised 三联书店 2008) is arguably the single most influential Chinese academic monograph on Lu Xun. Wang Hui's thesis: Lu Xun is not a writer of hope but a modernist whose entire ethical stance is the *resistance* to despair — a despair he cannot cure. The "iron house" passage from《呐喊·自序》is central. Wang explicitly dismantles the Qu Qiubai / Mao progression-narrative and replaces it with a synchronic structure of permanent contradiction ("历史的中间物," the figure of the intermediate).
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Qian Liqun 钱理群's trilogy —《心灵的探寻》(上海文艺出版社 1988),《与鲁迅相遇》(三联书店 2003),《鲁迅作品十五讲》(北京大学出版社 2003) — reads Lu Xun as "精神界之战士" (warrior of the spiritual realm), a humanist-individualist whose real enemy is "瞒和骗" (concealment and deception) rather than any particular class. Qian's reading is the standard humanist counterpoint to Wang Hui's modernist-pessimist reading; both camps are alive.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Wang Xiaoming 王晓明's《无法直面的人生：鲁迅传》(上海文艺出版社 1993) is the best-known Chinese literary biography of the post-1978 turn. Its thesis is in the title: Lu Xun's life was an "unfaceable" tragedy he could neither escape nor resolve. Wang Xiaoming is part of what became the "Shanghai school" of humanist criticism.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Wang Qiankun 王乾坤's《鲁迅的生命哲学》(人民文学出版社 1999) gives a philosophical reading rooted in European existentialism and Chinese Buddhist-existential resonances; it takes《野草》as Lu Xun's principal philosophical text.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Lin Xianzhi 林贤治's multi-volume《人间鲁迅》(花城出版社 1998 and later editions) and《鲁迅的最后十年》(中国社会科学出版社 2003) are extended literary biographies emphasizing Lu Xun's political dissent — a reading that has been controversial because it enlists Lu Xun for contemporary liberal positions.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Zhu Zheng 朱正's《一个人的呐喊：鲁迅1881–1936》(北京十月文艺出版社 2007) and earlier《鲁迅传略》editions are the gold standard for biographical fact-checking. Zhu corrected dozens of anecdotes repeated uncritically since the 1950s (see section II below).
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Chen Shuyu 陈漱渝, longtime senior researcher at the 北京鲁迅博物馆 and editor of《鲁迅研究月刊》, has produced the most meticulous archive-based corrections — his《搏击暗夜：鲁迅传》(作家出版社 2016) and edited collections consolidate fifty years of museum work.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Sun Yu 孙郁's《鲁迅与胡适》(长江文艺出版社 2007) and《鲁迅忧思录》(中国人民大学出版社 2012) read Lu Xun in dialogue with liberal contemporaries, recovering the comparability of Lu Xun and Hu Shi that Maoist scholarship had suppressed.

### 3. Third-generation Chinese scholarship (2000s–present)

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Gao Yuandong 高远东's《现代如何"拿来"》(复旦大学出版社 2009) is the best synthesis of how Lu Xun's "拿来主义" (grabbism, selective appropriation) functioned as a theory of cultural translation, pushing back against readings that make Lu Xun purely a negator.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Gao Yuanbao 郜元宝's《鲁迅六讲》(北京大学出版社 2007, expanded 2013) reads Lu Xun as a writer of Chinese-language difficulty rather than of transparent political message; a philological-literary reading.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Li Dongmu 李冬木 (Chinese scholar based at Bukkyō University, Kyoto) has produced the most important work on the *Japanese sources* of Lu Xun's "national character" 国民性 discourse. Key essays include "鲁迅与《支那人气质》" and "国民性话语的建构" (collected in 《鲁迅精神史探源》三联书店 2019 and related journal papers in 《鲁迅研究月刊》 and 《中国现代文学研究丛刊》). Li Dongmu demonstrates that the concept 国民性 reached Lu Xun primarily via Japanese Meiji translators and critics engaging Arthur Smith's *Chinese Characteristics* (1894); the concept is therefore a translingual, not indigenous, category. Together with Lydia Liu's work (below), this reframes the "national character critique" that is central to 《阿Q正传》.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` The 2007–2013 textbook-removal debate ("鲁迅大撤退") — the reduction or removal of pieces such as《药》,《阿Q正传》,《纪念刘和珍君》from PRC high-school 语文 textbooks — is best surveyed in contemporaneous《鲁迅研究月刊》special issues (2010–2011). Scholarly consensus is that the removals reflect pedagogical calculation rather than ideological rejection, but the polemic itself is a useful index of Lu Xun's continuing public weight.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` A 2010s trend treats《野草》and the late 杂文 as primary *literature* rather than documentary source: Sun Yu, Qian Liqun, and Wang Hui all return repeatedly to《野草》in this register. The turn is consolidated in English-language scholarship by Nicholas Kaldis (below).

### 4. English-language scholarship — foundational works

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` William A. Lyell's *Lu Hsün's Vision of Reality* (University of California Press, 1976) is the earliest systematic English monograph. Lyell's decisive contribution is the sustained attention to Shaoxing childhood, family decline, and the early medical-biological-evolutionary education — these framings shaped all subsequent anglophone work. Lyell is also the translator of the now-standard *Diary of a Madman and Other Stories* (University of Hawaii Press, 1990).
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Leo Ou-fan Lee 李欧梵, *Voices from the Iron House: A Study of Lu Xun* (Indiana University Press, 1987) is the foundational English-language critical biography. Lee's "iron house" reading — taking the metaphor from《呐喊·自序》as the unresolved dilemma of the reform-intellectual rather than a call to arms — converges with Wang Hui's independent Chinese-language reading, and together they define the post-1978 consensus that Lu Xun is a writer of irresolution.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Marston Anderson, *The Limits of Realism: Chinese Fiction in the Revolutionary Period* (University of California Press, 1990), ch. 2–3, gives the key account of Lu Xun's problematic relationship to realist fiction: the modernist-experimental elements in《呐喊》and《彷徨》undermine the realist project Lu Xun seems to commit to. This pre-empted the later English-language "modernist Lu Xun" consensus.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Jon Eugene von Kowallis, *The Lyrical Lu Xun: A Study of His Classical-style Verse* (University of Hawaii Press, 1996), recovers the *jiutishi* 旧体诗 corpus — the side of Lu Xun that Mao-era scholarship downplayed but that Lu Xun himself produced throughout life. Kowallis's *The Subtle Revolution: Poets of the "Old Schools" during Late Qing and Early Republican China* (UC Berkeley IEAS, 2006) and his translation of the early Wenyan essays (*Warriors of the Spirit*, UC Berkeley IEAS, 2023) complete the project of restoring Lu Xun's classical-literary depth to the English record.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` David Pollard, *The True Story of Lu Xun* (Chinese University of Hong Kong Press, 2002) is the most fact-oriented English biography — closer in method to Zhu Zheng than to Lee.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Theodore Huters, *Bringing the World Home: Appropriating the West in Late Qing and Early Republican China* (University of Hawaii Press, 2005), places Lu Xun in the wider intellectual-historical story of late-Qing/early-Republican translation and appropriation; a corrective to treating Lu Xun in isolation.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Gloria Davies, *Lu Xun's Revolution: Writing in a Time of Violence* (Harvard University Press, 2013), is the most sustained English-language study of the 1925–1936 period. Davies treats Lu Xun's late polemical prose as a practice of *political ethics* rather than as political commitment; her reading of the "Two Slogans" dispute and the League of Left-Wing Writers years is the current English-language benchmark.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Eileen J. Cheng, *Literary Remains: Death, Trauma, and Lu Xun's Refusal to Mourn* (University of Hawaii Press, 2013), gives the postmodern/psychoanalytic reading — Lu Xun's relation to death, mourning, and the unburied past. Cheng reads《野草》,《朝花夕拾》, and the late miscellany as a single project of refused mourning.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` David Der-wei Wang, *The Monster That Is History: History, Violence, and Fictional Writing in Twentieth-Century China* (University of California Press, 2004), ch. 1, gives the reading of Lu Xun that has dominated anglophone comparative literature since: Lu Xun's cannibalism motif in《狂人日记》as the structuring trope of twentieth-century Chinese historical writing.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Nicholas A. Kaldis, *The Chinese Prose Poem: A Study of Lu Xun's* Wild Grass (*Yecao*) (Cambria Press, 2014) is the dedicated monograph on《野草》and consolidates the English-language reading of the prose-poems as a single modernist work comparable to Baudelaire's *Petits poèmes en prose*.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Andrew F. Jones, *Developmental Fairy Tales: Evolutionary Thinking and Modern Chinese Culture* (Harvard University Press, 2011), ch. 1–2, traces Lu Xun's Lamarckian/Darwinian-Spencerian inheritance from the Jiangnan Naval Academy and Sendai years through《狂人日记》and《阿Q正传》, showing that "evolutionism" is not a youthful phase Lu Xun outgrew but a structuring commitment that complicates the Qu Qiubai progression-narrative.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Lydia H. Liu 刘禾, *Translingual Practice: Literature, National Character, and Translated Modernity — China, 1900–1937* (Stanford University Press, 1995), ch. 2 and 3, reads 阿Q and "national character" as products of translingual practice: "national character" (国民性 / *kokuminsei*) arrived in Lu Xun's Chinese via Japanese through Smith's English. With Li Dongmu's philological work, this has made the naive "Lu Xun critiques the Chinese people's essence" reading untenable.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Haiyan Lee, *Revolution of the Heart: A Genealogy of Love in China, 1900–1950* (Stanford University Press, 2007), gives the sharpest reading of《伤逝》 (Regret for the Past) and of Lu Xun's gender politics within the wider "structure of feeling" of Republican-era love discourse.

### 5. Japanese scholarship — the "Lu Xunology" tradition

Japanese scholarship matters disproportionately because Lu Xun lived in Japan 1902–1909 and absorbed his intellectual formation there; many archival breakthroughs come from Japan.

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Takeuchi Yoshimi 竹内好,《魯迅》(日本評論社 1944; revised editions 未來社 1961 and later; included in 《竹内好全集》 筑摩書房 1980–82, vol. 1) is the classic. Takeuchi's reading — that Lu Xun's decisive moment was not enlightenment but an inward *zhēngzhá* 挣扎 (struggle / agonistic self-confrontation), arrived at by around 1908 — remains the single most-cited non-Chinese reading. Takeuchi coined a contrast between "Japanese turnaround" (転向 as surface-switch) and Lu Xun's "Chinese *huixin* 回心" (conversion of the heart), making Lu Xun paradoxically the exemplar of a modernity *Japan* had failed to achieve. This is the seed of what later became the "Asia-as-method" tradition.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Maruyama Noboru 丸山昇,《魯迅——その文学と革命》 (平凡社 1965) and《魯迅と革命文学》 (紀伊國屋書店 1972) are the standard Japanese account of Lu Xun's late political life; Maruyama reads the 1927–1936 turn as *continuous* with Lu Xun's earlier individualism rather than a break — a position now dominant in both Chinese and English scholarship.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Itō Toramaru 伊藤虎丸,《魯迅と日本人：アジアの近代と「個」の思想》 (朝日出版社 1983; Chinese trans. 河北教育出版社 2000) — "Lu Xun and the Japanese: Asian modernity and the thought of 'the individual.'" Itō reads Lu Xun's Japan years as an encounter with a Meiji "individual" concept Lu Xun found in Kitamura Tōkoku and Takayama Chogyū's Nietzschean writings. Itō's "終末論的思想" (terminal / eschatological paradigm) reading is a productive counterpart to Wang Hui's "resistance to despair."
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Kitaoka Masako 北岡正子 has spent decades on forensic reconstruction of Lu Xun's Sendai-Tokyo years. Her essays collected as《魯迅 日本という異文化のなかで》 (関西大学出版部 2001) are the indispensable archival work on this period; she has also published the decisive sceptical reconsideration of the 1906 *huandengpian* 幻灯片 (slide-show) incident — see section III below.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Fujii Shōzō 藤井省三,《魯迅「故郷」の読書史》(創文社 1997; Chinese trans. 新世界出版社 2002) and《魯迅 東アジアを生きる文学》 (岩波書店 2011) are the standard East-Asian-reception studies; Fujii tracks how《故乡》(My Old Home) was read across Japanese, Korean, and Taiwanese school systems.

## II. Biographical Corrections — what post-1978 scholarship has fixed

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The Shaoxing childhood and family decline.** Lyell (*Vision of Reality*, UC Press 1976, ch. 1) and Zhu Zheng (《一个人的呐喊》 2007) established the forensic narrative: the 1893 Zhou Fuqing 周福清 (Lu Xun's paternal grandfather) examination-scandal imprisonment, and the ensuing family decline that sent the adolescent Lu Xun to the pawnshop on behalf of a tubercular father. The PRC textbook version sometimes flattened this into generic "feudal oppression"; the modern account restores the specificity.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The brothers' break of July 1923.** Zhou Zuoren 周作人 and Lu Xun's sudden rupture on 19 July 1923 has generated a century of speculation. The authoritative cautious synthesis is in Zhi An 止庵's editorial work on Zhou Zuoren (《周作人传》山东画报出版社 2009) and in Susan Daruvala's *Zhou Zuoren and an Alternative Chinese Response to Modernity* (Harvard University Asia Center, 2000). Both refuse to endorse the still-circulating gossip that implicates Habuto Nobuko 羽太信子 (Zhou Zuoren's Japanese wife) in an impropriety with Lu Xun; neither brother left documentary evidence sufficient to decide, and the gossip traces to interested later parties. Scholarly consensus is: household-financial conflict + cultural-political divergence is enough to explain the break; the sexual-scandal hypothesis is under-evidenced.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The 1906 slide-show 幻灯片 incident.** The canonical story — that Lu Xun in Sendai saw a newsreel slide of a Chinese man being beheaded by the Japanese while fellow Chinese watched passively, and that this turned him from medicine to literature — is in his own《呐喊·自序》(1922) and《藤野先生》(1926). Kitaoka Masako's archival work (《魯迅 日本という異文化のなかで》 関西大学出版部 2001) shows that the Sendai Medical School's surviving lantern-slide inventory does not contain the described slide, and that the incident as narrated combines at least two distinct visual experiences. Most scholars (Lee 1987; Davies 2013; Li Dongmu 2019) now accept that the narrated incident is a retrospective composite — a *true* psychological memory rendered as a *composed* scene. This does not falsify Lu Xun's reading of his own turn; it reframes the 1922 autobiographical genre.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **Dr. Sudō 須藤五百三 and Lu Xun's final illness.** Zhou Haiying 周海婴's《鲁迅与我七十年》 (南海出版公司 2001), the memoir by Lu Xun's son, raised the question of whether the delayed X-ray diagnosis by Dr. Sudō in 1936 was negligence or deliberate sabotage (Sudō had connections to Japanese militarist circles). Chen Shuyu 陈漱渝 (《鲁迅研究月刊》 essays 2001–2003) and Zhu Zheng (《一个人的呐喊》 2007, epilogue) reviewed the surviving medical records and rejected the sabotage hypothesis: the diagnostic failure is consistent with the state of 1936 TB-management practice in Shanghai; the relevant Japanese-physician context (including the American consultant Dr. Dunn's concurring diagnosis) does not support criminal intent. The scholarly consensus, with caveats, is that Lu Xun died of advanced pulmonary tuberculosis with pneumothorax and likely emphysema, not of a deliberately delayed diagnosis.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The "whipping students" anecdote and similar.** Several anecdotes of a stern, violent Lu Xun — beating students, humiliating opponents — trace to unreliable 1930s memoirs and hostile post-1927 political pamphlets. Zhu Zheng's editorial work systematically strips these out; Lee (1987) warns anglophone readers against them.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The saintly "民族魂" figure is a 1937+ construction.** The silk banner of "民族魂" placed on his coffin at the 1936 funeral was composed by a committee including Shen Junru 沈钧儒; the elevation to "soul of the nation" was consolidated in 1937–1942 Yan'an and Shanghai wartime discourse. Qian Liqun (《与鲁迅相遇》2003) and Wang Hui (*Resistance to Despair*) both treat the "民族魂" figure as a necessary wartime icon that nonetheless obscures the actual writer.

## III. Unresolved Controversies — the research frontier

### 1. Was the 1927 political turn sudden or long-prepared?

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` The "sudden break" reading (Qu Qiubai 1933; Feng Xuefeng 1952) treats the April 1927 Canton massacre and the subsequent flight to Shanghai as Lu Xun's decisive radicalization.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Maruyama Noboru (1965/1972), Leo Lee (1987), Wang Hui (1991/2008), and Gloria Davies (2013) all argue instead for *continuity*: the late polemical prose extends commitments visible in the 1907–1908 Wenyan essays (《文化偏至论》,《摩罗诗力说》) — individualism against the crowd, skepticism about instrumental reason, refusal of conventional modernization optimism. On this reading, the 1927 moment adjusts Lu Xun's *targets*, not his *stance*.

### 2. The League of Left-Wing Writers — instrumentalized or autonomous?

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` The charge that Zhou Yang 周扬 and the CCP cultural apparatus used Lu Xun as a figurehead is associated with Hu Feng's memoirs and with post-1978 unofficial reappraisals (Lin Xianzhi; Sun Yu).
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Davies (*Lu Xun's Revolution*, Harvard UP 2013) rejects this framing as too binary: Lu Xun knew he was being managed and refused it episodically (the《答徐懋庸并关于抗日统一战线问题》letter of August 1936 is Davies's decisive text), which establishes both that he was a target of instrumentalization *and* that he was not successfully instrumentalized.

### 3. The "Two Slogans" dispute of 1936

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` "民族革命战争的大众文学" (Lu Xun / Feng Xuefeng / Hu Feng camp) vs. "国防文学" (Zhou Yang camp). The 1950s–70s PRC reading treated Zhou Yang's slogan as orthodox and Lu Xun's as a "loyal-opposition" supplement; the post-1978 scholarship (Maruyama; Lee; Davies) reads the dispute as Lu Xun's last attempt to keep literary-ethical independence from direct Party-line dictation. The dispute is unresolved because *both* sides later claimed him.

### 4. Mao's "hardest bones" reading vs. Nietzschean-individualist reading

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` This is the single most important live contest. Mao's 1940 reading makes Lu Xun a revolutionary hero. The Wang Hui / Qian Liqun / Takeuchi / Lee reading makes him a modernist of irresolution whose "hardness" is precisely his refusal of any final position, including revolutionary optimism. Both camps continue to publish, teach, and argue; no synthesis is stable.

### 5. Critical reader of Marxism or true believer?

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Lu Xun translated Plekhanov and Lunacharsky in the late 1920s–early 1930s (see his translation collection 《艺术论》). The question is whether he read these as instruments of literary-theoretical clarification or as doctrinal commitments. Itō Toramaru (1983) and Davies (2013) argue for the former; some PRC Lu Xun studies in the 1950s–70s and a current "new-left" Chinese reading argue for the latter. Wang Hui's position — that Marxism functions for Lu Xun as *one more resource in the struggle against despair*, neither dogma nor decoration — remains the scholarly center of gravity.

### 6. The misogyny question

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]`《伤逝》 (Regret for the Past, 1925) has been read both as a critique of "new-woman" idealism (patriarchal-residual) and as a critique of the male narrator's complicity in Zijun's destruction (proto-feminist). Haiyan Lee (*Revolution of the Heart*, Stanford UP 2007) and Tze-lan Deborah Sang (*The Emerging Lesbian*, University of Chicago Press 2003) give the current English-language readings that tilt toward the latter. The biographical question — Lu Xun's own treatment of Zhu An 朱安 (the wife his mother arranged in 1906, effectively abandoned) and his late partnership with Xu Guangping 许广平 — is handled forensically in Zhu Zheng (2007) and Chen Shuyu (2016); scholarly consensus is that the treatment of Zhu An is the most ethically difficult fact in Lu Xun's private life and is not resolved by the relationship with Xu Guangping.

### 7. "National character" — Orientalist residue or radical self-critique?

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Lydia Liu (Stanford UP 1995) and Li Dongmu (三联书店 2019) have shown that Lu Xun's "国民性" discourse descends genealogically from Arthur Smith's *Chinese Characteristics* (1894) via Meiji Japanese translation, and therefore carries Orientalist pre-suppositions. The live controversy is whether this genealogical fact *taints* the critique (making 阿Q a racialist caricature) or whether Lu Xun's ironic reflexivity *turns* the borrowed concept into a radical self-critique in which the Chinese reader is asked to recognize himself. Most current scholarship (Anderson 1990; Wang 2004; Davies 2013) holds the latter; this remains a live pedagogical debate.

## IV. Revisionist Findings Now Stable in Scholarship

- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **Lu Xun was funnier and more sarcastic than Mao-era sanctification allowed.** Qian Liqun (《与鲁迅相遇》 2003) and Sun Yu (《鲁迅忧思录》 2012) are the standard Chinese rehabilitations of the *humorist* Lu Xun. Eileen Cheng (2013) and Kaldis (2014) make the parallel English case with reference to《野草》and the miscellany.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **Lu Xun's classical-poetry output matters.** Kowallis's *Lyrical Lu Xun* (University of Hawaii Press 1996) documents a working *jiutishi* poet beneath the vernacular reformer; this has displaced the old caricature of Lu Xun as a fiction-writer-plus-polemicist.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **"National character" is a translated concept, not an indigenous one.** Liu (1995); Li Dongmu (2019). Consolidated.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The 1906 slide-show story is a composite, not a verbatim memory.** Kitaoka Masako (2001). Consolidated.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The brothers' break is under-determined by the evidence; the Habuto Nobuko scandal is unsupported.** Daruvala (Harvard Asia Center 2000); Zhi An (2009). Consolidated.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **The sabotage theory of Lu Xun's death is unsupported.** Chen Shuyu; Zhu Zheng (2007). Consolidated.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **《野草》 is a modernist prose-poem sequence comparable to European fin-de-siècle experiments — not a set of political allegories.** Wang Qiankun (1999); Kaldis (2014); Cheng (2013). Consolidated.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **Lu Xun's evolutionism is structural, not a phase he outgrew.** Jones (*Developmental Fairy Tales*, Harvard UP 2011). Consolidated in anglophone work; less fully absorbed in PRC textbook tradition.
- `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **《阿Q正传》 is not a portrait of "the Chinese essence" but a diagnostic of the reader.** Anderson (1990); Wang (2004); Davies (2013). Consolidated in scholarship, contested in pedagogy.

## V. Sources and Journals

Primary journal of Chinese Lu Xun studies: **《鲁迅研究月刊》** (Beijing Lu Xun Museum, 1980–), indexed in CNKI. Also **《中国现代文学研究丛刊》** (Chinese Modern Literature Studies, 中国现代文学馆).

Principal anglophone journals: *Modern Chinese Literature and Culture* (MCLC, Ohio State); *Journal of Asian Studies* (Association for Asian Studies); *positions: asia critique* (Duke UP); *T'oung Pao* (Brill); *Modern China* (Sage).

Japanese: **《日本中国学会報》**, Iwanami 岩波書店 and Chikuma 筑摩書房 editions of Takeuchi Yoshimi, Maruyama Noboru, and Itō Toramaru.

Authoritative edition of Lu Xun's own writings: **《鲁迅全集》** 人民文学出版社 1981 edition (16 vols.) and 2005 revised edition (18 vols.) — the 2005 edition's annotation corrects many 1981 political-era annotations and is now the scholarly standard.
