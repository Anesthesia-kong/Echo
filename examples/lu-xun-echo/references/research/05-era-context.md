# Era Context — Lu Xun (1881–1936)

> Research dimension 5 of 6. Distillation target: the historical, political, cultural, economic, and intellectual conditions of late Qing through Republican China through which Zhou Shuren lived. Without this scaffolding, his writing reads as "literary pessimism." With it, it is a diagnostic response to measurable catastrophe.

Claim labels throughout:
- **[documented]** — fact directly recorded in primary sources or Lu Xun's own writings
- **[attested]** — widely corroborated in mainstream Chinese and Western historiography
- **[modern-scholarly-inference]** — interpretation advanced by modern specialists (Schwarcz, Lin Yü-sheng, Leo Lee, Hockx, Huters, Spence, Hsü, Fairbank, 陈旭麓, 余英时, 茅海建)

---

## 1. Macro-Historical Timeline (1840–1936): The Eight-Decade Collapse He Inherited and Lived Through

Lu Xun was born 25 September 1881 into the reign of the Guangxu Emperor, forty years into a civilizational crisis that had not yet bottomed out. He died 19 October 1936, less than nine months before the Marco Polo Bridge Incident touched off full Sino-Japanese war. His entire 55-year life is bracketed by catastrophe that accelerates rather than abates. [attested]

### 1.1 The pre-birth shocks that framed his inheritance

- **First Opium War 1839–42** — Treaty of Nanjing (1842): Hong Kong ceded, five treaty ports opened, indemnity 21 million silver dollars. [documented]
- **Second Opium War 1856–60** — Anglo-French sack of the Yuanmingyuan (1860); Treaties of Tianjin and Beijing institutionalized extraterritoriality, treaty-port system, foreign legations, missionary access, and the Maritime Customs Service under foreign administration. [documented]
- **Taiping Rebellion 1850–64** — the largest civil war of the 19th century; demographers estimate 20–30 million dead (Spence, *God's Chinese Son*). The devastation of the Lower Yangzi where Lu Xun would be born (Shaoxing is in Zhejiang, adjacent to the core Taiping theater) left multi-generational scars in the gentry class his family belonged to. [attested]
- **Nian Rebellion 1851–68, Hui/Muslim rebellions in Yunnan 1856–73 and the Northwest 1862–77** — further millions dead. Mass death was not an abstraction in the China of his grandfather's generation. [attested]
- **Self-Strengthening Movement 自強運動 1861–1895** — Zeng Guofan, Li Hongzhang, Zuo Zongtang and the "Tongzhi Restoration" attempted selective Western technology adoption ("中學為體, 西學為用"). By the time Lu Xun entered school the movement's failure was already visible. [attested]

### 1.2 His boyhood and the exam-system collapse (1881–1898)

- **1893 Zhou Fuqing exam-bribery scandal** — Lu Xun's paternal grandfather, a jinshi-degree holder in the Hanlin Academy, was arrested for attempting to bribe an examination official on behalf of Lu Xun's father Zhou Boyi (Zhou Fengyi). He was sentenced to death, commuted to imprisonment in Hangzhou until 1901. The family paid enormous bribes annually to keep the sentence commuted. [documented — Lu Xun's own recollections in 《呐喊》自序 and correspondence; biographies by 周作人 *鲁迅的故家*, 许寿裳 *亡友鲁迅印象记*]
- **c. 1896 death of Zhou Boyi** — Lu Xun's father, humiliated and blocked from examinations after the scandal, sickened (likely tuberculosis and dropsy) and died at 37 after years of treatment by traditional doctors whose prescriptions Lu Xun as a boy was sent to fill. This experience ("走了许多年那走熟了的药店柜台") is the origin of his lifelong contempt for traditional medicine and the autobiographical core of his 1922 preface to 《呐喊》. [documented]
- **Sino-Japanese War 1894–95** — defeat by Japan, Treaty of Shimonoseki: Taiwan ceded, Korea independence (= Japanese sphere), 200-million-tael indemnity. The psychological shock to Chinese elites was greater than all previous defeats combined: a "little brother" state the Chinese had looked down on for a millennium had overtaken them in a single generation. This is the moment that made Lu Xun's generation — born around 1880 — a specifically post-Shimonoseki cohort. [attested — Fairbank, *Cambridge History* Vol. 11; 陈旭麓 《近代中国社会的新陈代谢》]
- **1898 Hundred Days' Reform 戊戌變法** — Guangxu Emperor backed Kang Youwei and Liang Qichao's reform program; Cixi's coup on 21 September 1898 ended it in 103 days; "six gentlemen" including Tan Sitong beheaded at Caishikou. Lu Xun was 17, then in Nanjing entering the Jiangnan Naval Academy. [documented]

### 1.3 Adolescence and Japan years (1898–1909)

- **1898 Yan Fu's 《天演論》** (translation-paraphrase of T.H. Huxley's *Evolution and Ethics*) published. Lu Xun read it in Nanjing and later wrote it "opened my eyes"; the phrase 物競天擇, 適者生存 became the lingua franca of his generation. [documented — Lu Xun's 《朝花夕拾·琐记》]
- **1900 Boxer Uprising 義和團運動** — Eight-Nation Alliance (Britain, France, Germany, US, Russia, Japan, Italy, Austria-Hungary) occupied Beijing; 1901 Boxer Protocol imposed 450 million tael indemnity (one per Chinese) payable over 39 years. Foreign troops remained garrisoned in the legation quarter. [documented]
- **1902** — Lu Xun, having graduated from the School of Railways and Mines branch of the Jiangnan Naval Academy, sailed for Japan on a government scholarship at age 21. [documented]
- **1904–06 Sendai Medical School** — the famous "slide incident" 幻灯片事件: during a microbiology class, lantern slides of the Russo-Japanese war (fought 1904–05 on Chinese soil) showed a Chinese spy being executed by Japanese soldiers while a crowd of Chinese onlookers watched passively. The Japanese students cheered "Banzai!"; Lu Xun decided medicine could not cure the deeper disease and switched to literature. [documented — 《呐喊》自序 1922]
- **1906 arranged marriage to Zhu An 朱安** — recalled home by his mother on a fabricated illness pretext; married a woman with bound feet whom he had never met. He returned to Japan the same week; the marriage was never consummated, but he supported Zhu An financially until his death. [documented]
- **1905 abolition of the Imperial Examination System 科舉** — by imperial edict, the thousand-year-old path for literati into the state apparatus was simply terminated. The gentry class had no replacement institution. This is arguably the single most important structural event of Lu Xun's lifetime: it removed the civilizational operating system. [attested — 余英时 on "the dis-establishment of the scholar class"]
- **1908 Zhang Binglin 章太炎 in Tokyo** — Lu Xun and brother Zhou Zuoren attended private classes with the revolutionary, Buddhist-influenced, philologically brilliant Zhang. His classical-Buddhist-anarchist register shaped Lu Xun's early essay prose. [documented]
- **1907–08 early essays** — 《文化偏至論》, 《摩羅詩力說》, 《科學史教篇》, 《破惡聲論》 — Nietzsche, Stirner, Byron, Shelley, Petőfi, Kierkegaard make their Chinese-language entry in part through these essays. Written in dense classical Chinese; most readers ignored them at the time. [documented]

### 1.4 Return, the 1911 Revolution, and the "Wilderness Years" (1909–1917)

- **1909** Lu Xun returned to China, teaching in Hangzhou and Shaoxing. [documented]
- **1911 Xinhai Revolution 辛亥革命** — Wuchang Uprising 10 October 1911; Qing abdication 12 February 1912; Republic of China proclaimed. Lu Xun briefly joined the local revolutionary government in Shaoxing as an education officer and watched the revolution locally degenerate within weeks. This personal observation — revolution as surface change with unchanged power relations underneath — haunts all his mature fiction (《阿Q正傳》 1921–22 is its direct processing). [attested; fictional processing documented]
- **1912 Yuan Shikai presidency** — Sun Yat-sen ceded the presidency to Yuan to secure Qing abdication; Yuan moved the capital to Beijing. Lu Xun joined the Ministry of Education and moved to Beijing; he would work there as a mid-level civil servant until 1926. [documented]
- **1913 Second Revolution** crushed by Yuan; Song Jiaoren assassinated in Shanghai (March 1913). [documented]
- **1915–16 Yuan Shikai monarchy attempt 洪憲帝制** — Yuan declared himself emperor December 1915; reign nominally began 1 January 1916; rebellions forced abdication 22 March; Yuan dead 6 June 1916. Total disaster; leaves no successor regime; Beijing central government becomes a shell contested by warlord cliques. [documented]
- **1916–1928 Warlord Era 軍閥混戰** — the Beiyang system fragments. Major cliques: **Zhili** (直系, Feng Guozhang, Cao Kun, Wu Peifu), **Anhui** (皖系, Duan Qirui), **Fengtian** (奉系, Zhang Zuolin), **Shanxi** (Yan Xishan), **Guangxi** (new and old cliques), plus the Yunnan and Sichuan warlords. Beijing changed hands roughly a dozen times. The Ministry of Education where Lu Xun worked had its salary suspended for months at a time. [attested; Lu Xun's diaries record the unpaid-salary periods]
- **1915 onward New Culture Movement 新文化運動** — Chen Duxiu founded 《青年雜誌》 (renamed 《新青年》 New Youth) in September 1915. See §2 below. [documented]

### 1.5 The productive middle period (1918–1926)

- **May 1918** — 《狂人日記》 Diary of a Madman published in *New Youth* vol. 4 no. 5, first signed "Lu Xun" 鲁迅. First major modern-vernacular short story in Chinese. The diagnosis: four thousand years of Chinese history are written between the lines of Confucian moralism, and the hidden text reads 吃人 "eat people." [documented]
- **4 May 1919 May Fourth Movement 五四運動** — ~3,000 students from 13 Beijing universities protested the Treaty of Versailles awarding German concessions in Shandong to Japan rather than returning them to China; Cao Rulin's house burned; arrests; wave of nationwide strikes and merchant boycotts; Chinese delegation refused to sign the treaty. The single most important political-cultural rupture of the Republican period; its date becomes metonym for the entire intellectual-cultural movement. [documented]
- **1920–26 Beijing teaching and writing** — lecturer at Peking University, Beijing Women's Normal, Beijing Normal. Publishes 《呐喊》 (1923), 《彷徨》 (1926), 《中國小說史略》 (1923–24, a rigorously philological literary history that remains authoritative), 《華蓋集》 polemical essays, translations from Russian and Japanese. [documented]
- **1921 CCP founded in Shanghai (July)**; **1924 first KMT–CCP United Front** negotiated; **1925–27 Northern Expedition 北伐** to unify China begins under Chiang Kai-shek after Sun Yat-sen's death (March 1925). [documented]
- **30 May 1925 May Thirtieth Incident 五卅慘案** — Shanghai Municipal Police (British-commanded) fired on Chinese protesters; triggered nationwide anti-imperialist strike wave. [documented]
- **18 March 1926 Beijing Massacre 三·一八慘案** — Duan Qirui government troops fired on student protesters outside the State Council; 47 killed, ~200 injured. Lu Xun's student **Liu Hezhen 劉和珍** (22) was among the dead. His essay 《記念劉和珍君》 written weeks later is the most frequently anthologized modern Chinese essay in PRC education. [documented]
- **August 1926** Lu Xun, on a KMT/Beiyang blacklist, fled Beijing for Xiamen (Amoy) University in Fujian; moved to Sun Yat-sen University in Guangzhou in January 1927. [documented]

### 1.6 The decisive leftward turn and Shanghai years (1927–1936)

- **12 April 1927 Shanghai Purge 四·一二政變** — Chiang Kai-shek, secretly coordinating with Green Gang leader Du Yuesheng and Shanghai industrialists, massacred Communist and left-KMT organizers in Shanghai. Hundreds to thousands killed. Paralleled by purges in other cities through April and May. This inaugurates the **White Terror 白色恐怖** that will define the remainder of Lu Xun's life. [documented]
- **15 April 1927 Guangzhou purge** — Lu Xun watched the same pattern repeat at Sun Yat-sen University; protested when the university refused to intervene to save arrested students; resigned. He later wrote that his previous reliance on "evolutionary" (進化論) confidence that youth necessarily surpass their elders had been shattered — it turned out that young people arrested and killed young people. This is his documented decisive break with his earlier Yan-Fu-Darwinian framework. [documented — see Qian Liqun 錢理群's scholarship; Lu Xun's 1932 preface to 《三閑集》]
- **October 1927** Lu Xun moved to Shanghai with Xu Guangping 許廣平 (his former student, later common-law wife). He lived in the **Hongkou district of the International Settlement** until his death. He never held another official position; lived by royalties, editing, and occasional translation contracts. [documented]
- **1928 Nanjing Decade begins** — KMT unifies China (nominally) under the Nanjing government. Continuous low-intensity civil war with residual warlords, CCP base areas, and Japan. [attested]
- **1929–30 "Revolutionary Literature" debates 革命文學論爭** — Creation Society (Guo Moruo, Cheng Fangwu) and Sun Society (Jiang Guangci) attacked Lu Xun from the left as "outdated," "petty-bourgeois," even "double-dealing counter-revolutionary." Lu Xun replied polemically while simultaneously reading Plekhanov, Lunacharsky, Trotsky-on-literature in Japanese translation. He translated Plekhanov's 《藝術論》 (1929) and Lunacharsky's essays. His Marxism arrived via these polemics and was non-doctrinaire: he never joined the CCP. [documented]
- **2 March 1930 League of Left-Wing Writers 中國左翼作家聯盟 (左聯)** founded in Shanghai. Lu Xun was among the founders and gave the keynote; he was the single most important figure of the League but never its ideological enforcer. [documented]
- **7 February 1931** — the **"Five Martyrs of the Left League" 左聯五烈士** (Rou Shi 柔石, Hu Yepin, Li Weisen, Yin Fu, Feng Keng) were executed with 19 others by the KMT garrison at Longhua, Shanghai. Rou Shi was Lu Xun's close friend and protégé. Lu Xun's essay 《為了忘卻的記念》 For the Sake of Forgetting, Memorial (February 1933, written on the second anniversary) is the direct response. He himself went into hiding for a month. [documented]
- **18 September 1931 Mukden Incident 九·一八事變** — Japanese Kwantung Army staged the Liutiaohu railway explosion; seized Manchuria; established puppet state **Manchukuo 滿洲國** 1 March 1932 under the last Qing emperor Puyi. [documented]
- **28 January 1932 Shanghai Incident 一·二八事變** — Japanese naval landing and bombing of Zhabei district. Lu Xun's home was in the affected area; he and family fled to Uchiyama Shoten 內山書店 (Kanzo Uchiyama's Japanese-run bookstore that functioned as his de facto mail drop, refuge, and neutral meeting point from 1927 onward). [documented]
- **1932–34** Escalating KMT book bans, censorship, arrests. Lu Xun used **100+ pen names** during this period to get essays past the Central Propaganda Department's blacklists of "Lu Xun" as an author. Biographers catalog names such as 何家干, 隋洛文, 宴之敖, 旅隼, 白舌, 家干, 遊光, 豐之餘, 虞明, and many more. [documented]
- **1934–36** — Lu Xun's tuberculosis (diagnosed 1925, relatively stable through 1933) worsens sharply. He continues to work; produces his late essay collections 《且介亭雜文》 (1934, 1935, 1936 — the title puns on "半租界" "semi-concession" reflecting his Shanghai foreign-concession address at 施高塔路 Shikou Road, then North Sichuan Road).
- **1935–36 December 9th Movement 一二·九運動** (9 Dec 1935) — Beijing students protest the Japanese-orchestrated "North China Autonomy" push; wave of anti-Japanese youth mobilization. Lu Xun supports from Shanghai but is too ill to participate. [documented]
- **1935 Long March completed** — CCP arrives Yan'an; Mao emerges as leader post-Zunyi conference (January 1935). Lu Xun never visits, never joins CCP, but his sympathies and correspondence (with Feng Xuefeng acting as liaison) were with them. [attested]
- **August 1936 "Two Slogans Debate" 兩個口號論爭** — conflict between Zhou Yang's "國防文學" "National Defense Literature" slogan (CCP-aligned) and Lu Xun-Hu Feng's "民族革命戰爭的大眾文學" "Mass Literature of the National Revolutionary War" slogan. Lu Xun's published letter "Reply to Xu Maoyong and on the Question of the Anti-Japanese United Front" (《答徐懋庸並關於抗日統一戰線問題》, August 1936) was his last major intervention and reveals his refusal to be disciplined by CCP cultural cadres even while aligning with the anti-Japanese front. [documented]
- **19 October 1936** — Lu Xun dies in Shanghai of tuberculosis and emphysema at 05:25 at age 55. Tens of thousands attend the funeral procession to Wanguo Cemetery; a silk banner reading 「民族魂」 "Soul of the Nation" was draped over the coffin. [documented]

Eight months and eighteen days later, the Marco Polo Bridge Incident (7 July 1937) opens full Sino-Japanese war. Lu Xun had seen everything except the worst. [attested]

## 2. Intellectual Landscape

### 2.1 The late-Qing translator-ideologues

- **Yan Fu 嚴復 (1854–1921)** — British-trained naval officer turned translator; rendered Huxley's *Evolution and Ethics* (《天演論》 1898), Adam Smith's *Wealth of Nations* (《原富》), Mill's *On Liberty* (《群己權界論》), Spencer's *Study of Sociology* (《群學肄言》), Montesquieu's *Spirit of the Laws* (《法意》), Jevons's logic. Yan Fu's archaic-prose translations smuggled in **Spencerian social Darwinism** under the cover of Huxley's more ambivalent text — the framing that gave the late-Qing intelligentsia the metaphor of the Chinese "race" (種) as a biological unit threatened with extinction by "natural selection" in the international state-struggle. Lu Xun read 《天演論》 in Nanjing around 1898; the Darwinian frame is baseline for his entire generation. [documented — Benjamin Schwartz, *In Search of Wealth and Power*, 1964]
- **Liang Qichao 梁啓超 (1873–1929)** — reformer, journalist, exile in Japan post-1898; his 《新民說》 (1902–06, serialized in 《新民叢報》) articulated the 新民 "new citizen" discourse — the claim that China's problem was not weapons or institutions but the moral-psychological makeup of the Chinese people themselves, who needed to be "made new." This frame — that the problem is the people's character, and literature's task is to renovate it — is directly inherited by Lu Xun and is the logic of his Sendai decision to switch from medicine to literature. [attested — Joseph Levenson, *Liang Ch'i-ch'ao and the Mind of Modern China*, 1953; Hao Chang, *Liang Ch'i-ch'ao and Intellectual Transition in China*, 1971]
- **Zhang Binglin 章太炎 (1869–1936)** — philologist of the Ancient Script 古文 school, revolutionary, Buddhist-Daoist synthesist, anarchist-leaning, jailed by the Qing 1903–06 for the Su Bao 蘇報 case. Lu Xun attended his Tokyo classes in 1908 on 《說文解字》 and classical philology. Zhang's dense, antique prose register shaped Lu Xun's early essays and his lifelong ability to shift into classical Chinese for ironic effect. [documented — 許壽裳 recollections]
- **Kang Youwei 康有為 (1858–1927)** — by Lu Xun's mature period, Kang was the face of a discredited, monarchy-restoration-supporting conservatism; Lu Xun treated him as representative of the generation that had failed. [attested]

### 2.2 The New Culture Movement (新文化運動, ~1915–1923)

Centered on the magazine **《新青年》 New Youth** (edited by Chen Duxiu 陳獨秀 from September 1915, joined by Hu Shi 胡適, Li Dazhao 李大釗, Qian Xuantong 錢玄同, Liu Bannong 劉半農, Lu Xun, Zhou Zuoren). Core programs:

1. **Attack on Confucianism — 打倒孔家店** "down with the Confucius shop" (the phrase is often associated with the period though its exact origin is debated). The critique held that the Three Bonds and filial piety ideology were the foundations of authoritarian political culture. [attested]
2. **Vernacular Chinese — 白話文運動** — Hu Shi's 「文學改良芻議」 "A Preliminary Proposal for Literary Reform" (*New Youth*, January 1917) and Chen Duxiu's more militant 「文學革命論」 "On Literary Revolution" (February 1917) called for replacing 文言 classical Chinese with 白話 vernacular. Lu Xun's 《狂人日記》 (May 1918) was the first major *creative* vindication of the program. By the early 1920s the Ministry of Education mandated vernacular in primary-school textbooks — a swift and nearly total institutional victory. [documented]
3. **"Mr. Science" 賽先生 and "Mr. Democracy" 德先生** — Chen Duxiu's slogan (1919) encapsulating the program's imported values. [documented]
4. **European-literature translation** — Russian realism and late-Romantic rebel poetry dominated; Hu Shi's circle favored Anglo-American liberalism; Zhou brothers (Lu Xun and Zhou Zuoren) favored Russian, Japanese, and small-European-nation writers (Polish, Hungarian, Czech, Serbian) — the **"literatures of oppressed peoples"** 被壓迫民族的文學. Their 1909 《域外小說集》 (Collected Foreign Fiction) was a commercial failure that pre-figured their later curatorial stance. [documented]

### 2.3 The post-1921 literary-society landscape

- **文學研究會 Literary Research Association** (Beijing, January 1921) — Mao Dun 茅盾, Zheng Zhenduo 鄭振鐸, Ye Shengtao, Xu Dishan, Bing Xin, later Lao She. Slogan 「為人生」 "for life"; social realist orientation; published through Commercial Press's 《小說月報》 *Fiction Monthly*. [documented]
- **創造社 Creation Society** (Tokyo/Shanghai, July 1921) — Guo Moruo 郭沫若, Yu Dafu 郁達夫, Cheng Fangwu, Zhang Ziping. Initially 「為藝術」 "for art," German-Romantic, Whitmanesque; shifted sharply to Marxism around 1925–28 and became Lu Xun's left-flank antagonists in the 1928–30 debates. [documented]
- **新月社 Crescent Moon Society** (Beijing, 1923) — Xu Zhimo 徐志摩, Hu Shi, Liang Shiqiu 梁實秋, Wen Yiduo 聞一多, Shen Congwen 沈從文 (in its orbit). Anglo-American liberal, aesthetic, formally experimental; Liang Shiqiu's Irving-Babbitt-New-Humanism was Lu Xun's right-flank antagonist in the late 1920s. [documented]
- **語絲社 Threads of Talk** (1924, around the magazine 《語絲》) — Lu Xun, Zhou Zuoren, Lin Yutang, Qian Xuantong. Personal-essay-centered; Lu Xun's home base before Shanghai. [documented]
- **中國左翼作家聯盟 League of Left-Wing Writers** (Shanghai, 2 March 1930) — see §1.6. [documented]

### 2.4 External intellectual inputs absorbed by Lu Xun personally

- **Nietzsche** — entered Chinese via Japanese translations (e.g. Ikuta Chōkō 生田長江). Lu Xun's 1907 《文化偏至論》 paraphrases *Zarathustra*'s "overman" in classical Chinese; he translated the preface to *Also sprach Zarathustra* into vernacular in 1918 and 1920. The Nietzschean cultural-critic stance — rejecting both the mob and the self-satisfied elite — is a lifelong register. [documented — Raoul David Findeisen's scholarship on Nietzsche in China]
- **Max Stirner 施蒂納** (*Der Einzige und sein Eigentum*) — cited in 《文化偏至論》 1907; the extreme-individualist frame matters to the young Lu Xun though he did not sustain it. [documented]
- **Kierkegaard** — referenced in the same early essays but less direct influence. [attested]
- **Byron and Shelley** — central to 《摩羅詩力說》 1907–08 ("On the Power of Mara Poetry"); the Romantic rebel-poet as cultural antidote to a "voiceless China." Petőfi Sándor, the Polish Mickiewicz, and the Russian Pushkin-Lermontov-Gogol lineage appear in the same essay. [documented]
- **Sándor Petőfi 裴多菲** (1823–49) — the Hungarian revolutionary poet killed at 26; Lu Xun translated him and cited the line "希望本是無所謂有,無所謂無的" reformulation of "hope is vain as despair is vain." This Petőfi-line-as-epitaph is probably the single most quoted sentence in the Lu Xun corpus. [documented]
- **Gogol** — *Diary of a Madman* (1835) is the direct title-source for Lu Xun's 1918 debut. Lu Xun later translated Gogol's *Dead Souls* (1935–36, unfinished at his death). [documented]
- **Leonid Andreyev 安德烈夫** — Russian Symbolist-horror-realist whose 《默》 (*Silence*), 《謾》 (*The Lie*), 《紅笑》 (*The Red Laugh*) Lu Xun translated in 《域外小說集》. Andreyev's black ironic voice is one of the closest foreign registers to Lu Xun's own. [documented]
- **Chekhov, Dostoyevsky** — present but more diffuse; mediated through Japanese and German translations. [attested]
- **Natsume Sōseki 夏目漱石** — Lu Xun attended his public lectures in Tokyo and later rented a room in Sōseki's former house in Hongō. *Kokoro*'s register of quiet despair under Meiji modernization is a structural analog Lu Xun recognized. [documented]
- **Ernst Haeckel** — Lu Xun read Haeckel's *The Riddle of the Universe* (*Die Welträtsel* 1899) in Japanese and German; his 1908 《科學史教篇》 draws on it. Haeckel imported both a biological-evolutionary monism and a scientism the mature Lu Xun would distance from. [documented]
- **Marx, Plekhanov, Lunacharsky, Trotsky-on-literature** — absorbed 1928–30 in Shanghai, chiefly through Japanese translations. Lu Xun translated Plekhanov's 《藝術論》 (*Art and Social Life*) in 1929 and Lunacharsky's 《藝術論》 essays. His Marxism is literary-critical, not Bolshevik-operational; he never renounces non-Party independence. [documented]

### 2.5 The vernacular-prose revolution as a political act

The switch from 文言 to 白話 was never "merely stylistic." Classical Chinese required roughly a decade of dedicated study to write competently; vernacular Chinese was learnable in months. The choice was between a literature accessible only to the old scholar-official class and one that could reach the educated urban public — and eventually, in theory, the peasantry. Lu Xun's 《狂人日記》 (1918) opens with a classical-Chinese preface (signed by the narrator's elder brother, the "normal" voice) framing a vernacular madman's diary; the formal contrast stages the political stake of the vernacular itself. [modern-scholarly-inference — Theodore Huters, *Bringing the World Home*, 2005; Jing Tsu, *Kingdom of Characters*, 2022, on the script/language politics]

## 3. Material Conditions

### 3.1 Publishing ecology

- **Commercial Press 商務印書館** (Shanghai, founded 1897 by Xia Ruifang) and **Zhonghua Book Company 中華書局** (1912, Lufei Kui) dominated textbooks, encyclopedias, and mass-market reference. Their monthlies (《東方雜誌》, 《小說月報》) were central to the periodical market. [attested — Christopher Reed, *Gutenberg in Shanghai*, 2004]
- **Beixin Shudian 北新書局** (Li Xiaofeng 李小峰), **Kaiming Shudian 開明書店**, and smaller presses handled New Culture and progressive fiction. Lu Xun published most of his major collections through Beixin; he had a painful royalty dispute with Li Xiaofeng in 1929 that almost ended the relationship. [documented]
- **Lu Xun's own imprints** — he co-founded **三閒書屋 Sanxian Shushe** and **野草書屋 Yecao Shushe** to self-publish translation and art projects. [documented]
- **Uchiyama Shoten 內山書店** (Kanzo Uchiyama, North Sichuan Road, Shanghai) — Japanese-run bookstore in Hongkou, functioning as Lu Xun's effective mailing address, meeting point, refuge, and access to Japanese-language translations of Western and Russian material. The cross-national personal friendship with Uchiyama is documented and extensive. [documented]
- **Periodicals matter more than books** — the intellectual life of the Republican era was run on weeklies and monthlies (*New Youth*, *Threads of Talk* 語絲, *Mengya* 萌芽, *Benliu* 奔流, *Yiwen* 譯文, *Taibai* 太白, etc.). Lu Xun edited or co-edited at least a dozen. [documented — Michel Hockx, *Questions of Style*, 2003; *The Literary Field of Twentieth-Century China*, 1999]

### 3.2 Lu Xun's personal economy

- **1912–1926 Beijing** — Ministry of Education civil-service salary (~300 silver yuan/month at peak, often months in arrears under warlord governments), supplemented by university teaching fees and royalties. He supported his mother, Zhu An (in Beijing), his brother Zhou Zuoren's family until the 1923 break, and eventually himself and Xu Guangping. [documented — Zhou family ledgers in 《魯迅日記》]
- **1927 Guangzhou** — Sun Yat-sen University professorship, high salary but brief. [documented]
- **1927–30 Shanghai transition** — precarious; royalty disputes; the 1929 Beixin conflict. Lu Xun calculates that he must live by the pen and can accept no official appointment that would compromise him. [documented]
- **1931–36** — stable income through royalties, editing fees, and Japanese-language translation contracts; by the mid-1930s one of the few Chinese writers financially self-sufficient from writing alone. [attested — Leo Ou-fan Lee, *Voices from the Iron House*, 1987]

### 3.3 Censorship and the pen-name economy

- **KMT Central Propaganda Department** book-ban lists after 1929 targeted named leftist authors; "Lu Xun" was a name under near-continuous watch. [documented]
- **Pen names** — Lu Xun himself is a pen name (adopted 1918); he used 100+ others during the Shanghai period, especially 1931–36, to place essays in periodicals whose editors would otherwise be held liable. Cataloged in 《鲁迅笔名录》. [documented]
- **Shanghai foreign concessions as haven** — the International Settlement (Anglo-American-administered) and the French Concession operated under treaty-port extraterritoriality; Chinese police (KMT or local) could not enter to make arrests without Settlement-police cooperation. This semi-colonial legal structure was the condition of possibility for leftist publishing throughout the Nanjing Decade. The pattern is a paradox Lu Xun explicitly notes: the humiliating foreign concessions are the only place Chinese can criticize China. [attested — Leo Lee, *Shanghai Modern*, 1999; Frederic Wakeman, *Policing Shanghai*, 1995]

### 3.4 Tuberculosis and the literary generation

Tuberculosis was the characteristic disease of the Republican literary class: Lu Xun (diagnosed 1925, died 1936), Yu Dafu, Xiao Hong (died 1942), Rou Shi (infected, then executed 1931), Zhu Ziqing (died 1948), and dozens of others. Overcrowded urban housing, damp winters in unheated buildings, tobacco (Lu Xun smoked heavily throughout adulthood), and the absence of antibiotics (streptomycin only 1944) made it a slow-motion sentence. Lu Xun's notorious insomnia and late-night writing schedule (often midnight–3 a.m.) were partly medical — he could not lie flat comfortably. [documented — 魯迅日記 for 1934–36; Andrew Jones, *Developmental Fairy Tales*, 2011, on illness metaphorics]

### 3.5 Urban semi-colonial modernity

Shanghai 1927–36 was the world's fifth-largest city by population, with a Chinese-administered "Greater Shanghai" municipality, the International Settlement, and the French Concession operating under parallel sovereignty. Cinema (Mingxing 明星 and Lianhua 聯華 studios), radio (from 1923), department stores (Sincere, Wing On, Sun Sun, Dah Sun on Nanjing Road), coffee houses, cabarets, and a Chinese-owned stock exchange co-existed with sweatshops, opium dens, and rickshaw-puller districts where life expectancy was ~27 years. Lu Xun navigated this as resident-critic; his late essays (《二心集》, 《且介亭雜文》) repeatedly take the commercial press, movie culture, and mass fashion as objects of analysis. [attested — Leo Lee, *Shanghai Modern*]

## 4. The Confucian-Familial Order Against Which He Wrote

### 4.1 Structural ideology

- **Three Bonds 三綱** (ruler–subject 君為臣綱, father–son 父為子綱, husband–wife 夫為妻綱) and **Five Relationships 五倫** — codified as state ideology through Han-Tang-Song-Ming-Qing dynasties; internalized via the 《孝經》 Classic of Filial Piety and the 《女誡》 Precepts for Women and similar texts taught in lineage schools. [attested]
- **Filial piety 孝** as enforcing mechanism — an adult son owes obedience, physical service, material support, and ritual compliance to parents; refusal is both a legal offense (Qing Code, carried forward into early Republican codes with modification) and a social death sentence. [attested]
- **Ancestor worship and lineage governance 宗族** — the lineage-hall 祠堂, lineage elder 族長, and genealogy 族譜 ran local marriage, land, inheritance, and dispute-resolution in much of Qing China. Lu Xun's Shaoxing Zhou lineage was characteristic. [attested — Maurice Freedman, *Lineage Organization in Southeastern China*, 1958]

### 4.2 The female condition

- **Foot-binding 纏足** — still near-universal among Han women of gentry and aspirant classes at Lu Xun's birth (1881). Anti-foot-binding societies date from the 1890s; the practice was formally banned by the Republic in 1912, but persisted in inland areas through the 1930s. Zhu An, Lu Xun's wife, had bound feet. [documented]
- **Widow chastity 貞節** — widow remarriage was socially taboo and, for gentry widows, punished by removal of honors; chastity arches 貞節牌坊 were erected for those who refused remarriage or committed suicide. [attested]
- **Concubinage 納妾** — legal until the Republican Civil Code (officially 1930 but unevenly enforced); concubines were property of the wife's household and their children's legitimacy depended on the husband's recognition. [attested]
- **Arranged marriage 父母之命,媒妁之言** — the default; love-match marriage was a new phenomenon in Lu Xun's generation and fictional staple (Ding Ling's 《莎菲女士的日記》, Ba Jin's 《家》).

### 4.3 Lu Xun's autobiographical bedrock

- **1893 grandfather's exam-bribery imprisonment**: Lu Xun, 12, was sent from Shaoxing to relatives' homes while the family hid from authorities. The experience of collapsed gentry respectability and the sudden exposure of the examination system as a rigged bribery market is one of his **formative political perceptions**. [documented — 《呐喊》自序]
- **c. 1896 father's death** at 37 after years of traditional-medical treatment: Lu Xun's lifelong contempt for Chinese traditional medicine ("不过是一种有意的或无意的骗子" — "nothing but a witting or unwitting fraud," 1922 preface) originates here. [documented]
- **1906 arranged marriage to Zhu An**: Lu Xun could not divorce her without condemning her to social death (a returned "discarded" wife had no place in 1906 Shaoxing). He supported her financially for 30 years; she lived with his mother in Beijing; they shared a household but never a marriage. His essays 《我之節烈觀》 (1918) and 《我們現在怎樣做父親》 (1919) process this autobiographical bedrock into general critique. [documented]
- **1923 break with Zhou Zuoren** — his closest intellectual partner, over an incident involving Zuoren's Japanese wife Habuto Nobuko that remains not fully documented; the break was final. This loss of his primary intellectual companion preceded his shift into full polemical mode. [documented — the break date in his diary; the cause disputed; Susan Daruvala, *Zhou Zuoren and an Alternative Chinese Response to Modernity*, 2000, reviews the evidence]

### 4.4 State violence as family-scale normality

Public executions in Qing and early Republican China included **beheading**, the posting of severed heads in cages 梟首 at city gates, **slicing 凌遲** (officially abolished 1905 but the imagery persisted in living memory), and the **public crowds** who watched them. Lu Xun's 1906 Sendai slide experience — Chinese watching a Chinese execution — was shocking not because execution-watching was unusual but because it made him see the spectator-crowd as the real problem. His story 《藥》 (1919), 《示眾》 (1925), and 《阿Q正傳》 (execution by firing squad, 1921–22) all return to this image. [documented]

## 5. Era-Assumptions (What He Would Have Taken for Granted)

These are the load-bearing axioms a reasonable, educated Chinese man born in 1881 and writing in 1918–36 would have treated as facts requiring no argument. For each, the concrete grounding.

### 5.1 "Chinese civilization is in a genuine, not merely perceived, crisis."
*Ground:* 96 years of unbroken defeat-treaty-indemnity-rebellion between 1840 and 1936; Taiping 20–30M dead; exam system abolished with no replacement; a Qing abdication; a Republic that devolved into warlord civil war within four years; Japanese seizure of Manchuria in his last decade. The crisis was not a literary trope. It was the 55-year measurable data. [attested]

### 5.2 "Darwinian 'struggle for existence' is the default description of international relations between nation-states/peoples."
*Ground:* Yan Fu's 《天演論》 1898, which Lu Xun read at 17; the Sino-Japanese War demonstration that a smaller "brother" could eat its "elder"; the 1900 Eight-Nation occupation; 1915 Japanese Twenty-One Demands; 1919 Versailles; 1931 Manchuria. 物競天擇 was not a metaphor in 1881–1936 Chinese life; it was a political weather report. [attested]

### 5.3 "The individual (個人, 獨立人格) is a modern value to be imported, not a traditional Chinese virtue."
*Ground:* Confucian Three-Bonds ideology was explicitly anti-individualist — a person was a node in relational obligations. Nietzsche, Stirner, and the Romantic-poet rebels arrived in Lu Xun's 1907–08 essays precisely as carriers of the missing individual-principle. By the time of the New Culture Movement, 個性 and 人格 had become standard loan-translation concepts. [modern-scholarly-inference — Lin Yü-sheng, *The Crisis of Chinese Consciousness*, 1979, on the "cultural-intellectualistic" framing of this inheritance]

### 5.4 "Arranged marriage, concubinage, foot-binding, and filial tyranny are family-level facts, not hypotheticals."
*Ground:* His own mother's generation had bound feet. His own wife had bound feet. His own grandfather had concubines. His own marriage was arranged. He knew dozens of specific families where the same patterns applied. When he wrote 《祝福》 (New Year's Sacrifice, 1924) about Xianglin Sao, he was not inventing. [documented]

### 5.5 "Japan is simultaneously the model of modernization AND an imperialist threat — a permanent unresolvable double-bind."
*Ground:* Lu Xun spent his formative years (1902–09) in Meiji Japan; he read most of his Western philosophy in Japanese translation; his bookstore refuge in Shanghai was Japanese-run; his closest personal friend in the 1930s was Uchiyama Kanzo. Simultaneously, Japan took Taiwan (1895), forced the Twenty-One Demands (1915), took the Shandong German concessions (1919), took Manchuria (1931), bombed his Shanghai neighborhood (1932), and was clearly preparing what became the 1937 war. There was no coherent single stance available; he had to hold both. [attested — see Kiyama Hideo 木山英雄, *文学復古と文学革命*, and Leo Lee on the Japan-Shanghai literary traffic]

### 5.6 "Vernacular Chinese is a political act, not a neutral stylistic choice."
*Ground:* 1917 Hu Shi manifesto; 1918 Lu Xun vernacular debut; 1920 Ministry of Education mandate; continuous conservative resistance (Lin Shu's 1919 "Letter to Cai Yuanpei") through the 1920s; Liang Shiqiu's 1927 defense of classical registers. Choosing 白話 was aligning with New Culture; choosing 文言 was aligning against it. Lu Xun's late use of classical-inflected registers (e.g. 《故事新編》 1935) was a deliberate **return** inside an already-won vernacular frame. [documented]

### 5.7 "The literati's historical identity 士大夫 is over, but what replaces it is unclear."
*Ground:* 1905 exam abolition removed the institutional anchor; 1911 revolution removed the dynastic-service telos; the warlord state offered no career for scholar-officials; the new universities offered academic careers only for a small fraction; the commercial press offered freelance writing, but precariously. Lu Xun's entire adult working life is a 28-year experiment in what a Chinese intellectual could even BE when the 士大夫 institution has collapsed. [modern-scholarly-inference — 余英時 《士與中國文化》 1987]

### 5.8 "'Savage' executions, public head-displays, and collective punishments are ordinary state practice in my lifetime."
*Ground:* Qing beheadings and slicings into the 1900s; warlord-era public executions as a matter of course; 1926 Beijing Massacre; 1927 Shanghai and Guangzhou purges; 1931 Longhua executions of his own friends; 1930s KMT garrison courts. A Chinese writer working in Shanghai in 1934 did not theorize state violence; he negotiated around it daily through pen names and the French Concession address. [documented]

### 5.9 "Mass death is scale-normal."
*Ground:* Taiping 20–30M; Nian, Muslim rebellions millions more; 1876–79 North China famine ~9–13M; 1918–19 influenza an additional several million in China; warlord wars; 1931 Yangtze flood ~145,000–4M depending on count; 1928–30 North China famine. Numbers in the ten-thousands are unit-scale; numbers in the millions are era-scale. Lu Xun's famous line about "一种无可告语的沉默" (an unutterable silence) around mass death is responding to mass death as a specific accumulated feature of his era. [attested]

### 5.10 "I, writing, have enemies who would have me arrested or killed if they could locate me."
*Ground:* From 1926 Beiyang warrants, through 1927 post-purge Guangzhou, through 1931 Longhua executions of his friends, through continuous 1930–36 KMT pressure — this was never paranoia. He signed more than 100 pen names into print for this reason; he did not sleep in his registered address for weeks in 1930 and 1934; he used Uchiyama Shoten as a dead drop because his own mail was read. [documented]

### 5.11 "The 'people' 國民 / 民眾 whom I am trying to awaken may be the very thing most ruined by the old order — they may cheer my execution, not save me."
*Ground:* The 1906 Sendai slide of Chinese spectators cheering an execution; the 《藥》 story (1919) of the people buying the revolutionary's blood-soaked bun as consumption-cure; the Ah Q figure as the "national character" trapped in self-deceiving 精神勝利法 spiritual-victory method; the 1926 Liu Hezhen massacre where the students were not defended by the ordinary residents of Beijing. This is possibly the single most distinguishing Lu-Xun era-assumption relative to other May Fourth writers: he did not romanticize the 民眾. [modern-scholarly-inference — Wang Hui 汪暉 《反抗絕望》 1991; Qian Liqun 錢理群 《心靈的探尋》 1988]

### 5.12 "Hope and despair are symmetric; neither is epistemically privileged."
*Ground:* The Petőfi line "希望本是無所謂有,無所謂無的" reformulated by Lu Xun; the ending of 《故鄉》 (Hometown, 1921) — "地上本沒有路,走的人多了,也便成了路." Hope is not a forecast but a practice, because forecasts are unavailable. [documented]

## Synthesis: Why the Era Makes the Work Make Sense

Pull any one of Lu Xun's signature moves out of context and it looks like modernist pessimism:

- The "iron house" 鐵屋子 metaphor (1922 preface to 《呐喊》) — in which a few awake inside a windowless iron house full of people suffocating in their sleep, and the question is whether it is cruel or kind to wake them — is not *Waste Land* existentialism. It is a specific description of the literate reformer's dilemma in 1922 Beijing: wake them to a crisis for which no exit is yet designed? [attested]
- The "eating people" 吃人 thesis of 《狂人日記》 (1918) — history as a ledger whose moral entries conceal a cannibal economy — is not a surrealist trope. It is a condensed summary of: the Confucian familial order's enforcement of widow-suicide and filial-sacrifice; the examination system's consumption of his own father; the warlord state's consumption of students; the imperialist powers' consumption of sovereignty. [attested]
- The 精神勝利法 "spiritual victory method" of Ah Q (1921–22) — the defeated subject who reframes humiliations as victories — is not a psychological curiosity. It is the diagnosis of a nation that has been losing for 80 years and has accumulated an entire discourse (Confucian-culturalist, neo-traditionalist, Boxer-supernaturalist) to pretend it has not. [attested]
- The use of **100+ pen names** (1930–36) is not a literary game. It is the material-conditions signature of a man writing under a state that would kill him if it caught him. [documented]

The era produced a specific diagnostic demand: describe a civilizational collapse in progress, when no stable successor-civilization is yet available, without either (a) nostalgically defending the collapsing order, (b) triumphantly embracing the foreign order that is simultaneously the aggressor, or (c) lying that things are fine. Lu Xun's style — the irony, the compression, the refusal of programmatic answers, the insistence that *writing honestly about this is itself the work* — is precisely the style that fits that demand. [modern-scholarly-inference]

## Researcher note on gaps

- **Regional variation** in my account is thin: the Shaoxing–Nanjing–Tokyo–Beijing–Xiamen–Guangzhou–Shanghai spatial sequence of Lu Xun's life could be more granularly mapped against local political micro-climates; this draft foregrounds national timeline.
- **Economic texture** (rural land-tenure patterns, urban wage structures) is present at a gestural level; a fuller draft would cross-reference Ramon Myers / Philip Huang rural-economy scholarship.
- **Women's-movement context** (1915–35 women's suffrage, anti-footbinding associations, Xiang Jingyu, Ding Ling's milieu) is compressed and deserves its own research pass, especially for reading 《傷逝》 (1925), 《祝福》, and the essays on "Nora after leaving."
- **Non-Han and peripheral contexts** (Tibet, Mongolia, the Muslim Northwest, the 1912–14 Mongolian independence, Tibetan expulsion of Qing forces) are not covered here because they do not appear materially in Lu Xun's writing; but the absence itself is a datum about the Han-coastal-intellectual horizon he inhabited.
