# Lu Xun: Reception History, 1936–2026

**Research Agent 6 of 6 — Reception Dimension**

This file documents how Lu Xun (鲁迅, pen name of Zhou Shuren 周树人, 1881–1936) has been read, rewritten, canonized, weaponized, and contested across the nine decades since his death. The central premise: much of what "Lu Xun" means today is not the historical man but the construction of later eras. Claims are labeled:

- **[documented]** — attested in dated primary sources (edicts, newspapers, official editions, speeches in print)
- **[attested]** — reported by named contemporaries or near-contemporaries in memoirs/letters
- **[attributed]** — circulated under Lu Xun's name but authorship/exact wording uncertain
- **[modern-scholarly-inference]** — argument made by post-1978 scholarship synthesizing evidence

---

## I. 1936–1949: Canonization Begins

### The 1936 Funeral as Founding Event

Lu Xun died 19 October 1936 in Shanghai. The funeral on 22 October drew crowds variously estimated at 20,000–50,000 mourners over the preceding three days of public mourning at the Wanguo Funeral Parlor (萬國殯儀館) [documented — Shanghai press coverage compiled in 鲁迅研究资料 series; Gloria Davies *Lu Xun's Revolution* 2013 ch. 1]. The pallbearers and organizers formed a politically heterogeneous coalition: Song Qingling 宋庆龄, Cai Yuanpei 蔡元培, Shen Junru 沈钧儒, Zhang Naiqi 章乃器, Li Gongpu 李公朴, Ba Jin 巴金, Hu Feng 胡风, Xiao Jun 萧军, Nie Er's circle, and representatives of the League of Left-Wing Writers [documented — funeral records in 《鲁迅先生纪念集》1937, ed. Xu Guangping].

The crucial iconographic moment: Shen Junru placed a banner reading **"民族魂"** ("Soul of the Nation") over the coffin [documented — photograph reproduced in nearly all Lu Xun museum collections; cited in 朱正 《鲁迅传》2007]. This three-character phrase is the originating artifact of Lu Xun canonization. It transferred him from "leading left-wing writer" to "national symbol" in a single gesture. The framers were united-front democrats, not Communists — important context for later Party appropriation.

Memorial essays in late 1936 by Feng Xuefeng 冯雪峰, Hu Feng, Xu Guangping 许广平, Xiao Hong 萧红, Ba Jin, Yu Dafu 郁达夫, and others established the initial template: Lu Xun as tireless fighter, darkness-piercing truthteller, teacher of youth [documented — collected in 《鲁迅先生纪念集》1937]. Yu Dafu's essay contained the sentence later quoted for decades: "没有伟大的人物出现的民族，是世界上最可怜的生物之群；有了伟大的人物，而不知拥护、爱戴、崇仰的国家，是没有希望的奴隶之邦" ("A people without great figures is the most pitiable herd; a country that has great figures but fails to support, love, and revere them is a nation of hopeless slaves") [documented — Yu Dafu, 《怀鲁迅》, November 1936].

### The 1938 《鲁迅全集》

The first *Complete Works* (20 volumes) appeared in 1938 under wartime Shanghai conditions, edited by a committee organized around Xu Guangping with Cai Yuanpei contributing the preface [documented — 《鲁迅全集》1938 edition, Fuxing Publishing 复社]. The edition was a remarkable logistical achievement under Japanese occupation but was politically curated. Notably:

- Earlier polemical opponents' pieces were tactfully excluded from framing material
- Zhou Zuoren's 周作人 prefaces and correspondence were minimized [modern-scholarly-inference — Qian Liqun 《周作人传》1990; Susan Daruvala *Zhou Zuoren and an Alternative Chinese Response to Modernity* 2000]
- The Two Slogans dispute (两个口号论争) correspondence was included but not foregrounded
- Many of Lu Xun's letters critical of CCP literary functionaries (including the August 1936 Xu Maoyong letter, 《答徐懋庸并关于抗日统一战线问题》) were printed but their implications were soon submerged by wartime unity rhetoric [modern-scholarly-inference — Wang Hui 汪晖 《反抗绝望》1991; Davies 2013]

### Mao Zedong's Foundational Readings

Two Mao texts fixed the Party line for fifty years:

1. **1937 "在纪念鲁迅先生逝世一周年大会上"** (Yan'an speech on the first anniversary of Lu Xun's death) [documented — text in 《毛泽东文集》vol. 2].
2. **1940 《新民主主义论》** containing the formula: "鲁迅是中国文化革命的主将...鲁迅的骨头是最硬的" ("Lu Xun is the chief commander of China's cultural revolution... Lu Xun's bones are the hardest") [documented — 《新民主主义论》January 1940, 《解放》no. 98–99].

This passage became the scriptural core of PRC Lu Xun pedagogy. Mao added that Lu Xun was "伟大的文学家、思想家、革命家" ("great writer, thinker, revolutionary") — the triple epithet repeated in every PRC textbook for decades.

### 1942 Yan'an Forum and the Irony

At the May 1942 Yan'an Forum on Literature and Art, Mao used Lu Xun's authority to support Party-controlled literature — the subordination of writers to revolutionary discipline. This is historically ironic: Lu Xun's August 1936 open letter to Xu Maoyong 徐懋庸 had attacked precisely this kind of Party literary instrumentalization, accusing CCP-aligned critics of "以鸣鞭为唯一的业绩" (treating crack-of-whip enforcement as their sole achievement) [documented — Lu Xun, 《答徐懋庸并关于抗日统一战线问题》August 1936, collected in 《且介亭杂文末编》]. The Two Slogans dispute (National Defense Literature 国防文学 vs. Mass Literature of National Revolutionary War 民族革命战争的大众文学) pitted Lu Xun against Zhou Yang 周扬 and Xia Yan 夏衍 — the very Party literary bureaucrats who would dominate PRC letters after 1949. This foundational tension in the historical record was routinely suppressed in the Mao era and is now a central axis of revisionist scholarship [Davies 2013; T.A. Hsia *The Gate of Darkness* 1968; Wang Hui 1991].

### Anti-Japanese War Reprintings

Throughout 1937–45, Lu Xun's anti-imperialist essays circulated in underground presses in occupied territory and in the Chongqing-Kunming free zones. The Xinhua Bookstore 新华书店 systematized distribution in CCP base areas [documented — party publishing records; 丁言昭 《鲁迅与中国左翼文化运动》].

## II. 1949–1976: PRC Sanctification and Weaponization

### Institutional Apparatus

- **1951**: Shanghai Lu Xun Memorial Hall opens at Hongkou [documented]
- **1956**: Lu Xun's grave relocated to Hongkou Park (later Lu Xun Park), mausoleum inscribed in Mao's calligraphy [documented]
- **1956**: Beijing Lu Xun Museum opens at 阜成门内宫门口二条 (his 1924–26 Beijing residence); Shaoxing Lu Xun Memorial opens [documented]
- **1956, 1958, 1981**: successive 《鲁迅全集》 state editions, each more heavily annotated [documented — Renmin Wenxue Chubanshe editions]

### Textbook Saturation

By the late 1950s, more than twenty Lu Xun essays and stories were fixed in primary and secondary curricula: 《故乡》, 《祝福》, 《药》, 《孔乙己》, 《阿Q正传》, 《记念刘和珍君》, 《从百草园到三味书屋》, 《藤野先生》, 《拿来主义》, 《论雷峰塔的倒掉》, and many others [documented — PRC curriculum archives; 温儒敏 studies on modern Chinese textbook history]. Memorization was often mandatory. This made him, statistically, the most-taught modern Chinese author for three generations.

### 1957 Anti-Rightist Campaign — Feng Xuefeng

The most painful case: Feng Xuefeng 冯雪峰, Lu Xun's closest CCP liaison of the 1930s, personally attacked by Lu Xun in letters but also his most devoted Party-side defender, was labeled a rightist in 1957 [documented — Feng Xuefeng biography, Chen Zaoxiang 陈早春 1989]. He was stripped of positions, later further persecuted in the Cultural Revolution, and died in 1976 still unrehabilitated (posthumously rehabilitated 1979). The pattern: the people who actually knew Lu Xun were purged; orthodox readings hardened around those who had not.

### The 1958 Annotated Edition

The 10-volume 1958 《鲁迅全集》 introduced the annotation apparatus that shaped PRC reading: footnotes framed Lu Xun's contemporaries as "反动文人" (reactionary literati) when they were Hu Shi 胡适, Liang Shiqiu 梁实秋, Lin Yutang 林语堂, the Crescent Moon Society 新月派, etc. [documented — Renmin Wenxue Chubanshe 1958]. This framed Lu Xun's polemics as heroic combat against clearly identified class enemies, erasing the tangled, often personal, often mutually Anglophile-modernist texture of actual 1920s–30s Chinese literary life [modern-scholarly-inference — Leo Ou-fan Lee *Voices from the Iron House* 1987; Davies 2013].

### Cultural Revolution (1966–76): Selective Weaponization

Two simultaneous moves:

1. **Weaponization of the polemical Lu Xun**: his combat essays against Liang Shiqiu, against "reactionary" intellectuals, against the "Third Category" 第三种人 were re-deployed against 1960s intellectuals deemed revisionist. Red Guard publications quoted him against their targets constantly [documented — CR-era tabloids and factional publications]
2. **Burial of the humanist Lu Xun**: his modernist, despair-haunted work (especially 《野草》 *Wild Grass*) was sidelined; the ambivalent, self-interrogating Lu Xun of "我的确时时解剖别人，然而更多的是更无情地解剖我自己" ("I indeed constantly dissect others, but still more I dissect myself more mercilessly") [documented — 《写在〈坟〉后面》1926] was not taught

**1971 "Lu Xun quotations" pamphlets** (《鲁迅语录》) circulated as quasi-scriptural companions to Mao's Little Red Book [documented — multiple 1971–73 editions]. This is the peak of instrumentalization: Lu Xun reduced to a quotable oracle.

### 1976: The Turn

After the Gang of Four arrest in October 1976, Zhou Haiying 周海婴 (Lu Xun's son, b. 1929) publicly criticized their use of his father's name and writings [documented — Zhou Haiying statements collected in his 《鲁迅与我七十年》2001]. This small event marked the beginning of de-instrumentalization.

## III. 1978–1990: New Enlightenment Re-reading

### The 1981 《鲁迅全集》

The 16-volume 1981 edition became the definitive reform-era reference [documented — Renmin Wenxue Chubanshe, marking the centenary of Lu Xun's birth]. The annotation apparatus was overhauled: many "反动文人" labels were softened or replaced with neutral biographical footnotes; letters previously suppressed or truncated were restored. This was a quiet but consequential act of scholarly rehabilitation — the annotation framework of Chinese literary modernity was substantially de-Maoified.

### Academic Infrastructure

- 《鲁迅研究月刊》 founded 1980 (originally 《鲁迅研究动态》, renamed 1990) [documented]
- China Lu Xun Research Society 中国鲁迅研究会 organized 1979 [documented]
- Graduate programs proliferated; "Lu Xun Studies" (鲁迅学) became a field

### Three Re-readings That Reshaped the Field

1. **Li Zehou 李泽厚, "救亡压倒启蒙" (Salvation Overrode Enlightenment), 1987** [documented — 《中国现代思想史论》Dongfang Chubanshe 1987]. The thesis: China's May Fourth enlightenment project was overwhelmed by the existential urgency of national salvation, forcing thinkers like Lu Xun into instrumentalized revolutionary roles that sacrificed their true humanist/individualist work. This argument re-framed Lu Xun's post-1927 turn as loss, not fulfillment — directly contradicting Mao-era narrative.

2. **Qian Liqun 钱理群, 《心灵的探寻》 (Exploration of the Soul), 1988** [documented — Shanghai Wenyi Chubanshe 1988]. Lu Xun as solitary warrior of the spirit, wrestling with darkness within himself, not a confident Party commander.

3. **Wang Hui 汪晖, 《反抗绝望》 (Resistance of Despair / Against Despair), 1991** [documented — Renmin Chubanshe 1991; based on his dissertation under Tang Tao 唐弢]. The central argument: Lu Xun's essential posture is "绝望之为虚妄，正与希望相同" ("despair, as delusion, is exactly like hope" — Lu Xun's own phrase in *Wild Grass* from a Petőfi citation). He is not a revolutionary optimist but a modernist of ineliminable ambivalence who fights despite knowing futility. This became the most influential non-Marxist reading of Lu Xun in contemporary Chinese scholarship.

### Other 1980s Developments

- Wang Furen 王富仁, 《中国反封建思想革命的一面镜子》1986 — rereading of 《呐喊》《彷徨》 [documented]
- Lin Yü-sheng (Lin Yusheng) 林毓生, *The Crisis of Chinese Consciousness* (1979, Chinese trans. 1988) — Lu Xun as exemplar of "totalistic anti-traditionalism" whose destructive impulse shaped modern Chinese political culture [documented — University of Wisconsin Press]

## IV. 1990s–2000s: Market-Era Ambivalence

### The Wang Shuo Attack

In 2000, novelist Wang Shuo 王朔 published 《我看鲁迅》 ("My View of Lu Xun") in 《收获》 magazine, arguing Lu Xun was overrated, that his "lack of a long novel" signaled limited achievement, and that his sainthood was a political artifact [documented — 《收获》2000 no. 2]. The essay triggered massive controversy. It functioned as a market-era rebellion against Mao-era sanctification — Lu Xun as the bronze statue that needed to be toppled to free cultural space [Davies 2013 ch. 6 discusses this].

### Republican Nostalgia 民国热

The 2000s saw broad rehabilitation of figures Lu Xun had attacked:
- Hu Shi 胡适 — reread as the true liberal modernizer
- Liang Shiqiu 梁实秋 — human-nature literary critic, no longer "capitalist running dog"
- Zhou Zuoren 周作人 — the "other Zhou brother," aesthetic essayist, now seen as offering a road-not-taken alternative to his brother's combativeness [Susan Daruvala 2000; David Pollard *A Chinese Look at Literature* 2002]
- Chen Yuan 陈源 / Xu Zhimo 徐志摩 / Shao Xunmei 邵洵美 — Crescent Moon modernists

Lu Xun's sharp polemical style was now read by some as prefiguring Cultural Revolution attack-style discourse. Hu Shi's measured liberalism and Zhou Zuoren's ironic detachment were preferred by many urban intellectual readers.

### Textbook Controversies — "鲁迅大撤退"

- **2007**: 《药》 ("Medicine") removed from People's Education Press senior high school textbooks [documented — 人民教育出版社 curriculum revisions]
- **2010**: additional reductions of Lu Xun pieces in junior high, replaced by contemporary authors including Jin Yong 金庸
- Public label "鲁迅大撤退" (Lu Xun's Grand Retreat) circulated in 2010 press
- Editors clarified they were reducing repetition and adjusting age-appropriateness, not removing Lu Xun [documented — Ministry of Education statements]. Total Lu Xun pieces in the national curriculum remained substantial but were reduced from ~20 peak-era to ~10–12

### Institutionalization and Its Critics

"鲁迅学" (Lu Xun Studies) became one of the most institutionally supported fields in modern Chinese literary studies. Zhang Mengyang 张梦阳's 《中国鲁迅学通史》 (2001–2005, 3 vols) is the authoritative historiography of the field itself [documented — Guangdong Jiaoyu Chubanshe]. Critics within the field, including Qian Liqun and younger scholars, repeatedly worried that the discipline had become stagnant, ritualistic, and self-referential [Qian Liqun, various essays 2000s].

## V. 2010s–2020s: Meme Culture and Fabricated Quotes

### "鲁迅说过" as Internet Meme

Beginning roughly 2010 and peaking 2015–2020, the phrase "鲁迅说过..." ("Lu Xun said...") became a Chinese internet meme format: users attach any opinion — often humorously anachronistic, sometimes topical, sometimes deliberately absurd — to the phrase for rhetorical weight. The meme is self-aware: part of its humor is precisely that most internet users no longer know exactly what Lu Xun did or did not say.

The mass fabrication produced a real scholarly problem. In 2019, the Beijing Lu Xun Museum launched an online quotation-verification database (鲁迅博物馆 资料查询在线检索系统) allowing anyone to search the corpus to verify whether an attributed quote is real [documented — www.luxunmuseum.com.cn verification tool]. It became itself an internet phenomenon as users tested favorite "Lu Xun quotes" and discovered most were fake.

Examples of widely circulated fabrications:
- "晚安" (just "good night," framed as Lu Xun's profound wisdom) — fabricated [documented — verification tool]
- Various aphorisms about romantic love, urban life, contemporary technology — impossible anachronisms
- Real phrases distorted in transmission

### Leftist Revival on Chinese Internet ~2015+

Younger left-leaning internet users, disillusioned with post-2010 social and economic conditions, rediscovered Lu Xun's social critique — especially 《狂人日记》's "吃人" ("man-eating") and 《阿Q正传》's Ah Q's spiritual victories read as critiques of contemporary compensatory nationalism. Zhihu 知乎, Bilibili, and WeChat publics saw recurrent Lu Xun renaissances, distinct from state-sponsored veneration [modern-scholarly-inference — recent China Internet studies; Davies's ongoing work].

### Recent Academic Scholarship

- **Gloria Davies, *Lu Xun's Revolution: Writing in a Time of Violence* (Harvard UP 2013)** — the most important English-language study of Lu Xun's 1930s, reception-sensitive throughout
- **Eileen J. Cheng, *Literary Remains: Death, Trauma, and Lu Xun's Refusal to Mourn* (Hawaii UP 2013)** and ***Jottings under Lamplight* (Harvard UP 2017)** — Cheng recovers Lu Xun's melancholic, elegiac, mourning-saturated register against both Maoist optimism and dismissive market-era critics
- **Nicolai Volland**, **Andrew F. Jones**, **David Der-wei Wang** — contextual work on modern Chinese letters repositioning Lu Xun
- **Julia Lovell's 2009 Penguin translation** *The Real Story of Ah-Q and Other Tales of China* — brought Lu Xun to trade Anglophone readers
- Chinese-language: Qian Liqun continued publishing, 《鲁迅作品十五讲》 (2003) and subsequent; 王得后 on Lu Xun thought; 孙郁 on Lu Xun and his contemporaries; 李怡 on Lu Xun and modernity
- 朱正 《鲁迅传》 2007 (expanded editions) — revisionist biographical work correcting legendary material

### "Wild Grass" Revival

《野草》 (*Wild Grass*, 1927) is now widely regarded as Lu Xun's literary masterpiece — a position that would have been unthinkable in 1958, when its modernist obscurity embarrassed orthodox readings. Contemporary scholarship by Cheng, Davies, Leo Lee, and others treats *Wild Grass* as comparable to Baudelaire's prose poems in density [Lee 1987; Cheng 2013].

## VI. Overseas Reception

### Japan — Takeuchi Yoshimi and After

**Takeuchi Yoshimi 竹内好, 《魯迅》 (1944)** is the foundational non-Chinese study and arguably the single most influential text in overseas Lu Xun reception [documented — Nihon Hyōronsha 1944, rev. 1961]. Written under wartime Japan, it articulates Lu Xun as the model of subaltern-modernity: the non-European latecomer's authentic resistance not through mimicking Europe but through bitter, interior, "zhengzha" (挣扎, struggle) — a concept Takeuchi made famous in postwar Japanese intellectual discourse. Postwar Japanese leftist-humanist tradition (Maruyama Masao's circle, later) treated Lu Xun as an Asian philosophical figure, not merely a writer.

Subsequent Japanese scholarship:
- **Maruyama Noboru 丸山昇**, biographical and political-historical
- **Itō Toramaru 伊藤虎丸**, Lu Xun and early 20th-c. European thought
- **Kitaoka Masako 北岡正子**, Lu Xun's Sendai years, his engagement with Nietzsche, his Japan-period readings
- **Tarumoto Teruo 樽本照雄**, meticulous bibliographic work on late-Qing–Republican publishing including Lu Xun's editorial activity

### Taiwan — Banned Until 1987

Under Nationalist martial law (1949–1987), Lu Xun was banned in Taiwan as a leftist author [documented]. Underground circulation existed among the dangwai 党外 intellectual circle. After lifting of martial law in 1987, Lu Xun editions rapidly appeared; by the 1990s he was a standard topic in Taiwanese modern Chinese literary studies, though without the canonical pedagogical saturation of the PRC.

### Korea, Vietnam

Korean reception runs through the colonial period (Lu Xun was translated into Korean in the 1920s–30s), ROK leftist circles (many of whom faced their own suppressions), and post-democratization scholarship. Vietnamese reception was channeled through the DRV's cultural alignment with the PRC from the 1950s. Both traditions emphasized anti-colonial Lu Xun.

### Soviet / Russian

Soviet translation of Lu Xun began in the 1920s (small selection) and expanded massively post-1949 [L.D. Pozdneeva, V.F. Sorokin, and others]. Lu Xun was framed as a "revolutionary-democrat" in the lineage Soviet critics used for Belinsky, Chernyshevsky — i.e., pre-Marxist but pointing toward revolution.

### English-language Reception

- **1936**: Edgar Snow & Nym Wales *Living China: Modern Chinese Short Stories* — first substantial Lu Xun translations in English [documented — George G. Harrap]
- **1956–1981**: Yang Hsien-yi 杨宪益 and Gladys Yang at Foreign Languages Press produced the four-volume *Selected Works* that dominated English-language reading of Lu Xun for decades [documented — FLP Beijing]
- **1987**: Leo Ou-fan Lee, *Voices from the Iron House: A Study of Lu Xun* — first major English monograph, reframed him as complex modernist [Indiana UP]
- **1990**: William A. Lyell, *Diary of a Madman and Other Stories* (Hawaii UP) — important retranslation
- **2009**: Julia Lovell, *The Real Story of Ah-Q and Other Tales of China* (Penguin Classics) — brought Lu Xun to trade readership
- **2013–2017**: Eileen Cheng's twin monographs
- **2013**: Gloria Davies's *Lu Xun's Revolution*
- Theodore Huters, Marston Anderson (*The Limits of Realism* 1990), Lydia Liu (*Translingual Practice* 1995) — important contextual studies

## VII. Myth vs. Historical Man

The following popular beliefs circulating in Chinese public culture are not supported by the primary record as currently reconstructed by post-1978 scholarship:

### 1. Myth: "Lu Xun the Communist"
**Reality**: Lu Xun never joined the Chinese Communist Party [documented — absence of any Party record; Feng Xuefeng's own recollections confirm]. In 1936 he was in acrimonious dispute with the Party's Shanghai literary apparatus (Zhou Yang, Xia Yan, Xu Maoyong) over the Two Slogans controversy. His August 1936 letter to Xu Maoyong is one of the angriest polemical texts in his corpus and is directed at CCP-aligned literary bureaucrats [documented — 《且介亭杂文末编》]. He was a fellow-traveler who sympathized with revolution but insisted on intellectual independence to the end.

### 2. Myth: "Lu Xun the socialist realist"
**Reality**: Lu Xun's fiction is largely modernist — symbolic, allegorical, sometimes surreal. 《狂人日记》 is Gogol- and Andreev-inflected; 《野草》 is closer to Baudelaire and Nietzsche than to socialist realism; 《故事新编》 rewrites classical material with ironic distance. He translated significant modernist and anarcho-modernist material (Andreev, Eroshenko, Artsybashev's 《工人绥惠略夫》) [documented — his translation corpus]. Socialist realism as a doctrine postdates his active writing.

### 3. Myth: "Lu Xun the optimist fighter with iron bones"
**Reality**: The Mao-era "硬骨头" ("hardest bones") framing strips out what is perhaps Lu Xun's most distinctive feature — his absorption in despair, futility, and self-questioning. 《野草》's 《影的告别》 ("The Shadow's Farewell"), 《希望》 ("Hope"), 《过客》 ("The Passer-by") are texts of ineliminable ambivalence. His own 《呐喊·自序》 describes his writing as an attempt to answer his younger self who believed in iron-house awakening — and admits he no longer shares that confidence fully [documented — 1923 preface]. Wang Hui's《反抗绝望》 1991 is the decisive re-reading.

### 4. Myth: "Lu Xun the saint"
**Reality**: Contemporaries and his own letters describe him as sharp-tongued, often vindictive in polemic, capable of holding grudges for years, famously declaring "让他们怨恨去，我也一个都不宽恕" ("let them resent me, I shall not forgive a single one") in 《死》 shortly before his death [documented — 《且介亭杂文末编·死》October 1936]. His polemics could be personal and brutal. Sainthood is a post-1936 construction.

### 5. Myth: "Lu Xun the humorless moralist"
**Reality**: Lu Xun was sardonic, ironic, sometimes self-mocking. 《阿Q正传》 is savage comedy; his zawen frequently deploy mock-academic tones; his letters to friends are warm, joking, self-deprecating. Xu Guangping's and Xiao Hong's memoirs describe a man who laughed, told stories, loved sweets, was kind to children [attested — 《欣慰的纪念》, 《回忆鲁迅先生》]. The solemn statue image is post-1949 monumentalization.

### 6. Myth: "Lu Xun hated everything Chinese / totalistic anti-traditionalist"
**Reality**: Reductive. Lin Yü-sheng's 1979 "totalistic anti-traditionalism" thesis captures half the picture. The other half: Lu Xun authored 《中国小说史略》 (A Brief History of Chinese Fiction, 1923–24), one of the foundational works of modern Chinese literary scholarship; he collected 汉画像石 (Han stone reliefs) and 六朝造像 rubbings throughout his life; wrote classical-style poetry of high quality; edited 《唐宋传奇集》 1927. His relationship to Chinese tradition was critical, complex, and deeply scholarly — not blanket rejection [documented — his scholarly corpus; Eileen Cheng 2013 on Lu Xun's mourning for Chinese past forms].

### 7. Myth: "Lu Xun rejected classical culture for vernacular modernism"
**Reality**: He continued writing classical verse (旧体诗) throughout his life; some of his most personal statements ("无情未必真豪杰", "横眉冷对千夫指", "惯于长夜过春时") are in classical form [documented — 《集外集》 classical-poetry selection]. The vernacular/classical divide in his practice was dialectical, not oppositional.

### 8. Myth: "Lu Xun was a simple anti-imperialist regarding Japan"
**Reality**: He was deeply shaped by Japanese intellectual culture (his 1902–09 Japan years, his readings of Natsume Sōseki, Mori Ōgai, Kuriyagawa Hakuson whose 《苦闷的象征》 he translated). His friendship with Uchiyama Kanzō 内山完造 (owner of the Shanghai Uchiyama Bookstore) was among his closest late-life relationships. His anti-Japanese-imperialism coexisted with profound personal and cultural entanglement with Japan [documented — correspondence; Kitaoka Masako scholarship; Davies 2013].

### 9. Myth: "Lu Xun was a champion of women's liberation"
**Reality**: Partially. 《娜拉走后怎样》 (1923) and 《我之节烈观》 (1918) are important feminist statements. But 《伤逝》 (Regret for the Past, 1925) is read by contemporary feminist critics as ambivalent toward women's autonomy; the depiction of 祥林嫂 in 《祝福》 is sympathetic-but-distanced; his personal treatment of his arranged wife 朱安 was ambivalent; his household arrangements with Xu Guangping raised questions. Feminist readings since the 1990s (Meng Yue 孟悦, Dai Jinhua 戴锦华, Tonglin Lu) have produced a more complicated picture [Dai Jinhua *Invisible Writing* 浮出历史地表 1989 with Meng Yue].

### 10. Myth: "Mao understood Lu Xun"
**Reality**: Highly contested. The most famous reception-history moment on this question is Mao's own 1957 remark, widely attested but with uncertain exact wording, reported by 罗稷南 (Luo Jinan/Luo Jinan): asked what Lu Xun would be doing if still alive, Mao reportedly replied, "以我的估计，要么是关在牢里还要写，要么是识大体不做声" ("in my estimation, he would either be in prison still writing, or grasp the situation and keep silent") [attested — reported by Zhou Haiying in 《鲁迅与我七十年》 2001, citing his mother Xu Guangping's testimony about the 1957 Shanghai conversation; corroborating accounts vary in wording]. The remark — if accurate — is itself a reception-history artifact: Mao acknowledging that the Lu Xun he canonized would not survive the system built in Lu Xun's name. This is now one of the most cited moments in Chinese-language reception discussion.

---

## VIII. Current State of the Field (circa 2020s)

The field has moved decisively beyond both Mao-era orthodoxy and 1990s market-era dismissal. Dominant contemporary frames:

1. **Lu Xun as modernist of despair** (Wang Hui, Qian Liqun, Eileen Cheng)
2. **Lu Xun as complex East Asian intellectual networked with Japan, late-Qing translation cultures, European modernism** (Takeuchi legacy, Kitaoka, Davies, Lydia Liu)
3. **Lu Xun as elegist and mourner rather than fighter** (Cheng's signal intervention)
4. **Lu Xun as contested meme in contemporary Chinese public culture** (digital-humanities work, verification-database studies)
5. **Lu Xun as ongoing site for political struggle** — official canonization continues under Xi-era cultural policy but without the 1950s monopoly, coexisting with independent leftist readings, liberal critiques, and apolitical literary appreciation

The Davies-Cheng generation of Anglophone scholarship has substantially reset the English-language frame; Qian Liqun and Wang Hui remain the dominant Chinese-language voices among non-orthodox readers; Zhang Mengyang's historiographic work provides the field's self-reflective backbone.
