# Napoleon: Primary Works & Attested Authorship

Research dossier 1/6 for the Napoleon Echo. Scope: what Napoleon *actually* wrote or dictated, what was shaped by editors/hagiographers, and what is pure legend. All claims are labeled with provenance tier.

Label key: [documented] in authenticated primary source; [attested] multiple independent contemporary witnesses; [attributed] later source only, no contemporary corroboration; [legendary] popular but unverifiable or demonstrably corrupted; [modern-scholarly-inference] = modern historian synthesis.

---

## 1. The Two Correspondances — The Core of the Documentary Record

### 1.1 The Second Empire Correspondance (1858–1870)
- [documented] *Correspondance de Napoléon Ier, publiée par ordre de l'Empereur Napoléon III*, 32 volumes, Imprimerie Impériale, Paris, 1858–1869. Final 4 volumes are St. Helena dictations. Approximately 22,000 letters / proclamations. [web: https://catalogue.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/cb310033769]
- [modern-scholarly-inference] Explicitly hagiographic mandate. The commission under Achille Fould was told to exclude anything that could "undermine the idealization of Napoleon I and his era" the Second Empire wished to project. Letters were cut, suppressed, or silently edited. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/publication-of-the-complete-correspondance-of-napoleon-i-initial-results-of-the-fondation-napoleons-grand-project/]
- [documented] Supplementary collections by Léonce de Brotonne (1898, 1903) and Léon Lecestre (c. 1897) published material excluded from the Imperial Press edition. These are the first cracks in the Second Empire varnish.
- **Implication for the Echo:** any quote sourced *only* to the 1858–70 Correspondance and absent from the modern scholarly edition should be flagged as possibly sanitized.

### 1.2 The Fondation Napoléon *Correspondance générale* (2004–2018)
- [documented] 15 volumes, Fayard, edited under Thierry Lentz. 40,497 letters. 58% absent from the Second Empire edition; 30% entirely unpublished until this project. Drawn from 223 archives, French and foreign. Vol. 15 ("Les Chutes") covers January 1814 – May 1821 plus a 1788–1813 supplement. Published fully online on Napoleonica since January 2024. [web: https://fondationnapoleon.org/en/activities-and-services/telling-history/correspondence-napoleon/] [web: https://fondationnapoleon.org/en/2024/01/03/the-general-correspondence-of-napoleon-bonaparte-all-the-letters-are-now-available-on-napoleonica-les-archives/]
- **This is the gold-standard source** for any letter, order, memorandum, or state paper. The Echo should treat *Correspondance générale* > older *Correspondance* > memoirs as default hierarchy.

## 2. The St. Helena Corpus — All Mediated, All Suspect to Varying Degrees

The "four evangelists" (Las Cases, Bertrand, Gourgaud, Montholon) plus the physician O'Meara constitute the most quoted Napoleon — and the most unreliable as a record of his *actual words*.

### 2.1 Las Cases, *Mémorial de Sainte-Hélène* (1823)
- [documented] First published 1823, 8 vols. The single most influential source for the Napoleonic legend. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Memorial_of_Saint_Helena]
- [documented] In 2004 Peter Hicks discovered at the British Library (Bathurst papers) a copy of Las Cases's *original* manuscript, confiscated by Hudson Lowe in 1816 and presumed lost. Transcribed 2008–2011 by Lentz, Hicks, Houdecek, Prévot; published Perrin 2017 as *Le Mémorial de Sainte-Hélène. Le manuscrit retrouvé*. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/magazine/interviews/discovery-manuscript-memorial-de-sainte-helene-three-questions-peter-hicks-october-2017/]
- [modern-scholarly-inference] The 2017 edition shows the 1823 printed *Mémorial* is substantially more literary, more heroic, more ideologically shaped than what Napoleon actually said. Las Cases added flourishes, re-ordered dicta, and smoothed contradictions. The original manuscript is terser, more tactical, more cynical.
- **The Echo must NOT treat the 1823 *Mémorial* as transcript.** It is the founding document of *légende* construction, not of testimony. Prefer the 2017 reconstructed manuscript for anything cited as "Napoleon said at St. Helena."

### 2.2 Bertrand, *Cahiers de Sainte-Hélène*
- [documented] Grand Marshal Henri-Gatien Bertrand's private notebooks, deciphered by Paul Fleuriot de Langle in the 1950s. Generally considered the most neutrally-kept of the St. Helena diaries — Bertrand was not writing for publication. Valuable precisely because Bertrand had no Montholon-style commercial motive and no Las Cases-style monumentalizing motive.

### 2.3 Gourgaud, *Journal*
- [documented] Gaspard Gourgaud's diary, suppressed by his family for decades. A heavily bowdlerized version appeared c. 1899 and post-WWII; Jacques Macé published the *Journal intégral 1815-1818* (Perrin) only after papers were deposited at the Archives Nationales. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/gourgauds-complete-journal-or-the-exile-on-st-helena-seen-from-both-ends-of-the-telescope/]
- [attested] Gourgaud's prickly, jealous tone makes his record of Napoleon's *moods* valuable; his record of Napoleon's *words* is warped by his resentment.

### 2.4 Montholon, memoirs
- [modern-scholarly-inference] Least reliable. Hudson Lowe's secretary Gorrequer nicknamed Montholon "Buggiardo" (Italian for liar). His text was reconstituted while imprisoned at Ham with the future Napoleon III (1840–46) — meaning it was shaped by post-facto Bonapartist politics. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/four-for-the-gospel-makers-the-eye-witness-memoirs-of-napoleon-on-st-helena/]

### 2.5 Napoleon's own St. Helena dictations (as opposed to companions' diaries)
- [documented] Napoleon dictated commentaries on his own campaigns (Toulon, Italy, Egypt, the Hundred Days) and on historical military subjects. Published posthumously in the 8-vol *Mémoires pour servir à l'histoire de France, sous Napoléon* (Bossange, 1823–25; contract signed 1821 by Montholon and Gourgaud for 30,000 francs).
- [documented] *Précis des guerres de César*, dictated to valet Louis Marchand, first published Gosselin 1836. Two books: *La Guerre des Gaules* and *Guerres civiles*. Napoleon works from limited St. Helena library — his analysis reveals his *own* operational preoccupations projected onto Caesar (concentration, decisive point, moral ascendancy). [web: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k6516583j.texteImage]
- **Use these for his *theory of war*, not for biographical fact** — he mythologizes his own earlier campaigns.

## 3. Proclamations and Bulletins — Napoleon's Industrial-Scale Rhetoric

### 3.1 Italian Army proclamation, 27 March 1796 (Nice)
- [documented] "Soldats, vous êtes nus, mal nourris; le gouvernement vous doit beaucoup, il ne peut rien vous donner. [...] Je veux vous conduire dans les plus fertiles plaines du monde. De riches provinces, de grandes villes seront en votre pouvoir; vous y trouverez honneur, gloire et richesses." [web: https://www.historyguide.org/intellect/nap1796.html]
- [modern-scholarly-inference] Scholars including J. David Markham note Napoleon *later revised* this proclamation for his St. Helena memoirs to appear more polished. The version schoolchildren memorize is the St. Helena rewrite; the genuine 1796 text is terser.

### 3.2 Pre-Austerlitz address and post-battle proclamation
- [documented] The "Soldats! Je suis content de vous" proclamation is dated 3 December 1805 (12 frimaire an XIV) — the day *after* the battle, not the eve. Printed on the presses of the Grande Armée. "Vous avez décoré vos aigles d'une immortelle gloire." [web: https://www.drouot-estimations.com/en/lot/169134/]
- [attested] Eve-of-battle address ("Il suffira que vous disiez: 'J'étais à la bataille d'Austerlitz'") is reported by multiple participants but exact wording is not stable across sources.

### 3.3 Return from Elba / Golfe-Juan proclamations, 1 March 1815
- [documented] Prepared in advance, copied aboard the brig *Inconstant* during crossing. [web: https://100days.org.uk/items/show/11]
- [documented] "L'Aigle, avec les couleurs nationales, volera de clocher en clocher jusqu'aux tours de Notre-Dame." Authentic, published at Golfe-Juan/Cannes. [web: https://www.napoleon-empire.org/en/flight-eagle.php]

### 3.4 Bulletins de la Grande Armée
- [documented] Drafted under Napoleon's direct supervision or by his own hand; printed in *Le Moniteur universel*; posted on churches and town halls. The first modern systematic state propaganda apparatus.
- [attested] The French slang "mentir comme un Bulletin" ("to lie like a Bulletin") was current in Paris and among his own soldiers by the mid-reign. [web: https://www.napoleon-series.org/research/napoleon/c_spindoctor.html] [web: https://www.shapell.org/historical-perspectives/articles/napoleon-the-original-fake-news/]
- **For the Echo:** Napoleon *knowingly* falsified for effect. The Bulletins are authentic Napoleonic *voice* but unreliable Napoleonic *fact*.

## 4. The Code Civil — Napoleon's Interventions

- [documented] Napoleon presided at 55 of the 107 Conseil d'État sessions devoted to the draft Code. Primary transcription: Jean-Guillaume Locré, *Procès-verbaux du Conseil d'État contenant la discussion du projet de Code civil* (1803–04, 5 vols.); later expanded as *La Législation civile, commerciale et criminelle de la France* (31 vols., 1827–32), known as *L'Esprit du Code Napoléon*. [web: https://www.cairn.info/revue-napoleonica-la-revue-2012-2-page-25.htm]
- [documented] Napoleon pushed hard on divorce (he wanted it retained — he was already contemplating divorcing Joséphine), on paternal authority (reinforced), on property transmission, and on the husband-as-head model. Locré's procès-verbaux record him intervening directly, often cutting through jurists' technicalities with political reasoning.
- [attributed] Many *obiter dicta* from these sessions circulate as "Napoleon quotes" but need verification against Locré volume-and-page.

## 5. *Maximes de guerre et pensées* — Partially Authentic, Largely Compiled

- [documented] *Maximes de guerre de Napoléon* and *Maximes et pensées de Napoléon* were **posthumous compilations**. Gaudy jeune compiled one edition in 1838; the 1863 *Maximes de guerre et pensées de Napoléon Ier* (5th ed.) lists author as "Anonymous." [web: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k864783.image]
- [modern-scholarly-inference] The 114 "military maxims" and ~400 citations are drawn from genuine correspondence, proclamations, and St. Helena dictations, but the *ordering, numbering, and aphoristic crystallization* are editorial. A "maxim" phrasing is not Napoleon's phrasing — it is an editor's distillation of Napoleon's phrasing.
- **Echo should not cite "Maxim XXXII" as direct Napoleonic utterance** without tracing back to the underlying letter or dictation.

## 6. Early Youthful Writings

- [documented] *Lettres sur la Corse* (1789, unpublished in his lifetime): juvenile Rousseauian-Corsican essays. Shows the pre-French Napoleon — Paoli-admiring, Corsican nationalist.
- [documented] *Le Souper de Beaucaire* (July/August 1793), pamphlet. Dialogue defending Jacobin/Montagnard government and Carteaux's actions. Published at public expense by Augustin Robespierre; first step in his political visibility. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_souper_de_Beaucaire]
- [documented] *Discours de Lyon* (1791), essay for an Académie de Lyon prize on "what truths and sentiments should be instilled in men for their happiness." Contains the "meteor" line about genius at age 22.
- [documented] Brienne/Auxonne/Valence notebooks (preserved in fragments). Reading notes on Plutarch, Rousseau, Caesar, Plato, Macpherson's Ossian, Frederick II's military writings, Raynal. Polybius, Plutarch, Arrian are favorites at Brienne. Ossian is a lifelong obsession. [web: https://www.napoleon-series.org/biographies/napoleon/napoleon-the-reader-the-early-years/]

## 7. Famous Quotations — Authenticated, Attributed, or Legendary

| Quote | Status | Source |
|---|---|---|
| "An army marches on its stomach" | [legendary / misattributed] | First reliably documented in English 1862; also attributed to Frederick the Great (Carlyle, 1858). Probably *condensed* from a passage in Las Cases, *Mémorial*, vol. 4, 14 Nov. 1816 — meaning even the "source" is mediated. Historians note the irony: Napoleon's actual logistics record (Egypt, Russia) is catastrophic. [web: https://quoteinvestigator.com/2017/10/15/army/] |
| "Impossible is not French" / "Ce n'est pas possible, cela n'est pas français" | [documented] | Letter to General Jean Lemarois, Dresden, 9 July 1813, during the defense of Magdeburg. Full: "Ce n'est pas possible, m'écrivez-vous; cela n'est pas français." Authentic and in the Correspondance. [web: https://shannonselin.com/2014/08/10-napoleon-bonaparte-quotes-context/] |
| "Impossible is a word found only in the dictionary of fools" | [legendary] | No primary source. A popular nineteenth-century expansion/corruption of the Lemarois letter. Absent from Correspondance générale. Blacklist all quote-site attributions. |
| "Never interrupt an enemy who is making a mistake" | [attributed] | Closest authentic source: Jomini, *Vie politique et militaire de Napoléon* (1827), p. 180: "Quand l'ennemi fait un faux mouvement, il faut se garder de l'interrompre." Paraphrased by Alison 1836. The modern aphoristic version is a century-plus of polishing, not Napoleon's words. [web: https://quoteinvestigator.com/2010/07/06/never-interfere/] |
| "Men of genius are meteors destined to burn to illuminate their century" | [documented] | *Discours de Lyon*, 1791, age 22. |
| "I regarded myself for the first time not as a simple general, but as a man called to influence the fate of peoples" | [attributed] | Reported by Napoleon on St. Helena re. Lodi 1796 — i.e., self-mythologizing retrospective via the evangelists, not contemporaneous. |
| "From the moment I had power, I hastened to re-establish religion. I used it as foundation and root" | [attributed] | Las Cases *Mémorial*. Compare 2017 manuscrit retrouvé for exact phrasing. |
| "If I had to choose a religion, the sun..." | [legendary] | Widely attributed, no clean primary source. |
| "Masses de granit" | [documented] | Council of State, 18 floréal an X (8 May 1802), speech on the Legion of Honor: "nous ne l'aurons pas si nous ne jetons pas sur le sol de France quelques masses de granit." [web: https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masses_de_granit] |
| "L'Aigle volera de clocher en clocher..." | [documented] | Golfe-Juan proclamation, 1 March 1815. |

**Blacklist note:** brainyquote, azquotes, goodreads, quotefancy all attribute dozens of "Napoleon quotes" that trace to no primary source. The Echo should refuse any quote whose earliest traceable attribution is post-1830 secondary compilation.

## 8. Napoleon's Own Conceptual Vocabulary

Recurring and *self-coined or heavily-favored* terms — present in Correspondance and/or authenticated speeches:

- [documented] **gloire** — supreme value-term; in the 1797 letter to the Directory: "Mon pouvoir tient à ma gloire, et ma gloire aux victoires."
- [documented] **étoile / destin** — his Corsican-inflected superstition; appears in letters and recorded conversation.
- [documented] **masses de granit** — 1802, for his stabilizing institutions (Legion of Honor, Concordat, Code, lycées, Banque de France, prefects).
- [documented] **fusion / ralliement** — post-Brumaire vocabulary for his policy of healing revolutionary fracture by co-opting royalists, Jacobins, and the former nobility into one regime. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/napoleonic-legitimacies-and-the-proclamation-of-empire/]
- [attested] **amour-propre national** — national self-love as political lever; recurs in his political writings and conversation.
- [documented] **ligne d'opération, point décisif, concentration des forces** — operational vocabulary inherited from Bourcet (*Principes de la guerre de montagne*, 1775) and Guibert (*Essai général de tactique*, 1770). [web: https://apps.dtic.mil/sti/pdfs/ADA357424.pdf]
- [attested] **génie** — used both for himself (rarely directly) and as an analytic category for great captains.
- [attested] **imagination** — for Napoleon, imagination *governs* the world; a recurring St. Helena theme.

## 9. Cited Influences (in his own words and recorded conversations)

- [documented] **Caesar** — object of the *Précis*, model of combined political-military leadership.
- [documented] **Alexander** — lifelong fascination; Egypt explicitly framed as Alexandrine.
- [documented] **Hannibal** — invoked in Italian campaign, esp. the Alps crossing.
- [documented] **Frederick the Great** — read since cadet school; tomb visited at Potsdam 1806 (took his sword).
- [documented] **Turenne** — the 17th-c. French marshal; cited as model commander.
- [documented] **Bourcet** — mountain-war theorist; direct ancestor of Napoleon's dispersed-march / concentrated-battle operational grammar.
- [documented] **Guibert** — *Essai général de tactique* (1770); army-living-off-the-country and mobility.
- [documented] **Rousseau** — youthful passion; later cooled ("without him France would not have had the Revolution," St. Helena — approving and regretful at once).
- [documented] **Plutarch** — lifelong; the *Lives* traveled in the campaign library.
- [documented] **Ossian** — Macpherson's pseudo-Celtic epics; campaign library staple.

## 10. Methodological Flags for the Echo

1. **Three filters between Napoleon and us:** (a) Second Empire hagiographic editing, (b) St. Helena companions' shaping and commercial motives, (c) the 19th-century aphoristic-compilation industry. The Echo must not speak in quote-card voice — that voice is not his.
2. **The 2017 *Mémorial manuscrit retrouvé* is a recent shock to the corpus.** Any older secondary work citing the 1823 *Mémorial* should be cross-checked — what Las Cases embellished vs. what Napoleon said shifts the tone (the 2017 text is more sardonic, less elegiac).
3. **Voice changes by medium.** Letters (terse, imperative, numerical) ≠ Bulletins (theatrical, bombastic) ≠ Conseil d'État (analytical, blunt) ≠ St. Helena dictations (retrospective, mythologizing). The Echo needs to know which register it is in.
4. **Contradictions are real, not artifacts.** Napoleon on religion varies between cynic and utilitarian. Napoleon on destiny varies between fatalist and voluntarist. Preserve these; do not smooth them.
