# 02 — Contemporary Records & Eyewitness Accounts

Research purpose: separate what people who actually observed Napoleon wrote from later legend. Every witness here is biased. Bias does not disqualify — it localizes the testimony. The goal is to see Napoleon *through* these lenses, knowing their angles.

Tag legend:
- **[documented]** — contemporaneous record (letter, journal kept daily, protocol, stenographed session)
- **[attested]** — written down by a direct eyewitness within living memory of the event, but reconstructed (memoirs, late recollections)
- **[attributed]** — reported at one remove, or through a witness with a known axe to grind
- **[legendary]** — circulating but source chain broken or pure later embellishment
- **[modern-scholarly-inference]** — historian's judgment on the witness's reliability

## 1. Talleyrand — *Mémoires* (written ~1816, published 1891)

**Bias profile:** Betrayed Napoleon from roughly 1807 onward; served the Bourbon Restoration; memoirs were **deliberately self-exculpatory** and written to justify his career to the returning monarchy. He ordered publication 30 years posthumously; editor de Bacourt reconstructed connected narrative from papers Talleyrand himself had thinned. [modern-scholarly-inference] [web: https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Historical_Essays_and_Studies/Talleyrand's_Memoirs]

- **Working method observed**: Talleyrand attended Napoleon's working sessions from the Consulate through 1807 — direct eyewitness. Describes Napoleon's impatience, capacity to carry multiple dossiers, his tendency to demand instant answers and punish hesitation. [attested]
- **Tilsit (1807)** — Talleyrand was physically present at the raft and the subsequent conferences; his account of Napoleon's personal seduction of Alexander I is eyewitness, but filtered through Talleyrand's later conviction (expressed to Alexander himself, secretly) that Napoleon had passed the point of rational policy. [attested, with hostile re-framing]
- **Spanish ulcer (1808)** — Talleyrand claims he opposed the Spanish intervention; documentary record (his own prior memos) partially supports this but also shows him complicit in the Bayonne abduction of the Bourbons. His memoirs retrospectively brighten his opposition. [attributed — self-polished]
- **1814 collapse** — Talleyrand was the principal internal agent of the collapse; his account is the testimony of the man who engineered it. Useful for *mechanics*, unreliable for *motive*.

**Usefulness:** indispensable for diplomatic atmospherics and for Napoleon-in-council; discount any passage where Talleyrand's own foresight or principles are at stake.

[web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/biographies/talleyrand-perigord-charles-maurice-de/]
[web: https://www.worldhistory.org/Charles-Maurice_de_Talleyrand-Perigord/]

## 2. Metternich — *Mémoires* and the Dresden Interview (26 June 1813)

**Bias profile:** Napoleon's principal adversarial diplomat from 1809. His *Mémoires* were compiled/edited late, with post-1815 Habsburg restoration framing.

**The "million men" episode** — Dresden, in the Marcolini Palace, an 8½-hour interview. Metternich reminded Napoleon of the youth of the conscripts dying in Russia; Napoleon (by Metternich's account) said roughly: "A man such as I am does not trouble himself with the lives of a million men" — some sources render this more profanely. [attested — but only via Metternich]

**Source-chain caution:** there are three published contemporary accounts (two by Metternich — one in letter to his wife Eleonore two days later, one later polished; one by Baron Fain, Napoleon's secretary). **They are contradictory and incomplete.** A fourth account exists via Caulaincourt, who took the narrative down from Napoleon "a few hours after it ended" — this is the closest thing to a Napoleon-side check on Metternich. [modern-scholarly-inference; see Munro Price, "Napoleon and Metternich in 1813," *French History* 26:4, 2012, web: https://academic.oup.com/fh/article-abstract/26/4/482/650576]

So: the *sentiment* is well-attested; the *exact words* are Metternich-mediated and should not be treated as a verbatim quote.

[web: https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v42/n19/christopher-clark/a-rock-of-order]

## 3. Bourrienne — *Mémoires sur Napoléon* (pub. 1829–31)

**Bias profile:** Childhood friend from Brienne; Napoleon's first private secretary; dismissed in 1802 for financial irregularities; later rehabilitated, then disgraced again under the Restoration. Deeply resentful. **Memoirs were ghost-written in part by Charles-Maximilien de Villemarest** — a fact established by 19th-century critics. Lanfrey, Masson, and Tulard all flag large passages as unreliable or fabricated — especially anything post-1802, which Bourrienne did not witness. [modern-scholarly-inference]

- Vivid on Brienne, Italy 1796, Egypt, the Consulate setup — where he was actually present. [attested, with caution]
- Post-1802 material treats rumor as eyewitness. [attributed → legendary in places]

**Rule of thumb (Tulard):** cross-check every Bourrienne anecdote against Méneval or Roederer before using.

[web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/biographies/ — Bourrienne entry]
[web: https://www.napoleon-series.org/research/eyewitness/c_truth.html]

## 4. Baron de Méneval — *Mémoires* (pub. 1827, expanded 1894)

**Bias profile:** Napoleon's private secretary 1802–1813, loyal to the end, accompanied Marie-Louise to Vienna. Generally pro-Napoleon but **temperamentally measured**; the least sensational of the secretaries. Tulard and Masson treat him as the most reliable intimate witness for working habits.

- **Working hours**: Méneval records Napoleon telling him he had "constantly worked 16 hours a day ever since leaving military school." [attested]
- "Come back at one, or at four, and we will work" — night working pattern. [attested]
- Methodical description of dictation style: pacing, rapid delivery, interruption mid-sentence to handle another dossier, then resumption without losing thread.

[web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/biographies/meneval-claude-francois/]
[web: https://shannonselin.com/2022/01/napoleon-workaholic/]

## 5. Savary, duc de Rovigo — *Mémoires* (pub. 1828, 8 vols.)

**Bias profile:** Police minister from 1810, Napoleon's most unconditional executor (including the Enghien abduction of 1804). Memoirs are openly Bonapartist, framed as a defense of his own legacy. Published under Bourbon suspicion. [modern-scholarly-inference]

- Value: direct witness to the police apparatus, to the Enghien affair (self-serving), to Napoleon's 1805–1807 field behavior (he was an aide-de-camp before the police posting), and to Napoleon's pre-Austerlitz deception mission to Allied HQ at Olmütz, where he was ordered to appear nervous and evasive to bait the Allies forward. [attested]
- Discount: any passage defending his own role in Enghien.

[web: https://www.napoleon-empire.org/en/personalities/savary.php]

## 6. Caulaincourt — *Mémoires* (pub. 1933, ed. Jean Hanoteau)

**Bias profile:** Grand Equerry, ambassador to Russia 1807–1811 (opposed the 1812 war), foreign minister 1813–14. Personally wounded by Napoleon (the Emperor let him take blame for the Enghien affair) but remained loyal to the end. Hanoteau's scholarly edition is **the single most important Napoleonic intimate source recovered in the 20th century.** [modern-scholarly-inference]

Caulaincourt kept **contemporaneous notes** of conversations — so passages are often at the [documented] level rather than merely [attested].

Key eyewitness episodes:
- **Moscow** — Caulaincourt was with Napoleon in the Kremlin during the fires; recorded Napoleon's first reaction and subsequent rationalizations. [documented — from notes]
- **The Return from Russia** — shared the sleigh with Napoleon from Smorgoni to Paris (December 1812); 13-day journey of continuous conversation. Probably the single most privileged transcript of Napoleon-unfiltered in existence. [documented]
- **Dresden 1813 Metternich interview** — Napoleon narrated it to Caulaincourt within hours; corroborates and partially corrects Metternich. [attested]
- **Fontainebleau 1814** — present for the first abdication and the suicide attempt (the vial of opium/belladonna carried since Russia, taken the night of 12–13 April 1814; too weak after 18 months to kill him). [attested]

[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armand-Augustin-Louis_de_Caulaincourt]
[web: https://archive.org/details/withnapoleoninru00caul]

## 7. Madame de Rémusat — *Mémoires* (pub. 1879–80)

**Bias profile:** Dame du palais to Josephine 1802–. Family aligned with the Beauharnais faction; politically de-favored after Josephine's repudiation (1809); **destroyed her first manuscript in 1815** fearing political danger, then re-wrote. Retrospectively Bourbonist. Critical of Napoleon. [modern-scholarly-inference]

- Strong on court atmosphere, on Napoleon's behavior toward women, on Josephine–Napoleon dynamics 1802–1807. [attested]
- Weaker on politics, which she heard secondhand via her husband. [attributed]

[web: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/49175]

## 8. Roederer — *Journal* and *Œuvres* (Journal recently re-edited by Thierry Lentz, Perrin 2025)

**Bias profile:** Accomplice of Brumaire; president of the Interior section of the Council of State during the Consulate. Loyal but analytic. His journal is **kept daily or near-daily** — by far the most contemporaneous of the major sources. [documented]

- Value: catches Napoleon *thinking out loud* during the 1800–1804 constitutional/legal reforms; quotations from Roederer's journal are routinely reused by subsequent historians because they are dated, specific, and near-stenographic. [documented]
- Example: Napoleon's self-description as needing to "found, not revolutionize" France (Consulate-era conversation) is first attested in Roederer.

[web: https://shs.cairn.info/journal-napoleonica-la-revue-2013-1-page-17]
[web: https://shs.cairn.info/revue-napoleonica-the-journal-2025-2-page-211]

## 9. The Marshals and Officers — Marmont, Marbot, Ségur

- **Marmont** (*Mémoires*, pub. 1857): written after his 1814 defection; an extended apologia. "Ragusard" entered French as a synonym for traitor. Every judgment on Napoleon should be read as a self-defense. [modern-scholarly-inference]
- **Marbot** (*Mémoires*, pub. 1891): vivid hussar memoir, the direct inspiration for Conan Doyle's Brigadier Gerard. "Story-teller's exaggeration and selectivity" is the consensus; good on atmosphere, untrustworthy on specifics. Conan Doyle called it "the first of all soldier books." [legendary-adjacent in places; attested for atmosphere]
- **Ségur** (*Histoire de Napoléon et de la Grande Armée pendant l'année 1812*, pub. 1824): literary rather than archival; duelled Gourgaud, who disputed parts of it. Vivid and canonical for the Moscow fire, the retreat, the Berezina — but Ségur shapes for effect. [attested, with literary polish]

[web: https://www.napoleon-series.org/research/eyewitness/c_truth.html]

## 10. St Helena witnesses — Las Cases, O'Meara, Antommarchi, Bertrand, Montholon, Gourgaud; British: Lowe, Balmain

- **Las Cases** (*Mémorial de Sainte-Hélène*, 1823): the single most influential book in constructing the Napoleonic legend; Napoleon knew he was dictating for posterity. Shapes the record. [attested, shaped]
- **O'Meara** (*A Voice from St Helena*, 1822): British naval surgeon, dismissed 1818 after colluding with the French suite. Polemic against Governor Hudson Lowe. Thomas Creevey endorsed O'Meara's fidelity to Napoleon's actual words; but the framing is hostile to Lowe. [attested, anti-Lowe]
- **Antommarchi** (*Derniers moments*, 1825): Corsican physician, largely external to Longwood, disliked by Napoleon; "romantic version" giving himself the lead role. [legendary in parts]
- **Gourgaud's Journal** (pub. 1899): jealous, irritable, honest — valuable precisely because he records Napoleon's small bad moods.
- **Hudson Lowe papers** (British Library Add MSS): administrative, hostile, precise. Counterweight to O'Meara. [documented]
- **Balmain** (Russian commissioner): neutral observer reports. [documented]

[web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/four-for-the-gospel-makers-the-eye-witness-memoirs-of-napoleon-on-st-helena/]
[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_O'Meara]

## 11. Opponents' eyewitness — Wellington, Alexander I, Blücher

- **Wellington, via Stanhope (1886) and Croker (*Croker Papers*, 1884)**: "I used to say of him that his presence on the field made the difference of forty thousand men" — Wellington to Croker, 4 September 1852, *Croker Papers* III, p. 276; variant in Stanhope's *Notes of Conversations*. [attested]
- Wellington generally declined to criticize Napoleon openly out of professional propriety toward a beaten opponent. [modern-scholarly-inference]
- **Alexander I**: correspondence 1807–1812 is documentary; his Tilsit impressions are recoverable from letters to his sister Catherine and to his mother. [documented]

[web: https://en.wikiquote.org/wiki/Arthur_Wellesley,_1st_Duke_of_Wellington]

## 12. Council of State — Locré, *Esprit du Code Napoléon* and *Procès-verbaux du Conseil d'État*

Napoleon presided over 55 of 107 Council sessions during Civil Code drafting. Locré, Secretary-General of the Conseil d'État, compiled the *procès-verbaux* — official minutes capturing Napoleon **mid-argument** with Cambacérès, Portalis, Tronchet. These are the closest thing to a transcript of Napoleon's legal and administrative reasoning. [documented]

[web: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/bpt6k4261609]

## 13. Physical description and working habits — composite

- Chaptal (interior minister, chemist): noted Napoleon's Corsican-inflected French and his cold-sensitivity (always near a roaring fire). [attested]
- Hot-bath dictation ("bain de chaleur"): multiple witnesses (Méneval, Constant the valet, Roustam the Mameluke). Napoleon would ring at 1 or 2 a.m., call for a light, sometimes for a very hot bath, and begin dictation. [attested, multi-witness]
- Short polyphasic sleep: ~6 hours total, broken. Multiple contemporaries including Méneval and Constant agree. [attested]
- Rapid dictation, walking while dictating, holding multiple dossiers simultaneously: Méneval, Fain, Roederer all converge. [attested]
- Physical description 1798 (pre-power): Alexander von Humboldt's eyewitness account — thin, intense, sallow — before the well-known post-1807 weight gain. [attested, web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/a-physical-description-of-napoleon-and-josephine-in-1798-eyewitness-accounts-by-alexander-wilhelm-von-humboldt/]

## 14. Cross-witness convergence — what eyewitnesses agree on

Across hostile and friendly witnesses (Talleyrand, Metternich, Caulaincourt, Méneval, Roederer, Chaptal, Wellington-via-Stanhope), the **convergent portrait** is: extraordinary working capacity, rapid decision cycling, impatience with slow interlocutors, memory for detail (orders-of-battle, tax rolls, individual pensions), charm deployed tactically, willingness to spend men for strategic position, and — increasingly after 1808 — refusal to hear contrary counsel. This convergence across witnesses with opposed biases is the strongest evidentiary form available for a personality.
