# Napoleon: Letters, Documented Speech & Attested Sayings

Research Agent 3/6. Scope: Napoleon's personal voice across four phases — Italian-campaign romantic, Consulate brisk-administrative, Imperial-peak imperious, St. Helena reflective-mythologizing — plus provenance triage of famous sayings. Quote-labels: **[documented]** (in Napoleon's own hand or signed dictation), **[attested]** (recorded by a named eyewitness in memoirs), **[attributed]** (claimed but sourcing weak), **[legendary]** (apocryphal; earliest source post-dates Napoleon significantly).

---

## 1. The Correspondence Corpus (Baseline)

The modern scholarly edition is the *Correspondance générale de Napoléon Bonaparte* published by the Fondation Napoléon (Fayard, 15 volumes, 2004–2018), superseding the 32-volume *Correspondance de Napoléon Ier publiée par ordre de Napoléon III* (Paris, 1858–1870), which sanitized embarrassing letters (especially to his brothers). The full corpus — c. 40,000 letters — is now searchable on napoleonica.org. [web: https://fondationnapoleon.org/en/activities-and-services/telling-history/correspondence-napoleon/] [web: https://www.napoleonica.org/en/collections/correspondance]

**Signature evolution** [documented, handwriting analysis]: "Buonaparte" (Corsican, pre-1796) → "Bonaparte" (1796–1804) → "Napoléon" (after coronation, 1804) → abbreviated "Napol.", "Nap.", "Np.", "N." as workload increased and handwriting deteriorated into a "medley of unconnected and illegible signs" (Méneval). [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/images/letter-from-bonaparte-1765-1821-to-josephine-marked-nice-le-10-germinal/]

## 2. Letters to Josephine (1796–1810)

Some 275 letters survive, the most explosive clustered in spring–summer 1796 during the Italian campaign. Authenticated and published in Henry Foljambe Hall (1901); modernized in Jean Tulard and Chantal de Tourtier-Bonazzi's editions; a recent French edition is *Lettres à Joséphine* (ed. Chantal de Tourtier-Bonazzi, Fondation Napoléon). [web: https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/37499]

**Sample [documented] — Nice, 10 Germinal An IV (30 March 1796)**, a few days after the marriage:
> *"Je n'ai pas passé un jour sans t'aimer; je n'ai pas passé une nuit sans te serrer dans mes bras..."*
> ("I have not spent a day without loving you; I have not spent a night without clasping you in my arms...")

**Sample [documented] — Verona, 17 November 1796**:
> *"Je ne t'aime plus du tout; au contraire, je te déteste. Tu es une vilaine, bien gauche, bien bête, bien Cendrillon..."*
> ("I no longer love you at all; on the contrary, I detest you. You are a horrid, awkward, stupid little Cinderella...") — sarcastic tenderness, then collapses into demand that she come to him.

**Voice features of the Italian letters**: violent oscillation — adoration, then jealousy, then petulance; short imperatives ("*Viens! Pars! Écris!*"); obsessive bodily references; dashes instead of commas; spelling errors in his own hand he never corrected. [web: https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Translation:Napoleon_Bonaparte%27s_Letter_to_Josephine,_Nice,_le_10_germinal]

**"Ne te lave pas, j'accours..."** — **[legendary]**. Scholars including Alain Corbin have searched the authenticated corpus (Hall 1901; Fayard 1981; *Correspondance générale* v.1) and cannot locate it. It does not appear on the cited pages of *Lettres d'amour à Joséphine* (Fayard, 1981, p. 155, which is in fact about decorative plants and a blister). The line is also variously attributed to Henri IV (to Gabrielle d'Estrées) and to Baudelaire. No 19th-century French source attaches it to Napoleon. Earliest traceable 20th-century circulation. [web: https://skeptics.stackexchange.com/questions/54339/did-napol%C3%A9on-bonaparte-ask-his-wife-josephine-not-to-bathe-for-several-days-in-a] [web: https://www.artandpopularculture.com/Ne_te_lave_pas..._J%27arrive%21]

## 3. Letters to Marie-Louise (1810–1814)

318 autograph letters survive at the BnF (NAF 20042–20044), edited by Louis Madelin as *Lettres inédites de Napoléon Ier à Marie-Louise écrites de 1810 à 1814* (1935). Voice shift is dramatic: clipped domestic bulletins. [web: https://gallica.bnf.fr/ark:/12148/btv1b10515472c.image] [web: https://archive.org/stream/in.ernet.dli.2015.81167/2015.81167.The-Letters-Of-Napoleon-To-Marie-louise_djvu.txt]

**[documented] Typical opening from Russia/Saxony**: *"Mon amie, j'ai reçu ta lettre du... Je me porte bien. Embrasse mon fils. Tout à toi, Nap."* ("My friend, I received your letter of... I am well. Kiss my son. All yours, Nap.") He signs "Nap" or "N" almost universally in this correspondence.

**"Papa François"** — affectionate shorthand Marie-Louise uses (returned by Napoleon) for Emperor Francis I of Austria; Napoleon writes *"embrasse papa François de ma part"* repeatedly in 1812–13 — a chilling domesticity while simultaneously warring against the man's coalitions.

## 4. Letters to Brothers

Editions: *Correspondance de Napoléon avec son frère Joseph* (ed. Du Casse, 1854, partially expurgated); the 1858–70 *Correspondance* omitted many of the harshest letters to Louis and Jérôme; restored in the Fondation Napoléon edition. [web: https://archive.org/stream/confidentialcorr01naporich/confidentialcorr01naporich_djvu.txt]

**[documented] To Louis (King of Holland), 3 April 1807**, on Louis going native and favoring Dutch interests: *"Vous gouvernez trop cette nation. Un roi qui veut gouverner est tout l'opposé d'un roi qui règne."* The voice here is the imperial-peak voice: didactic, imperative, uses "vous" to brother-kings (a deliberate distancing from tu).

**[documented] To Jérôme, on his American marriage to Elizabeth Patterson (1803–1805)**: Napoleon orders the marriage annulled, refuses to receive Elizabeth in Europe, cuts off Jérôme's allowance. Letters alternate cold sentence ("*Cette union est nulle*") with torrential pages of strategic reasoning for why a Bonaparte cannot marry a commoner.

**[documented] To Joseph (King of Spain), passim 1808–1813**: demands operational counts, scolds slowness, contradicts himself across campaigns. Joseph complained privately that Napoleon's letters treated him as a lieutenant, not a sovereign — and the tone bears this out.

## 5. Letters to Ministers (Working Voice Under Stress)

To Cambacérès (Arch-Chancellor), Fouché and later Savary (Police), Berthier (War, chief of staff), Clarke (War after 1807) — the "administrative brisk" voice. Short sentences. Numbered items. Verbs in imperative. Often written at 3 a.m. and sent by relay courier.

**[documented] Typical opening**: *"Mon cousin, faites-moi connaître..."* — routine form to ministers.

**During the Russian retreat**, letters to Maret (Foreign Minister at Vilnius) become staccato, almost panicked in their compression: "*Il faut... il faut... il faut...*" chains. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/biographies/meneval-claude-francois/]

---

## 6. Letters to Sovereigns (Diplomatic Voice)

To Alexander I, Francis I, Pope Pius VII, George III (unanswered), the Prince Regent (the Bellerophon letter of 13 July 1815).

**[documented] To the Prince Regent, 13 July 1815** (after Waterloo): *"Altesse Royale, en butte aux factions qui divisent mon pays et à l'inimitié des plus grandes puissances de l'Europe, j'ai terminé ma carrière politique et je viens, comme Thémistocle, m'asseoir sur le foyer du peuple britannique..."* — Plutarchian self-dramatization; the Themistocles comparison is deliberate. The British did not play Admetus.

**[documented] To Alexander I, 1807–1811**: warm, almost seductive during Tilsit honeymoon (*"Sire, mon frère..."*); clipped and accusatory by 1811.

## 7. Love Letters — Walewska, Grassini, Mlle George

**Marie Walewska [documented]**, 1807, note sent to her at Talleyrand's dinner: *"Je n'ai vu que vous, je n'ai admiré que vous, je ne désire que vous. Une réponse bien prompte pour calmer l'impatiente ardeur de N."* Letters preserved by the Walewska family, first published in full by André Castelot, *Napoléon* (1968). [web: https://colonnawalewski.ch/corrnapoleonmarie/]

The voice to Walewska is peremptory, hurried, almost command-register — contrast the Josephine letters' torrential emotion. He signs "N." to her. Brief. Repetitive. Almost instrumental.

## 8. Dictation Style (Méneval, Fain, Bourrienne)

Eyewitness testimony from three secretaries: Bourrienne (to 1802), Méneval (1802–1813), Fain (1813–1815). Fain's *Manuscrit de 1814* is especially valuable.

**Pace**: Napoleon dictated while pacing. The faster the thought, the faster the walk. "He would dictate with amazing fluency and clarity, and so rapidly that it was necessary to invent a kind of short-hand, and to fill up gaps from memory or conjecture; for he hated to be interrupted." Secretaries captured tone and salient points, reconstructing prose later for his signature. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/biographies/meneval-claude-francois/] [web: https://napoleonichistoricalsociety.org/a-day-in-the-life-of-napoleon-part-ii/]

**Parallel dictation**: at Saint-Cloud and on campaign, Napoleon sometimes dictated to Méneval and Bourrienne simultaneously on different subjects — tested and confirmed by both men in separate memoirs.

Implication: most "Napoleon letters" are Napoleon's *phrasing filtered through a secretary's pen*. The voice is his; the punctuation and polish are not.

## 9. Speeches to Troops

**Pre-Italian campaign, "Soldats, vous êtes nus..."** — **[legendary, dictated 1823]**. The famous ringing version ("*Je veux vous conduire dans les plus fertiles plaines du monde...*") is NOT what Napoleon actually delivered at Nice in March 1796. It was dictated to Montholon on St. Helena c. 1819–1820 and published in the *Mémoires* (1823). The actual 27 March 1796 proclamation is briefer, flatter: "*Soldats, vous êtes nus, mal nourris. Le gouvernement vous doit beaucoup, il ne peut rien vous donner...*" The legendary version — modeled on Livy's Hannibal speech — replaced the real one in memory. Peter Hicks (Fondation Napoléon) demonstrates this clearly. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/talking-point-with-peter-hicks-soldiers-you-are-naked/]

**Post-Austerlitz proclamation, 3 December 1805** — **[documented]**: *"Soldats, je suis content de vous..."* Printed on the Grande Armée's press next day, signed by Berthier; text stable across all editions. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/proclamation-after-austerlitz-12-frimaire-an-xiv-3-december-1805/]

**Adieux de Fontainebleau, 20 April 1814** — **[attested, Fain]**: *"Soldats de ma vieille garde, je vous fais mes adieux. Depuis vingt ans, je vous ai trouvés constamment sur le chemin de l'honneur et de la gloire..."* Improvised; reconstructed by Fain in his *Manuscrit de 1814* from witness testimony. Text is close to verbatim but not stenographically captured. [web: https://www.napoleon.org/histoire-des-2-empires/articles/les-adieux-de-fontainebleau-a-la-vieille-garde-20-avril-1814/]

## 10. Famous Sayings — Triage

| Quote | Status | Source |
|---|---|---|
| "Impossible n'est pas français" | **[documented, paraphrased]** Letter to Gen. Le Marois, 9 July 1813: actual wording is *"Il n'est pas possible, m'écrivez-vous: cela n'est pas français."* The compressed form was popularized by Balzac, *Maximes et pensées de Napoléon* (1838). [web: https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impossible_n%27est_pas_fran%C3%A7ais] |
| "An army marches on its stomach" | **[legendary/misattributed]** First strong match: Carlyle's *Frederick the Great* (1858), attributed to Frederick II. Attributed to Napoleon only from 1862. No French original. [web: https://quoteinvestigator.com/2017/10/15/army/] |
| "Never interrupt an enemy making a mistake" | **[attributed, reconstructed]** Earliest: Archibald Alison, *History of Europe* (1836), as "*When the enemy is making a false movement, we must take good care not to interrupt him.*" Modern snappy form is 20th-century (Theodore White, 1965). |
| "Moral is to the physical as three to one" | **[documented]** *Observations sur les affaires d'Espagne*, Saint-Cloud, 27 August 1808, *Correspondance* Vol. 17, pp. 471–72 (Paris 1868). Intended for Joseph. [web: https://militaryhistorynow.com/2014/08/20/the-quotable-bonaparte-nine-of-napoleons-best-known-remarks/] |
| "A soldier will fight for a bit of colored ribbon" | **[attested]** Said aboard HMS *Bellerophon*, 15 July 1815, to Capt. Frederick Maitland; recorded in Maitland's *Narrative of the Surrender of Buonaparte* (1826). Justifying the Légion d'honneur (est. 1802). |
| "Religion is excellent stuff for keeping common people quiet" | **[attributed, weak]** Widely circulated but no primary-source attestation located in *Correspondance* or in Las Cases/Gourgaud/Montholon. Likely a paraphrase of authentic remarks (Napoleon DID make instrumentalist statements about religion to Roederer and at the Conseil d'État) that hardened into this specific wording. Treat as legendary-phrasing of documented-attitude. |
| "I love power as a musician loves his violin" | **[attested]** Conversation with Pierre-Louis Roederer, February 1809, recorded in Roederer's journal (*Autour de Bonaparte*, ed. Vitrac, Daragon, 1909). Paraphrased by Roederer, not verbatim. |
| "From the sublime to the ridiculous is but a step" | **[attested]** To the Abbé de Pradt, Warsaw, 10 December 1812, during the retreat from Moscow. Recorded in de Pradt, *Histoire de l'ambassade dans le Grand Duché de Varsovie en 1812* (Paris, 1815), pp. 214–15. The idea (but not the phrasing) predates Napoleon in Paine, *Age of Reason* (1795). |
| "Not tonight, Josephine" | **[legendary]** Zero attestation in any 19th-century source. Earliest print: 1904 (English-language music hall). Never in French. Completely apocryphal. [web: https://wordhistories.net/2025/02/13/not-tonight-josephine/] |
| "England is a nation of shopkeepers" | **[legendary/misattributed]** Phrase originates with Adam Smith, *Wealth of Nations* (1776), non-pejorative. Used pejoratively by Bertrand Barère in the National Convention, 11 June 1794. No contemporary French source puts it in Napoleon's mouth. British press later ascribed it to him. [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nation_of_shopkeepers] |
| "Every soldier carries a marshal's baton in his knapsack" | **[legendary/composite]** Army proverb of the Revolutionary period; consolidated from utterances of Napoleon, Mme de Staël, and Louis XVIII (the latter's 1819 army-law speech is best attested). No single Napoleon letter or speech contains it verbatim. |

## 11. Private Doubt & Reflection

**After Aspern-Essling (May 1809)** — first major tactical defeat. Silence in letters is louder than statements; he writes to Marie-Louise's predecessors-in-correspondence normally, but to Berthier his orders become more brittle. No explicit admission.

**After Borodino (7 September 1812)** — privately to Caulaincourt that evening: *"De toutes mes batailles, la plus terrible..."* — attested in Caulaincourt's *Mémoires* (written 1822–27, published 1933). [attested]

**At St. Helena**, dictating to Las Cases and Montholon, he mythologizes more than he repents. But to O'Meara (British doctor) he said in a private moment: *"I ought to have died at Moscow"* — **[attested]** via O'Meara, *Napoleon in Exile* (1822); O'Meara's reliability is contested but this line appears corroborated by Gourgaud.

## 12. Style Across Four Phases

1. **Italian-campaign (1796–99) — romantic-volcanic**: long sentences, exclamations, jealous violence, bad spelling, direct address to Josephine in *tu*. Signs "Bonaparte."
2. **Consulate (1799–1804) — brisk-administrative**: legislative reform voice, numbered points, *Code civil* drafting sessions (Cambacérès testifies to blistering pace). Signs "Bonaparte" then "Le Premier Consul."
3. **Imperial peak (1804–1812) — imperious**: uses "nous" to sovereigns and subordinates; "vous" to brothers (distancing); "tu" only to Marie-Louise and his son. Dictates at torrent pace; hates being interrupted. Signs "Napoléon" descending to "N."
4. **St. Helena (1815–1821) — reflective-mythologizing**: in dictated *Mémoires* constructs a Napoleon for posterity — retrofits the 1796 proclamation, recasts Waterloo as betrayal, writes himself as Plutarchian hero. Las Cases's *Mémorial* (1823) is the primary vehicle; Gourgaud calls it "more a composition than a real journal."

## Misattributions (Summary Blacklist)

Do NOT treat as documented without qualification:
- "Not tonight, Josephine" — never said (1904+ music-hall invention).
- "An army marches on its stomach" — Frederick the Great attribution by Carlyle 1858; Napoleon attribution 1862.
- "England is a nation of shopkeepers" — Smith 1776 / Barère 1794; no Napoleon attestation.
- "Every soldier carries a marshal's baton in his knapsack" — composite; best attested in Louis XVIII 1819.
- "Religion is excellent stuff for keeping common people quiet" — phrasing unsourced; the attitude is documented, the sentence is not.
- "Never interrupt your enemy when he is making a mistake" — modernized paraphrase of a longer 1836 English rendering.
- "Ne te lave pas, j'accours" — apocryphal, also attached to Henri IV and Baudelaire.
