# Modern Critical Scholarship on Napoleon (post-1950)

Scope: Anglophone and French historiography after David Chandler's 1966 *Campaigns*, with emphasis on the biographical renaissance since the opening of the *Correspondance générale* (published by the Fondation Napoléon, 2004–2018). Organized by debate rather than by author so that rival readings appear in dialogue. "Evidentiary" claims summarize what a given scholar asserts; "[modern-scholarly-inference]" tags mark interpretive positions that the historiography itself contests.

---

## 1. Great Man vs. Structure

The deepest post-1950 fault line. One camp reads Napoleon as a coherent willed project; the other reads the Consulate-Empire as a system running on inherited Revolutionary machinery plus luck.

- **Andrew Roberts, *Napoleon: A Life* (2014)** — broadly sympathetic, mining the 33,000 letters of the new *Correspondance générale*. Core argument: Napoleon as Enlightenment consolidator who finished what 1789 started — meritocracy, legal uniformity, religious settlement, centralized administration — and whose wars were largely reactive to coalitions financed by Britain. Won the 2015 Los Angeles Times Book Prize for biography [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon:_A_Life]. Criticisms: the *Telegraph* noted "regular lapses into hero-worship"; reviewers flagged the thin treatment of Haiti and the 1802 slavery reinstatement [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon:_A_Life].
- **Adam Zamoyski, *Napoleon: The Man Behind the Myth* (2018)** — deflationary counterweight. Presents Napoleon as "a rather ordinary" man whose career was "a triumph of luck and accident as much as the invincible genius of the legend" [web: https://www.adamzamoyski.com/books/napoleon]. Argues his rise required the specific circumstances of 1793–99 and his success rested on the quality he demanded of his generals: luck.
- **Paul Schroeder, *The Transformation of European Politics, 1763–1848* (1994)** — structuralist indictment. Napoleon is a system-destroyer whose hegemonic overreach forced Europe to invent a new, consensus-based order at Vienna. Durable peace came not from balance of power but from the abandonment of the 18th-century balance-of-power logic Napoleon had pushed to its destructive limit [web: https://global.oup.com/academic/product/the-transformation-of-european-politics-1763-1848-9780198221197]. [modern-scholarly-inference]: Napoleon's real historical effect was negative — he exhausted a system rather than building one.
- **Isser Woloch, *Napoleon and His Collaborators* (2001)** — deconstructs "great man alone." Cambacérès, Boulay de Meurthe, Berlier, Thibaudeau, Regnaud, and Carnot supplied the legal craftsmanship and the "veneer of legality"; they imagined themselves a "final, residual barrier to unchecked tyranny" while in fact enabling the dictatorship [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/magazine/publications/napoleon-and-his-collaborators-the-making-of-a-dictatorship/].

The *Correspondance générale* (Fondation Napoléon) has paradoxically fed both sides — Roberts uses it to show Napoleon's extraordinary range; Woloch and Dwyer use it to show how much Napoleon depended on operators around him.

## 2. Enlightenment Heir vs. Its Betrayer

- **Steven Englund, *Napoleon: A Political Life* (2004)** — argues Napoleon "may ultimately be seen as a liberal" because he sought through law and institutions to elude "profound political conflict." The Republic/Empire question is his central lens: the Empire was a political form, not an ideological rupture. Won the AHA's J. Russell Major Prize [web: https://www.napoleon-series.org/reviews/biographies/c_englund.html].
- **Michael Broers, *Napoleon* trilogy (2014, 2018, 2022)** — *Soldier of Destiny*, *The Spirit of the Age*, *The Decline and Fall of an Empire*. Napoleon as European state-builder whose real achievement was "the creation of the modern state through the application of rational and impersonal Enlightenment principles to law, administration, and the military" [web: https://openlettersreview.com/posts/napoleon-the-spirit-of-the-age-1805-1810-by-michael-broers]. Broers deliberately departs from Franco-centric frames and attends to annexed and satellite territories.
- **David Bell, *The First Total War* (2007)** — Enlightenment-betrayal reading. The philosophes' dream that war could be rationalized and contained perversely produced "total war": conscription, mass mobilization, ideological demonization of the enemy. Napoleon is the apotheosis, not the reversal, of this trajectory — the militarization of civil society that Bell dates from 1799 [web: https://www.h-net.org/reviews/showrev.php?id=13615]. [modern-scholarly-inference]: Napoleonic warfare was the Enlightenment's shadow, not its violation.

The Englund/Broers vs. Bell division is not purely factual — they agree on most events — but about what to name the pattern. Englund emphasizes the Civil Code, Concordat, Conseil d'État; Bell emphasizes the Grande Armée, the *levée en masse* legacy, and the symbiosis of state and army.

## 3. Consolidator of Revolution vs. Reactionary

- **Jean Tulard, *Napoléon, ou le mythe du sauveur* (Fayard, 1977; multiple revised editions)** — the foundational French reading. Tulard places Napoleon in a specifically French political pattern: a bourgeoisie facing internal and external threats that "repeatedly invents saviours" — Napoleon opens the path that leads through Cavaignac, Louis-Napoléon, Thiers, Pétain, and de Gaulle [web: https://www.persee.fr/doc/bec_0373-6237_1978_num_136_1_450125_t1_0185_0000_2]. On this reading Napoleon consolidates the social gains of 1789 (abolition of feudal privilege, civil equality, property redistributions) while closing off its political radicalism.
- **Thierry Lentz, *Nouvelle histoire du Premier Empire* (Fayard, 2002–2010, 4 vols incl. *Les Cent-Jours*)** — as director of the Fondation Napoléon, Lentz provides the current French synthesis, drawing heavily on the *Correspondance générale* (whose edition he oversaw). He largely defends the consolidator reading while stress-testing it against the collapse of 1810–1814 [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thierry_Lentz].
- **Philip Dwyer, three-volume biography — *Napoleon: The Path to Power* (2007), *Citizen Emperor* (2013), *Napoleon: Passion, Death and Resurrection* (2018)** — much more skeptical. Dwyer's Napoleon is "ruthless in his ambition for power, concealing his defeats, exaggerating his victories, never hesitating to blame others for his own failings" [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/magazine/publications/citizen-emperor-napoleon-in-power-1799-1815/]. Strongest on self-fashioning and propaganda — a public-perception apparatus Dwyer says earlier biographies skated past. The legend was manufactured, beginning with the Italian campaign bulletins and continuing through *Le Mémorial de Sainte-Hélène*.
- **Annie Jourdan, *Napoléon: Héros, Imperator, Mécène* (1998)** — companion French scholarship on image-construction through art, iconography, and patronage. Convergent with Dwyer on the manufacturing thesis.

## 4. Civilizing Administrator vs. Racist Imperialist

The most politically charged front since the 2021 bicentennial.

- **Broers** and (with caveats) **Roberts** foreground the administrative revolution — prefects, the Conseil d'État, the Civil Code, codified commercial law, *lycées*, the *cadastre*, the Banque de France.
- **Laurent Dubois, *Avengers of the New World* (2004)** and **Marlene Daut** (*Tropics of Haiti*, 2015; *Awakening the Ashes*, 2023) establish the Haitian Revolution as foundational to modern democracy and human rights, and treat Napoleon's 1802 Leclerc expedition and the reinstatement of slavery as central, not peripheral, to his regime [web: https://lareviewofbooks.org/article/haiti-reimagined-on-marlene-l-dauts-awakening-the-ashes-and-jake-johnstons-aid-state/].
- **The 1802 reinstatement** — by the law of 20 May 1802 Bonaparte restored slavery in French colonies where it had survived, and reauthorized the slave trade; France thereby became the only state in history to re-establish chattel slavery after abolishing it [web: https://www.nofi.media/en/2025/05/law-of-may-20-1802-when-bonaparte/95194]. Historiographical split: **Yves Benot (*La démence coloniale sous Napoléon*, 1992)** reads it as deliberate and ideological; 21st-century revisionists read it as hesitation and opportunistic calculation driven by planter lobbying, Josephine's Creole milieu, and geopolitical gambles on Louisiana [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinstatement_of_slavery_by_Napoleon_Bonaparte]. Daut stresses the racial-ideological content of Napoleon's stated justifications.

[modern-scholarly-inference] The "civilizing administrator" and "racist imperialist" readings can be stated as both true: the same regime that codified civil equality in metropolitan France reimposed chattel slavery on Black populations in the Caribbean. The historiographical question is whether to treat this as hypocrisy, contradiction, or structural feature of Napoleonic liberalism.

## 5. Gender and the Code Civil

- **Suzanne Desan, *The Family on Trial in Revolutionary France* (2004)** — the Revolution (1792 divorce law, partible inheritance, relaxed paternal authority) redefined the family as a cornerstone of republican citizenship and gave women unprecedented legal agency.
- **Jennifer Heuer, *The Family and the Nation* (2005)** — traces how citizenship was reorganized around family and gender from 1789 to 1830.
- **The 1804 Code Civil** reversed much of this. It reinstated patriarchal authority (*puissance maritale*), restricted divorce, eliminated women's property independence within marriage, and re-subordinated children to paternal authority. [modern-scholarly-inference]: the Code's elegance as a legal monument has historically obscured that it was, on gender, a reactionary document measured against 1792–99.

## 6. War Causation

- **Charles Esdaile, *The Wars of Napoleon* (1995) and *Napoleon's Wars: An International History, 1803–1815* (2007)** — revisionist. The wars did not stem from an ideological crusade against France but from "Napoleon's own aggression, egomania, and lust for power," with fame as chief motive [web: https://muse.jhu.edu/article/262511/summary]. Esdaile is not purely personalistic: he assigns weight to structural factors (decline of Sweden, Poland, Ottomans; colonial and commercial conflict; other rulers' calculated risks) but reverses the older "forced into war by the coalitions" narrative.
- **Roberts** and **Lentz** push back, emphasizing British subsidies, Alexander I's duplicity, and the genuine existential threat posed by coalition warfare to any post-Revolutionary French regime.

## 7. Military-Operational Assessment

- **David Chandler, *The Campaigns of Napoleon* (1966)** — still the English-language standard. Codified the analytical vocabulary: *strategy of the central position* (interior lines against a divided enemy); *strategy of envelopment / la manoeuvre sur les derrières* (the cavalry screen plus *bataillon carré* to turn the enemy's rear and cut communications); economy of force to concentrate superior combat power at the *point of decision* [web: https://napolun.com/mirror/napoleonistyka.atspace.com/Napoleon_tactics.htm]. Elements of success: speed, communication, deception, envelopment, concentrated force, combined arms.
- **Peak and diminishing returns.** Post-1950 consensus identifies Ulm-Austerlitz (1805) and Jena-Auerstädt (1806) as operational peak. After 1806 the pattern shifts: Eylau (Feb 1807) is a bloody draw Napoleon called a "victory"; Friedland (1807) restored decisive victory but at mounting cost; Aspern-Essling (1809) was his first clear field defeat; Wagram (1809) a costly win against a now-adapting Austrian army; Borodino (1812) a pyrrhic victory that failed to destroy Kutuzov's army — Napoleon notably refused to commit the Guard. [modern-scholarly-inference]: enemies learned the system. The Austrian 1809 reforms (Archduke Charles) and Prussian reforms (Scharnhorst, Gneisenau) imitated the corps system; Napoleon's comparative advantage eroded as his opponents adopted his own doctrine.
- **Alan Forrest, *Napoleon's Men* (2002)** and *Napoleon: Life, Legacy and Image* (2011) — shifts attention from Napoleon to the soldiers, using the unusually rich personal correspondence of the Grande Armée. Finds that conscripts' minds dwelt on family, food, and home far more than on glory or the Emperor [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/magazine/publications/napoleons-men-the-soldiers-of-the-revolution-and-empire/]. Corrective to hagiographic "soldiers who loved him" tropes.
- **Rafe Blaufarb** (*The French Army 1750–1820*, 2002) on officer-corps social composition; **Michael Rowe** (*From Reich to State*, 2003) on the Rhineland; **Katherine Aaslestad** on the Hanseatic cities — together establish the "Napoleonic Europe" subfield treating occupied and annexed zones as historical subjects, not passive recipients.

## 8. Myth-Debunking Register

- **Height.** Napoleon measured 5'2" in *pouces* (French inches of 2.71 cm), which converts to ~5'6"–5'7" modern inches (~1.68–1.70 m) — average or slightly above average for French males of the era. The "short" myth is largely James Gillray's 1803 "Little Boney" caricature propagated by British propaganda [web: https://www.britannica.com/story/was-napoleon-short; https://www.nationalgeographic.com/history/article/napoleon-complex-height-discrimination-men-history].
- **Arsenic poisoning.** Ruled out. Hair analyses (University of Pavia and others) show elevated arsenic *throughout his life*, not concentrated in the St Helena period. The 2021 bicentenary international study confirmed the autopsy-era diagnosis: advanced gastric carcinoma with covered perforated ulcer and upper GI bleeding [web: https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8572813/].
- **"Not tonight, Josephine."** Fabrication — no contemporary source; a later music-hall and cinematic invention.
- **"He planned a Jewish state."** Overstated. The 1799 Acre proclamation addressed to Asian and African Jews is contested in authorship and authenticity; the 1806–1808 Grand Sanhedrin and 1808 *décret infâme* restricted Jewish economic activity in eastern France. Scholarly consensus: Napoleonic Jewish policy was emancipatory in civic form but disciplinary in practice, not Zionist in intent.
- **"Napoleon complex."** A 20th-century Adlerian-era coinage; no contemporary evidence of the syndrome it names.

## Cross-cutting Synthesis

[modern-scholarly-inference] The post-1950 historiography has not produced a consensus Napoleon; it has produced a sharper map of the choices historians must make. Whether Napoleon is consolidator or destroyer, heir or betrayer of the Enlightenment, liberal or tyrant, depends heavily on (a) which archive you privilege — the *Correspondance générale* and legislative record, or the Haitian/colonial record, or the memoir and propaganda apparatus; (b) whether you weight metropolitan France or the Empire's periphery; and (c) whether you read him with or against the Vienna settlement that followed. The most durable recent move — shared by Dwyer, Broers, Forrest, Zamoyski, Daut, Dubois — is the methodological decentering of Napoleon himself: the regime as a collaborative, propagandistic, and imperially-dispersed phenomenon rather than the unfolding of one will.
