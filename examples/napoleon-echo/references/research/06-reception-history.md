# Reception History: The Many Napoleons After Napoleon

No other modern figure has been reshaped more often, or more deliberately, than Napoleon. He began the work himself at St. Helena, and two centuries of successor regimes, rival ideologies, novelists, cartoonists, filmmakers, and management gurus have kept rebuilding him. Any echo that claims to reach "the historical Napoleon" has to separate him from the layers of posthumous plaster — and name who did the plastering.

## 1. St. Helena and the Legend-Forge (1815–1823)

The most important single source for 19th-century Napoleon is *Le Mémorial de Sainte-Hélène*, compiled by the Comte de Las Cases from conversations during exile and published in 1823. It became one of the century's biggest best-sellers and was described by Jean Tulard as "the breviary of Bonapartism" — the text that prevented Bonapartism from dissolving into generic liberalism [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Memorial_of_Saint_Helena].

**What it added:** Napoleon the liberator of peoples; Napoleon the Enlightenment-constitutionalist betrayed by feudal Europe; Napoleon the prophet of European unity; Napoleon the martyr of a British-led reaction. Much of what "everyone knows" Napoleon believed comes from this book, not from his actions 1799–1814.

Thierry Lentz, Peter Hicks, François Houdecek and Chantal Prévot's 2017 rediscovery of Las Cases's original manuscript at the British Library lets scholars see exactly what Las Cases embellished. Many of the "Napoleonic maxims" now canonical are absent from the original manuscript — Las Cases put them in Napoleon's mouth after the fact [web: https://fondationnapoleon.org/en/publication/thierry-lentz-peter-hicks-francois-houdecek-chantal-prevot-le-memorial-de-sainte-helene-le-manuscrit-original-retrouve-memorial-st-helena-original-manuscript-rediscovered/] [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/trials-tribulations-letter-napoleons-farewell-comte-de-las-cases-st-helena-11-december-1816/].

**Misappropriation flag:** Quoting the *Mémorial* as Napoleon's own voice is almost always wrong. Treat it as Las Cases's political theology written with Napoleon's cooperation.

## 2. Restoration Hostility and Underground Cult (1815–1830)

The Bourbons banned Napoleonic symbols, but the legend grew underground through Béranger's enormously popular chansons, veterans' memoirs, and pedlar prints. Béranger became "the most famous celebrant of the cult of Napoleon in song" — his Napoleon is a republican-flavoured son of the Revolution, avenging the Restoration's clerical reaction [web: https://academic.oup.com/fh/article/36/1/100/6280362].

Meanwhile in Britain, James Gillray's "Little Boney" caricatures shaped the enduring English-speaking image of Napoleon as a tantrum-throwing dwarf — Napoleon reportedly said Gillray "did more than all the armies of Europe to bring me down" [web: https://publicdomainreview.org/essay/little-boney/]. This visual lineage feeds directly into the modern Napoleon-complex stereotype (see §15).

**What it added:** the underdog/martyr Napoleon (France) and the cartoonish tyrant-dwarf Napoleon (Britain) — two images formed in opposition to each other.

## 3. July Monarchy and the Return of the Ashes (1840)

Thiers and Louis-Philippe staged the *Retour des cendres* in December 1840 as political theatre: a gesture to absorb Bonapartist sentiment into the Orléanist regime [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retour_des_cendres] [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/close-up/close-up-on-the-retour-des-cendres-napoleons-body-is-returned-to-paris/]. Thiers's own *Histoire du Consulat et de l'Empire* (1845–62) canonised "Napoleon the genius" in twenty volumes of broadly hagiographic narrative.

**What it added:** Napoleon as sacred founder of modern France — a ceremonial Napoleon whose tomb at Les Invalides becomes a national shrine.

## 4. Second Empire and the Crisis of Bonapartism (1852–1875)

Louis-Napoléon's coup made Napoleon III the political heir; his regime built an explicit cult. Victor Hugo, in exile, answered with *Napoléon le Petit* (1852) and *Les Châtiments* — destroying the nephew while (characteristically) still exalting the uncle [web: https://profadamroberts.substack.com/p/napoleon-le-grand-napoleon-le-petit]. Pierre Lanfrey's *Histoire de Napoléon Ier* (1867–75) was the republican counter-history: a forensic demolition that first treated Napoleon as a destructive tyrant on empirical grounds rather than republican principle alone.

Marx's *Eighteenth Brumaire of Louis Bonaparte* (1852) delivered the most lasting theoretical response: "first as tragedy, then as farce," and the concept of "Bonapartism" as a semi-autonomous state representing no single class, rising when class struggle reaches deadlock [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Eighteenth_Brumaire_of_Louis_Bonaparte]. Trotsky later generalised "Bonapartism" as a recurring category — the man on horseback resolving crises of bourgeois rule.

**What it added:** Napoleon as template for the modern plebiscitary strongman; Napoleon as farce-original whose imitators reveal him. Also: the republican-democratic Napoleon (Second-Empire PR) and the structural-political Napoleon (Marxist analysis).

## 5. German Reception: Hegel, Clausewitz, Heine, Nietzsche

Hegel, finishing the *Phenomenology* in occupied Jena, wrote to Niethammer on 13 October 1806 that he had seen "the Emperor — this world-soul — riding out of the city on reconnaissance… a wonderful sensation to see such an individual, who, concentrated here at a single point, astride a horse, reaches out over the world and masters it" [web: https://www.marxists.org/reference/archive/hegel/works/letters/1806-10-13.htm]. This single line installed Napoleon permanently as the paradigm world-historical individual.

Clausewitz studied Napoleon as *the* modern practitioner; *Vom Kriege* is in large part a theoretical reconstruction of Napoleonic warfare — mass mobilisation, decisive battle, the primacy of political will [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_von_Clausewitz].

Heine's ambivalence, Goethe's admiration, then Nietzsche: in *On the Genealogy of Morals* (II, §24) Napoleon appears as "the most isolated and late-born man there has ever been… this synthesis of the inhuman and superhuman" — the noble ideal made flesh, a signpost back to an aristocratic future [web: http://nietzsche.holtof.com/Nietzsche_on_the_genealogy_of_morals/on_the_genealogy_of_morals.htm]. Nietzsche's Napoleon is not the liberator but the great *exception*; the misappropriation chain Nietzsche → Napoleon → 20th-century authoritarianism runs partly through this reading.

**Misappropriation flag:** Wilhelmine and then Nazi Germany selectively claimed Napoleon as a Great Man while denouncing French imperialism — the contradiction was never resolved, only exploited.

## 6. Tolstoy and the Russian Refusal

*War and Peace* (1869) is the most sustained literary assault on the whole "great man" reading. Tolstoy's Napoleon is petty, overconfident, self-theatrical — a label stuck onto events by historians who need a subject for their verbs. "Great men are merely labels that give a name to an event" [web: https://www.litcharts.com/lit/war-and-peace/themes/theory-of-history] [web: https://erraticus.co/2021/08/12/tolstoy-war-and-peace-history-great-men-napoleon/]. This reading deeply shapes Russian and later left-wing scepticism toward the Napoleonic cult.

## 7. British Reception: From Gillray to Liddell Hart

After Gillray's wartime caricature, Victorian ambivalence: Thomas Carlyle admires him as hero; Macaulay condemns him as despot. In the 20th century, J.F.C. Fuller and B.H. Liddell Hart drew extensively on Napoleonic campaigns; Liddell Hart's "indirect approach" thesis used Napoleon's early Italian campaigns as exemplars while arguing his later battles degenerated into brute mass [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B._H._Liddell_Hart].

## 8. Haitian and Caribbean Reception

The most damning counter-tradition. C.L.R. James's *The Black Jacobins* (1938) re-centred Napoleon as the man who tried to restore slavery in Saint-Domingue and had Toussaint Louverture die in a French fortress prison [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Black_Jacobins]. Marlene Daut, in the 2021 bicentenary debates, called Napoleon "an icon of white supremacy" [web: https://theconversation.com/napoleons-bicentenary-why-celebrating-the-french-emperor-has-become-so-controversial-160313]. Any honest echo must hold the slavery restoration (20 May 1802) at the centre, not the margin.

## 9. American Reception

Early republic: Federalist fear of the military dictator vs. Jeffersonian admiration for the republican general. Emerson's "Napoleon; or, the Man of the World" in *Representative Men* (1850) cast him as "the agent or attorney of the middle class of modern society" — a bourgeois, not aristocratic, hero [web: https://www.napoleon.org/en/history-of-the-two-empires/articles/napoleon-or-the-man-of-the-world-from-representative-men-1850/]. This is recognisably the ancestor of the 21st-century American "leadership-lessons-from-Napoleon" pop genre, which is historically thin.

## 10. 20th-Century Misappropriations: Fascism, Nazism, Totalitarianism

Mussolini's primary models were Caesar and Augustus, not Napoleon, though the plebiscitary-dictator template is recognisably Bonapartist [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_fascism]. Hitler visited Napoleon's tomb on 28 June 1940 and had the remains of the Aiglon moved to Paris — a deliberate dynastic gesture — then invaded the USSR the next year, ignoring the lesson the visit was supposed to teach [web: https://www.history.com/this-day-in-history/june-28/hitler-takes-a-tour-of-paris].

Paul Johnson's *Napoleon: A Life* (2002) reads him as "the prototype of the 20th-century totalitarian dictator" — secret police, propaganda, plebiscites, perpetual war — while conceding he lacked the ideological blood-lust of Hitler or Stalin [web: https://claremontreviewofbooks.com/the-little-tyrant]. Owen Connelly's *Blundering to Glory* develops a related demystification of Napoleonic generalship.

## 11. French 20th–21st-Century Historiography

Abel Gance's *Napoléon* (1927) — technically revolutionary, politically read by Léon Moussinac as "a Bonaparte for nascent fascists" [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napol%C3%A9on_(1927_film)]. Georges Lefebvre's *Napoléon* (1936) gave the canonical Annales-Marxist synthesis. Jean Tulard's *Napoléon, ou le mythe du sauveur* (1977) is the decisive modern French study: Napoleon as product of a demand for a saviour, the myth structurally older than the man [web: https://www.napoleon.org/magazine/livres/napoleon-ou-le-mythe-du-sauveur/]. The 2021 bicentenary, with Macron's "enlightened commemoration," detonated over the slavery restoration [web: https://www.aljazeera.com/news/2021/5/5/frances-macron-marks-bicentenary-of-napoleons-death].

## 12. Film and Pop-Culture Napoleon

Gance 1927, Sacha Guitry 1955, the unmade Kubrick project, Ridley Scott's *Napoleon* (2023). Scott's film drew blistering criticism from French historians (Patrice Gueniffey on Austerlitz; Dan Snow on the pyramids); Scott's response: "Get a life" [web: https://variety.com/2023/film/news/napoleon-inaccuracies-french-historians-pyramids-1235823975/]. The cultural Napoleon is now a franchise more than a person.

## 13. The "Napoleon Complex"

Pure folklore. Napoleon was about 1.67 m (roughly 5'6" imperial) — average for his era. The myth comes from confusion between the French *pouce* (2.7 cm) and the English inch (2.54 cm) plus Gillray's caricatures [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Napoleon_complex]. Any echo that leans on short-man-syndrome psychology is importing 19th-century British propaganda as biography.

## Misappropriation Flags (Summary)

- **Napoleon the liberator of peoples** — St. Helena / Bonapartist projection; contradicted by slavery restoration, occupation regimes.
- **Napoleon the democrat** — Second-Empire rhetoric; his actual constitutions were plebiscitary autocracies.
- **Napoleon the European unifier** — post-WWII EU-era projection; he unified Europe by conquest, not consent.
- **Napoleon the pure tyrant** — hostile Bourbon/British/Hugolian projection; flattens the Civil Code, meritocracy, administrative reforms.
- **Napoleon the management genius** — late-20th-century American business-culture overlay; historically thin.
- **Napoleon the proto-Hitler** — Paul-Johnson framing; useful as warning, anachronistic as equivalence.
- **Napoleon as short-man-syndrome case** — folk psychology based on mistranslated measurement.

An honest Napoleon-echo should speak primarily from 1799–1815 documents, his own Code and correspondence, and critical scholarship (Lentz, Tulard, Lefebvre, Englund, Roberts, Dwyer), and should name the *Mémorial*, the Bonapartist tradition, and the hostile-British tradition as filters rather than windows.
