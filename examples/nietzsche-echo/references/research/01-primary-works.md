# Agent 1 — Primary Works

**Scope**: Nietzsche's authenticated published corpus 1872–1888, plus the late 1888 works edited by Nietzsche himself before the January 1889 Turin collapse. Excludes *The Will to Power* compilation (sister's artifact; Agent 6). Nachlass (KSA notebooks) is referenced sparingly and flagged `[attested]`, not `[documented]`.

**Summary (3 sentences)**: Nietzsche's published corpus is a non-systematic, evolving set of positions unified by a diagnostic stance — Europe has inherited a moral-metaphysical tradition (Platonic-Christian) that is now self-refuting, and the task of the free spirit is to make this nihilism explicit, endure it, and revalue. His signature moves are genealogical unmasking (asking *who* benefits from a value, not whether it is true), perspectival pluralism (there are no facts, only interpretations — but interpretations vary in power and honesty), and a life-affirming criterion (does this value increase or diminish the feeling of power, of ascending life?). Across phases he contradicts himself openly — on Wagner, Schopenhauer, science, democracy, even pity — and he knew it: *Beyond Good and Evil* §296 laments that "what we can find words for is something already dead in our hearts," and *Twilight* §26 calls the "will to system" a lack of integrity.

## Concepts (each defined from Nietzsche's own published texts)

### Will to Power (Wille zur Macht)
The drive of all life — not merely self-preservation (he explicitly attacks Spinoza and Darwin on this) but the discharge of strength, the seeking of resistance to overcome. `[documented]` *Beyond Good and Evil* §13: "A living thing seeks above all to *discharge* its strength — life itself is *will to power*; self-preservation is only one of the indirect and most frequent *results*" (Kaufmann trans.). `[documented]` *BGE* §36 offers the conditional hypothesis that *all* efficient force might be will to power. `[documented]` *Genealogy* II §12 uses it to reinterpret the history of punishment. `[attested]` The systematic metaphysical reading ("the world is will to power and nothing besides") appears in Nachlass notes compiled by his sister into *The Will to Power* — not endorsed in published form. Recurs ≥3 times: BGE §13, §22, §36, §186, §259; GM II §12, §18, III §7; *Antichrist* §2, §6; *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §14; *Zarathustra* II "On Self-Overcoming."

### Übermensch (overman / overhuman)
Not a biological superman and not (in published text) a racial type — the *overcoming* of the human as currently constituted; the one who gives himself his own values, affirms eternal recurrence, and represents the meaning of the earth. `[documented]` *Zarathustra* Prologue §3 (Del Caro trans.): "I teach you the overman. Human being is something that must be overcome." `[documented]` *Zarathustra* Prologue §4: "The overman shall be the meaning of the earth!" `[documented]` *Ecce Homo* "Why I Write Such Good Books" §1: Nietzsche complains the term has been misread as a Darwinian or "heroic" type. Published references are almost entirely confined to *Zarathustra* and *Ecce Homo*; the concept is conspicuously *absent* from BGE and GM — a major interpretive puzzle [modern-scholarly-inference]: Kaufmann and Clark read this as evidence it was a rhetorical-pedagogical figure for a specific book, not a central doctrine.

### Eternal Recurrence (die ewige Wiederkunft / Wiederkehr des Gleichen)
The thought-experiment: would you affirm your life if every moment, joy and agony alike, returned eternally, identically, infinite times? `[documented]` *Gay Science* §341 "The greatest weight" (Kaufmann): "This life as you now live it and have lived it, you will have to live once more and innumerable times more." `[documented]` *Zarathustra* III "On the Vision and the Riddle" and III "The Convalescent" stage it as Zarathustra's abyss-thought. `[documented]` *Ecce Homo* "Birth of Tragedy" §3 calls it "the fundamental conception" of *Zarathustra*. `[attested]` Cosmological-proof versions exist only in Nachlass; published texts present it as an existential test, not a physics. Recurs: GS §285, §341; Z III ×2, IV "The Drunken Song"; BGE §56; EH on Z §1, §6.

### Slave Morality / Master Morality
Two value-creating stances. Master morality: the noble type *actively* posits itself as "good" (= strong, noble, truthful, full) and derivatively names what differs "bad" (= common, cowardly). Slave morality: the powerless *reactively* invert this — the oppressor is "evil," and by contrast the powerless is "good" (= meek, patient, humble). `[documented]` *Beyond Good and Evil* §260 is the locus classicus. `[documented]` *Genealogy* I §10: "The slave revolt in morality begins when ressentiment itself becomes creative and gives birth to values." `[documented]` *Antichrist* §24 reads Jewish priestly morality as the slave revolt's origin, §43 attacks Christianity as its continuation. Not two "races" — Nietzsche says both can coexist in a single soul (*BGE* §260).

### Ressentiment
French term used deliberately (German *Groll* too weak). The creative-reactive emotion of the weak: unable to discharge aggression outward, they internalize it, and invent a morality that condemns their oppressors as metaphysically evil. `[documented]` *Genealogy* I §10 (Kaufmann): "While every noble morality develops from a triumphant affirmation of itself, slave morality from the outset says No to what is 'outside,' what is 'different,' what is 'not itself'; and *this No is its creative deed*." `[documented]` *Genealogy* III §14–§15 diagnoses the priest as the engineer of ressentiment. `[documented]` *Ecce Homo* "Why I Am So Wise" §6 uses the term diagnostically about himself. Recurs: GM I §10, §11, §13, §14, II §11, III §14, §15, §16; *Antichrist* §22, §24; EH "Wise" §6.

### Apollonian / Dionysian
The two art-drives of Greek culture, first elaborated in *Birth of Tragedy*. Apollonian = dream, individuation, form, measure, plastic image, Homer. Dionysian = intoxication, dissolution of the individual, music, ecstatic chorus, Archilochus. Greek tragedy is their fusion. `[documented]` *Birth of Tragedy* §1 (Kaufmann): "Much will have been gained for aesthetics once we have succeeded in apprehending directly — rather than merely ascertaining — that art derives its continuous development from the *duality* of the *Apollonian* and *Dionysian*." `[documented]` Late career: in *Twilight* "What I Owe the Ancients" §5 and *EH* on BT, Nietzsche retains only "the Dionysian" as the affirmative pagan category opposed to the Crucified; Apollo is demoted. This is a real evolution [documented].

### The Last Man (der letzte Mensch)
The contented, unheroic, comfort-seeking, risk-averse modern — the inverse of the Übermensch. "No shepherd, and one herd! Everybody wants the same, everybody is the same." `[documented]` *Zarathustra* Prologue §5 (Kaufmann): "'We have invented happiness,' say the last men, and they blink." The crowd in the marketplace cheers not for the Übermensch but for the last man — "give us this last man!" Recurs almost exclusively in Z Prologue, but the *type* is re-described throughout: BGE §203 (the "diminution of man"), GM I §11, *Antichrist* §43.

### Genealogical Method
A historical-diagnostic method that asks not "is X true?" or "is X good?" but "under what conditions, for whose benefit, from what affective source, did *the value of X* come to be posited?" `[documented]` *Genealogy* Preface §6 (Clark/Swensen): "We need a *critique of moral values, the value of these values themselves must first be called into question* — and for this there is needed a knowledge of the conditions and circumstances out of which they grew, under which they evolved and changed." `[documented]` Already operative in *Human, All Too Human* §1–§2 (the "historical philosophizing" attack on miraculous origins), *Daybreak* §44, GS §345. The method is older than the 1887 book that named it.

### Perspectivism
There is no view from nowhere; every knowing is an interpretation from some affect-laden standpoint; objectivity is not the absence of perspective but the disciplined coordination of *more* perspectives. `[documented]` *Genealogy* III §12 (Kaufmann): "There is *only* a perspective seeing, *only* a perspective 'knowing'; and *the more* affects we allow to speak about one thing, the more eyes, different eyes, we can use to observe one thing, the more complete will our 'concept' of this thing, our 'objectivity,' be." `[documented]` *Beyond Good and Evil* §34; *Gay Science* §374 (the "new infinite"). Critical nuance: perspectivism is not relativism — Nietzsche ranks perspectives by honesty, strength, comprehensiveness.

### Amor Fati (love of fate)
Not resignation — active affirmation. To want nothing to be other than it is, backward and forward. The criterion of the well-turned-out human. `[documented]` *Gay Science* §276 (Kaufmann, opens Book IV): "I want to learn more and more to see as beautiful what is necessary in things; then I shall be one of those who make things beautiful. *Amor fati*: let that be my love henceforth!" `[documented]` *Ecce Homo* "Why I Am So Clever" §10: "My formula for greatness in a human being is *amor fati*: that one wants nothing to be different, not forward, not backward, not in all eternity. Not merely bear what is necessary, still less conceal it — all idealism is mendaciousness in the face of what is necessary — but *love* it." `[documented]` *Nietzsche contra Wagner* "Epilogue" §1.

### "God is dead" (Gott ist tot)
Not an atheist's triumph but a cultural-diagnostic statement: the highest Christian-Platonic values have undermined their own plausibility conditions; Europeans have killed their God through their own intellectual honesty (the Christian *will to truth* turning on Christianity itself), but have not yet felt the consequences. `[documented]` *Gay Science* §125 "The Madman" (Kaufmann): "Whither is God? I shall tell you. *We have killed him* — you and I. All of us are his murderers... Is not the greatness of this deed too great for us? Must we not ourselves become gods simply to seem worthy of it?" `[documented]` Also *GS* §108, §343; Z Prologue §2 ("this old saint in the forest has not yet heard that *God is dead*"); *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §5.

### Revaluation of All Values (Umwertung aller Werte)
The task: to invert the Platonic-Christian table of values not by moralistic reversal but by subjecting every received value to the genealogical and life-affirming test. `[documented]` *Antichrist* announces itself on its title page as the first book of *Umwertung aller Werte* (originally conceived as a four-book project — abandoned). `[documented]` *Ecce Homo* "Why I Am a Destiny" §1: "I am by far the most terrible human being who has existed so far; this does not mean I shall not be the most beneficial. I know the delight in *destroying* to a degree that accords with my strength to destroy." `[documented]` *Twilight* Foreword; GM III §27.

## Core Arguments that Recur ≥3 Times

1. **Christianity is Platonism for the people / metaphysics is a symptom**. BGE Preface; *Twilight* "'Reason' in Philosophy" §§1–6 and "How the 'True World' Finally Became a Fable"; *Antichrist* §§10–18, §43; GS §344.

2. **The ascetic ideal has, until now, been the only meaning humans could give to suffering — and this is why nihilism is terrifying but also an opportunity**. *Genealogy* III §28 (the closing line: "man would rather will *nothingness* than *not* will"); *Antichrist* §7; GM Preface §5; *Gay Science* §346.

3. **Morality (as Europe has understood it) is a *symptom* — one must ask what kind of life it expresses, not whether it is obeyed**. *Daybreak* Preface §3–§4; BGE §187, §202; GM Preface §3; *Twilight* "Morality as Anti-Nature."

4. **Consciousness / the "I" is a surface phenomenon, a late biological invention, and a grammatical illusion — we are not unified subjects but a plurality of drives**. *Gay Science* §354 (consciousness evolved for communication, hence is shallow); BGE §17 ("a thought comes when *it* wishes, not when *I* wish"), §19 (the will is not one thing); *Twilight* "'Reason' in Philosophy" §5 (grammar's God).

5. **Suffering is not an objection to life; the demand for its abolition is**. *Gay Science* §338; BGE §225; GM II §7; *EH* "Why I Am So Wise" §2.

6. **Free will and causa sui are philosophical fictions produced by the need to blame and to credit**. *Twilight* "The Four Great Errors"; BGE §21; *Human, All Too Human* §39; *Daybreak* §148.

7. **"Truth" is a moral prejudice — the *will to truth* must itself be subjected to critique**. GS §344; BGE §1; GM III §24; *Twilight* "How the 'True World' Finally Became a Fable."

8. **Equality (democratic, socialist, Christian) is an expression of ressentiment and a leveling force hostile to ascending life**. *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §37–§48; BGE §202, §203, §242, §257–§260; GM I §§5–11; *Antichrist* §43, §57.

9. **Great individuals (his "higher types") are the justification of an age, not its average**. BGE §126, §212, §257; *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §44; *Schopenhauer as Educator* (UM III) §6; *Antichrist* §3–§4.

10. **Philosophers have been unconscious memoirists: every great philosophy is an "involuntary and unconscious memoir"**. BGE §6 (Kaufmann): "It has gradually become clear to me what every great philosophy so far has been: namely, the personal confession of its author and a kind of involuntary and unconscious memoir." Echoes: *Daybreak* Preface §4; *EH* Preface §3; GS Preface §2.

## Vocabulary Signature

**Words he returns to obsessively**: *Macht* (power), *Leben* (life), *Geist* (spirit), *Instinkt* (instinct), *vornehm* (noble / distinguished — a key positive term), *schlecht* vs *böse* (bad vs evil — the GM I §§4–5 distinction), *Wille* (will), *Kraft* (force), *Typus* (type), *décadence* (French, used as German loanword, especially 1888), *Rang* / *Rangordnung* (rank, order of rank), *Pathos der Distanz* ("pathos of distance," BGE §257, GM I §2), *gefährlich* (dangerous), *Jasager* / *Neinsager* (yes-sayer / no-sayer), *Fernstenliebe* ("love of the farthest," contra neighbor-love, Z I "On Love of the Neighbor"), *Selbstüberwindung* (self-overcoming), *Redlichkeit* (intellectual honesty — a virtue he accepts), *Umwertung* (revaluation), *Verneinung* (negation).

**Conspicuous absences / terms he avoids or ridicules**: he almost never uses *Pflicht* (duty) approvingly — it is the Kantian enemy; *das Gute an sich* (the Good-in-itself) is a target; *Menschheit* (humanity-as-such, as moral totality) he mocks; "altruism" and "egoism" he treats as false alternatives (*Daybreak* §131–§148).

**Rhetorical devices**:
- **Aphorism**: dominant in *Human, All Too Human* through *Gay Science*, and returns in BGE. He explicitly values the form: *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §51: "It is my ambition to say in ten sentences what everyone else says in a book — what everyone else does *not* say in a book." `[documented]`
- **Genealogy / fictive history**: GM's three essays; parts of *Antichrist*.
- **Dithyramb / prose poem**: *Zarathustra*; *Dionysus Dithyrambs* (1888).
- **Parody of scripture**: *Zarathustra* parodies Luther's Bible cadences continuously.
- **Polemic / invective**: *Case of Wagner*, *Antichrist*, *Ecce Homo*.
- **Hyperbole and self-mythologizing**: EH chapter titles ("Why I Am So Wise," "Why I Write Such Good Books," "Why I Am a Destiny"). Read against EH §2 of the foreword where he cautions the reader not to mistake him for someone else — the hyperbole is partly strategic masking.
- **Mask / persona**: BGE §40 "Every profound spirit needs a mask"; BGE §289.

## Predecessors He Himself Cites

### Schopenhauer
Central early influence (*BT*, *Schopenhauer as Educator* = UM III). By BGE Nietzsche treats Schopenhauer as a *problem* — the paradigmatic "pessimist of strength" turned life-denier. `[documented]` *Schopenhauer as Educator* §3 (Hollingdale): "Schopenhauer as educator" — he models the philosopher as exemplar, not system-builder. `[documented]` *BGE* §56 reverses Schopenhauer directly: to have glanced to the depth of the world-pessimism is to find oneself wanting the "*da capo* ... not only to oneself but to the whole play and spectacle" — the proto-eternal-recurrence move. `[documented]` *GM* III §§5–8 diagnoses Schopenhauer's aesthetic disinterest (following Kant) as the ascetic ideal in philosophical disguise. `[documented]` *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §21–§22 attacks Schopenhauer on pity, music, genius. The contradiction is preserved: Nietzsche never disowns the early debt (cf. EH on BT §§1–4).

### Heraclitus (and the pre-Socratics)
His declared pagan kin. `[documented]` *Ecce Homo* "Birth of Tragedy" §3 (Kaufmann): "I have the right to understand myself as the first *tragic philosopher* — that is, the most extreme opposite and antipode of a pessimistic philosopher. Before me this transposition of the Dionysian into a philosophical *pathos* did not exist: *tragic wisdom* was lacking... *The affirmation of passing away and destroying* ... *saying Yes to opposition and war*, becoming — in this I recognized at the deepest level my own previous path... For this reason alone I *must* perhaps do justice to Heraclitus, in whose vicinity I feel altogether warmer and better than anywhere else." `[documented]` *Twilight* "'Reason' in Philosophy" §2: Heraclitus alone among the Greeks rightly grasped becoming over being. `[attested]` The early lecture *Philosophy in the Tragic Age of the Greeks* (1873, unpublished in his lifetime) elaborates this.

### Emerson
Lifelong companion read. `[documented]` *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §13 (Kaufmann): "Emerson — much more enlightened, roving, manifold, refined than Carlyle; above all, happier... one who instinctively nourishes himself only on ambrosia, leaving behind what is indigestible in things." `[attested]` His personal copy of Emerson's *Essays* is heavily annotated (Bauerlein, Stack scholarship); GS §92 and the motto of *Gay Science* 1st ed. are Emersonian [documented]. Kaufmann's commentary traces "self-reliance" → *Selbstüberwindung*.

### La Rochefoucauld (and the French moralistes: Chamfort, Vauvenargues, Pascal as antagonist)
Model for the psychological-aphoristic method of the middle period. `[documented]` *Human, All Too Human* §35–§36 (Hollingdale): Nietzsche explicitly praises "La Rochefoucauld and those other French masters of soul-searching" and complains Germans have no such tradition. `[documented]` *Assorted Opinions and Maxims* (HAH II) §§214, 216 discuss La Rochefoucauld by name. The structural debt: the maxim that unmasks self-flattering motives.

### Stendhal
Admired as psychologist of the "higher man." `[documented]` *Beyond Good and Evil* §39 (Kaufmann): Stendhal "that remarkable anticipatory and forerunning human being who with a Napoleonic *tempo* traversed *his* Europe, several centuries of the European soul, as a surveyor and discoverer of this soul." `[documented]` *BGE* §254 groups Stendhal with the French as Europe's genuine psychologists. `[documented]` *EH* "Why I Am So Clever" §3 names Stendhal alongside Heine as what Germany gave him last. Stendhal's formula "beauty is a promise of happiness" (*la beauté est une promesse de bonheur*) Nietzsche quotes against Kant's disinterested beauty in *GM* III §6.

### Dostoevsky
Late discovery (1887, in French translation). `[documented]` *Twilight* "Skirmishes" §45 (Kaufmann): "Dostoevsky, the only psychologist, incidentally, from whom I had something to learn; he ranks among the most beautiful strokes of fortune in my life." `[documented]` *Antichrist* §31 reads Christ as a Dostoevskian "idiot" — naive, non-resistant. Interpretive caution: Nietzsche's Dostoevsky is *Notes from Underground*, *House of the Dead*, and the shorter fiction; there is no published evidence he read *The Brothers Karamazov* despite frequent modern claims [modern-scholarly-inference].

### Thucydides
His preferred Greek historian, held against Plato. `[documented]` *Twilight* "What I Owe the Ancients" §2 (Kaufmann): "My recreation, my preference, my cure from all Platonism has always been Thucydides... Thucydides, and perhaps Machiavelli's *Principe*, are most closely related to me by the unconditional will not to delude oneself and to see reason in *reality* — not in 'reason,' still less in 'morality.'" `[documented]` *Daybreak* §168 and *BGE* §28 also praise Thucydidean hardness.

### Others frequently named
**Goethe** — the positive German counter-type, affirmed late as the "*European* event" and a "spirit who has become free" (*Twilight* "Skirmishes" §49) `[documented]`. **Napoleon** — the "synthesis of *Unmensch* [monster] and *Übermensch*" (*GM* I §16) `[documented]`. **Voltaire** — HAH I was dedicated to Voltaire on the centenary of his death `[documented]`. **Spinoza** — "a precursor, and what a precursor!" (postcard to Overbeck 30 July 1881 — `[attested]`, correspondence not published work); BGE §13 attacks Spinoza's conatus `[documented]`. **Heine** — the German poet he actually loves (*EH* "Why I Am So Clever" §4).

## Evolution Across Phases

**Phase I — Romantic metaphysics of art (1872–76): *Birth of Tragedy*, *Untimely Meditations*.**
Wagnerian and Schopenhauerian. Art (music, tragedy) redeems existence; the Greeks are the paradigm; modern culture is decadent and must be reborn through Wagner. Vocabulary is German-romantic, metaphysical. `[documented]` *BT* §5: "only as an aesthetic phenomenon is existence and the world *eternally justified*." Later self-criticism is severe: `[documented]` *BT* "Attempt at a Self-Criticism" (1886 preface): "What spoke here was... a 'new soul' — and not a personality... an alien and newly discovered voice, the disciple of a still 'unknown god.'"

**Phase II — Positivist / skeptical middle period (1878–82): *Human, All Too Human* (I, 1878; *Assorted Opinions* 1879; *The Wanderer and His Shadow* 1880), *Daybreak* (1881), *Gay Science* I–IV (1882).**
Break with Wagner and Schopenhauer; embrace of Voltaire, science, the French moralistes. Aphoristic, cool, historical-psychological. God-is-dead announced (GS §125) but without Zarathustra's pathos. "Free spirit" is the hero-figure. Influence of Paul Rée and Lou Salomé. `[documented]` *HAH* I §1: "*Chemistry* of concepts and sensations... How can something originate in its opposite?" *Daybreak* subtitled "Thoughts on the Prejudices of Morality."

**Phase III — Zarathustra and the prophetic voice (1883–85): *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* I–IV.**
Aphorism abandoned for dithyrambic prose-poem; biblical parody; the Übermensch, eternal recurrence, and will to power are staged narratively. The most *constructive* and also the most easily misread of his works. Part IV printed privately in 40 copies — not truly published in his lifetime beyond that (`[documented]` bibliographically; `[modern-scholarly-inference]` interpretively this matters for gauging what he considered finished public doctrine).

**Phase IV — Mature critical-systematic prose (1886–87): *Beyond Good and Evil*, *On the Genealogy of Morals*, new prefaces to earlier books, Book V of *Gay Science* (1887).**
Return to argumentative prose but now with the full conceptual apparatus. *BGE* subtitled "Prelude to a Philosophy of the Future." *GM* is his most sustained argumentative book — three numbered essays, a deliberately un-aphoristic structure he advertises in the preface.

**Phase V — Late polemic / Turin 1888: *Case of Wagner*, *Twilight of the Idols*, *Antichrist*, *Ecce Homo*, *Nietzsche contra Wagner*, *Dionysus Dithyrambs*.**
Accelerated, compressed, polemical, grandiose. Multiple works drafted within months. Tone of *Ecce Homo* and *Antichrist* has been read both as philosophical climax and as early clinical sign [modern-scholarly-inference — Kaufmann defends lucidity, others more cautious]. He corrected proofs of *Twilight* and *Nietzsche contra Wagner* himself; *Antichrist* and *EH* he saw to press-ready state; the *Ecce Homo* manuscript passed through Peter Gast and was not published until 1908, raising textual questions (Montinari restored it in KGW).

## Contradictions Preserved (do not harmonize)

1. **On Wagner**: Early hero (*BT*, UM IV "Richard Wagner in Bayreuth") → break (HAH) → polemical enemy (*Case of Wagner*, *Nietzsche contra Wagner*) → but in *Ecce Homo* "Why I Am So Clever" §6 he says "I still loved Wagner" and names the *Tristan* prelude as irreplaceable. `[documented]`

2. **On Schopenhauer**: Educator (UM III, BT) → paradigmatic life-denier to be overcome (BGE §56, GM III §§5–8) → but the affirmation-in-face-of-pessimism structure is *inherited* from the Schopenhauerian problem. Nietzsche keeps the question while rejecting the answer.

3. **On science**: Positivist middle period celebrates science against metaphysics (HAH, *Daybreak*). Later: science is the *latest form of the ascetic ideal* because it retains the unconditional will to truth (*GM* III §§23–27; *GS* §344). He does not retract the earlier praise; he historicizes it.

4. **On democracy / equality**: Consistently attacked as leveling ressentiment (*BGE*, *Twilight*, *Antichrist*). But in *HAH* I §472 and §463 the middle-period Nietzsche accepts representative institutions instrumentally. The later anti-democratic thunder is not uniform across the corpus.

5. **On pity (Mitleid)**: Attacked as the affect that *multiplies* suffering (*Antichrist* §7) and as Schopenhauer's central error. But *EH* "Why I Am So Wise" §4 admits pity as his own besetting temptation — the vice he must master, not one he lacks.

6. **On the Jews**: Extended praise of Jewish intelligence, endurance, European importance (*BGE* §§250–251, *Daybreak* §205, *GS* §348) coexists with the *Genealogy*'s identification of priestly Judaism as the origin of slave morality (*GM* I §§6–8, *Antichrist* §§24–26). He is ferociously anti-antisemitic in letters (repeatedly breaking with his sister and Bernhard Förster on this — `[attested]` correspondence) while diagnosing what he calls the Jewish-priestly moral revolution as world-historically disastrous. The sister later forged the simpler picture; the published texts do not support it.

7. **On himself as systematic philosopher**: *Twilight* "Maxims" §26 (Kaufmann): "I mistrust all systematizers and avoid them. The will to a system is a lack of integrity." Against this, his Nachlass projects and *Antichrist*'s subtitle (*Revaluation of All Values*: Book One) show an architectural ambition. The published posture is anti-system; the working drafts betray the pull toward one.

## Quote Provenance Notes

- **Default translator**: Kaufmann (*Basic Writings of Nietzsche*, *Portable Nietzsche*, *Genealogy* with Hollingdale) — Kaufmann's post-WWII project de-Nazified Nietzsche and set the English voice.
- **For *Zarathustra***: Kaufmann reads fluently but Del Caro (Cambridge, 2006) restores Luther-Bible register and is now the scholarly default.
- **For *Genealogy***: Clark/Swensen (Hackett, 1998) is the most philologically careful English edition and is the academic citation standard over Kaufmann/Hollingdale.
- **For *Human, All Too Human*, *Daybreak*, *Untimely Meditations***: Hollingdale (Cambridge) is the workhorse.
- **Cautionary notes**:
  - The line "That which does not kill me makes me stronger" is *Twilight* "Maxims" §8 `[documented]` — but almost always quoted shorn of its context (it follows a maxim on the school of war). It is *not* a general self-help principle in Nietzsche.
  - "Become who you are" (*werde, der du bist*) — subtitle of *Ecce Homo* `[documented]`; quoted from Pindar's *Pythian* 2. Nietzsche uses it with full awareness of its Pindaric origin (GS §270, §335).
  - "When you stare into the abyss, the abyss stares back" is *BGE* §146 `[documented]`: "And if you gaze long into an abyss, the abyss will also gaze into you" (Kaufmann). Almost always quoted without the preceding first half: "Whoever fights monsters should see to it that in the process he does not become a monster."
  - "God is dead" — the phrase is pre-Nietzschean (Hegel, Hölderlin, Jean Paul); Nietzsche's distinctive move is not the declaration but the treatment as a cultural-epochal event whose consequences are not yet felt (GS §125, §343).
  - *The Will to Power* as a book — `[attributed]`, not `[documented]` — handled by Agent 6.

## Central vs Transitional Works

- **Most central** (the books he himself foregrounds in *Ecce Homo*): *Zarathustra* (self-described as his gift to humanity), *Beyond Good and Evil*, *Genealogy*, *Twilight of the Idols*. *Gay Science* (especially Book V) joins this tier.
- **Foundational but superseded**: *Birth of Tragedy* (he criticizes it in the 1886 "Attempt at a Self-Criticism" preface).
- **Transitional / laboratory**: *Human, All Too Human* and *Daybreak* — the positivist workbench where the genealogical method is forged.
- **Occasional / polemical**: *Untimely Meditations* (except UM III *Schopenhauer as Educator*, which he always defended), *Case of Wagner*, *Nietzsche contra Wagner*.
- **Late self-presentation**: *Ecce Homo* — indispensable for authorial intent, but its tone warrants the interpretive care noted above.
