# Agent 2 — Contemporary Records

**Summary.** Nietzsche lived in relative obscurity during his productive years (1869–1889); the people who knew him personally form a small, well-documented circle whose testimony is preserved in Cosima Wagner's diaries, Franz Overbeck's Erinnerungen and Briefwechsel, the Peter Gast correspondence, Lou Andreas-Salomé's 1894 book, and scattered later memoirs from visitors. Contemporaries almost uniformly describe a soft-spoken, exaggeratedly polite, myopic and chronically ill man — a striking counter-image to the rhetorical violence of his prose — and until Georg Brandes's 1888 Copenhagen lectures he had almost no academic or public readership. The record is distorted at one critical point: Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche reshaped the archive after 1893, so her testimony and the letter-texts she edited must be treated as reception-history rather than contemporary witness wherever independent corroboration is absent.

## Witnesses

### Franz Overbeck (1837–1905)
Basel theology colleague from 1870, housemate in the Baumann "Baumannshöhle" period, and Nietzsche's single most reliable lifelong friend. His *Erinnerungen an Friedrich Nietzsche* (published posthumously 1906 in *Die neue Rundschau*, later in C.A. Bernoulli's *Franz Overbeck und Friedrich Nietzsche: Eine Freundschaft*, 2 vols., 1908) and the complete Overbeck–Nietzsche *Briefwechsel* (ed. Katrin Meyer & Barbara von Reibnitz, 2000) are the core independent source. [documented]

- Describes Nietzsche as courteous almost to the point of formality, "the politest of men," who even in conversation maintained a soft voice and measured pace. [documented — Overbeck *Erinnerungen*]
- Records Nietzsche's physical condition: violent migraines, vomiting attacks lasting days, extreme myopia forcing him to hold paper inches from his eyes or dictate. [documented — Overbeck correspondence to Gast, Jan–Feb 1889]
- Overbeck made the Turin rescue trip (arrived Jan 8, 1889, brought Nietzsche back to Basel Jan 10). His letters to Gast and to his wife Ida Overbeck describing Nietzsche's state in the Via Carlo Alberto apartment are the single most important eyewitness record of the collapse. [documented — Overbeck-Gast letters Jan 1889, reprinted Bernoulli vol. 2]
- Ida Overbeck's own memoirs (*Erinnerungen*, in Bernoulli) add domestic-level detail of his Basel years. [documented]

### Erwin Rohde (1845–1898)
Philologist, university friend from Leipzig (met 1867). Correspondence collected in *Friedrich Nietzsche – Erwin Rohde: Briefwechsel* (ed. Fritz Schöll, 1923; complete in KGB). Rohde publicly defended *Die Geburt der Tragödie* against Wilamowitz's 1872 attack *Zukunftsphilologie!*. [documented]

- Their letters document Nietzsche's intellectual excitement in the early 1870s and the slow cooling of the friendship through the 1880s as Nietzsche's thinking radicalised. [documented]
- A late meeting in 1886 (Leipzig) left Rohde disturbed — he wrote privately that Nietzsche seemed "as if he came from a country where no one else lives." [documented — Rohde's letter, cited in Janz vol. 2 and Safranski]

### Richard Wagner (1813–1883) and Cosima Wagner (1837–1930)
The relationship ran from the November 1868 Leipzig meeting through the intense Tribschen period (1869–1872, Nietzsche visited 23 times) to the rupture around *Menschliches, Allzumenschliches* (1878) and Wagner's final *Bayreuther Blätter* jab (an 1878 essay *Publikum und Popularität* which contemporaries and Nietzsche himself read as a personal attack). [documented]

- **Cosima Wagner's Diaries** (*Die Tagebücher*, ed. Gregor-Dellin & Mack, 2 vols., 1976–77; English 1978–80) are the single richest day-by-day record of Nietzsche as a young man. She records hundreds of entries mentioning him between 1869 and 1877. [documented]
  - Tribschen Christmas 1870: Nietzsche present, brings the *Fünf Vorreden* manuscript as a gift to Cosima. [documented]
  - 1872–74: increasingly mixed assessments — admiration of his gifts alongside notes of his "peculiarity," health crises, and what Cosima calls his "Jewish" friends (her anti-Semitic framing of Paul Rée). [documented — Cosima diary entries 1876, 1878]
  - After the 1878 break Cosima's diary turns hostile; by 1882 she writes of his work with open contempt. [documented]
- Wagner himself wrote to Nietzsche's doctor Otto Eiser in October 1877 speculating that Nietzsche's illness was caused by masturbation — a letter Nietzsche only learned of years later and which he treated as the decisive betrayal. [documented — letter preserved, published Westernhagen 1956; see Köhler, *Nietzsche and Wagner*, 1998]
- Nietzsche's and Wagner's versions of the break diverge: Wagner framed it as Nietzsche's ingratitude and illness; Nietzsche in *Ecce Homo* and *Nietzsche contra Wagner* framed it as his own emancipation from a decadent master. The Eiser letter, once known, functions for Nietzsche as objective evidence of Wagner's disloyalty. **Preserve this disagreement.** [attested]

### Peter Gast / Heinrich Köselitz (1854–1918)
Student who attended Nietzsche's Basel lectures in 1875 and became de-facto amanuensis from 1876 onward. Transcribed manuscripts (*Morgenröte*, *Die fröhliche Wissenschaft*, parts of *Zarathustra*). Their correspondence is among the largest in the Nietzsche archive. [documented]

- Gast's letters to Overbeck in 1888 are the first contemporary warning signs — he notices grandiosity in Nietzsche's Turin letters (the "kings" letter, the self-description in *Ecce Homo* drafts). [documented — Gast-Overbeck correspondence Dec 1888, Bernoulli vol. 2]
- Gast describes Nietzsche's working habits: dictation, endless walks (Sils-Maria, Nice, Rapallo, Turin), the meticulous revision of proofs. [documented]
- *Caveat*: after the collapse Gast collaborated with Elisabeth on the early Nachlass editions and on the first *Wille zur Macht* compilation (1901, expanded 1906). His late memoir material is partially compromised by that collaboration; his pre-1889 letters are not. [documented / post-1889 attributed]

### Lou Andreas-Salomé (1861–1937)
Met Nietzsche April 1882 in Rome through Malwida von Meysenbug and Paul Rée. The Lucerne photograph (May 1882, she and Rée harnessed to a cart with Nietzsche wielding a whip) is the famous visual document. Her *Friedrich Nietzsche in seinen Werken* (1894) is the first serious philosophical portrait by someone who had known him. [documented]

- She records his voice as quiet, his manner reserved, his eyes — because of his myopia — producing a peculiar inward gaze. [documented — Salomé 1894]
- The summer 1882 Tautenburg period (three weeks of intensive philosophical conversation) is the high point; the rupture followed in autumn 1882, mediated by jealousy, Elisabeth's interference, and the Rée triangle. [documented]
- Nietzsche's furious 1882–83 letters about Salomé (many to his mother and sister, some to Overbeck) are extant; they should be read alongside her own account because Elisabeth's hand shapes some of the surviving texts. [documented / Elisabeth-edited — flag]

### Paul Rée (1849–1901)
Positivist philosopher, author of *Der Ursprung der moralischen Empfindungen* (1877) which Nietzsche admired. Close companion during the Sorrento winter 1876–77 with Malwida and Brenner, then the Salomé "trinity" of 1882. [documented]

- Rée's own letters to Nietzsche and to Salomé are preserved (cf. *Paul Rée: Die Illusion der Hinterwelt*, and the Pfeiffer edition *Friedrich Nietzsche, Paul Rée, Lou von Salomé: Die Dokumente ihrer Begegnung*, 1970). [documented]
- He is the intellectual counter-voice of the middle period (*Menschliches, Allzumenschliches*); the standard scholarly reading is that Nietzsche's turn to psychological genealogy in the late 1870s is partly shaped by Rée. [documented]

### Malwida von Meysenbug (1816–1903)
1848 revolutionary, author of *Memoiren einer Idealistin* (1876). She hosted the Sorrento commune (winter 1876–77: Nietzsche, Rée, Albert Brenner, herself) in the Villa Rubinacci. Introduced Nietzsche to Salomé in 1882. [documented]

- Her letters and memoirs depict Nietzsche as gentle, somewhat sad, physically fragile, deferential to women of her generation. [documented — Malwida's correspondence, published in *Der Lebensabend einer Idealistin*]
- She is a maternal-figure witness: useful as corrective to his public rhetoric, but she herself was scandalised by his later work and distanced from him by 1886. [documented]

### Later visitors (memoirs)
- **Reinhart von Seydlitz** — correspondent and occasional visitor; left brief reminiscences (in Podach's *Nietzsche's Zusammenbruch*, 1930, and in the *Nietzsche-Studien* record). [documented]
- **Resa von Schirnhofer** (1855–1948) — visited Nietzsche in Nice 1884, left substantial memoirs published by Podach 1938 (*Begegnungen mit Nietzsche*). Describes a courteous, humorous man whose eyes were "veiled" and who spoke with a slight Saxon intonation; notes his sudden bursts of agitation. [documented]
- **Meta von Salis-Marschlins** (1855–1929) — doctoral-educated Swiss aristocrat, friend of the late 1880s. Her memoir *Philosoph und Edelmensch* (1897) is an admiring but literate contemporary source; she bought the Weimar Villa Silberblick that became the archive. [documented — though her post-1890 choices align her with Elisabeth, her 1880s observations are independent]

### Basel colleagues
- **Jacob Burckhardt** (1818–1897) — the elder historian Nietzsche most admired. Their correspondence is slight but intense. Burckhardt consistently declined to follow Nietzsche's philosophical path while remaining personally cordial. Burckhardt received one of the January 1889 "madness letters" (the "Dear Professor" note signed "Dionysos" / "The Crucified"), and his immediate response was to show it to Overbeck — which triggered the rescue trip. [documented — the letter survives; Overbeck narrates in Erinnerungen]
- **Jakob Wackernagel, J.J. Bachofen** — Basel circle, courteous rather than warm; scattered references in Basel correspondence. [documented]
- **Students' impressions**: his Basel students (Baumgartner, Brenner, Gast, Romundt) all describe him as meticulous, undramatic, slightly shy in lecture; not the fire-breathing prophet of the prose. [documented — Baumgartner correspondence, Brenner letters]

### Family
- **Franziska Nietzsche (mother, 1826–1897)** — her letters to and about her son are preserved. She continued to address him as a dutiful Lutheran son long after *Zarathustra*. After the collapse she nursed him in Naumburg from 1890 until her death. Her testimony is loving but intellectually non-engaged and theologically censorious of his work. [documented]
- **Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche (sister, 1846–1935)** — see dedicated section below. **Treat as unreliable.**

### Publishers
- **E.W. Fritzsch** (first publisher, *Geburt der Tragödie* etc.) and **C.G. Naumann** (Leipzig, published most of the mature work from *Zarathustra III* onward). Nietzsche often paid for his own printing costs. Correspondence preserved in KGB. [documented]
- **Ernst Schmeitzner** (the middle-period publisher) was politically an anti-Semite, which Nietzsche increasingly resented — a recurring theme in 1884–85 letters to Overbeck about extricating himself from Schmeitzner's house. [documented]

## Key Episodes

### The Wagner meeting and Tribschen years (1868–1872)
First meeting 8 November 1868 at the Brockhaus house in Leipzig. Nietzsche's visits to Tribschen (Lake Lucerne) begin Pentecost 1869. Cosima's diary records his role as near-family member, including the Christmas 1870 presentation of the *Beethoven* essay and participation in the Siegfried Idyll household. [documented — Cosima Tagebücher]

### *Die Geburt der Tragödie* and the Wilamowitz controversy (1872)
Published Jan 1872. Ulrich von Wilamowitz-Moellendorff's pamphlet *Zukunftsphilologie!* (May 1872) effectively destroyed Nietzsche's standing in professional philology; enrolment in his Basel lectures collapsed. Rohde's reply *Afterphilologie* (Oct 1872) was an act of loyalty. Wagner's own open letter defending Nietzsche appeared in the *Norddeutsche Allgemeine Zeitung*. [documented]

### The Sorrento winter (Oct 1876 – May 1877)
Commune with Malwida, Rée, Brenner at the Villa Rubinacci. Last meeting with Wagner (Nov 1876, visit at the Hotel Vittoria). Wagner was working on *Parsifal*; Nietzsche was turning toward the aphoristic method of *Menschliches*. Contemporaries (Malwida's letters, Brenner's diary fragments) document the tonal break rather than a single dramatic quarrel. [documented]

### The Wagner break (1878)
Nietzsche sent Wagner *Menschliches, Allzumenschliches* (May 1878) as Wagner sent him the *Parsifal* libretto. They never wrote to each other again. Cosima recorded the event with contempt. [documented]

### The Salomé affair (April–November 1882)
- April 1882: introduction in Rome at St Peter's (per Salomé's memoir and Meysenbug's letters). [documented]
- May 1882: Lucerne photograph. [documented]
- Summer: Tautenburg, intensive conversation. [documented]
- Autumn: collapse of the plan to form a philosophical household; Elisabeth's active interference documented in contemporary letters. Nietzsche's "Lou letters" of late 1882 oscillate between fury and longing. [documented — with the caveat that Elisabeth later edited some of Nietzsche's letters from this period; Colli-Montinari KGB restores critical text]

### Productive isolation (1883–1888)
Sils-Maria summers, Riviera winters (Nice, Genoa, Rapallo, Venice). *Zarathustra* I–IV (1883–85, IV privately printed), *Jenseits* (1886), *Zur Genealogie* (1887), then the 1888 explosion: *Der Fall Wagner*, *Götzen-Dämmerung*, *Der Antichrist*, *Ecce Homo*, *Nietzsche contra Wagner*. Witness testimony from this period is mostly correspondence-based (Gast, Overbeck) plus Schirnhofer's and von Salis's visits. [documented]

### Brandes's Copenhagen lectures (April–May 1888)
Georg Brandes delivered a series of lectures on Nietzsche at the University of Copenhagen — the first academic treatment. Brandes coined "aristocratic radicalism" to describe the position; Nietzsche accepted the label gratefully in correspondence. This is the moment Nietzsche becomes aware he is beginning to be read. [documented — Brandes-Nietzsche correspondence, published Brandes *Friedrich Nietzsche*, 1889]

### The Turin collapse (Jan 3–10, 1889)
- Jan 3: the celebrated (but *not* contemporaneously documented) horse-cabman incident on the Piazza Carlo Alberto is **[legendary]** — it first appears in print decades later, without eyewitness attestation. Flag as unverified. [legendary — see Verrecchia, *La catastrofe di Nietzsche a Torino*, 1978; Young, *Philosophical Biography* p. 532, treats the story with explicit skepticism]
- Jan 3–6: the "madness letters" (*Wahnsinnszettel*) are sent to Burckhardt, Cosima ("Ariadne, I love you"), Brandes, Overbeck, Strindberg, the King of Italy, and others. These texts survive in the archive and are dated. [documented — KGB III/5]
- Jan 8: Overbeck arrives in Turin. Finds Nietzsche at the piano, weeping, unrecognising at first, then embracing him. [documented — Overbeck to Gast, Jan 15 1889]
- Jan 10: Overbeck returns Nietzsche to Basel, then Jena clinic (Jan 18). Mother takes him to Naumburg May 1890. [documented]

**After Jan 10, 1889 contemporary observation ceases to inform our object.** Everything that follows is of an incapacitated man and is reception-history.

## Private vs. Public Persona — the Discrepancy

Every contemporary who left a first-hand description — Overbeck, Gast, Rohde (in private letters), Malwida, Salomé, Schirnhofer, von Salis, Burckhardt, Ida Overbeck — converges on a counter-image:

- **Manner**: exaggeratedly polite, old-Prussian courteous, ceremonious; "almost feminine in gentleness" (Malwida). [attested]
- **Voice**: soft, low, slightly hesitant, Saxon intonation. Never raised. [attested — Overbeck, Schirnhofer, von Salis]
- **Physical**: near-blind (could not read print without holding it to his face; often dictated), severe migraines and vomiting episodes of 30+ hours, chronic insomnia treated with chloral hydrate, Veronal, and other sedatives he self-medicated with. [documented — own letters to doctors Eiser and Breiting, Overbeck correspondence]
- **Social**: long silences, then sudden bursts of laughter or agitation; avoided confrontation; disliked crowds. [attested — Schirnhofer, von Salis, Gast]
- **Humour**: frequently remarked on — Salomé, Schirnhofer, von Salis all describe him as wry and playful in person, not the thundering prophet of *Zarathustra*. [attested]

This gap between the reported private demeanour and the prose persona is itself a load-bearing fact for any echo: **the textual voice is deliberately amplified; the operating personality was quiet, courteous, and ill.**

## Life-time Reception

- Sales of his books were negligible until 1888. *Menschliches, Allzumenschliches* (1878) sold perhaps 120 copies in a year. The fourth part of *Zarathustra* was privately printed in 40 copies (1885) because no publisher would take it. [documented — Schmeitzner / Naumann correspondence, KGB]
- Academic reception: effectively none in Germany until the 1890s. Philology had discarded him in 1872. Philosophy faculties ignored him. [documented]
- First serious external recognition: Brandes, Copenhagen 1888. [documented]
- First foreign translations during his productive life: extremely limited — Polish press interest (via Wojciech Dzieduszycki), some French notices by Hippolyte Taine (who corresponded with Nietzsche briefly in 1887–88). [documented — Taine-Nietzsche correspondence]
- Strindberg corresponded enthusiastically with Nietzsche in late 1888 — one of the very few contemporaneously famous writers to recognise him in real time. [documented — Strindberg letters Dec 1888]
- The explosion of his reputation is post-1890 and is not part of the life we are echoing.

## The Elisabeth Problem — Specific Flags

Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche (1846–1935):

1. Married anti-Semite Bernhard Förster 1885; founded Nueva Germania colony in Paraguay 1886 — Nietzsche repeatedly and angrily condemned both the marriage and the project in letters to Overbeck and to his mother (*flag*: some of these letters were posthumously softened or excised by Elisabeth herself). [documented, with textual caution]
2. Returned to Germany 1893, took control of the archive (Naumburg, then Weimar Silberblick from 1897), founded the Nietzsche-Archiv.
3. **Textual tampering documented by modern scholarship**: she altered letter texts, fabricated letters (notably some supposed letters to her), suppressed letters hostile to herself or to Förster, and directed the construction of *Der Wille zur Macht* as a "book" Nietzsche never assembled. The Colli–Montinari KGW/KGB editions (1967–) systematically restore the original texts. [documented — Montinari, *Nietzsche lesen*, 1982; Podach, *Ein Blick in Notizbücher Nietzsches*, 1963]
4. Wrote a three-volume biography *Das Leben Friedrich Nietzsche's* (1895–1904) that is the source of many famous "Nietzsche anecdotes" repeated in popular books. **These anecdotes should not be taken as contemporary witness unless independently corroborated.** Examples to flag:
   - Childhood "little pastor" stories.
   - Her account of the Salomé affair (systematically self-exculpatory).
   - Her narration of the Turin horse incident (one of the channels by which the story entered the canon).
   - The "last meeting" scenes between her and her brother in his incapacity (1890s) — these are not records of the thinking Nietzsche.
5. Courted Mussolini (who visited the archive in the 1930s) and Hitler (who attended her funeral 1935). This political instrumentalisation retroactively contaminated the archive's presentation of Nietzsche and is a historical datum in its own right.

**Working rule**: any "Nietzsche said / did X" where the only source is Elisabeth = `[attributed]` at best, and may be `[legendary]`.

## Quote Provenance Notes

- Famous aphorisms ("What does not kill me makes me stronger," "God is dead," "gaze into the abyss") are **genuinely Nietzsche's** and cite-able from the published works (*Götzen-Dämmerung* Sprüche 8; *Fröhliche Wissenschaft* §108 and §125; *Jenseits* §146). These are not the contested zone. [documented]
- Contested zone is the **biographical anecdote** and the **personal-letter paraphrase**, where Elisabeth's hand may have shaped the version in circulation. For any letter quotation, cite KGB (Colli-Montinari Briefwechsel) rather than the 1900-era *Gesammelte Briefe* editions.
- For letters to and from Overbeck: use the 2000 critical edition (Meyer/von Reibnitz).
- For Cosima: the 1976–77 Gregor-Dellin/Mack edition from the original diaries (bypasses the earlier filtered extracts).
- For Salomé: Pfeiffer's 1970 *Die Dokumente* is the standard source-critical edition of the Nietzsche/Rée/Salomé correspondence.
