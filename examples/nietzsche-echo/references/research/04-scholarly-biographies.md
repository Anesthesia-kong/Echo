# Agent 4 — Scholarly Biographies and Monographs

**Summary (3 sentences):** Modern Nietzsche scholarship since Kaufmann's 1950 rehabilitation has moved decisively to separate Nietzsche from Nietzscheanism, debunking the Nazi appropriation (engineered by his sister Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche), correcting the popular medical narrative of tertiary syphilis (recent medical scholarship proposes CADASIL, retro-orbital meningioma, or frontotemporal dementia), and rejecting readings of the Übermensch as racial/eugenic. The field is divided between an Anglophone analytic tradition (Clark, Leiter, Richardson, Reginster, Pippin) that treats Nietzsche as a systematic naturalist philosopher with arguments worth reconstructing, and a continental tradition (Deleuze, Foucault, Nehamas, Klossowski) that treats him as a stylist, genealogist, and destabilizer of philosophical identity. The deepest live controversies concern whether will-to-power is a cosmological doctrine or a psychological one, whether eternal recurrence is literal or regulative, whether Nietzsche is a moral anti-realist or a revaluator with his own substantive values, and — since Shaw and Drochon — how to take his "great politics" seriously without either apologetics or Nazi-era distortion.

## Major Biographies

- **Walter Kaufmann, *Nietzsche: Philosopher, Psychologist, Antichrist* (1950, 4th ed. 1974)** [scholarly-consensus: foundational]
  - The postwar rehabilitation; rescued Nietzsche from the Nazi reading by emphasizing self-overcoming (*Selbstüberwindung*), the discontinuity between Nietzsche and Elisabeth's *Will to Power* compilation, and Nietzsche's explicit anti-anti-Semitism [modern-scholarly-inference — Kaufmann].
  - Kaufmann's translations (for Viking/Random House) became the Anglophone standard and set the vocabulary used ever since [scholarly-consensus].
  - **[contested]** Later scholars (Conway, Detwiler, Dombowsky, Drochon, Shaw) argue Kaufmann was *over-corrective*: he downplayed Nietzsche's aristocratic politics, his hostility to democracy/socialism/feminism, and his "great politics" talk, rendering him too much the gentle existentialist humanist [modern-scholarly-inference — Drochon 2016; Dombowsky 2004].

- **R.J. Hollingdale, *Nietzsche: The Man and His Philosophy* (1965, rev. 1999)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Hollingdale]
  - Paired with Kaufmann as the foundational Anglophone biography; Hollingdale's Penguin translations (*Zarathustra*, *Twilight*, *Genealogy*, *Ecce Homo*, *Daybreak*, *Human, All Too Human*) remain widely used.
  - Emphasizes continuity with German philosophical tradition (Schopenhauer above all) and the psychological unity of Nietzsche's thought [modern-scholarly-inference — Hollingdale].

- **Rüdiger Safranski, *Nietzsche: A Philosophical Biography* (German 2000, English trans. 2002)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Safranski]
  - Thematic rather than strictly chronological; treats Nietzsche as a "thought-experimenter" (*Denkversuche*), foregrounds the struggle with Wagner and Schopenhauer, and reads the late works as an intensifying "experiment with truth" rather than a descent into madness [modern-scholarly-inference — Safranski].
  - Strong on the cultural-historical German context; weak on Anglophone analytic reconstruction.

- **Julian Young, *Friedrich Nietzsche: A Philosophical Biography* (2010)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Young]
  - Currently the most comprehensive single-volume philosophical biography in English; integrates life and doctrine chapter-by-chapter.
  - Young argues for a "communitarian" Nietzsche — a thinker fundamentally concerned with cultural and religious renewal, not just individual flourishing [modern-scholarly-inference — Young 2010]. **[contested]** — Leiter and others dispute the communitarian reading.
  - Endorses the retro-orbital meningioma / tumor hypothesis over syphilis for Nietzsche's collapse, following Leonard Sax and Schain [modern-scholarly-inference — Young 2010; Sax 2003].

- **Curtis Cate, *Friedrich Nietzsche* (2002)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Cate]
  - Popular-narrative biography, strongest on the social and cultural milieu (Basel, Wagner circle, Sils Maria, Turin). Less philosophically rigorous than Young or Safranski.

- **Ronald Hayman, *Nietzsche: A Critical Life* (1980)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Hayman]
  - A standard pre-Young English biography; accepts the older syphilis diagnosis largely without question (published before the medical revisionism). Still cited for narrative detail.

- **Sue Prideaux, *I Am Dynamite! A Life of Friedrich Nietzsche* (2018)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Prideaux]
  - Won the 2018 Hawthornden Prize; the best recent popular biography. Strong on Lou Salomé, the Wagner rupture, and Elisabeth's posthumous falsification of the archive.
  - Foregrounds Nietzsche's explicit and repeated disgust with anti-Semitism and German nationalism [modern-scholarly-inference — Prideaux 2018, drawing on Schaberg, Diethe].

- **Additional biographical/archival work**:
  - **Carol Diethe, *Nietzsche's Sister and the Will to Power* (2003)** — definitive scholarly treatment of Elisabeth's falsification of the *Nachlass* and her Nazi-era curation of the Weimar archive [scholarly-consensus — Diethe].
  - **William H. Schaberg, *The Nietzsche Canon* (1995)** — bibliographical history of Nietzsche's publications, essential for understanding what he actually published versus what Elisabeth assembled [scholarly-consensus].

## Major Monographs (Analytic and Continental)

### Anglophone / analytic

- **Maudemarie Clark, *Nietzsche on Truth and Philosophy* (Cambridge 1990)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Clark]
  - Landmark. Argues Nietzsche's mature position (post-*Human, All Too Human*) *abandons* the early radical denial of truth (the "lie thesis" of *On Truth and Lies in a Nonmoral Sense*) and arrives at a deflationary but non-skeptical view: truths are possible within our cognitive-linguistic frame even without a "thing in itself."
  - Reads will-to-power as a *psychological* empirical-ish hypothesis, not a metaphysical cosmology [modern-scholarly-inference — Clark 1990, ch. 7]. **[contested]** — Richardson 1996 defends a more robustly metaphysical reading.

- **Brian Leiter, *Nietzsche on Morality* (Routledge 2002, 2nd ed. 2015)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Leiter]
  - Most influential analytic monograph of the last 25 years. Argues Nietzsche is a "methodological naturalist" whose critique of morality is continuous with the empirical sciences of his day (physiology, psychology).
  - Core thesis: Nietzsche attacks "morality in the pejorative sense" (MPS) — the specific slave-moral, life-denying, universalist, egalitarian package — not all possible moralities. He is a *moral anti-realist* about MPS values but has his own evaluative standpoint grounded in "higher types" [modern-scholarly-inference — Leiter 2002/2015].
  - Argues Nietzsche is a *fatalist* about character: we are what our drives make us, and self-creation rhetoric is mostly illusory [modern-scholarly-inference — Leiter]. **[contested]** — Gemes, Richardson, and Pippin all push back.
  - The Leiter Naturalism Reading is now the dominant analytic baseline, though contested in detail.

- **Bernard Reginster, *The Affirmation of Life: Nietzsche on Overcoming Nihilism* (Harvard 2006)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Reginster]
  - Argues Nietzsche's central problem is *nihilism* — the collapse of value following the death of God — and his positive project is its overcoming through life-affirmation.
  - Reconstructs will-to-power as "the will to overcome resistance": what we fundamentally want is not pleasure but the experience of overcoming obstacles. This dissolves the apparent paradox that suffering can be part of what we affirm [modern-scholarly-inference — Reginster 2006].
  - Reads eternal recurrence as the *test* of whether one has genuinely overcome nihilism [modern-scholarly-inference — Reginster].
  - Widely regarded as the best single reconstruction of the positive Nietzsche [scholarly-consensus within analytic camp].

- **Alexander Nehamas, *Nietzsche: Life as Literature* (Harvard 1985)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Nehamas]
  - Reads Nietzsche through the frame of aestheticism: the self is a literary character to be crafted, the world is "a work of art that gives birth to itself" [*WP* 796 — but note this is a *Nachlass* fragment].
  - Nietzsche's perspectivism: every view is interpretation, including his own; his style is inseparable from his doctrine.
  - Straddles the analytic/continental divide; widely read on both sides.

- **Robert Pippin, *Nietzsche, Psychology, and First Philosophy* (Chicago 2010)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Pippin]
  - Hegelian-inflected reading. Nietzsche's "psychology" is really first philosophy — an account of the conditions under which anything can count as a reason, value, or commitment for a modern European.
  - Resists the Leiter naturalist reading: Nietzsche's psychology is not reducible to empirical drive-theory; it is normative-reconstructive [modern-scholarly-inference — Pippin 2010]. **[contested]**

- **Richard Schacht, *Nietzsche* (Routledge, Arguments of the Philosophers, 1983)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Schacht]
  - Earlier systematic reconstruction; presents Nietzsche as offering a coherent (if experimental) philosophical view on truth, value, knowledge, and self.
  - Bridges Kaufmann-era humanism and the more technical Clark/Leiter era.

- **John Richardson, *Nietzsche's System* (Oxford 1996)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Richardson]
  - Argues Nietzsche *does* have a system, centered on will-to-power as a metaphysical/ontological doctrine about power-structures (drives competing, organizing, being overcome).
  - Will-to-power is more than psychology: it is the ontological category through which all life is structured [modern-scholarly-inference — Richardson 1996]. **[contested]** — directly opposes Clark.

- **John Richardson, *Nietzsche's New Darwinism* (Oxford 2004)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Richardson]
  - Reads Nietzsche as offering a distinctively Darwinian (though anti-Spencerian) account: values, drives, and types are selected within populations of drives and within cultural reproduction. Deflationary about the cosmological reading of will-to-power.

- **Raymond Geuss** — essays in *Morality, Culture, and History* (1999) and *Outside Ethics* (2005) [modern-scholarly-inference — Geuss]
  - Treats Nietzsche as a genealogist whose critique of morality is political-historical, not merely philosophical. Sharpens the continuity between Nietzsche and Foucault, Williams.

- **Tamsin Shaw, *Nietzsche's Political Skepticism* (Princeton 2007)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Shaw]
  - Major revisionist move. Argues Nietzsche's political problem is the *legitimation crisis* after the death of God: modern states cannot rely on shared values to generate legitimacy.
  - Restores the political Nietzsche without making him a proto-fascist [modern-scholarly-inference — Shaw 2007].

- **Hugo Drochon, *Nietzsche's Great Politics* (Princeton 2016)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Drochon]
  - Argues "great politics" (*grosse Politik*) is a substantive and coherent political program: Nietzsche favors a unified post-national European aristocratic order, opposed to mass democracy, socialism, and German nationalism.
  - Against Kaufmann's apolitical reading and against Nazi appropriation: Nietzsche's politics is aristocratic-European, not German-racial [modern-scholarly-inference — Drochon 2016].

- **Ken Gemes (essays, e.g., "Nietzsche on Free Will, Autonomy and the Sovereign Individual" 2006, and *Nietzsche on Freedom and Autonomy* ed. with Simon May 2009)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Gemes]
  - Argues Nietzsche distinguishes a *deserts* free will (which he rejects) from an *agency* free will (which he affirms for higher types as self-integration of drives).
  - Pushes back against Leiter's strong fatalism [contested].

- **Rebecca Bamford** — editor of *Nietzsche's Free Spirit Philosophy* (2015) and work on Nietzsche's philosophy of science and health; contributor to *Journal of Nietzsche Studies*.

- **Mark Warren, *Nietzsche and Political Thought* (MIT 1988)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Warren]
  - Earlier political reading; argues Nietzsche has a coherent philosophy of power but an incoherent positive politics. Precursor to Shaw/Drochon.

- **Other analytic-tradition monographs worth noting**:
  - **Peter Poellner, *Nietzsche and Metaphysics* (Oxford 1995)** — detailed study of the epistemology and metaphysics.
  - **Paul Katsafanas, *Agency and the Foundations of Ethics: Nietzschean Constitutivism* (Oxford 2013)** — defends a Korsgaard-style constitutivist reading grounded in will-to-power.
  - **Lawrence Hatab, *Nietzsche's Life Sentence: Coming to Terms with Eternal Recurrence* (Routledge 2005)** — major monograph on recurrence, reading it as existential/regulative.
  - **Simon May, *Nietzsche's Ethics and His War on "Morality"* (Oxford 1999)** — important reconstruction of the ethical positive view.
  - **Aaron Ridley, *Nietzsche's Conscience* (Cornell 1998)** — close reading of *Genealogy*.
  - **Christopher Janaway, *Beyond Selflessness: Reading Nietzsche's Genealogy* (Oxford 2007)** — argues the *Genealogy*'s affective/rhetorical strategy is philosophically essential, not ornamental.

### Continental

- **Gilles Deleuze, *Nietzsche and Philosophy* (French 1962, English 1983)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Deleuze]
  - The decisive postwar French reading. Reconstructs Nietzsche as the philosopher of difference and affirmation against dialectic (anti-Hegel).
  - Will-to-power as a *differential* force (active vs. reactive); eternal recurrence as the "selective ontological principle" that returns only affirmative/active difference.
  - Engine of the "French Nietzsche" received by Foucault, Derrida, Klossowski, Lyotard.

- **Michel Foucault, "Nietzsche, Genealogy, History" (1971)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Foucault]
  - Programmatic statement of genealogy as method: not origin-seeking but the analysis of descent (*Herkunft*) and emergence (*Entstehung*) of contingent power-knowledge formations.
  - Foucault's entire methodology (discipline, biopower) is explicitly Nietzschean.

- **Pierre Klossowski, *Nietzsche and the Vicious Circle* (French 1969, English 1997)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Klossowski]
  - Reads Nietzsche through the Turin collapse and the sign-economy of the body; eternal recurrence as the dissolution of the self.

- **Karl Löwith, *Nietzsche's Philosophy of the Eternal Recurrence of the Same* (German 1935/1956, English 1997)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Löwith]
  - Early-mid century German reading that places recurrence at the center of Nietzsche's thought as a secularization of Christian eschatology. Still cited.

- **Martin Heidegger, *Nietzsche* (lectures 1936–46, pub. 1961, English 1979–87, 4 vols.)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Heidegger]
  - Reads Nietzsche as "the last metaphysician of the West" — the culmination and completion of metaphysics as the history of being, in which being is reduced to value and will-to-power.
  - Highly contested as a reading *of Nietzsche* (many scholars — Müller-Lauter, Kaufmann, Clark — regard it as Heidegger ventriloquizing his own concerns) but enormously influential on subsequent continental reception [contested].

- **Wolfgang Müller-Lauter, *Nietzsche: His Philosophy of Contradictions and the Contradictions of His Philosophy* (German 1971, English 1999)** [modern-scholarly-inference — Müller-Lauter]
  - Central German *Nietzsche-Forschung* monograph. Argues will-to-power is pluralistic: a multiplicity of competing power-quanta, not a unified metaphysical principle. Opposes Heidegger's reading.
  - Also co-edited the *Nietzsche-Studien* journal and the Colli-Montinari *Kritische Gesamtausgabe* [scholarly-consensus — foundational textual work].

- **Other continental-tradition figures**:
  - **Sarah Kofman, *Nietzsche and Metaphor* (1972, English 1993)** — deconstructive reading of style and metaphor.
  - **Jacques Derrida, *Spurs: Nietzsche's Styles* (1978)** — on the irreducibility of Nietzsche's styles to a single position.
  - **Eric Blondel, *Nietzsche: The Body and Culture* (1986, English 1991)** — physiological/cultural reading.
  - **Babette Babich** — sustained work on Nietzsche's philosophy of science and rhetoric.

### Textual / editorial foundation

- **Giorgio Colli and Mazzino Montinari, *Kritische Gesamtausgabe* (KGW, 1967–)** and the paperback *Kritische Studienausgabe* (KSA, 1980, 15 vols.) [scholarly-consensus — the standard critical edition]
  - Definitively displaced the Elisabeth-era *Grossoktav* edition and the *Wille zur Macht* compilation as the scholarly baseline. All serious scholarship now cites KSA/KGW.

## Scholarly Consensus (where almost all serious scholars agree)

1. **Nietzsche is not a Nazi, and *The Will to Power* is not his final work.** [scholarly-consensus]
   - The book titled *Der Wille zur Macht* was assembled from *Nachlass* fragments by Elisabeth and Peter Gast (Heinrich Köselitz) after Nietzsche's collapse, with significant editorial manipulation; Nietzsche himself *abandoned* the project of a book with that title around 1888 (see Montinari, *Reading Nietzsche* 2003; Magnus, "Nietzsche's Philosophy in 1888" 1986; Schaberg 1995).
   - Nazi appropriation depended on (a) Elisabeth's archive politics, (b) Baeumler's 1931 reading, (c) the falsified *Will to Power* text. Kaufmann 1950, Montinari, and Diethe 2003 document this in detail.

2. **Nietzsche was not an anti-Semite and explicitly attacked anti-Semitism** [scholarly-consensus]
   - Evidence: *BGE* §251, *GM* I and III, the break with Wagner partly over the *Bayreuth* circle's anti-Semitism, the break with Elisabeth and her husband Bernhard Förster (a professional anti-Semite) that culminated in the *Antisemiten-Ablehnungs-Briefe*, the late letters (Dec 1887 to Fritsch, Dec 1888 to Overbeck, and the "mad" letters to Burckhardt and others) in which he calls for anti-Semites to be shot.
   - The ambivalence people perceive is confined almost entirely to early-period passing remarks and to his critique of *Christianity-as-a-Jewish-product* (*Antichrist*), which is a different target.
   - See Yirmiyahu Yovel, *Dark Riddle: Hegel, Nietzsche, and the Jews* (1998); Robert Holub, *Nietzsche's Jewish Problem* (2016) — Holub gives the most nuanced recent account, noting Nietzsche grew up in a culturally anti-Semitic milieu but explicitly rejected political anti-Semitism by the 1880s.

3. **The Übermensch is not a racial, eugenic, or biological-master-race ideal** [scholarly-consensus]
   - It is an individual ethical-aesthetic ideal: the human who can affirm life, create values after the death of God, and withstand eternal recurrence. Goethe and Napoleon are Nietzsche's own examples of approximations, not "Aryans."
   - Kaufmann 1950, Hollingdale, Young, Safranski, Reginster, Leiter, Gemes — all converge on this.

4. **Nietzsche's thought is non-systematic in form but not incoherent** [scholarly-consensus, with caveats]
   - Aphoristic, experimental, and rhetorical — but substantive threads are reconstructible: critique of morality, genealogy of values, will-to-power (in some form), perspectivism, eternal recurrence, affirmation.
   - Richardson 1996 argues for a more systematic reading than most; Nehamas 1985 argues for an aesthetic unity rather than a propositional system; both reject the view that Nietzsche is *merely* literary or *merely* unsystematic.

5. **The critique of (slave) morality is the *center* of Nietzsche's project** [scholarly-consensus]
   - This is one point of total agreement across analytic (Leiter), continental (Deleuze, Foucault), and biographical (Kaufmann, Young) readings.
   - *Genealogy of Morals* (1887) is the key text.

6. **Schopenhauer and Wagner are formative; Nietzsche's trajectory is one of self-overcoming against both** [scholarly-consensus]
   - The 1876 break with Wagner and the abandonment of Schopenhauerian pessimism by *Human, All Too Human* (1878) are the biographical axis of the middle and late work.

7. **The Colli-Montinari KSA/KGW is the textual baseline.** [scholarly-consensus]

## Active Controversies

### A. Metaphysics of will-to-power: cosmological or psychological?
- **Cosmological / ontological reading**: Heidegger, Müller-Lauter (pluralistic version), Richardson 1996, Poellner — will-to-power is a claim about the fundamental structure of reality (or at least of all life). [contested]
- **Psychological / deflationary reading**: Clark 1990, Leiter 2002/2015, Richardson 2004 (revising earlier view toward Darwinian) — will-to-power is a psychological empirical hypothesis about human drives, not a metaphysics; the cosmological passages are mostly in the *Nachlass* and in *BGE* §36 (which is itself hypothetical: "supposing...").
- **[contested]** — this is the single most durable dispute in Nietzsche scholarship.

### B. Eternal recurrence: literal cosmological doctrine or regulative thought-experiment?
- **Literal/cosmological**: Nietzsche believed time recurs; the *Nachlass* contains a "proof" from the finitude of force and infinite time. Löwith, Heidegger (partly), Arthur Danto *Nietzsche as Philosopher* 1965.
- **Existential/regulative/practical**: Recurrence is a *test* of affirmation (Reginster 2006, Hatab 2005, Nehamas 1985, Clark 1990) — "how would you have to live to will the return of this moment infinitely?"
- **[contested]** — most contemporary scholars lean regulative, but a minority hold that the distinction itself is false to Nietzsche.

### C. Moral anti-realism vs. "inverted realism"
- **Anti-realist / error theory**: Leiter 2002 — all moral claims are systematically false; Nietzsche rejects moral realism wholesale and grounds his evaluations in non-moral goods (power, health, flourishing).
- **Fictionalist / constructivist**: Nadeem Hussain ("Honest Illusion," 2007) — Nietzsche advocates knowingly pretending certain values are objective.
- **Constitutivist**: Katsafanas 2013 — will-to-power grounds objective (constitutive) norms of agency.
- **Revaluator with substantive values**: Reginster, Gemes, Pippin, May — Nietzsche rejects *slave* morality but affirms a determinate ethical standpoint (life-affirmation, honesty, courage, creativity).
- **[contested]**

### D. Freedom and the self: fatalism vs. self-creation
- **Strong fatalism**: Leiter — we are the drives we happen to have; Nietzsche's *amor fati* is literal.
- **Self-creation / agency reading**: Nehamas (self as literary creation), Gemes (higher types integrate drives into a unified agency), Pippin (Hegelian self-determination).
- **[contested]**

### E. Politics: apolitical, aristocratic-European, or proto-authoritarian?
- **Apolitical / ethical individualist**: Kaufmann 1950, Nehamas 1985, Leiter (mostly) — the political remarks are derivative of the ethical project.
- **Serious aristocratic politics**: Detwiler *Nietzsche and the Politics of Aristocratic Radicalism* (1990), Dombowsky *Nietzsche's Machiavellian Politics* (2004), Appel *Nietzsche contra Democracy* (1999), Drochon 2016.
- **Political skeptic (legitimation-crisis reading)**: Shaw 2007, Warren 1988.
- **[contested]** — this is the most consequential current revisionism.

### F. Truth and perspectivism: self-refuting or sophisticated?
- **Early Nietzsche's radical "lie" thesis** (*On Truth and Lies*) vs. mature perspectivism: Clark 1990 argues for a clear development in which the radical thesis is abandoned; Nehamas argues for continuity via perspectivism-all-the-way-down.
- **Is perspectivism self-refuting?** Most contemporary scholars say no — perspectives admit of better and worse, richer and poorer, and the perspectivist claim is itself advanced from a perspective that is argued for [modern-scholarly-inference — Clark, Leiter, Anderson "Truth and Objectivity in Perspectivism" 1998].
- **[contested]** but narrowing.

### G. Naturalism: methodological only, or substantive?
- Leiter's "methodological naturalism" vs. Richardson's "substantive Darwinian naturalism" vs. Pippin's denial that Nietzsche is really a naturalist in the modern sense [contested].

## Myth-Debunking (claims commonly repeated in popular/casual contexts but scholarly-refuted)

1. **Myth: *The Will to Power* is Nietzsche's final, culminating work.**
   - **Reality:** It is a posthumous compilation by Elisabeth and Gast from 1901/1906/1911, assembled from notebook fragments many of which Nietzsche had rejected. Nietzsche planned a book under that title for several years in the mid-1880s but *abandoned* the project in 1888 in favor of other works (*Antichrist*, *Twilight*, *Ecce Homo*). Montinari's *Reading Nietzsche* (2003) is definitive. [scholarly-consensus]

2. **Myth: Nietzsche directly influenced or endorsed the Nazis.**
   - **Reality:** Nietzsche died in 1900. Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche (1846–1935), an active anti-Semite and wife of Bernhard Förster, curated the Weimar *Nietzsche-Archiv* to align her brother with German nationalism and later received Hitler at the archive (1934). The *Will to Power* compilation, selective letter redaction, and suppression of the anti-anti-Semitic material were key tools. See Diethe 2003; Macintyre, *Forgotten Fatherland* (1992); Hollingdale's biographical epilogue. [scholarly-consensus]

3. **Myth: Nietzsche was a raging anti-Semite.**
   - **Reality:** He explicitly and repeatedly attacked anti-Semitism, broke with Wagner in part over it, broke with his sister and Förster over it, denounced the "anti-Semitic howlers" in letters, and in his final "mad" letters called for anti-Semites to be shot (letter to Overbeck, Jan 1889; letter to Burckhardt). The scholarly record is unambiguous. See Yovel 1998, Holub 2016, Santaniello, *Nietzsche, God, and the Jews* (1994). [scholarly-consensus — note Holub adds nuance: Nietzsche absorbed cultural stereotypes and his critique of *Christianity* sometimes draws on stereotypes about ancient Judaism, but political anti-Semitism he reviled]

4. **Myth: Nietzsche's 1889 collapse and subsequent decline were caused by tertiary syphilis.**
   - **Reality:** This was the standard 20th-century diagnosis (originating with Wille, Binswanger, and Möbius's 1902 *Über das Pathologische bei Nietzsche*), but is now seriously contested. Recent medical scholarship proposes alternatives:
     - **Retro-orbital meningioma (slow-growing benign brain tumor):** Leonard Sax, "What was the cause of Nietzsche's dementia?" *Journal of Medical Biography* 11 (2003): 47–54. Endorsed by Julian Young 2010.
     - **CADASIL (Cerebral Autosomal Dominant Arteriopathy with Subcortical Infarcts and Leukoencephalopathy):** Hemelsoet, Hemelsoet & Devreese, "The neurological illness of Friedrich Nietzsche," *Acta Neurologica Belgica* 108 (2008): 9–16. Notes family history of similar strokes/decline.
     - **Frontotemporal dementia:** proposed in more recent literature (Orth and Trimble, "Friedrich Nietzsche's mental illness — general paralysis of the insane vs. frontotemporal dementia," *Acta Psychiatrica Scandinavica* 114 (2006): 439–445).
   - **[contested among physicians and medical historians; scholarly-consensus that the popular "syphilis" narrative is not established and may well be wrong]**

5. **Myth: The Übermensch is an Aryan superman / racial ideal / eugenic goal.**
   - **Reality:** The Übermensch is an individual, ethical-aesthetic ideal — a post-theistic value-creator capable of affirming life and willing eternal recurrence. Nietzsche's exemplars (Goethe, Napoleon, Leonardo) are multinational and not racially selected. The conflation with the Aryan-ideal is a Nazi-era imposition. See Kaufmann 1950; Lampert, *Nietzsche's Teaching* (1986); Gooding-Williams, *Zarathustra's Dionysian Modernism* (2001). [scholarly-consensus]

6. **Myth: Nietzsche was a proto-fascist.**
   - **Reality:** Detwiler, Dombowsky, and Drochon argue he has a serious *aristocratic-radical* politics, not a fascist one: he opposes nationalism (especially German nationalism), opposes mass movements, opposes state-worship, and favors a unified post-national European elite. His quarrel with democracy/socialism is not a quarrel from the Right-wing mass-politics tradition (which would have appalled him) but from a neo-classical aristocratic-individualist tradition. [modern-scholarly-inference — Drochon 2016] [contested on degree of objectionability, not on the anti-fascist textual facts]

7. **Myth: Nietzsche is "crazy" or "irrational" in his late works (1888).**
   - **Reality:** *Twilight of the Idols*, *Antichrist*, *Genealogy of Morals*, *Ecce Homo*, and *The Case of Wagner* — all 1887–88 — show sustained argumentative control and stylistic peak. The collapse was sudden (Jan 3, 1889 in Turin). Safranski, Young, Prideaux all emphasize this.

8. **Myth: Nietzsche hated women / was a straightforward misogynist in a simple way.**
   - **Reality:** He wrote many objectionable remarks on women that are not rescuable. But the scholarly task is to distinguish (a) period/Schopenhauerian cultural misogyny from (b) his critiques of 19th-c. bourgeois feminism from (c) his actual close relationships (Lou Salomé, Malwida von Meysenbug, Cosima Wagner). See Carol Diethe, *Nietzsche's Women* (1996); Kelly Oliver and Marilyn Pearsall eds., *Feminist Interpretations of Nietzsche* (1998). [contested — scholars agree the textual misogyny is real but disagree on how central or escapable it is]

9. **Myth: Nietzsche is an atheist of the new-atheist / scientistic kind.**
   - **Reality:** "God is dead" is a *cultural-historical* diagnosis, not a triumphalist declaration. Nietzsche takes religion, especially Christianity, with *maximal* seriousness as a psychological-cultural phenomenon; he regards its collapse as a catastrophe whose consequences (nihilism) are the central problem of modernity. See Reginster 2006, Young, *Nietzsche's Philosophy of Religion* (2006). [scholarly-consensus]

## Analytic vs. Continental Divide

| Axis | Analytic (Clark, Leiter, Richardson, Reginster, Pippin, Gemes) | Continental (Deleuze, Foucault, Nehamas, Klossowski, Derrida) |
|---|---|---|
| **Method** | Propositional reconstruction; argument-evaluation | Rhetorical/stylistic reading; attention to aphorism, voice |
| **Will-to-power** | Psychological drive-theory (Clark, Leiter) or Darwinian (Richardson 2004) | Differential force (Deleuze), desire-economy (Klossowski) |
| **Truth** | Deflated but functional (Clark, Leiter) | Perspectivism with irreducible stylistic plurality (Nehamas, Derrida) |
| **Self** | Fatalist drive-bundle (Leiter) or self-integrated agent (Gemes) | Dissolved/literary (Nehamas, Klossowski, Foucault) |
| **Political** | Ethics-first, politics peripheral (Leiter); legitimation crisis (Shaw); aristocratic (Drochon) | Genealogy as politics (Foucault); micro-politics of power |
| **Relation to science** | Continuous with empirical psychology/biology (Leiter, Richardson) | Suspicious of scientism; genealogy as counter-science (Foucault) |
| **Canonical text** | *Genealogy of Morals* | *Genealogy*, *Zarathustra*, plus *Nachlass* |
| **Attitude to Heidegger** | Mostly negative — Heidegger ventriloquizes | Foundational — Deleuze and Derrida inherit Heidegger's reading while revising it |

**Key bridge figures:** Nehamas 1985 is read seriously in both camps; Pippin 2010 engages both; Raymond Geuss writes across the divide. The *Journal of Nietzsche Studies* and *Nietzsche-Studien* publish both traditions.

**Core disagreement:** The analytic tradition asks "what is Nietzsche's view, and is it true?" The continental tradition asks "what does Nietzsche *do* to philosophy, and what new operations does his text enact?" Both agree Nietzsche matters; they disagree on what it is in him that matters.

## Revisionist Readings (major post-Kaufmann moves)

1. **Leiter's naturalism** (2002/2015): Nietzsche as a methodological naturalist continuous with 19th-c. sciences; "morality in the pejorative sense" as target; moral anti-realism.
2. **Reginster's affirmation-of-life thesis** (2006): nihilism is the problem, will-to-power (as will-to-overcoming-resistance) is the structural answer, eternal recurrence is the test.
3. **Shaw's political skepticism** (2007): restoring the political Nietzsche as a theorist of legitimation-crisis in post-religious polities.
4. **Drochon's great-politics reading** (2016): Nietzsche has a coherent aristocratic-European politics, neither Kaufmann's apoliticism nor Nazi appropriation.
5. **Richardson's system + Darwinism** (1996, 2004): Nietzsche has a metaphysical/ontological system, and it is Darwinian in structure.
6. **Pippin's Hegelian/normative reading** (2010): Nietzsche's "psychology" is first philosophy — a theory of the conditions of modern agency, not empirical drive-theory.
7. **Medical revisionism** (Sax 2003, Hemelsoet 2008, Orth & Trimble 2006): the syphilis diagnosis is probably wrong.
8. **Archival revisionism** (Montinari, Schaberg, Diethe): Elisabeth's archive and *Will to Power* are unreliable as witnesses to Nietzsche's own position.
9. **Gemes on agency-freedom** (2006, 2009): higher types achieve a non-metaphysical freedom through drive-integration, against Leiter's strong fatalism.
10. **Holub's nuanced anti-Semitism analysis** (2016): Nietzsche absorbed cultural stereotypes but rejected political anti-Semitism — a more textured account than either apologetic or accusatory readings.
