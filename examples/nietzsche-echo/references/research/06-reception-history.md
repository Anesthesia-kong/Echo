# Agent 6 — Reception History

**Summary (3 sentences).** Nietzsche's reception is unusually load-bearing because his sister Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche, custodian of the archive after his 1889 collapse, edited, suppressed, and fabricated texts — most consequentially the pseudo-book *The Will to Power* — to align his thought with her völkisch-nationalist politics, a distortion that enabled Nazi appropriation and still contaminates popular quotation. The historical Nietzsche who denounced anti-Semitism, German nationalism, and the "herd" was recovered only in stages: Karl Jaspers under the regime, Walter Kaufmann's postwar rehabilitation in English, the French poststructuralists (Deleuze, Foucault, Derrida, Klossowski) who invented a generative "French Nietzsche," analytic naturalists (Clark, Leiter, Reginster, Richardson) who re-argumentized him, and the contemporary political-Nietzsche scholarship (Shaw, Drochon, Dombowsky) that re-exposes the aristocratic edge Kaufmann softened. For an Echo that speaks as Nietzsche, the operative rule is: distinguish the historical Nietzsche from the cultural figure "Nietzsche," flag misattributions, and treat any quotation sourced to *The Will to Power* as provisional at best.

## The Elisabeth Problem (central distortion — flagged first)

[reception] After Nietzsche's January 1889 collapse in Turin, he lived another eleven years incommunicado under the care first of his mother, then (from 1897) of his sister **Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche** (1846–1935). Elisabeth had in 1885 married **Bernhard Förster**, a prominent anti-Semitic agitator and co-founder of the Nueva Germania "Aryan" colony in Paraguay (where Förster killed himself in 1889 amid financial collapse). Nietzsche's published correspondence is explicit in loathing Förster and anti-Semitism as a movement — see the late letters to Overbeck and to Elisabeth, and the "madness notes" of early January 1889 in which he signs "The Crucified" and declares he is having "all anti-Semites shot."

[reception] In 1894 Elisabeth established the **Nietzsche-Archiv**, moving it to Weimar in 1896, and positioned herself as sole authorized editor. She:
- controlled access to manuscripts, Nachlass, and letters;
- edited the first collected works and the correspondence, suppressing or altering passages critical of her husband, of anti-Semitism, and of German nationalism;
- fabricated letters (notably some purportedly between Nietzsche and their mother) and reshaped others;
- courted political power: she received Mussolini's support for the archive in the 1930s, welcomed Hitler's visits (1932, 1934, 1935), and was buried in 1935 with Hitler in attendance. Nietzsche's walking-stick was presented to Mussolini as a gift.

[reception] **The Will to Power (*Der Wille zur Macht*).** Elisabeth, with Peter Gast (Heinrich Köselitz) and others, compiled notebook fragments Nietzsche had not published into a pseudo-book: first edition 1901 (483 sections), expanded canonical edition 1906 (1,067 sections), rearranged in 1911 under a four-part architectonic ("European Nihilism / Critique of Highest Values / Principle of a New Valuation / Discipline and Breeding") that Nietzsche himself had abandoned by August 1888 in favor of the *Revaluation of All Values* project which became *The Antichrist*.

[scholarly-correction] The decisive textual verdict: **there is no book called *The Will to Power* by Nietzsche.** It is an editorial construction from discarded notebooks (KGW VIII / the Colli-Montinari Nachlass), arranged to produce a systematic metaphysics Nietzsche did not write. Mazzino Montinari's essay *"La volontà di potenza" non esiste* (1992; "The Will to Power Does Not Exist") is the standard statement. The Colli-Montinari critical edition (*Kritische Gesamtausgabe*, 1967–), and its German study edition *KSA*, replaced the archive texts as the scholarly standard — but *WP* remains in print and still supplies most popular "Nietzsche quotes" on power, nihilism, and breeding. **Any WP citation should be checked against KSA Nachlass notebook dating and context before being trusted.**

[scholarly-correction] Key reconstructions of the Elisabeth distortion: H. F. Peters, *Zarathustra's Sister* (1977); Ben Macintyre, *Forgotten Fatherland* (1992, on Nueva Germania); Carol Diethe, *Nietzsche's Sister and the Will to Power* (2003); Steven Aschheim, *The Nietzsche Legacy in Germany 1890–1990* (1992), ch. 2 and 8.

## Reception Phase 1: Elisabeth & the Archive (1889–1935)

- **Driver:** Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche; Peter Gast as junior editor.
- **Reading produced:** Nietzsche as prophet of a *völkisch*, aristocratic, German-nationalist new order; author of a suppressed magnum opus on Will to Power as metaphysical principle.
- **Distortions:** text tampering; fabricated letters; the *WP* book; removal of anti-anti-Semitic and anti-German passages; public rituals linking Nietzsche to Italian Fascism and Nazism.
- **Correction:** Colli-Montinari critical edition; recovery of the 1888 letter to Fritz Koegel and to Overbeck; restoration of suppressed correspondence with Franz Overbeck and Heinrich Köselitz in the Briefwechsel edition (Colli-Montinari *Sämtliche Briefe*, KSB).

## Reception Phase 2: Fin-de-siècle & Early 20th c. (1888–1914)

- **Georg Brandes**, Copenhagen lectures on "aristocratic radicalism," spring 1888 — the first academic course on Nietzsche, endorsed by Nietzsche himself in their 1888 correspondence. This is the one reception Nietzsche lived to read and approve; Brandes' phrase "aristocratic radicalism" is Nietzsche-endorsed shorthand.
- **Vitalist / *Lebensphilosophie* reception:** Georg Simmel, *Schopenhauer und Nietzsche* (1907); Ludwig Klages; Oswald Spengler's *Decline of the West* (1918, 1922) absorbs Nietzsche as cultural pathologist.
- **Literary reception:** Stefan George circle; Thomas Mann (*Doctor Faustus*, 1947, but Nietzschean since the early essays); Hermann Hesse; Rainer Maria Rilke; Gottfried Benn; in France André Gide; in Britain W. B. Yeats and D. H. Lawrence; in Russia Andrei Bely, Dmitri Merezhkovsky.
- **American reception:** H. L. Mencken, *The Philosophy of Friedrich Nietzsche* (1908) — first English-language monograph; energetic but superficial, transmitted a combative anti-Christian Nietzsche to American readers.

[reception] This phase fused Nietzsche with vitalism, decadence, and aestheticism. It is where the cultural image of Nietzsche as "prophet of the Übermensch and the death of God" crystallized — largely accurate on tone, heavily selective on content (ignores, e.g., the entire middle-period aphoristic works *Human, All Too Human*; *Daybreak*; *Gay Science* I–IV, which are sober, skeptical, scientific in temper).

## Reception Phase 3: Nazi Appropriation (1920s–1945)

- **Alfred Bäumler**, *Nietzsche der Philosoph und Politiker* (1931), became the semi-official Nazi reading: Nietzsche as philosopher of heroic Will to Power, aligned with the *Volk*, stripped of his individualism and cosmopolitanism. Bäumler explicitly treated the eternal recurrence as a *non-essential* doctrine and cut out what didn't fit.
- **Heinrich Härtle**, *Nietzsche und der Nationalsozialismus* (1937), the Party-line handbook.
- **The archive pipeline:** Elisabeth's Weimar archive functioned as a Nazi shrine; state-sponsored editions; propaganda reproductions of Zarathustra pressed into soldiers' hands.
- **Counter-readings under the regime:**
  - **Karl Jaspers**, *Nietzsche: Einführung in das Verständnis seines Philosophierens* (1936) — read Nietzsche as philosopher of "communication" and of irreducible contradiction rather than of Party doctrine. Published under Nazi censorship; survived because Jaspers' style was dense enough and his claims abstract enough to escape clear rebuke, though Jaspers himself lost his chair in 1937.
  - **Karl Löwith**, *Nietzsches Philosophie der ewigen Wiederkehr des Gleichen* (1935, from exile in Rome) — centered eternal recurrence against Bäumler's dismissal; a decisive counter-move.
  - **Martin Heidegger**'s 1936–40 Freiburg Nietzsche lectures (published 1961) are more ambiguous: they reject Bäumler's crude reading but fold Nietzsche into Heidegger's own history-of-metaphysics narrative ("the last metaphysician"), which is itself a major reception layer.

[scholarly-correction] The Nazi reading required: (a) pretending *WP* was Nietzsche's last word; (b) ignoring his published denunciations of anti-Semitism (*Beyond Good and Evil* §251, *Genealogy* III, the late letters); (c) ignoring his praise of Jews as a type (*Daybreak* §205, *BGE* §250–251, *Antichrist* §24); (d) ignoring his explicit rejection of German nationalism (*Ecce Homo*, "Why I Am So Wise" §3, "The Case of Wagner"). See Walter Kaufmann, *Nietzsche: Philosopher, Psychologist, Antichrist* (1950), ch. 10; and Steven Aschheim, *The Nietzsche Legacy in Germany* (1992), chs. 8–9.

## Reception Phase 4: Postwar Rehabilitation — The Kaufmann Era (1950–1980)

- **Walter Kaufmann**, *Nietzsche: Philosopher, Psychologist, Antichrist* (Princeton, 1950; 4th ed. 1974). Decisive in Anglophone reception. Kaufmann argued:
  1. Nietzsche rejected anti-Semitism, explicitly and consistently.
  2. *The Will to Power* is not a book Nietzsche wrote; his published works are the canon.
  3. The Nazi reading is a falsification, not a legitimate interpretation.
  4. Nietzsche's central concept is *self-overcoming (Selbstüberwindung)* and sublimation, not domination.
- **Kaufmann's translations** (*The Portable Nietzsche*, 1954; *Basic Writings of Nietzsche*, 1968; plus co-translations with R. J. Hollingdale) became the standard English Nietzsche for two generations and still dominate course reading lists.
- **R. J. Hollingdale**, *Nietzsche: The Man and His Philosophy* (1965; rev. 1999), parallel project in Britain.

[scholarly-correction] Later critique of Kaufmann (valid and now standard): he over-domesticated Nietzsche — turned him into a liberal humanist "existentialist" congenial to 1950s American universities, downplayed the *political* and *aristocratic* strains (slavery, "order of rank," the critique of democracy and pity), and softened the rhetoric of cruelty and hardness. The corrective literature: Bruce Detwiler, *Nietzsche and the Politics of Aristocratic Radicalism* (1990); Fredrick Appel, *Nietzsche contra Democracy* (1999); Don Dombowsky, *Nietzsche's Machiavellian Politics* (2004); Hugo Drochon, *Nietzsche's Great Politics* (2016); Tamsin Shaw, *Nietzsche's Political Skepticism* (2007). These do not rehabilitate Bäumler; they argue Kaufmann went too far in the opposite direction.

## Reception Phase 5: French Nietzsche / Poststructuralist Turn (1962–1990s)

- **Gilles Deleuze**, *Nietzsche et la philosophie* (1962): Nietzsche as anti-dialectical philosopher of active/reactive forces, of difference and affirmation, against Hegel. This book reset French continental philosophy.
- **Pierre Klossowski**, *Nietzsche et le cercle vicieux* (1969): the eternal recurrence as a thought that dissolves the subject; Nietzsche as semiotician of the body.
- **Michel Foucault**, "Nietzsche, Genealogy, History" (1971; in *Hommage à Jean Hyppolite*); earlier *Les mots et les choses* (1966). Foucault draws from Nietzsche the method of *genealogy* (descent, emergence, contingency of "truth") and the power/knowledge problematic.
- **Jacques Derrida**, *Éperons: les styles de Nietzsche* (1978): Nietzsche's writing as that which undoes the presence-metaphysics Heidegger pinned on him.
- **Sarah Kofman**, *Nietzsche et la métaphore* (1972).
- **The 1972 Cerisy colloquium** *Nietzsche aujourd'hui?* (proceedings 1973) — consolidated the French Nietzsche as a shared project.

[reception] The French Nietzsche invented concepts: difference, simulacrum, genealogy-as-critique, the death of the author/subject, deconstruction of the logos. Philosophically generative; textually, it often reads Nietzsche through Deleuze or Heidegger rather than through the middle-period aphorisms. The French reception also relied heavily on *WP*-era Nachlass material, inheriting some of the Elisabeth distortion.

## Reception Phase 6: Analytic Nietzsche (1990–present)

- **Maudemarie Clark**, *Nietzsche on Truth and Philosophy* (1990): argues Nietzsche is not a global skeptic about truth; refines the "perspectivism" claim.
- **Brian Leiter**, *Nietzsche on Morality* (2002; 2nd ed. 2015): naturalist reading — Nietzsche as proto-empirical moral psychologist, drives and type-facts, rejecting the "free will" presupposition of morality.
- **Bernard Reginster**, *The Affirmation of Life: Nietzsche on Overcoming Nihilism* (Harvard, 2006): will to power as the will to overcome resistance; definitive analytic treatment of the affirmation/nihilism problem.
- **John Richardson**, *Nietzsche's System* (1996) and *Nietzsche's New Darwinism* (2004): drive psychology, teleology, power as structure.
- **Christopher Janaway**, *Beyond Selflessness: Reading Nietzsche's* Genealogy (2007).
- **Peter Kail**, **Ken Gemes**, **Simon May**, **Paul Katsafanas** (*Agency and the Foundations of Ethics*, 2013).
- Journal venue: *Journal of Nietzsche Studies* (Penn State Press).

[reception] The analytic reception re-argumentizes Nietzsche — treats texts as presenting positions with premises, conclusions, and objections. Its bias: naturalistic, sometimes under-weights the literary and rhetorical dimensions central to the French reading. Together the French and analytic receptions now coexist as the two main scholarly streams.

## Reception Phase 7: Political Nietzsche / Return of the Aristocrat (2000s–present)

- **Don Dombowsky**, *Nietzsche's Machiavellian Politics* (2004); *Nietzsche and Napoleon* (2014).
- **Tamsin Shaw**, *Nietzsche's Political Skepticism* (2007).
- **Hugo Drochon**, *Nietzsche's Great Politics* (Princeton, 2016): reconstructs a coherent Nietzschean political vision of post-democratic European unification under a new aristocratic caste — not fascism, but not liberalism either.
- **Ronald Beiner**, *Dangerous Minds: Nietzsche, Heidegger, and the Return of the Far Right* (2018) — warns of contemporary alt-right appropriations.

[reception] Contemporary **alt-right / "Bronze Age" / Richard Spencer–style** appropriations of Nietzsche structurally parallel the Nazi appropriation: heavy reliance on *WP* notebook fragments, selective Zarathustra reading, ignoring the explicit anti-anti-Semitism and anti-German-nationalism, aestheticization of domination. Scholarly responses: Beiner (above); Ronald Beiner and Sean Illing interviews; the online "Nietzsche and the alt-right" debate in *Journal of Nietzsche Studies* and *The Point*. **The Echo should treat this as a modern instance of the same misappropriation pattern Kaufmann identified in 1950, not as a legitimate interpretation.**

## Reception Phase 8: Popular-Cultural Nietzsche

- **Cinema & fiction:** Liliana Cavani, *Beyond Good and Evil* (*Al di là del bene e del male*, 1977, dramatizing the Nietzsche–Rée–Salomé triangle); *When Nietzsche Wept* (Irvin Yalom novel 1992, film 2007); Thomas Mann's *Doctor Faustus* (1947, Leverkühn as Nietzsche-figure); Kubrick's *2001* opens with Strauss's *Also sprach Zarathustra* — the popular shorthand for "Nietzsche" most viewers know.
- **Self-help / motivational Nietzsche:** "that which does not kill me..." as corporate-wall poster; posts attributing Stoic, Buddhist, and anonymous self-help lines to Nietzsche.
- **"Edgy teenager Nietzsche":** Nietzsche-as-anti-religion-hot-take, generally relying on *Twilight of the Idols* "How the True World Finally Became a Fable" and *Antichrist* without the aphoristic ironies; loses the self-overcoming and affirmation dimensions.
- **Academic-popular bridge:** Rüdiger Safranski, *Nietzsche: A Philosophical Biography* (2000, Eng. 2002); Sue Prideaux, *I Am Dynamite!* (2018).

## Major Misattributions (user-facing quote catalog)

[misattribution] Nietzsche is among the most-misquoted philosophers in English. Rule for the Echo: if a line isn't locatable in the published works (or a dated notebook entry in KSA), assume it is not his.

### Actually Nietzsche (often misremembered)

- **"What does not kill me makes me stronger."** — Real. *Twilight of the Idols*, "Maxims and Arrows" §8: *"Aus der Kriegsschule des Lebens. — Was mich nicht umbringt, macht mich stärker."* ("From life's school of war: What does not kill me makes me stronger.") Note the context: it is a maxim about the hardness of life as training, not self-help perseverance porn.
- **"God is dead."** — Real. First in *The Gay Science* §108 (1882) and most famously §125 ("The Madman"); also *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* Prologue §2 and Part IV. Context: *"Gott ist todt! Gott bleibt todt! Und wir haben ihn getödtet!"* — a diagnosis of what modern Europeans have done, voiced through a madman whom the crowd doesn't understand, not a triumphant slogan.
- **"Without music, life would be a mistake."** — Real. *Twilight of the Idols*, "Maxims and Arrows" §33: *"Ohne Musik wäre das Leben ein Irrthum."*
- **"He who fights with monsters should look to it that he himself does not become a monster. And when you gaze long into an abyss, the abyss also gazes into you."** — Real. *Beyond Good and Evil* §146.
- **"There are no facts, only interpretations."** — Real but qualified. Nachlass, KSA 12:7[60] (1886–87): *"Gegen den Positivismus, welcher bei den Phänomenen stehen bleibt 'es giebt nur Thatsachen', würde ich sagen: nein, gerade Thatsachen giebt es nicht, nur Interpretationen."* — a *Nachlass* (notebook) remark, not published. Often stripped of its anti-positivist context and quoted as blanket relativism.
- **"Become who you are."** — Real. *Ecce Homo* subtitle *"Wie man wird, was man ist"* ("How One Becomes What One Is"); *The Gay Science* §270 "What does your conscience say? — 'You shall become the person you are.'" Originally from Pindar, *Pythian* 2.72.
- **"The higher we soar, the smaller we appear to those who cannot fly."** — Real. *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* Part III, "Of the Spirit of Gravity" §1 (close paraphrase).

### NOT Nietzsche (common misattributions to flag)

[misattribution] **"When you stare into the abyss, the abyss stares back."** — Garbled. The actual BGE §146 is a conditional ("*when* you gaze long into an abyss..."), not the punchy declarative often quoted; the abyss line is also commonly stripped of the preceding monsters clause, changing the meaning from "moral risk of struggle" to "cosmic horror."

[misattribution] **"In heaven, all the interesting people are missing." / "All truly great thoughts are conceived while walking."** — The second one is roughly his. *Twilight*, "Maxims and Arrows" §34: *"Nur die ergangenen Gedanken haben Werth"* ("Only thoughts reached by walking have value"). Usually paraphrased into forms he did not write.

[misattribution] **"You have your way. I have my way. As for the right way, the correct way, and the only way, it does not exist."** — Not Nietzsche. A widely circulated fabrication/paraphrase; no source in the published works or KSA Nachlass. Loosely echoes *Zarathustra* III "Of the Spirit of Gravity" §2 ("'This is my way; where is yours?' — thus I answered those who asked me 'the way.' For the way — that does not exist"), but the circulating English version is a rewrite, not a translation.

[misattribution] **"It is not a lack of love, but a lack of friendship that makes unhappy marriages."** — Real. *Human, All Too Human* §378. (Included here because it circulates often without citation and is sometimes doubted.)

[misattribution] **"And those who were seen dancing were thought to be insane by those who could not hear the music."** — **Not Nietzsche.** No source anywhere in KSA. Common misattribution; origin unclear (possibly a paraphrase of Angela Monet or a modern fabrication).

[misattribution] **"There is always some madness in love. But there is also always some reason in madness."** — Real. *Thus Spoke Zarathustra* Part I, "On Reading and Writing."

[misattribution] **"Without music, life would be an error / a mistake."** — Real (see above). Both translations circulate.

[misattribution] **"He who has a why to live for can bear almost any how."** — Real. *Twilight of the Idols*, "Maxims and Arrows" §12. Often attributed to Viktor Frankl, who *quoted* it from Nietzsche in *Man's Search for Meaning*.

[misattribution] **"The individual has always had to struggle to keep from being overwhelmed by the tribe."** — **Not Nietzsche.** This is from Friedrich *Nietzsche*-attributed lists online; no KSA source. (Often traced to a misquote in an early 20th-century American periodical.)

[misattribution] **"We have art in order not to die of the truth."** — Real but *Nachlass*. KSA 13:16[40] (1888): *"Wir haben die Kunst, damit wir nicht an der Wahrheit zugrunde gehen."* Published only posthumously; often quoted as if from *The Birth of Tragedy*, which it is not.

[misattribution] **"Blessed are the forgetful, for they get the better even of their blunders."** — Real. *Beyond Good and Evil* §217.

[misattribution] **"To live is to suffer, to survive is to find meaning in the suffering."** — **Not Nietzsche.** Frankl-adjacent / internet fabrication. No KSA source.

[misattribution] **"I'm not upset that you lied to me, I'm upset that from now on I can't believe you."** — Real. *Beyond Good and Evil* §183.

[misattribution] **"One must still have chaos in oneself to give birth to a dancing star."** — Real. *Thus Spoke Zarathustra*, Prologue §5.

[misattribution] **"Whoever despises himself, nonetheless respects himself as one who despises."** — Real. *Beyond Good and Evil* §78.

[misattribution] **"Hope is the worst of evils, for it prolongs the torments of man."** — Real. *Human, All Too Human* §71 ("Hope").

[misattribution] **"Plato is boring."** — Real, and usually used to illustrate the distance between published Nietzsche and sanitized Nietzsche. *Twilight*, "What I Owe to the Ancients" §2.

[misattribution] **"Whoever fights monsters..." quoted as a standalone motivational line.** — The philosophical force is in the second half ("the abyss gazes back"), a warning against becoming what one fights; stripping the second half, as motivational quotations often do, inverts the sense.

### Rule of thumb the Echo should apply

1. If cited to *The Will to Power*: flag as "posthumous notebook fragment, editorially arranged by Elisabeth Förster-Nietzsche; treat as evidence of what Nietzsche was thinking about, not as a settled position."
2. If not locatable in the published works or KSA Nachlass: **do not assert as Nietzsche's.** Either attribute to the paraphraser or decline.
3. If real but from the Nachlass: say so. Nietzsche himself ruthlessly revised; published ≠ notebook.

## Corrections — Quick Reference

| Claim in popular culture | Historical correction |
|---|---|
| Nietzsche was an anti-Semite. | False. Explicit denunciations: *BGE* §251, *GM* III, *AC* §24, many 1886–89 letters. His break with Wagner was partly over Wagner's anti-Semitism. |
| Nietzsche was a proto-Nazi / German nationalist. | False. *Ecce Homo*, "Why I Am So Wise" §3: denies he is German; "The Case of Wagner" attacks German culture; repeatedly calls himself a "good European." |
| Nietzsche wrote *The Will to Power*. | False. Editorial compilation by Elisabeth & Peter Gast from 1901/1906, from notebooks Nietzsche did not publish and whose architecture he had abandoned. |
| Nietzsche endorsed domination / slavery straightforwardly. | Contested. He wrote in praise of "order of rank" and critiqued democracy and pity; he also insisted on self-overcoming and sublimation. The right level: he is an aristocratic radical (Brandes), not a totalitarian. See Drochon, Shaw. |
| Nietzsche was a nihilist. | False in his self-understanding. He *diagnoses* nihilism as European cultural condition and attempts to *overcome* it via revaluation and affirmation (amor fati, eternal recurrence as selective test). See Reginster, *The Affirmation of Life*. |
| Nietzsche went mad from syphilis. | Uncertain. The 1889 collapse was long attributed to tertiary syphilis; recent studies (Orth, Trimble 2006; Sax 2003) propose alternatives including CADASIL, frontotemporal dementia, meningioma. No consensus; do not assert syphilis as fact. |
| "Übermensch" means a biological super-race. | False as Nietzsche uses it. In *Zarathustra* it is a stage beyond the "last man," a self-overcoming type, not a racial category. Racialization is Elisabeth + Bäumler + Nazi period. |
