# Socrates — Primary Works (via Plato)

## Framing: The Socratic Problem as Design Choice

Socrates wrote nothing. All surviving philosophical content attributed to him is mediated by reporters — primarily **Plato** (~30 dialogues), secondarily **Xenophon**, with additional data points from **Aristophanes** (*Clouds* 423 BCE) and **Aristotle** (*Metaphysics*, *Nicomachean Ethics*). This echo uses **Plato's Socrates** as the source tradition — specifically the dialectical Socrates of the early and early-middle dialogues.

The "Socratic problem" is the question of how much of Plato's Socrates reflects the historical man versus Plato's own developing philosophy. This echo does not solve it; it chooses a layer and declares the choice.

## 1. Dialogue Chronology

Standard tripartite grouping (Vlastos, Kahn):

### Early / "Socratic" dialogues (likely closest to historical Socrates)
- **Apology** (Socrates's defense speech, 399 BCE dramatic date)
- **Crito** (prison, refusal to escape)
- **Euthyphro** (piety — aporetic)
- **Laches** (courage — aporetic)
- **Charmides** (temperance/sophrosyne — aporetic)
- **Lysis** (friendship — aporetic)
- **Euthydemus** (eristic sophists exposed)
- **Hippias Minor** / **Hippias Major** (disputed authorship for Major)
- **Ion** (rhapsody, inspiration)
- **Protagoras** (sophists, unity of virtue)
- **Gorgias** (rhetoric vs philosophy; better to suffer than do wrong)
- **Meno** (virtue, recollection — transitional)
- **Republic Book I** (justice — aporetic; likely written separately from Books II–X)

### Middle (Plato using Socrates as mouthpiece for Platonic metaphysics)
- **Phaedo** (soul's immortality, Forms, death scene)
- **Symposium** (eros, Diotima's ladder)
- **Republic II–X** (Forms, tripartite soul, philosopher-king)
- **Phaedrus** (soul-chariot, rhetoric, writing)

### Late (Socrates recedes or is replaced by other speakers)
- **Theaetetus** (knowledge; Socrates as midwife)
- **Parmenides** (Forms under critique)
- **Sophist, Statesman, Timaeus, Critias, Philebus, Laws** (Eleatic Stranger, Athenian Stranger replace Socrates)

### Scholarly layering positions
- **Vlastos (1991)**: Socrates_E (early) vs Socrates_M (middle/late). Ten distinguishing theses.
- **Kahn (1996)**: Plato is always Plato; "early" dialogues are proleptic (preparatory) for the mature philosophy. No historical-window view.
- **Brickhouse & Smith (1994)**: Plato (especially early) as best available source with cautious reconstruction.
- **Nails (2002, prosopography)**: many dramatic dates fictional; corpus is literary, not reportage.

## 2. Core Themes Recurring Across the Corpus

### (a) Elenchus — refutation as method
[trained-knowledge: Vlastos 1983 "The Socratic Elenchus"; Benson 2000]
Four-step pattern: elicit thesis *p* → secure premises *q, r* → derive ¬*p* from *q, r* → aporia. [documented across Euthyphro, Laches, Charmides, Lysis, Meno, Gorgias, Republic I]

### (b) Aporia — productive impasse
[Meno 80a–b (torpedo fish); Euthyphro 15c–e; Laches 200e–201c; Charmides 175a–176a]
Dialogues end without positive resolution. Not failure — the recognition of ignorance is the precondition for inquiry (Meno 84a–c). [documented]

### (c) Virtue is knowledge (arete = episteme)
[Protagoras 349b–360e; Meno 87c–89a; Laches 194d]
The unity-of-virtues thesis: courage, piety, temperance, justice are not separate but aspects of a single epistemic state. [documented]

### (d) No one errs willingly (oudeis hekon hamartanei)
[Protagoras 345d–e, 358c–d; Gorgias 468c, 509e; Meno 77b–78b]
Corollary of (c): all vice is ignorance. If you truly knew the good, you would do it. [documented]
Aristotle rejects this (EN VII.3) — "contradicts the phenomena" of akrasia.

### (e) Care for the soul (epimeleia tes psyches)
[Apology 29d–30b]
The supreme ethical duty. Money, reputation, body — secondary. [documented]

### (f) Human wisdom as knowledge of ignorance
[Apology 21d, 23a–b]
Not "I know that I know nothing" (Renaissance simplification) but: wisdom consists in not thinking one knows what one does not know. Second-order epistemic humility, compatible with robust first-order moral convictions. [documented]

### (g) The examined life
[Apology 38a]
"ho de anexetastos bios ou biotos anthropo" — the unexamined life is not worth living. [documented]

### (h) Better to suffer wrong than to do wrong
[Gorgias 469b–c, 473a, 508b–e; Crito 49a–d]
Firm conviction survives elenchus. Becomes Socratic ethical core. [documented]

### (i) Craft-analogy for ethics (techne)
[Gorgias 464b–466a; Laches 184e–185a; Protagoras 311b–e; Republic I 332c–334b]
Medicine, navigation, shoemaking, horse-training — models for what knowledge-of-the-good would look like. [documented]

## 3. Self-Coined / Signature Concepts

### Elenchus (ἔλεγχος)
"Testing, refutation." Socrates's primary method — not a technical term he invents but one he makes his own. [documented]

### Maieutic / midwifery (μαιευτική)
[Theaetetus 149a–151d]
Socrates as midwife — barren himself, delivers others' ideas, tests for "wind-eggs" (fake offspring). Imagery drawn from his mother Phaenarete, who (per Theaetetus 149a) was a midwife. [documented]

### Daimonion (δαιμόνιον / "divine something")
[Apology 31c–d, 40a–c; Euthyphro 3b; Theaetetus 151a; Phaedrus 242b–c; Republic 496c]
A private divine sign from childhood that **only dissuades, never commands**. Silence = implicit assent. Key to the "impiety" charge at his trial. [documented]

### Gadfly / myops (μύωψ)
[Apology 30e]
Self-image: Athens as a large, sluggish horse; he is the gadfly who keeps it awake. Bound to irritate. [documented]

### Socratic irony (eironeia)
Not his term — his characteristic style, named by Thrasymachus at Republic 337a and by later tradition. Vlastos 1991 distinguishes:
- **Simple irony**: straightforward dissimulation (traditional reading)
- **Complex irony** (Vlastos's contribution): false in one sense, true in another — both the disclaimed and the implied position hold, at different levels
[documented Platonic use at Rep. 337a; trained-knowledge on typology: Vlastos 1991; Nehamas 1998]

### Protreptic (προτρεπτικός)
Not always called this in Plato, but the turning-toward-philosophy mode is distinctive [Apology 29d–30b; Euthydemus 278e–282d; Clitophon (disputed authorship)]. [attested]

## 4. Teachers and Influences Plato's Socrates Names

- **Diotima of Mantinea** [Symposium 201d–212c] — teacher of erotics, source of the ascent-of-love speech. Debated: historical figure, literary creation, or cultic figure? [attributed / legendary]
- **Prodicus of Ceos** [Protagoras 341a; Meno 96d; Cratylus 384b; Charmides 163d] — sophist whose lectures on precise word-use Socrates mock-praises. [attested as contemporary]
- **Anaxagoras** [Phaedo 97b–99d] — whose book Socrates reports reading eagerly in youth, then abandoning when it gave mechanical rather than teleological causes. Autobiographical marker of the "second sailing." [documented]
- **Parmenides** [Parmenides, dramatic date in Socrates's youth] — Plato shows young Socrates corrected by Parmenides. Historicity of meeting debated. [attributed]
- **Aspasia of Miletus** [Menexenus 235e] — credited by Socrates as teacher of rhetoric (ironically?). [attested as contemporary figure, Pericles's consort]
- **"The sophists"** (collectively) — engaged, mocked, but taken seriously: Protagoras, Gorgias, Hippias, Thrasymachus, Callicles (whose historicity is debated). [attested]

## 6. The Apology as Closest-to-Historical Document

[trained-knowledge: Reeve 1989; Brickhouse & Smith 1989; de Strycker & Slings 1994]

The *Apology* (Socrates's defense speech at his trial, 399 BCE) is the most plausibly historical single document in the corpus, for several reasons:
- Short (≈15 pages in Cooper); speech, not dialogue
- Dramatic setting is public (500 jurors); many witnesses
- Published soon after 399 when witnesses still alive
- Xenophon's independent *Apology of Socrates* overlaps on core content (the daimonion, the Delphic oracle, the three charges, the death-penalty vote)

Key passages for voice reconstruction:
- 17a–18a — anti-rhetorical opening
- 20e–21d — Delphic oracle + human wisdom
- 28e–30b — care of the soul; divine station
- 30e–31a — the gadfly
- 31c–32e — the daimonion; political refusals (Arginusae, Leon of Salamis)
- 36d–37a — Prytaneum counter-proposal
- 38a — unexamined life
- 39c–d — prophecy to the jurors
- 40a–41d — death as blessing or dreamless sleep
- 42a — farewell

## 7. Reading Order for an Echo

Priority for voice distillation:
1. **Apology** — bedrock self-portrait
2. **Crito** — ethical reasoning under pressure
3. **Euthyphro** — elenchus on piety (the charge against him)
4. **Laches** — elenchus on courage, with craft-analogies
5. **Charmides** — elenchus on temperance
6. **Lysis** — elenchus on friendship
7. **Protagoras** — engagement with the sophists, virtue-unity
8. **Gorgias** — longer engagement, rhetoric vs philosophy, "better to suffer"
9. **Meno** — transitional; recollection; torpedo fish
10. **Republic I** — aporetic justice investigation
11. **Phaedo** (death scene at 115a–118a only) — end of life
12. **Symposium** (Alcibiades's speech, 215a–222b) — how he appeared to those who loved him

For *Plato's* philosophy (not the echo): Symposium's Diotima speech, Republic II–X, Phaedo immortality arguments, Phaedrus — but these are Plato speaking through Socrates.

## 8. Preserved Contradictions

- **Vlastos vs Kahn**: Is there a distinguishable Socrates_E? Unresolved.
- **Does Socrates teach?** Apology 33a–b says no, he has no students. Theaetetus 150c–151a says he is a midwife for others' ideas. [documented]
- **The daimonion**: a real divine sign (McPherran 1996) or a literary device / conscience-metaphor?
- **Diotima**: historical woman, cultic priestess, or Plato's creation?
- **Akrasia**: Socrates denies weakness of will (Protagoras 358b–c); Aristotle (EN VII.2–3) says this is absurd.
- **Positive doctrine vs only-elenchus**: Early dialogues end in aporia, yet Socrates repeatedly asserts "better to suffer than do wrong" (Gorgias) and "virtue is knowledge" as if known. [scholarly debate]
- **Plato's early dialogues**: Socratic witness (Vlastos) or Platonic proleptic (Kahn)?
