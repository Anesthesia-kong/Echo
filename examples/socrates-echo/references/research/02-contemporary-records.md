# Socrates — Contemporary Records

Research dimension: the near-contemporary and early-post-contemporary sources that let us reconstruct the historical Socrates (c. 470/469–399 BCE, Athens), who wrote nothing himself.

**Methodological preface (the "Socratic problem"):** Every claim about Socrates is mediated by a portrait with its own agenda. Four principal ancient witnesses — Aristophanes (hostile, comic, 423 BCE), Xenophon (admiring, practical, apologetic, often absent from Athens), Plato (devoted, philosophically transformative, increasingly using "Socrates" as mouthpiece), and Aristotle (one generation later, doxographic) — disagree. Modern scholars (Vlastos, Nails, Kahn, McPherran, Waterfield, Cartledge) have largely abandoned hope of a single "historical Socrates" in favor of tracking which layer of the tradition each datum belongs to.

## Xenophon

1. **Four Socratic works.** *Memorabilia* (four books of conversations), *Apology of Socrates*, *Symposium*, *Oeconomicus* (household management as Socratic dialogue). The non-Platonic "second voice." [documented] [trained-knowledge: Dorion/Bandini Budé ed. 2000–2011; SEP "Xenophon"]

2. **Practical and pious Socrates.** Concrete ethical advice on friendship, self-control (enkrateia), generalship, estate management. Less aporetic than Plato's Socrates; more positive doctrine; more conventionally religious — sacrifices, oracles, traditional piety. [attested] [trained-knowledge: Dorion 2013 *L'Autre Socrate*; Vander Waerdt 1994 *The Socratic Movement*]

3. **Xenophon absent for the late career.** Joined Cyrus the Younger 401 BCE (*Anabasis*), exiled, did not witness the trial. *Apology* and *Memorabilia* composed years/decades after 399, leaning on Hermogenes and other informants. Biggest single bias. [modern-scholarly-inference] [trained-knowledge: Waterfield 2004 *Xenophon's Retreat*; Nails 2002 *People of Plato* s.v. Xenophon]

4. **Apologetic framing.** *Memorabilia* I.1–2 explicitly rebuts the accusers, especially the pamphleteer Polycrates (c. 393 BCE) whose lost *Accusation of Socrates* shaped the post-trial controversy. Defense-driven episode selection, not biography. [modern-scholarly-inference] [trained-knowledge: Chroust 1957; Dorion 2013]

## Aristophanes

5. **Clouds (Νεφέλαι) 423 BCE — earliest surviving portrait.** Socrates alive in the audience (surviving text is the partially revised c. 418 version). Socrates heads a Thinkery (φροντιστήριον), teaches Strepsiades debt-dodging via "Worse Argument," studies gnat anatomy and flea-jumps, is hoisted in a basket to study the sun, denies Olympians for Clouds/Vortex (Δῖνος). [documented] [trained-knowledge: Dover ed. *Clouds* 1968; Nails 2002]

6. **Hostile composite portrait.** Fuses genuine Socratic traits (poverty, barefoot, questioning, circle of young men) with features of figures Socrates actually opposed — natural philosophers (Anaxagoras, Diogenes of Apollonia) and fee-taking sophists (Protagoras, Prodicus). Plato *Apology* 18a–d blames "the old accusers," Aristophanes first, for decades of juror prejudice. [attested] [trained-knowledge: Dover 1968; Nussbaum 1980 "Aristophanes and Socrates"]

7. **Residual historical value.** The caricature only lands if the core image was recognizable in 423 BCE. Strong contemporary attestation for: physical oddity, poverty, dress/shoe indifference, young-men-circle, relentless questioning, intellectual reputation. Natural-philosophy and fee-taking are comic distortion. [modern-scholarly-inference] [trained-knowledge: Kahn 1996 *Plato and the Socratic Dialogue*; McPherran 1996 *The Religion of Socrates*]

## The Trial (399 BCE)

8. **Formal charges (γραφή).** Meletus (minor poet, nominal prosecutor), Anytus (democratic politician/tanner, son-related grievance), Lycon (rhetor). Indictment preserved by Diogenes Laertius II.40 via Favorinus citing the Metroon archive: *Socrates does injustice by not believing in the gods the city believes in, and by introducing other new divinities (καινὰ δαιμόνια); and he does injustice by corrupting the young. Penalty: death.* [documented] [trained-knowledge: D.L. II.40; Brickhouse & Smith 1989 *Socrates on Trial*]

9. **Vote and execution.** Jury ~500; narrow conviction (Plato *Apology* 36a — 30-vote swing would have acquitted; often reconstructed 280–220). After the defiant counter-proposal (upkeep in the Prytaneum, then a token fine), the death-penalty vote widened. ~30-day delay for the Delian sacred embassy; refusal of Crito's escape plan; hemlock (κώνειον) in the state prison. [attested] [trained-knowledge: Plato *Apology*, *Crito*, *Phaedo*; Waterfield 2009 *Why Socrates Died*]

10. **Modern political reading (Waterfield, Cartledge).** The trial is inseparable from post-war Athenian trauma: Peloponnesian War ends 404 with Spartan victory and the Thirty Tyrants; 403 democratic restoration carries an amnesty forbidding prosecution for pre-403 political crimes. Waterfield (2009) and Cartledge (2009, *Ancient Greek Political Thought in Practice*) argue Socrates was a symbolic scapegoat for his association with Critias and Alcibiades, via the religious-legal workaround of "impiety." [modern-scholarly-inference] [trained-knowledge: Waterfield 2009; Cartledge 2009; Stone 1988 *The Trial of Socrates*]

## Military Service

11. **Three campaigns.** (a) **Potidaea** 432–429 BCE — Plato *Symposium* 219e–220e (Alcibiades) has Socrates save Alcibiades' life and cede the prize of valor; (b) **Delium** 424 BCE — *Symposium* 221a–b, *Laches* 181b, composed retreat with Laches; (c) **Amphipolis** 422 BCE — *Apology* 28e. Hoplite status places him in the property census. Ages ~38–48. [attested] [trained-knowledge: Plato *Symposium*, *Apology*, *Laches*; Nails 2002 s.v. Socrates]

12. **Endurance anecdotes.** *Symposium* 220a–d: motionless thought at Potidaea for a day and night; cold endurance barefoot; out-drinking everyone without effect. Eulogistic framing, but the *Clouds* caricature independently notes hardiness, so most scholars (Nails, Kahn, McPherran) treat the reputation for ἐγκράτεια as genuine. [attributed] [trained-knowledge: Nails 2002; Kahn 1996]

## Family and Appearance

13. **Family.** Father **Sophroniscus** (traditionally stonemason/sculptor — λιθουργός — but Nails 2002 flags this as possibly a later inference from Socrates' joke about Daedalus as ancestor); mother **Phaenarete** (traditionally midwife — μαῖα — attested only via Plato *Theaetetus* 149a, possibly a philosophical metaphor). Wife **Xanthippe** (*Phaedo* 60a; *Memorabilia* II.2); disputed second wife/concubine **Myrto** (Aristotle fragment via Aristoxenus — Nails argues against). Three sons at death: **Lamprocles** (adolescent), **Sophroniscus**, **Menexenus** (both small). [attested]; parental-trade details [legendary] [trained-knowledge: Nails 2002 entries Socrates, Xanthippe, Lamprocles, Myrto]

14. **Appearance.** Snub-nosed (σιμός), bulging eyes, thick lips, paunchy, bald, barefoot, single cloak year-round. **Silenus/satyr comparison** — Alcibiades in Plato *Symposium* 215a–b, 221d–e: ugly like silenus-figurine shop statues, divine virtues concealed within. Independently echoed in Xenophon *Symposium* 4–5, where Socrates mock-defends his features as functionally superior. [attested] [trained-knowledge: Plato *Symposium*; Xenophon *Symposium*; Zanker 1995 *The Mask of Socrates*]

## Students and Associates

15. **The Socratic circle (οἱ Σωκρατικοί) produced divergent lineages** — itself evidence of an aporetic, non-doctrinal method: **Plato** (Academy); **Xenophon** (practical ethics); **Antisthenes** (ascetic, proto-Cynic → Diogenes → Stoics); **Aristippus of Cyrene** (hedonist — Cyrenaic); **Euclides of Megara** (logic/eristic — Megarian); **Phaedo of Elis** (Elian). Also **Alcibiades** (brilliant, treasonous general) and **Critias** (leader of the Thirty). One teacher producing both Antisthenes and Aristippus is itself diagnostic. [documented, as intellectual-historical fact] [trained-knowledge: Kahn 1996; Nails 2002; Diogenes Laertius II–VI]

## Political Episodes (the "three refusals")

16. **Arginusae, 406 BCE.** Serving as prytanis, Socrates alone resisted the unconstitutional en-bloc trial of the eight generals who had failed to recover shipwrecked crews after victory. Six generals were executed. [attested] [trained-knowledge: *Apology* 32b; *Memorabilia* I.1.18]

17. **Leon of Salamis, 404 BCE.** Under the Thirty, Socrates and four others were ordered to arrest Leon for summary execution and property confiscation. Socrates went home; the others complied. [attested] [trained-knowledge: *Apology* 32c–d; Plato *Letter* VII (disputed); *Memorabilia* IV.4.3] Evidence he was not an oligarchic collaborator — but also did not publicly denounce the regime.

18. **Refusal to escape (Crito, 399 BCE).** Escape arranged and funded; Socrates refused, arguing (in Plato's *Crito*) that the Laws of Athens had a quasi-parental claim. That he refused is near-universally accepted; whether the argument is historically Socratic or Platonic reconstruction is debated. [documented (the refusal) / attributed (the argument)] [trained-knowledge: Brickhouse & Smith 2004 *Plato and the Trial of Socrates*; Kraut 1984 *Socrates and the State*]

## Aristotle

19. **Aristotle (384–322 BCE)** knew Socrates only secondhand via the Academy, but he treats Plato's Socrates as a source to disagree with. **Metaphysics** M.4 (1078b17–32) and A.6 (987b1–6) credit Socrates with two real contributions — inductive arguments (ἐπακτικοὶ λόγοι) and universal definition (τὸ ὁρίζεσθαι καθόλου) — and state that Socrates did *not* separate the universals, whereas Plato did. Principal ancient basis for distinguishing the historical Socrates from Plato's theory of Forms. [documented] [trained-knowledge: Ross ed. *Metaphysics*; Vlastos 1991 *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher*]

20. **Socratic intellectualism.** Virtue is knowledge; no one errs willingly (οὐδεὶς ἑκὼν ἁμαρτάνει); weakness of will (ἀκρασία) is impossible because to know the good is to do it. Attributed to **Socrates (not Plato)** by Aristotle in **Nicomachean Ethics** VII.2–3 (1145b21–27), where Aristotle rejects it. Doxographic anchor for isolating a distinctively Socratic ethical position. [documented] [trained-knowledge: Vlastos 1991 ch. 2; Penner 1992 "Socrates and the Early Dialogues" in CHCP]

## Preserved contradictions

- **Xenophon's Socrates ≠ Plato's Socrates.** Xenophon: concrete positive advice, sacrifices, oracles, conventional piety. Plato: elenchus, aporia, professed ignorance, a daimonion that only forbids. Each probably abstracts one side of a genuinely complex figure. [modern-scholarly-inference]
- **Aristophanes' "natural philosopher" Socrates ≠ Plato's Socrates.** *Apology* 19c denies he ever studied τὰ μετέωρα; *Phaedo* 96a–100a narrates a youthful natural-philosophical phase he abandoned. Aristophanes may preserve memory of an early phase Plato's Socrates disowned. [modern-scholarly-inference] [trained-knowledge: Kahn 1996; McPherran 1996]
- **The δαιμόνιον.** Prominent in Plato (*Apology* 31d, *Phaedrus* 242b, *Euthyphro* 3b) and in Xenophon (*Memorabilia* I.1.2–5, *Apology* 12–13), where Xenophon tries to normalize it as conventional divination. The divergence is itself evidence the sign was historically real and controversial enough that both apologists had to address it. [modern-scholarly-inference] [trained-knowledge: McPherran 1996]
