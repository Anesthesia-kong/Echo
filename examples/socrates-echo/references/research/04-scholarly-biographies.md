# Socrates — Scholarly Biographies and Monographs

**Summary:** Modern Socrates scholarship is structured above all by the "Socratic Problem" — how (or whether) to extract the historical Socrates from the discrepant portraits in Plato, Xenophon, Aristophanes, and Aristotle. Since Vlastos's *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher* (1991) the field has been split between developmentalists who treat Plato's early dialogues as reasonably Socratic (Vlastos; Brickhouse & Smith; Benson; Penner; Irwin) and unitarians/literary skeptics who insist Plato is always Plato (Kahn 1996; Annas; Rowe). Substantive controversies continue over Socratic irony (simple vs. Vlastos's "complex irony"; Nehamas's unparaphrasable irony), the *daimonion* (genuine piety vs. metaphor for conscience), Socratic intellectualism (strong Penner/early-Vlastos vs. Brickhouse & Smith's 2010 moderated reading), and whether the 399 BCE trial was primarily religious or — as Waterfield (2009), Stone (1988), and Cartledge (2009) argue — a political reckoning for Socrates's association with Critias and the Thirty Tyrants, prosecuted under the 403 amnesty via the impiety workaround. Post-2000 trends (Nails's prosopography; McPherran's religious Socrates; Long's fragmented Socratic tradition; Dorion's rehabilitation of Xenophon) push back against both the old secular-rationalist Socrates and the "only elenchus, only aporia" Socrates.

## 1. The Socratic Problem

Ancient sources in conflict:
- **Plato** (c. 427–347 BCE): ~30 dialogues with Socrates as speaker [primary]
- **Xenophon** (c. 430–354 BCE): *Memorabilia*, *Apology*, *Symposium*, *Oeconomicus* — practical, conventional Socrates [primary]
- **Aristophanes**, *Clouds* (423 BCE): sophist-natural-philosopher caricature [primary but comic]
- **Aristotle** (384–322 BCE): *Metaphysics* I.6, XIII.4; *Nicomachean Ethics* VI–VII [second-hand but authoritative via Academy oral tradition]

### Four positions on the Socratic Problem

**(a) Developmentalist / Vlastosian orthodoxy (~1970–2000 dominance)**
- **Gregory Vlastos, *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher* (Cambridge 1991)** [scholarly-consensus as landmark; contested on detail]. Distinguishes **Socrates_E** (early "Socratic" dialogues: *Apology, Crito, Euthyphro, Laches, Charmides, Lysis, Euthydemus, Protagoras, Gorgias, Hippias Minor, Ion, Rep. I*) from **Socrates_M** (middle dialogues: *Phaedo, Rep. II–X, Symposium, Phaedrus*). Famous **ten theses** (ch. 2) systematically enumerate differences, including:
  1. Socrates_E = exclusively moral philosopher; Socrates_M = metaphysics, epistemology, politics, art
  2. No Theory of Forms in Socrates_E
  3. No tripartite soul in Socrates_E
  4. Socrates_E denies akrasia (intellectualism); Socrates_M allows it via soul-parts
  5. Socrates_E's method = elenchus; Socrates_M's = hypothetical/dialectical ascent
  6. Socrates_E's religion = personal piety + daimonion; Socrates_M = mystical metaphysical theology
  7. Socrates_E disclaims knowledge; Socrates_M possesses it
- **Terence Irwin, *Plato's Moral Theory* (1977), *Plato's Ethics* (1995)** [Irwin]: pre-Vlastos developmentalist, reconstructs early dialogues as coherent instrumentalist-eudaimonist ethical theory later modified by Plato.

**(b) Literary-unitarian rejection (Kahn)**
- **Charles H. Kahn, *Plato and the Socratic Dialogue* (Cambridge 1996)** [Kahn]. Core thesis: Plato is always *Plato*. Early-middle-late division is a 19th-century stylometric artefact. The "early" dialogues are **proleptic** — pedagogical introductions to Platonism for readers not yet ready for the metaphysics. Therefore they cannot be a privileged historical window onto Socrates_E. [contested — Brickhouse & Smith, Benson, Reeve dissent]
- **Julia Annas, *Platonic Ethics, Old and New* (Cornell 1999)** [Annas]: sympathetic to Kahn; reconstructs continuous Platonic ethical project.
- **Christopher Rowe, *Plato and the Art of Philosophical Writing* (Cambridge 2007)** [Rowe]: strongest post-Kahn unitarian; every dialogue rhetorically engineered for its dramatic situation.

**(c) Core-reliable-witness middle position**
- **Thomas C. Brickhouse and Nicholas D. Smith**: *Socrates on Trial* (Princeton 1989); *Plato's Socrates* (Oxford 1994); *The Philosophy of Socrates* (Westview 2000); *The Trial and Execution of Socrates* (Oxford 2002); *Socrates' Divine Sign* (2005); *Socratic Moral Psychology* (Cambridge 2010) [Brickhouse & Smith]. Plato (esp. early dialogues) as best source, with caution; more restrained than Vlastos on doctrinal specifics.
- **C.D.C. Reeve, *Socrates in the Apology* (Hackett 1989)** [Reeve]: treats *Apology* as best single source for Socrates's self-understanding; reconstructs "human wisdom" as awareness of non-expertise.

**(d) Prosopographical / cautious approach**
- **Debra Nails, *The People of Plato* (Hackett 2002)** [Nails; scholarly-consensus as reference tool]. Comprehensive biographical register of every named person in Platonic/Xenophontic corpora; constrains historical reconstructions by showing many Platonic dramatic dates are fictional.

**(e) Pessimist / dissolutionist lineage**
- **Olof Gigon, *Sokrates* (Bern 1947)** [Gigon]: historical Socrates unrecoverable; all sources are literary constructions.
- **Louis-André Dorion, "The Rise and Fall of the Socratic Problem,"** in *Cambridge Companion to Socrates* (2011) [Dorion]: dissolve rather than solve; study *Sokratikoi logoi* as a literary genre.

## 2. Major Modern Monographs

### Foundational / landmark
- **Vlastos 1991** — introduced "complex irony" (Socrates's disclaimers are ironic in sense P, true in sense Q), reconstructed elenchus as positively epistemic, defended Socratic eudaimonism and intellectualism.
- **Vlastos, *Socratic Studies*, ed. Burnyeat (Cambridge 1994)** — includes "The Socratic Elenchus: Method Is All" (1983) and "Is the 'Socratic Fallacy' Socratic?" (1990).

### Religion and daimonion
- **Mark L. McPherran, *The Religion of Socrates* (Penn State 1996)** [landmark]. Against the 19th-c. rationalist Socrates: Socrates was genuinely religious — sacrificed, prayed, trusted oracles, took the *daimonion* as real divine sign. Coherent Socratic theology: gods wholly good, omniscient; piety = service promoting virtue.
- **Nicholas D. Smith and Paul B. Woodruff, eds., *Reason and Religion in Socratic Philosophy* (Oxford 2000)**
- **Brickhouse & Smith, *Socrates' Divine Sign* (2005)**: daimonion as genuine divine communication.

### Trial and death
- **I.F. Stone, *The Trial of Socrates* (Little, Brown 1988)**: journalist's argument that Athens had a real case — Socrates's pupils (Critias, Charmides, Alcibiades) damaged the city; the free-speech martyrology is Platonic apologetics.
- **Brickhouse & Smith, *Socrates on Trial* (1989)**: standard scholarly reconstruction; charges taken at face value as religious/moral with political backdrop.
- **Robin Waterfield, *Why Socrates Died* (Norton 2009)**: trial was fundamentally political. The 403 amnesty protected Socrates from direct prosecution for association with Critias (leader of Thirty), Charmides (Plato's uncle, one of the Thirty), and Alcibiades. *Asebeia* (impiety) was not covered by the amnesty, so Anytus, Meletus, Lycon used it as a **legal workaround**. Anytus (democrat exiled under the Thirty) is the political driver.
- **Paul Cartledge, *Ancient Greek Political Thought in Practice* (Cambridge 2009)**: broadly sympathetic to political reading; restored democracy's anxieties about oligarchic resurgence.
- **Emily Wilson, *The Death of Socrates* (Harvard/Profile 2007)**: reception-oriented; traces the image of the dying Socrates from antiquity through David's 1787 painting, Nietzsche's *Birth of Tragedy*, modern film. Notes *Phaedo* death-scene speeches are Plato's composition, not stenographic record.
- **Josiah Ober, "Socrates and Democratic Athens,"** in *Cambridge Companion* (2011): Socrates as internal critic of democracy using democratic norms (*parrhēsia*, *isēgoria*), not an enemy of it.

### General philosophical
- **Donald R. Morrison, ed., *Cambridge Companion to Socrates* (2011)** [field survey]
- **Sara Ahbel-Rappe and Rachana Kamtekar, eds., *A Companion to Socrates* (Blackwell 2006)**
- **Hugh H. Benson, *Socratic Wisdom* (Oxford 2000)**: Socrates has a model of expert craft-knowledge (*technē*) against which elenchus measures claims.
- **Richard Kraut, *Socrates and the State* (Princeton 1984)**: landmark on Socratic politics, *Crito*'s argument for obedience; defends qualified obedience compatible with Apology-style civil disobedience.
- **Terry Penner, "The Unity of Virtue," *Philosophical Review* 82 (1973)**: rigorous strong-intellectualist reading — virtue is a single epistemic state; akrasia impossible.
- **Alexander Nehamas, *The Art of Living* (California 1998); *Virtues of Authenticity* (Princeton 1999)**: Socrates as originator of philosophy-as-self-shaping; irony is irreducibly personal, not paraphrasable as P/Q.
- **Paul Johnson, *Socrates: A Man for Our Times* (Viking 2011)**: popular biography; broadly Vlastosian.
- **Bettany Hughes, *The Hemlock Cup* (Knopf 2011)**: popular; strong on archaeological-material context.
- **A.A. Long, *Greek Models of Mind and Self* (Harvard 2015); "Socrates in Hellenistic Philosophy," *CQ* 38 (1988)**: each Hellenistic school inherited a different Socrates (Cynic, Cyrenaic, Academic skeptic, Stoic, Megarian).

### Xenophon's Socrates (rehabilitation)
- **Louis-André Dorion, *L'autre Socrate* (Vrin 2013)**: Xenophon philosophically serious; "Xenophontic Socrates" internally coherent.
- **Vivienne Gray, *The Framing of Socrates* (Steiner 1998)**: *Memorabilia* as purposeful literary composition.

### Aristophanes's Socrates
- **Kenneth J. Dover, *Aristophanes: Clouds* (Oxford 1968)**: caricature composite of sophists and natural philosophers; not accurate portrait but testifies to popular perception.
- **Martha Nussbaum, "Aristophanes and Socrates on Learning Practical Wisdom," *YCS* 26 (1980)**: grain of truth — young Socrates may have had natural-philosophical interests (cf. autobiographical *Phaedo* 96a–99d).

## 3. Key Findings (labeled)

1. **The Socratic Problem is not solved; dominant frame has shifted.** [scholarly-consensus on shift; contested on replacement] — Vlastos's developmentalism dominated 1970–2000; since Kahn 1996, unitarianism and literary-first readings have gained major ground.

2. **Vlastos's "ten theses" remain the reference point even for critics.** [Vlastos 1991 ch. 2; scholarly-consensus on canonical status] — Kahn, Rowe, Annas, Nehamas all argue *against* the ten theses without displacing them as framework.

3. **"Complex irony" is Vlastos's enduring contribution.** [Vlastos 1991 ch. 1; scholarly-consensus on importance; contested on form] — sense-P false, sense-Q true. Nehamas 1998 pushes back: irony is irreducibly personal/stylistic.

4. **Socrates was genuinely religious, not a secular rationalist.** [scholarly-consensus since McPherran 1996] — sacrificed (*Phaedo* 118a cock to Asclepius, Xen. *Mem.* 1.1.2), trusted Delphi (Chaerephon oracle, *Apology* 20e–21a), heeded *daimonion*, composed hymns in prison (*Phaedo* 60d–61b).

5. **The *daimonion* is most plausibly a genuine religious experience, not conscience-metaphor.** [McPherran 1996; Brickhouse & Smith 2005; contested by Reeve-style reading] — the "introducing new divinities" charge makes best sense if Athenians took the daimonion as a real claimed divine sign.

6. **Socratic intellectualism is live but qualified.** [contested] — Strong thesis (virtue = knowledge, no akrasia): Penner 1973, 1992; earlier Vlastos. Aristotle's critique (*EN* VII.3, 1145b21ff.): "contradicts the phenomena." Moderated: Brickhouse & Smith 2010 *Socratic Moral Psychology* — non-rational desires exist but cannot override held evaluative judgment.

7. **The trial was politically shaped, not purely religious.** [Waterfield 2009; Stone 1988; Cartledge 2009; contested by Brickhouse & Smith 1989] — Anytus/Meletus/Lycon used *asebeia* as amnesty workaround for Socrates's associations with Critias, Charmides, Alcibiades.

8. **Modern "Socratic method" ≠ ancient elenchus.** [scholarly-consensus] — Langdell's 1870s Harvard-Law method is top-down to a predetermined doctrine; ancient elenchus (Vlastos 1983; Benson 2000) is aporetic, aimed at exposing inconsistency in interlocutor's beliefs.

9. **"I know that I know nothing" is a verbatim misquote.** [scholarly-consensus; Fine 2008] — *Apology* 21d says only that Socrates does not think he knows what he does not know, and that is his *only* advantage. Socrates elsewhere asserts robust moral convictions (*Apology* 29b, 38a; *Crito* 49a–d; *Gorgias* 469b–c, 474b, 479e, 508e). Latin "*scio me nihil scire*" is a Renaissance simplification.

10. **The *Phaedo* death-scene speeches are Plato's composition; hemlock is historical.** [scholarly-consensus] — elaborate immortality arguments and Theory of Forms are Platonic. Plato himself was absent ("Plato, I believe, was ill," *Phaedo* 59b) — a literary signal. Bare facts (month's imprisonment during Delos ship's absence, hemlock, close followers present) are historical.

11. **Nails's prosopography constrains but does not solve the source problem.** [Nails 2002; scholarly-consensus as reference] — many Platonic dramatic dates demonstrably fictional, eroding naive "Plato as reporter" readings.

12. **Xenophon has been rehabilitated as an independent source.** [Dorion 2013; Gray 1998; still a minority "equal status" view] — 20th-c. Schleiermacher-Maier-Vlastos dismissal is breaking down; where Plato and Xenophon converge (moral seriousness, daimonion, elenctic questioning, trial outline, hemlock) we have corroboration.

13. **No single "Socratic politics"; options constrained but not determined.** [contested; Kraut 1984; Ober 2011] — *Crito*'s Laws-of-Athens obedience vs. *Apology* 32c–d refusal to arrest Leon of Salamis under the Thirty and refusal to join the unconstitutional Arginusae mass-trial as *prytanis* in 406. Neither straightforward anti-democrat (contra Stone) nor pro-democrat; internal critic using democratic norms (Ober).

14. **The "Socratic tradition" fragments across Hellenistic schools.** [Long 2006, 2015; scholarly-consensus on fact of fragmentation] — Cynics (Antisthenes→Diogenes): anti-conventional ascetic. Cyrenaics (Aristippus): hedonist-with-mastery. Academic skeptics (Arcesilaus, Carneades): aporetic elenchus-machine. Stoics (Zeno→Epictetus): proto-Stoic virtue-sage. Megarians (Euclides): dialectician of Good-as-One.

15. **The "Socratic fallacy" (Geach 1966) is a live meta-issue.** [contested] — Peter Geach, "Plato's *Euthyphro*," *Monist* 50 (1966): Socrates seems to demand a definition of X before any instance of X can be known. Vlastos 1990: Socrates not committed to it. Benson 2000: partially defends — requirement only for expert *technē* knowledge, not ordinary belief.

16. **Socratic *erōs* is sublimated, not sexual.** [scholarly-consensus on Alcibiades episode; contested on broader implications] — *Symposium* 217a–219e: Socrates declines Alcibiades's advance, redirects erotic energy toward philosophical teaching.

## 4. Active Controversies (preserving disagreements)

**A. Anti-democratic vs. radical internal critic**
- Anti-democratic/conservative: Stone 1988 — Socrates as engine of Athens's anti-democratic faction.
- Internal democratic critic: Ober 2011.
- Egalitarian-in-reasoning / elitist-in-expert-judgment: Vlastos 1983.

**B. Socratic irony**
- Complex (Vlastos 1991): P-false / Q-true, paraphrasable.
- Simple (older default): dissimulation.
- Unparaphrasable (Nehamas 1998): irreducibly personal-stylistic.
- Strategic-pedagogical (Rowe 2007): property of Platonic writer, not historical Socrates.

**C. The daimonion**
- Genuine religious sign (McPherran 1996; Brickhouse & Smith 2005).
- Conscience/moral intuition (older rationalist; some Vlastos).
- Platonic literary device (rare; some post-Kahn unitarians).

**D. The trial: religious or political or both**
- Primarily religious: Brickhouse & Smith 1989; Burnyeat, "The Impiety of Socrates," *Ancient Philosophy* 17 (1997).
- Primarily political (amnesty workaround): Waterfield 2009; Stone 1988; Cartledge 2009.
- Both, religion foregrounded as politically expedient: Ober 2011; Nails 2002.

**E. Positive doctrines vs. only-elenchus-and-aporia**
- Only-elenchus: Academic skeptics; some modern cautious readers. Early dialogues (*Ion, Euthyphro, Charmides, Laches, Lysis, Hippias Minor*) all end in aporia.
- Positive ethics: Vlastos 1991; Brickhouse & Smith 2000; Benson 2000. Tested convictions survive repeated elenchus: virtue is knowledge; it is better to suffer than to do injustice (*Gorgias* 469b–c, 474b, 508e); virtue sufficient for eudaimonia; unexamined life not worth living (*Apology* 38a).

**F. Socratic intellectualism**
- Yes (strong): Penner; earlier Vlastos.
- No akrasia but non-rational desires exist: Brickhouse & Smith 2010.
- Aristotle's critique stands (*EN* VII.3): against the phenomena of akrasia, intellectualism is false.

**G. Plato's early dialogues: Socratic or proleptic?**
- Socratic: Vlastos 1991; Brickhouse & Smith; Irwin 1977.
- Proleptic: Kahn 1996; Rowe 2007; Annas 1999.

**H. Xenophon: equal source or distant second?**
- Distant second: traditional, Vlastos.
- Serious independent source: Dorion 2013; Gray 1998.

## 5. Myth-Debunking

1. **Myth: "I know that I know nothing."** — Nearest passage *Apology* 21d is relative epistemic humility about expert *sophia*, paired with robust moral convictions. The Latin *scio me nihil scire* is Renaissance. [scholarly-consensus; Fine 2008]

2. **Myth: Socrates wrote nothing because Athens was pre-literate.** — Athens was highly literate. The best explanation is philosophical choice (truth lives in dialectic, not texts, *Phaedrus* 274c–275b). [scholarly-consensus]

3. **Myth: Socrates was a pacifist or non-political gadfly.** — Hoplite at Potidaea (432), Delium (424, praised for courage on retreat, *Laches* 181b, *Symp.* 220e–221c), Amphipolis (422). Council member (*boulē*) 406, resisted unconstitutional Arginusae mass trial as *prytanis* (*Apology* 32b–c). [scholarly-consensus; Nails 2002]

4. **Myth: The "Socratic method" is Langdell-style interrogation.** — Langdell 1870s Harvard-Law invention. Ancient elenchus is aporetic, aimed at inconsistency, not at a predetermined doctrinal answer. [scholarly-consensus; Kimball, *Orators and Philosophers* 1986]

5. **Myth: Socrates was a proto-Enlightenment secularist.** — Sacrificed, prayed, trusted Delphi, heeded daimonion, composed hymns in prison. The 17th–18th-c. secular-rationalist Socrates is a reconstruction. [scholarly-consensus since McPherran 1996]

6. **Myth: *Phaedo* is a stenographic record.** — Classical Theory of Forms, sophisticated immortality arguments, elaborate psychology all Platonic; Plato signals absence ("Plato, I believe, was ill," 59b). [scholarly-consensus; Wilson 2007]

7. **Myth: Xenophon is an unphilosophical soldier.** — 19th-20th-c. prejudice (Schleiermacher, Maier 1913, Vlastos) now breaking. Xenophon's *Memorabilia* and *Apology* are purposeful philosophical compositions. [Dorion 2013; Gray 1998]

8. **Myth: Socrates's students were all oligarchs (or all democrats).** — Circle was politically mixed: oligarchs (Critias, Charmides, Alcibiades-drifted); democrats (Chaerephon, exiled under Thirty, returned with Thrasybulus). This mix made him politically suspect to both sides. [Nails 2002; Waterfield 2009]

9. **Myth: Socrates's poverty was chosen ascetic discipline.** — Partly temperamental, partly consequence of choosing philosophy over paid work. Xanthippe and three sons (youngest still a child at the trial, *Apology* 34d) depended on friends' support. [Nails 2002; Waterfield 2009]

10. **Myth: The jury "nearly acquitted" Socrates.** — Conviction margin was close (~280–220 in jury of 501, DL 2.41, *Apology* 36a). Death-penalty vote (after his counter-proposal of free meals at the Prytaneum) was wider; his counter-proposal antagonised those who had voted to acquit. [Brickhouse & Smith 1989; Waterfield 2009]

11. **Myth: Socrates invented philosophy ex nihilo.** — Preceded by Ionian natural philosophers (Thales–Heraclitus–Anaxagoras), Eleatics (Parmenides), atomists (Leucippus, Democritus), sophists (Protagoras, Gorgias, Prodicus, Hippias). Cicero's "called philosophy down from the heavens" (*Tusc. Disp.* 5.4.10) marks a *turn* to ethics, not an absolute origin. *Phaedo* 96a–99d autobiographically describes early natural-philosophical engagement. [scholarly-consensus; Nussbaum 1980]

12. **Myth: Hemlock death was peaceful as in *Phaedo*.** — *Conium maculatum* actually causes ascending paralysis, respiratory failure, often convulsions and vomiting. *Phaedo* 117e–118a's serene account is idealised. [contested on medical detail; scholarly-consensus on stylisation]

## 6. Political Interpretation — The Waterfield Thesis

**Robin Waterfield, *Why Socrates Died* (Norton 2009)** — core argument:

1. **Context (Athens 404–399 BCE).** Athens defeated by Sparta 404. Spartans imposed the **Thirty Tyrants** under Critias (Socratic associate, *Charmides* prol.; Xen. *Mem.* 1.2.12–38), supported by Charmides (Plato's uncle, also Socratic). Thirty executed ~1,500 citizens/metics without trial, confiscated property, tried to implicate democrats (hence the order to five men including Socrates to arrest Leon of Salamis, *Apology* 32c–d; Socrates refused and went home).

2. **Democratic restoration and amnesty.** In 403, Thrasybulus's exiles returned, defeated the Thirty at Munychia, and agreed to an **amnesty** forbidding prosecution for political acts under the Thirty.

3. **Amnesty's constraint.** Anytus (democrat exiled under the Thirty) could not prosecute Socrates directly for association with Critias, Charmides, Alcibiades. Direct political charges barred.

4. **Workaround.** *Asebeia* (impiety) was a separate statutory charge, not covered by the amnesty. Anytus, Meletus (minor poet), Lycon (orator) brought the indictment in early 399: (i) not believing in city's gods, (ii) introducing new divinities (plausibly the *daimonion*), (iii) corrupting the youth. Religious on face; political in function.

5. **Socrates's trial conduct.** *Apology*: refuses to plead, mocks Meletus, proposes free meals at the Prytaneum — all maximising death sentence. Waterfield: Socrates chose martyrdom over exile because exile would vindicate prosecutors, death would vindicate philosophy.

6. **Significance for an echo of Socrates.** If Waterfield is even partly right, Socrates is not merely a martyr to free inquiry but a politically entangled figure whose refusal to distance himself from Critias and Alcibiades — rooted in his view that political faction is irrelevant to moral testing — made him fatally available to Athens's post-war settling of scores.
