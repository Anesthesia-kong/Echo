# Socrates — Era Context (Classical Athens, c. 470/469 – 399 BCE)

Socrates lived through the most turbulent period in Greek history. He was born about a decade after the Persian Wars, reached maturity during the Athenian imperial zenith, fought as a hoplite through the Peloponnesian War, survived the plague, the Sicilian disaster, the Thirty Tyrants, and the restoration of democracy — and was executed by that restored democracy. This context is not background; it is the substance his thinking responds to.

## 1. Political Context: The Athenian Polis

**1. Direct democracy (demokratia).** Citizen assembly (*ekklesia*) on the Pnyx decided war, peace, laws, death sentences by simple majority of those present (6,000 quorum for major matters). No representatives. [documented; trained-knowledge: Hansen, *The Athenian Democracy in the Age of Demosthenes* 1991]

**2. Restricted citizenship.** Pericles's citizenship law of 451/450 BCE: both parents must be Athenian for citizenship. Socrates qualified. Of roughly 250,000 inhabitants of Attica, perhaps 30,000–40,000 were adult male citizens. Women, slaves, metics (resident foreigners) excluded from political life. [documented; trained-knowledge: Hansen 1991; Osborne]

**3. Polis as moral horizon.** The city was the natural unit of ethical and religious life. To be exiled was near-death. Socrates's refusal of exile (Apology 37c–38b; Crito 52b–c) fits this horizon — to him, leaving Athens at 70 to live in a foreign city was not a life. [documented]

**4. Small scale.** Adult male citizen population ~30,000. Socrates knew, and was known by, the whole political class personally. Trial jurors included men who had voted with him in assembly, fought beside him at Potidaea or Delium, drunk with him at symposia. [modern-scholarly-inference; Hansen 1991; Nails 2002]

## 2. The Peloponnesian War (431–404 BCE) and Its Aftermath

**5. Socrates's three campaigns as hoplite.** Potidaea (432–429 BCE, ages ~38–41), Delium (424, age ~46), Amphipolis (422, age ~48). Plato (Symposium 220a–d, 221a–b; Apology 28e) and Laches (181b) attest. Hoplite status meant he owned enough property to furnish his own arms — the middle property-census. [attested]

**6. Plague of Athens (430–427 BCE).** Killed Pericles and perhaps a third of the population. Shook civic morality; Thucydides II.47–54 describes the collapse of conventional religious and ethical restraints. Socrates was ~39–42. [documented: Thucydides]

**7. Demagogic turn after Pericles.** Cleon, Hyperbolus, Cleophon — leaders without Pericles's aristocratic authority. The rhetorical culture intensified; the assembly became more volatile. Aristophanes's comedies document (and satirize) the shift.

**8. Alcibiades (c. 450–404 BCE).** Socrates's famous associate, brilliant general, defected to Sparta after the Sicilian Expedition disaster (415 BCE), then to Persia, then back to Athens, then assassinated. Embodied talent detached from virtue. Socrates's closeness to him shadowed the 399 trial. [attested]

**9. Arginusae trial (406 BCE).** After a naval victory, eight Athenian generals were tried en bloc by the assembly for failing to recover shipwrecked crews. Socrates, serving as *prytanis* (rotating day-chair of the Council), alone resisted the unconstitutional procedure. Six were executed. (Apology 32b–c; Xenophon *Hellenica* I.7; *Memorabilia* I.1.18.) Moment of visible civic courage. [documented]

**10. Athenian defeat (404 BCE).** Sparta's Lysander starves Athens out. Long Walls demolished. Empire lost. Socrates is ~65. The trauma conditions everything that follows. [documented: Xenophon *Hellenica* II]

## 3. Democracy Mechanics (The Machinery That Killed Him)

**11. The *dikasteria* (people's courts).** Juries of 200, 500, or (for capital cases) 501 citizens over 30, selected by lot from a pool of 6,000 annual jurors. Trials completed in a single day. Each side gave a timed speech measured by water-clock (*klepsydra*). [documented; Hansen 1991]

**12. Private prosecution.** No public prosecutor. Any citizen (*ho boulomenos* — "whoever wishes") could bring charges. Anytus, Meletus, Lycon in 399 were not state officials; they acted as private citizens.

**13. No defense lawyers.** Defendants spoke for themselves (sometimes with speeches written by professional logographers — as Lysias, per tradition, offered Socrates). Rhetorical skill was life-or-death. Socrates's anti-rhetorical Apology (17a–18a) is therefore a deliberate risk.

**14. Voting by ballot.** Two urns, bronze discs. Majority wins. At Socrates's trial, 501 jurors; guilty margin close (~280–220 per reconstruction of Apology 36a). After Socrates's deliberately defiant counter-proposal of free meals at the Prytaneum (36d–37a), the death-penalty vote widened — his own counter-proposal turned some acquitters against him.

**15. Executed by the RESTORED democracy.** Key historical irony: Socrates was not killed by tyrants but by the democracy that had returned in 403. This matters for interpreting the trial politically. [Waterfield 2009; Ober 2011]

## 4. The Thirty Tyrants (404–403 BCE) and the Trial

**16. The Thirty.** Spartan-imposed oligarchy under Critias (Plato's cousin, Socrates's ex-associate) and Charmides (Plato's uncle, Socrates's ex-associate). Eight-month reign of terror; ~1,500 executions; property confiscations; exiles.

**17. Leon of Salamis episode (404 BCE).** The Thirty ordered five men including Socrates to arrest the innocent Leon for summary execution. Socrates went home (Apology 32c–d). Not arrested himself — evidence the Thirty saw him as sympathetic enough not to punish, or too prominent to touch. Ambiguous.

**18. Democratic restoration (403 BCE).** Thrasybulus's exiles defeat the Thirty at Munychia. Amnesty (*amnēstia* — "no remembering") forbids prosecution for political acts under the Thirty.

**19. The amnesty's unintended consequence.** Anytus — democratic politician exiled by the Thirty — cannot prosecute Socrates directly for his oligarchic associations. Solution: charges of *asebeia* (impiety), which are not covered by the amnesty. The workaround thesis. [Waterfield 2009; Stone 1988; Cartledge 2009]

**20. Formal charges (399 BCE).** Preserved by Diogenes Laertius II.40 from the Metroon archives: *"Socrates does wrong: (i) by not believing in the gods the city believes in, and (ii) by introducing other new divinities; and (iii) he does wrong by corrupting the young. Penalty: death."* The "new divinities" refers most plausibly to the daimonion.

**21. Prosecutors.** **Meletus** (minor poet, nominal prosecutor, young). **Anytus** (major democratic politician, tanner, had lost his son to Socratic circles per later tradition — real political driver). **Lycon** (rhetor). [documented: Apology 18b, 23e; Diogenes Laertius]

## 5. Religion

**22. Polis religion, not creedal.** Athenian religion was civic practice: sacrifices, festivals (Panathenaea, Dionysia, Eleusinian Mysteries), oracles, temple maintenance. No creed; no holy book. "Belief" meant ritual participation and acknowledgment of city gods. [trained-knowledge: Parker, *Athenian Religion* 1996]

**23. Piety as civic duty.** The city and the gods were inseparable. Neglecting public religious duties was politically suspect. The *asebeia* charge hit this nexus: Socrates's daimonion (a private sign) vs polis religion (public practice).

**24. The daimonion as religious innovation.** A private divine sign that *only dissuaded, never commanded* (Apology 31c–d, 40a–c). Unusual enough that it could plausibly ground "introducing new divinities." [documented]

**25. Precedents for impiety trials.** Anaxagoras (Pericles's friend, natural philosopher) tried c. 430s BCE; Diagoras of Melos ("the atheist") condemned to death in absentia c. 415; Protagoras (agnostic "On the Gods") exiled; Euripides suspected. Socrates was not the first philosopher prosecuted for religious heterodoxy. [attested; trained-knowledge]

**26. Socrates's own religious practice.** Sacrificed (Phaedo 118a — the cock to Asclepius); prayed (Phaedrus 279b — the prayer to Pan); composed hymns in prison (Phaedo 60d); trusted the Delphic oracle (Apology 20e–21a); spoke of the gods with conventional respect. Not a secular rationalist. [McPherran 1996]

## 6. The Sophistic Movement

**27. The sophists (mid-5th c. BCE onward).** Itinerant teachers of rhetoric and *aretē* for fees, drawn to Athens's wealth and political intensity. Protagoras, Gorgias, Prodicus, Hippias, Thrasymachus. Socrates engaged all of them. [documented across Plato's dialogues]

**28. Protagoras of Abdera: "Man is the measure of all things"** (*pantōn chrēmatōn metron anthrōpos*). Relativism: truth indexed to perceiver. Socrates's Theaetetus and Protagoras attack this. [documented]

**29. Aristophanes's *Clouds* (423 BCE) — cultural collateral damage.** Socrates made a composite sophist/naturalist in the comedy. Plato's Apology 18a–d blames "the old accusers" (Aristophanes first among them) for poisoning the jury against him over 24 years. [documented]

**30. Socrates's four distinguishing features (vs sophists).** He (i) refused fees; (ii) questioned rather than lectured; (iii) claimed ignorance rather than wisdom; (iv) was an Athenian citizen, not an itinerant. These distinctions mattered to him (Apology 19d–20c, 31b–c), less so to his enemies. [documented]

**31. The "second sailing" turn to ethics** (*deuteros plous*). Phaedo 96a–100a: Socrates reports youthful attraction to natural philosophy (Anaxagoras), then disappointment when Anaxagoras gave mechanical rather than teleological causes. Turned to *logoi* (arguments about concepts) as the safer route. This is Socrates's self-reported intellectual autobiography — though the Phaedo passage is Platonic composition. [attributed]

## 7. Social Structures

**32. Slavery.** Attica had perhaps 80,000–150,000 slaves — roughly as many as adult male citizens. Mining slaves at Laurium in brutal conditions; household slaves in private homes. Slavery was unquestioned background furniture. **Socrates never challenged it.** [trained-knowledge: Garlan, *Slavery in Ancient Greece* 1988]

**33. Schole — citizen leisure.** Citizens (including the poor Socrates) did not do manual labor if they could avoid it; that was for slaves and metics. *Schole* ("leisure") was the precondition for philosophy. Socrates's poverty was chosen-ish: he did not pursue his father's stonemasonry after some point, living on friends' support.

**34. Oikos confinement of women.** Citizen women were largely confined to the household (*oikos*), denied political participation, and (ideally) not seen in public by unrelated men. Marriage at ~15 to a man ~30. Socrates's wife Xanthippe and sons appear only briefly in the dialogues (Phaedo 60a, 116b).

**35. The Diotima / Aspasia anomaly.** Socrates credits women as his teachers: Diotima (Symposium 201d–212c, on *erōs*), Aspasia (Menexenus 235e, on rhetoric). Historicity of Diotima is debated; Aspasia was a real metic woman, Pericles's consort. This is anomalous for classical Athens — and perhaps one of the few places Socrates's thinking genuinely breaks from his era's assumptions. [attested for Aspasia; attributed for Diotima]

**36. Greek pederastic culture.** Socially institutionalized *erastes* (adult lover) / *eromenos* (youth beloved) relationships among aristocratic men, with educational content. Socrates operates inside this convention but famously inverts it (Alcibiades's speech at Symposium 217a–219e): the beautiful young Alcibiades propositions the ugly old Socrates, who declines. Plato presents this as moral victory. [documented]

**37. Family background.** Father Sophroniscus, reportedly a stonemason (*lithourgos*) — possibly a later inference from a Socrates joke about descent from Daedalus. Mother Phaenarete, reportedly a midwife (*maia*) — cited at Theaetetus 149a, possibly also metaphorical. Socrates was born into modest middle-census circumstances, not poverty. His adult poverty was more choice than origin. [attested, with caveats; Nails 2002]

## 8. Era-Assumptions Socrates Took For Granted (That Modern Readers Don't)

**38. Polytheism as the default.** Multiple gods, with specific civic cults, festivals, and myths, were the obvious furniture of reality. Atheism was rare and suspect. Socrates's religious innovations (daimonion, the god's mission given at Delphi) operate *within* polytheism.

**39. The psyche as a real entity.** The soul was not a metaphor; it was the seat of character and the locus of ethical stakes. Socrates's "care for the soul" is a claim about caring for a real thing — not a psychological metaphor.

**40. Slavery as natural.** See above. Aristotle would later theorize it (Politics I.4–7); Socrates just lived in it. **An era-view modern readers find troubling; must be handled via Moral Distance Declaration.**

**41. Citizen / non-citizen distinction as natural.** Metics, slaves, women, barbarians — each category had its place. The polis was not egalitarian. Socrates did not argue against this structure.

**42. Writing as inferior to living dialogue.** Phaedrus 274c–278e: writing is orphaned *logos*, unable to defend itself when challenged. Living speech between persons is the only medium where truth can be pursued and tested. **This is why Socrates did not write.** [documented]

**43. Philosophical life as the highest human activity.** Not debated, asserted. The examined life is not *a* good life; it is *the* good life for a human being (Apology 38a).

**44. Virtue-is-knowledge paradoxes.** If virtue is knowledge, virtue should be teachable like any *technē*. But Meno 89d–96c shows that virtue cannot be taught (since good men cannot teach it to their sons). This unresolved tension is Socratic territory.

**45. Hierarchical dualisms.** Soul > body, philosopher > craftsman, reason > appetite. Not invented by Socrates (older than him), but systematized by his line.

**46. Polis-as-parent (Crito's Laws of Athens).** Crito 50a–54d: the Laws speak as Socrates's quasi-parents. You owe your polis what you owe your father. This is era-specific piety, not universal political theory.
