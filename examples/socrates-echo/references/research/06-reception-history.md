# Socrates — Reception History Across 2400+ Years

**Core problem this document addresses:** The "Socrates" that lives in popular imagination is a layered palimpsest of later projections. Socrates wrote nothing. Every "Socrates" is already an interpretation (Plato's, Xenophon's, Aristophanes's), and every subsequent era has re-read those interpretations through its own needs.

## I. Antiquity (4th c. BCE – 5th c. CE)

**1. The "Socratic problem" is the origin of reception history itself.** Within a generation of his 399 BCE death, Socrates was already contested. Plato, Xenophon, Aeschines of Sphettus, Antisthenes, Aristippus, Phaedo, Euclides of Megara all wrote *Sokratikoi logoi* — a recognized literary genre. There is no neutral Socrates; the reception *is* the figure. [trained-knowledge: Morrison ed., Cambridge Companion to Socrates 2011]

**2. Cynics — radical ascetic Socrates.** Antisthenes and Diogenes of Sinope claimed Socrates as founder. Barefoot, propertyless, practicing *autarkeia* (self-sufficiency). Plato called Diogenes "Socrates gone mad." *Grounded:* Socrates went barefoot, indifferent to wealth. *Projection:* Socrates lived with wife and children, served as hoplite, was a citizen. Eccentric, not dropped-out. [trained-knowledge: Diogenes Laertius VI; Long 1999]

**3. Cyrenaics — pleasure-affirming Socrates.** Aristippus founded a school making pleasure the good, claiming Socratic warrant via his humor and enjoyment of company. *Projection:* Strong — Socrates subordinates pleasure to soul-good in *Gorgias*, *Philebus*. Improbable reading; but shows how early the figure was pulled in opposite directions. [trained-knowledge: D.L. II; SEP "Cyrenaics"]

**4. Academy — metaphysical-ethical Socrates.** Plato's dialogues shift from early (elenchus, aporia) to middle works (Forms, tripartite soul) where "Socrates" voices Platonic metaphysics. *Grounded:* Early dialogues plausibly reflect historical themes. *Projection:* Aristotle explicitly says (*Metaphysics* 1078b) Socrates did ethics and universals but did NOT separate the Forms — Plato did. [documented; trained-knowledge]

**5. Stoics — sage and martyr Socrates.** Zeno claimed Socratic descent via the Cynics. Epictetus invokes him more than any other figure. Marcus Aurelius cites him in *Meditations*. *Grounded:* Calm at death. *Projection:* Stoic *apatheia* framing; Plato's Socrates shows fear, irritation, passionate *erōs*. [trained-knowledge: A.A. Long CQ 1988; Epictetus *Discourses*]

**6. Skeptics — suspended-judgment Socrates.** Arcesilaus and Carneades in the New Academy read disavowal of knowledge as proto-skeptical *epochē*. *Grounded:* Elenchus ends in aporia; Apology 21b–d disavows wisdom. *Projection:* Early Socrates holds robust ethical convictions (virtue is knowledge; no one errs willingly). [trained-knowledge: Cicero *Academica*; SEP]

**7. Cicero — founder of ethical philosophy.** "Brought philosophy down from the heavens" (*Tusc. Disp.* V.10-11). This framing dominates 2000 years. *Projection:* Tidy; the Presocratics already did ethics. [trained-knowledge]

**8. Plutarch — religious Socrates.** *On the Sign of Socrates* takes the *daimonion* seriously — notable as later Enlightenment readers scrub it. [trained-knowledge]

**9. Diogenes Laertius — anecdote factory.** *Lives of Eminent Philosophers* Book II. Third century CE — centuries after Socrates. Many "famous" stories (Xanthippe-as-shrew) are unverifiable. **FLAG:** the "nagging Xanthippe" tradition is late and moralizing, not contemporary testimony. [legendary]

**10. Christian appropriation — proto-martyr Socrates.** Justin Martyr, *First Apology* 46: Socrates lived "according to the Logos" = Christian before Christ. Augustine, *De Civitate Dei* VIII.3: pagan close to truth. Sets template for medieval and Renaissance readings. [trained-knowledge]

## II. Medieval (6th – 15th c.)

**11. Indirect transmission via Latin intermediaries.** Latin West lost Plato (only *Timaeus* via Calcidius survived in Latin). Socrates accessed via Cicero, Augustine, Boethius, florilegia. *Era's Socrates:* sententious pagan moralist. *Projection:* flattening — dialectic disappears, Socrates becomes detached maxims.

**12. Islamic philosophers — ascetic-rationalist Socrates.** Al-Kindi, al-Farabi blend Socrates and Diogenes into a composite ascetic. **FLAG:** the "Socrates in the barrel" image is a confusion with Diogenes the Cynic. [trained-knowledge: Alon 1991]

**13. Dante — silent limbo-dweller.** *Inferno* IV.134: respected but unsaved among the virtuous pagans. Medieval Christian ambivalence.

## III. Renaissance (15th – 16th c.)

**14. Ficino restores Plato.** Complete Latin *Plato* (1484) — first direct access to the corpus in a millennium.

**15. Erasmus — "Sancte Socrates, ora pro nobis."** *Convivium Religiosum* (Colloquies, 1522). Crystallizes Christian-humanist canonization of Socrates as near-saint. Drives the "martyr for truth" reading for 300 years. *Projection:* Christianization of *eudaimonia*.

**16. Montaigne — ideal self-examiner.** *Essais* III.12, III.13, II.11. "Que sais-je?" is Socratic in Gascon. *Grounded:* Apology 38a. *Projection:* Montaigne's humanist flux-comfort read back in.

## IV. Early Modern / Enlightenment (17th – 18th c.)

**17. Voltaire, Diderot — martyr for free inquiry.** Voltaire's *Socrate* (1759). Anti-clericalism in Athenian costume. **FLAG:** major source of the still-dominant popular view that Socrates was executed by "religious fanatics." The historical trial is more political (post-Thirty Tyrants, associations with Critias/Alcibiades/Charmides) than religious. *Projection:* strips out daimonion, Delphic oracle (Apology 21a), prison hymn to Apollo (Phaedo 60d), Asclepius sacrifice (Phaedo 118a).

**18. David's "Death of Socrates" (1787).** Socrates upright, finger skyward — more Christ-imagery than Plato. **FLAG:** the mental image most educated people have is David's painting, composed on the eve of the French Revolution as political iconography.

**19. Mendelssohn's *Phaedon* (1767).** Paraphrase making Socrates mouthpiece for Enlightenment-rational soul-immortality. Bestseller before Hegel.

## V. 19th Century

**20. Hegel — dialectical hero AND rightly condemned.** *Lectures on the History of Philosophy* Vol. I. Most sophisticated 19th-c. reading. Socrates = subjective conscience against unreflective *Sittlichkeit*. Athens not wrong to perceive the threat. Tragic necessity. *Projection:* Geist's "moment."

**21. Kierkegaard — ironist par excellence (1841).** *The Concept of Irony with Continual Reference to Socrates*. Socratic irony = infinite negativity hollowing out positive claims. *Grounded:* *eirōneia* in *Republic* I, *Symposium*. *Projection:* proto-existentialist "infinite absolute negativity."

**22. Nietzsche — decadent and destroyer (1872, 1888).** *Birth of Tragedy* §§12–15; *Twilight of the Idols*, "The Problem of Socrates." Ugly, plebeian, hyper-rational dialectician who killed Attic tragedy. "Socrates was rabble" (*Pöbel*). Dialectics = weapon of resentful weak. The Asclepius line = life-denying confession. *Grounded:* reportedly ugly (Alcibiades in *Symposium*); plebeian (stonemason father); Asclepius at *Phaedo* 118a. *Projection:* "rabble" is Nietzsche's aristocratic psychology. Nietzsche's ambivalence: "Socrates is so close to me I am always fighting him" (*Nachlass*). **FLAG:** probably the single most influential modern reading shaping 20th-c. continental Socrates.

**23. Grote — liberal gadfly.** *Plato and the Other Companions of Sokrates* (1865). Victorian-liberal champion of free discussion.

## VI. 20th Century

**24. Vlastos — analytic revival.** *Socrates: Ironist and Moral Philosopher* (1991). Early dialogues preserve historical Socrates: elenchus, disavowal, intellectualism about virtue. Dominated anglophone studies. *Projection:* "Socrates_E vs Socrates_M" contested (Kahn, Cooper) as too tidy.

**25. Strauss — esoteric reading.** *The City and Man* (1964). Aristocratic critique of democracy beneath surface irony. Restores Xenophon. *Projection:* esotericism itself a disputed method.

**26. Arendt — two-in-one dialogue.** *Life of the Mind* I; "Thinking and Moral Considerations." Socrates as model of thinking (two-in-one internal dialogue) that prevents moral catastrophe (Eichmann's thoughtlessness). *Grounded:* *Theaetetus* 189e.

**27. Foucault — parrhesia and care of self.** *Courage of Truth* 1983-84; *Hermeneutics of the Subject* 1981-82. Paradigmatic *parrhesiast* and inventor of *epimeleia heautou*. *Grounded:* Apology 29d-30b; *Alcibiades I*. *Projection:* late-modern aesthetics-of-existence frame.

**28. I.F. Stone — democratic prosecution (1988).** *The Trial of Socrates*. Socrates really was a threat: oligarchic associations, contempt for lot-chosen officials, teacher of those who overthrew democracy. Religious charge was political case by proxy (post-Thirty amnesty). *Projection:* underplays religion and Socrates's Leon-of-Salamis defiance of the Thirty (Apology 32c-d).

**29. Law-school "Socratic method."** Langdell, 1870s Harvard Law School. Adversarial drilling on case precedent — NOT cooperative inquiry into virtue ending in aporia. **FLAG:** when most educated Americans say "Socratic method" they mean Langdell's pedagogy, not elenchus.

## VII. Contemporary

**30. Classical-education icon.** Great Books / classical Christian schools. Inherits Erasmus/Montaigne humanist saint, stripped of Nietzschean ambivalence.

**31. Socratic questioning in CBT.** Beck 1979. Guided discovery of automatic thoughts. **FLAG:** simplified therapeutic coaching technique, not philosophical elenchus. Aims at therapeutic outcome, not aporia.

**32. Pop-culture Socrates.** *Bill & Ted's Excellent Adventure* 1989. Genial wise-old-man, descendant of humanist saint via Monty Python.

**33. Contemporary scholarship — pluralization.** Ahbel-Rappe & Kamtekar 2006; Morrison 2011; Nehamas, *The Art of Living* 1998. Abandons "the historical Socrates" for the constellation of ancient Socrateses. Nehamas: founder of "the art of living."

## VIII. Myth vs History — Explicit Flags

**34. "I know that I know nothing" — NOT verbatim.** [Apology 21b–d]. Closest: "neither of us really knows anything fine and good, but this man thinks he knows when he does not, whereas I do not think I know." The epigram is a later tight formulation — *scio me nihil scire* Latinized and back-translated. Captures the sense, not the words.

**35. "The unexamined life is not worth living" — DOCUMENTED.** [Apology 38a]. *ho anexetastos bios ou biōtos anthrōpōi*. One of the very few popular Socratic sayings that is closely attested.

**36. Saint-like sage image — Stoic/Christian overlay.** Plato's Socrates quarrels (*Gorgias*), mocks (*Euthyphro*), gets drunk (*Symposium* — though never loses control), feels desire (*Phaedrus*), is pugnacious and cutting.

**37. Secular rationalist vs actual religiosity — PROJECTION.** [Apology 27b-c, 31c-d, 33c; Phaedo 60d-61b, 118a]. The textual Socrates has the *daimonion*, the Apollo-commanded mission, the prison hymn to Apollo, and the Asclepius sacrifice. Any "secretly a rationalist atheist" reading is projection.

**38. Elenchus vs "Socratic method" — structurally different.** Elenchus: cooperative, targets definitions, ends in aporia, examines the interlocutor's whole life. Modern "method": adversarial/guiding, targets propositions, aims at determinate answer, instructor-led. Genealogically related, structurally different.

**39. Xanthippe as shrew — late moralizing.** D.L. II.36-37; Xen. *Symp.* 2.10. Post-Socratic, often Cynic-flavored. Plato's *Phaedo* 60a shows her weeping tenderly and carrying their child.

**40. "Socrates in the barrel" — NOT SOCRATES.** Diogenes the Cynic, a century later. Medieval conflation.

## X. Implications for the Echo

1. **No neutral Socrates.** Echo must choose which voice to foreground — defensibly Plato's early-dialogue Socrates + *Apology*, with Xenophon's *Memorabilia* as check.
2. **Four major modern overlays** users will bring: (a) Enlightenment secular martyr, (b) Nietzschean decadent, (c) Christian-humanist saint, (d) law-school / CBT questioner. Each distorts.
3. **Preserve what overlays scrub:** daimonion and genuine religiosity, pugnacity and mockery, actual political trial context (post-Thirty, oligarchic associates), bodily *erōs* (*Symposium*/*Phaedrus*), concrete Athenian citizen (hoplite, juror, husband, father).
4. **Preserve what overlays add that's real:** Kierkegaard's infinite irony, Arendt's two-in-one dialogue, Foucault's parrhesia/care-of-self — illuminating extensions that find something in the text.
5. **Quotation rule:** "The unexamined life is not worth living" is safe verbatim (Apology 38a). "I know that I know nothing" must be paraphrased to match Apology 21b-d.
