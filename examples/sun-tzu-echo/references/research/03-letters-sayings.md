# Agent 3 — Letters, Sayings, and Voice: Sun Wu

## Scope note

Sun Wu left no verified letters, private correspondence, dialogues, or recorded conversations. The only voice-bearing corpus is the *Sunzi Bingfa* (孫子兵法). The earliest strong witness is the **Yinqueshan Han bamboo-slip manuscript** (Silver Sparrow Mountain, Shandong; tomb sealed c. 140-118 BCE, excavated 1972), confirming the 13-chapter structure and most received readings.

Labels: `[documented]` = received text; `[attested]` = additionally confirmed in Yinqueshan; `[attributed]` = later accretion / contested.

---

## 1. Prose style of the 13 chapters

**Core consensus.** Sun Wu writes in aphoristic, gnomic, telegraphic classical Chinese. The received text runs only ~6,000 characters across 13 chapters; content-density is higher than any comparable pre-Qin military treatise.

- "Characterized by its aphoristic style ... often highly abbreviated and elliptical, with both subject and object as well as other parts of the sentence omitted, and even whole clauses dispensed with - a telegraphic style at times carried to an extreme, making the text extraordinarily terse and maddeningly obscure." [web: https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/literature-and-writing/sunzi]
- "A characteristic structural feature of the Sunzi is that its maxims are frequently loosely linked by the conjunction *gu* ('therefore')."
- Steven C. Combs names the mode **"the rhetoric of parsimony"** — "extreme economy in the expenditure of resources ... peaceful and passive, favoring silence over speech." [web: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/00335630009384297]
- Minford: "lucid, epigrammatic, almost poetic style."

**Dominant structural devices**
1. **Parallelism and antithesis.** Paired cola, 2- or 4-part symmetry. Core pairs: 形/勢 (form/configuration), 虛/實 (empty/full), 奇/正 (unorthodox/orthodox), 攻/守, 勞/佚, 主/客.
2. **Enumerated lists.** 五事, 七計, 九變, 九地, 五危, 六敗, 五間.
3. **Conditional constructions.** 若...則..., 能...則..., 不...不....
4. **Antithetical couplets.** E.g. Ch. 7: "高陵勿向，背丘勿逆，佯北勿從，銳卒勿攻，餌兵勿食，歸師勿遏，圍師必闕，窮寇勿迫."
5. **Loose 故 linkage.** Maxims chained with 故 and 是故 without explicit argument.
6. **Compression through ellipsis** (subjects/objects dropped).

**Composition history.** Text assembled c. 5th–3rd c. BCE, from oral military instruction through layered composition, editorial consolidation, and centuries of commentary. Yinqueshan confirms 13-chapter structure by early Han.

## 2. Signature sentence patterns

| Pattern | Gloss | Function |
|---|---|---|
| 孫子曰… | "Master Sun said…" | Opens each chapter |
| 故曰… (*gù yuē*) | "Therefore it is said…" | Capstones a maxim |
| 是故… (*shì gù*) | "For this reason…" | Strong cause-effect |
| 故… (*gù*) | "Therefore…" | Loose chain connector |
| 凡… (*fán*) | "In all cases of…" | Opens universal principle / taxonomy |
| 善…者 (*shàn…zhě*) | "One skilled at X…" | Defines ideal actor |
| 知…者 (*zhī…zhě*) | "One who knows…" | Epistemic conditional |
| …者，…也 | "X is Y." | Definitional copula — the text's hardest lines |
| 不可不… | "Cannot not…" | Absolute imperative |
| …勿… | "Do not…" | Prohibition in enumeration |
| 必… (*bì*) | "Must, surely, inevitably" | Certainty marker |

Frequency: 故 is the single most frequent logical connector; 凡 opens normative claims; 善…者 defines Sun Wu's preferred idealized subject.

## 3. Iconic quotations (26 passages) with provenance

All below are **[documented]** in the received text; those also **[attested]** in Yinqueshan flagged explicitly.

### Chapter 1 — 始計 Laying Plans

**Q1** [documented; attested] 兵者，國之大事，死生之地，存亡之道，不可不察也。 *Bīng zhě, guó zhī dà shì, sǐ shēng zhī dì, cún wáng zhī dào, bù kě bù chá yě.* — Ames 1993: "War is a vital matter of state. It is the field on which life or death is determined and the road that leads to either survival or ruin."

**Q2** [documented; attested] 兵者，詭道也。 *Bīng zhě, guǐ dào yě.* — Giles: "All warfare is based on deception." Followed by: 能而示之不能，用而示之不用，近而示之遠，遠而示之近.

**Q3** [documented] 故經之以五事…一曰道，二曰天，三曰地，四曰將，五曰法。 — Note 天 here = weather/seasons, not moral Heaven.

### Chapter 2 — 作戰 Waging War

**Q4** [documented] 兵聞拙速，未睹巧之久也。 "In war we have heard of clumsy speed, but we have never seen cleverness in protraction."

**Q5** [documented] 故智將務食於敵。 "A wise general makes a point of foraging on the enemy."

### Chapter 3 — 謀攻 Attack by Stratagem

**Q6** [documented; attested] 故上兵伐謀，其次伐交，其次伐兵，其下攻城。 Sawyer: "The highest realization of warfare is to attack the enemy's plans; next is to attack their alliances; next to attack their army; and the lowest is to attack their fortified cities."

**Q7** [documented; attested] 是故百戰百勝，非善之善者也；不戰而屈人之兵，善之善者也。 "To fight and conquer in all your battles is not supreme excellence; supreme excellence consists in breaking the enemy's resistance without fighting."

**Q8** [documented; attested] 知彼知己，百戰不殆；不知彼而知己，一勝一負；不知彼，不知己，每戰必殆。 The **tripartite** epistemic structure — pop quotes only the first clause.

### Chapter 4 — 軍形 Dispositions

**Q9** [documented] 昔之善戰者，先為不可勝，以待敵之可勝。不可勝在己，可勝在敵。 "The good fighters of old first put themselves beyond the possibility of defeat, and then waited for an opportunity of defeating the enemy."

**Q10** [documented] 故善戰者之勝也，無智名，無勇功。 "His victories bring him neither reputation for wisdom nor credit for courage ... He wins his battles by making no mistakes." **The anti-heroic signature maxim.**

### Chapter 5 — 兵勢 Energy / Strategic Configuration

**Q11** [documented; attested] 凡戰者，以正合，以奇勝。 Sawyer: "In battle one engages with the orthodox and gains victory through the unorthodox."

**Q12** [documented] 奇正之變，不可勝窮也。奇正相生，如循環之無端。 "The variations of unorthodox and orthodox cannot be exhausted; they mutually give birth to each other like an endless cycle."

**Q13** [documented] 鷙鳥之疾，至於毀折者，節也。 "The quality of decision is like the well-timed swoop of a falcon which enables it to strike and destroy its victim." The point is timing (*jié*), not violence.

**Q14** [documented] 故善戰人之勢，如轉圓石於千仞之山者，勢也。 "The *shi* of a skilled warrior is like rolling a round boulder down a mountain a thousand fathoms high."

### Chapter 6 — 虛實 Empty and Full

**Q15** [documented; attested] 故善戰者，致人而不致於人。 "The clever combatant imposes his will on the enemy, but does not allow the enemy's will to be imposed on him."

**Q16** [documented; attested] 夫兵形象水…水因地而制流，兵因敵而制勝。故兵無常勢，水無常形。 "Military tactics are like water... Water shapes its course according to the ground; the soldier works out his victory in relation to his foe. Therefore, as water retains no constant shape, so in warfare there are no constant conditions." **Sun Wu's only extended analogy.**

**Q17** [documented] 故形兵之極，至於無形。無形，則深間不能窺，智者不能謀。 "The ultimate disposition is to be without ascertainable shape. Then the most penetrating spies cannot pry in, nor can the wise lay plans against you."

### Chapter 7 — 軍爭 Maneuvering

**Q18** [documented] 故其疾如風，其徐如林，侵掠如火，不動如山。 "Let your rapidity be that of the wind, your compactness that of the forest. In raiding and plundering be like fire, be immovable like a mountain." Basis of Takeda Shingen's 風林火山 banner (with reception-overlay caveats — see Agent 6).

### Chapter 8 — 九變 Variations

**Q19** [documented] 故將有五危：必死可殺也，必生可虜也，忿速可侮也，廉潔可辱也，愛民可煩也。 "There are five dangerous faults which may affect a general: recklessness, cowardice, a hasty temper, a delicacy of honor, over-solicitude for his men." **Virtues diagnosed as exploitable liabilities.**

### Chapter 9 — 行軍 On the March

**Q20** [documented] 辭卑而益備者，進也；辭強而進驅者，退也。 "Humble words and increased preparations are signs the enemy is about to advance; violent language and driving forward as if to attack are signs of retreat." Observational-anthropological tells.

### Chapter 10 — 地形 Terrain

**Q21** [documented] 知彼知己，勝乃不殆；知天知地，勝乃可全。 "If you know the enemy and know yourself, your victory will not stand in doubt; if you know Heaven and know Earth, you may make your victory complete." Completing pair to Q8.

### Chapter 11 — 九地 Nine Situations

**Q22** [documented] 兵之情主速，乘人之不及。 "The spirit-essence of war is speed; take advantage of the enemy's unreadiness."

**Q23** [documented] 投之亡地然後存，陷之死地然後生。 "Throw them into a position from which there is no escape, and they will prefer death to flight... Plunge an army into desperate straits and it will come off in safety."

### Chapter 12 — 火攻 Attack by Fire

**Q24** [documented] 主不可以怒而興師，將不可以慍而致戰。 "No ruler should put troops into the field merely to gratify his own spleen; no general should fight a battle simply out of pique." The near-ethical line — but still pragmatic.

### Chapter 13 — 用間 Use of Spies

**Q25** [documented; attested] 先知者，不可取於鬼神，不可象於事，不可驗於度，必取於人，知敵之情者也。 "Now this foreknowledge cannot be elicited from spirits; it cannot be obtained inductively from experience, nor by any deductive calculation. Knowledge of the enemy's dispositions can only be obtained from other men." **The single most philosophically loaded line** — explicit rejection of divination, analogy, and a priori calculation.

**Q26** [documented] 故用間有五：有鄉間，有內間，有反間，有死間，有生間。 Local / inward / converted / doomed / surviving spies. Treatise closes on this cold taxonomy — no peroration, no moral coda.

## 4. Rhetoric: near-absence of analogy

Distinctive feature. Sun Wu uses **only a handful of analogies** across the whole treatise, all drawn from nature or physical dynamics — never from social life or moral exemplars:

1. **Water 水** — Ch. 6. Only analogy developed at paragraph length.
2. **Falcon / raptor 鷙鳥** — Ch. 5. One sentence, the timing of a stoop.
3. **Rolling round boulder** — Ch. 5. Shi as gravitational potential.
4. **Wind, forest, fire, mountain** — Ch. 7. Four similes in strict parallelism.
5. **Endless cycle 環** — Ch. 5. Qi-zheng mutual generation.
6. **Fire and flood** — Ch. 12.

**No historical exempla. No Yao, Shun, Yu, Tang, Wen, Wu, Zhou Gong. No legendary cases. No named predecessors.** Anomalous for pre-Qin Chinese texts.

**Generation rule for an Echo:** analogies only from physical nature (water, wind, fire, terrain, raptor, rolling stone) — never domestic/social life, never legendary figures.

## 5. Tone: detached, clinical, cold

- "Sun Tzu is the ultimate pragmatist; winning without fighting is a pragmatic goal, not a moral ideal."
- **No Mandate-of-Heaven argument.** When Sun Wu uses 天 *tiān* it is operational meteorology — "day and night, cold and heat, times and seasons" (Ch. 1). Sharp departure from *Shujing*-style military rhetoric.
- **Rejection of divination.** Ch. 13's 不可取於鬼神 is programmatic. Sun Wu **de-sacralizes war**; it becomes a technical problem.
- **No emotional register.** No anger, patriotism, compassion-rhetoric, glorification of battle, contempt for enemy. Soldiers are "driven like sheep" (若驅群羊, Ch. 11). Q19 lists personal honour and love-of-men as exploitable faults.

---

## 6. Certainty signaling

Sun Wu speaks in **maxims and definitions**, not hedges.
- **必 (*bì*)** — dominant modal. 必以全爭於天下 (Ch. 3), 必取於人 (Ch. 13), 必敗/必勝/必殆.
- **是故 / 故** — blunt causal conclusions.
- **…者，…也** — pure definitional copula; carries the most lapidary lines.
- **無…** absolute negatives: 兵無常勢，水無常形.
- 或 (*huò*) as hedge is rare.

Voice register: engineer stating tolerances, not philosopher exploring possibilities.

---

## 7. Citation practice: Sun Wu cites no one

- No Confucius, no Taigong by name, no sage-kings, no "the ancients say" (beyond impersonal 故曰).
- No historical battles as exempla. Unlike Sun Bin's *Bingfa* (also at Yinqueshan) which cites Guiling, Sun Wu's received text is ahistorical.
- Andrew Meyer: "Master Sun" was constructed as a Warring States "Master" on par with Confucius, Mozi, Zhuangzi, engaging in the Masters discourse as an original voice.

Rhetorical effect: mathematician stating theorems. Doctrine presented as axiomatic.

## 8. Voice through translation: five traditions

| Translator | Idiom | Voice impact | Best for |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Giles 1910** | Victorian/Edwardian scholarly; "Hence," "he who..." | Softens clinical edge into gentlemanly staff-officer voice; imports Christianizing moral register | Historical reception; public-domain reference |
| **Ames 1993** | Philosophical, process-oriented; Hall-Ames program; first to incorporate Yinqueshan | Recasts Sun Wu as philosopher of situational potential; interpretive overlay can expand terseness | Conceptual architecture (*shi*/*xing*/*qi*-*zheng*) |
| **Mair 2007** | Plain, literal, philologically exacting; reproduces classical Chinese grammar | **Best preserves terseness and ellipsis at sentence level** | Defensible literal baseline |
| **Sawyer 1994** | Military-historical, technical | Makes Sun Wu sound like a serious military theorist; operational clarity, little literary grace | Military-historical analysis |
| **Minford 2002** | Literary, crisp, epigrammatic | **Best preserves aphoristic punch in English** — maxim-rhythm, wisdom-literature quality | Feeling Sun Wu as a stylist |

**Recommended target register for this Echo: Mair-Minford hybrid** — Mair's grammatical compression plus Minford's epigrammatic polish. Avoid Giles's moralizing warmth and Sawyer's staff-college flatness.

## 9. Operational checklist for reproducing Sun Wu's voice

1. Short. Parallel. Paired. If A, then not-A. Subjects optional.
2. Chain with 故 ("therefore"). Conclude, don't argue.
3. Open universal claims with 凡.
4. Positive principles via 善…者 ("one skilled at…").
5. Knowledge claims via 知…者.
6. Strongest lines as 者…也 definitional copula.
7. 必 as certainty marker. No hedges.
8. Enumerate: fives, sevens, nines.
9. No historical exempla, no named predecessors, no sage-kings.
10. Analogies only from nature (water, wind, fire, mountain, terrain, raptor, rolling stone).
11. No Heaven's will, no moralizing, no emotion. Cold-engineer register.
12. State virtues as vulnerabilities (honour, courage, love-of-men are exploitable).
13. War as a cost-bounded technical problem. Winning-without-fighting > winning-by-fighting. Protraction always worse than clumsy speed.
14. Argument carried by antithetical pairs: 形/勢, 虛/實, 奇/正, 攻/守, 主/客, 勞/佚.
15. Ideal victory leaves no trace: 無智名，無勇功.
