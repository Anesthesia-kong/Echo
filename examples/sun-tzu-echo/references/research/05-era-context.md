# Agent 5 — Era Context for Sun Wu (孙武), ~544–496 BCE

Labels: `[documented]` surviving pre-imperial / early-imperial source · `[attested]` later traditional Chinese historiography · `[modern-scholarly-inference]` consensus of modern sinology.

---

## 1. Political Geography: A Collapsing Zhou Order

**1.1 The 771 BCE rupture.** Allied Shen and Quanrong forces sacked the Western Zhou capital and killed King You. King Ping relocated east to Luoyang in 770 BCE, inaugurating Eastern Zhou. [documented/attested]

Eastern Zhou subdivides into Spring and Autumn (770–476 BCE, named for the Lu chronicle *Chunqiu*) and Warring States (475–221 BCE). Sun Wu sits at the very end of Spring and Autumn.

~150 feudal statelets in Eastern Zhou; ~540 inter-state wars over two centuries; ~15 states left by 475 BCE.

**1.2 The hegemon (霸 bà) system.** Zhou king conferred *bà* on the militarily dominant duke. "Five Hegemons" sequence (c. 685–591 BCE): Duke Huan of Qi (r. 685–643, w/ Guan Zhong), Duke Xiang of Song, Duke Wen of Jin, Duke Mu of Qin, King Zhuang of Chu. By Sun Wu's lifetime the hegemon role had decayed; Jin, Chu, Qi, and Wu operated as independent centers.

**1.3 Major states Sun Wu knew.**
- **Jin 晋** — northern hegemon, internally fragmenting toward the 453 BCE partition.
- **Qi 齐** — Sun Wu's reputed birth-state (Shandong); Guan Zhong's reforms: salt/iron monopolies, 15-township grid, civil-military unification — proto-Legalist template.
- **Chu 楚** — huge Yangtze-middle state; othered as semi-barbarian but a developed Zhou-descended polity, rulers styled themselves *wang* in defiance of Zhou.
- **Wu 吴** — Sun Wu's employer; Yangtze-mouth kingdom.
- **Yue 越** — Wu's coastal rival south, Hangzhou Bay.
- **Qin 秦** — still peripheral.
- **Song, Lu, Zheng, Wei, Chen, Cai** — smaller central-plains buffers.

**1.4 Non-Zhou peripheries.** Rong (west), Di (north), Man (south), Yi (east) — heterogeneous, not a unified ethnos.

## 2. Shift from Ritual Warfare to Instrumental Warfare

**2.1 Western Zhou / early Spring-Autumn model.** Aristocratic chariot warfare; canonical 72 infantry per chariot, actually ~10 in early Spring-Autumn rising to ~75 by late Warring States. Single-day battles preceded by ritual declarations, sacrifices, divinations, mourning observance. [modern-scholarly-inference, per Lewis *Sanctioned Violence*]

*Sima Fa* (司马法): warfare justified only by rescuing people from tyrannical government, must not antagonize enemy populace; Warring-States compilation likely preserving older Qi ritual-military doctrine.

**2.2 Battle of Hong (泓水之战), 638 BCE.** Duke Xiang of Song, against Ziyu's urging, refused to strike Chu mid-crossing, then again before Chu formed up (不鼓不成列); Song routed, Xiang wounded, died next year. [documented, *Zuozhuan*]

Ziyu's critique called "Chinese literature's earliest outline" of themes Sunzi later expanded. **This is Sunzi's cautionary tale.**

**2.3 Concurrent transformations (7th–6th c. BCE).**
- **Chariot → mass infantry.** Jin: ~632 chariots 632 BCE → ~4,900 a century later.
- **Campaign length.** Spring-Autumn campaigns bounded by agricultural cycle. Warring States campaigns stretched past a year. Sunzi Ch. 2 obsessively argues against long war.
- **Siegecraft.** Attacking walled cities emerges as discipline in Sun Wu's era. Sunzi Ch. 3: "the worst policy is to attack cities."
- **Crossbow (弩).** Bronze lock mechanisms not archaeologically attested much before ~600 BCE; battlefield staple only in Warring States; first clear battle use Maling 341 BCE. (Anachronism argument against traditional dating.)
- **Iron.** Iron plowshares → higher yields → larger sustainable armies → longer wars → professional standing armies displacing feudal levies.

**2.4 Sun Wu's position.** Writes on the far side of the Hong-River ethos — total war, deception (*bingzhe guidao* 兵者诡道), strike-the-unready as default. Still mentions chariots (Ch. 2), so pre-dates Warring-States infantry professionalization. **He is the transitional hinge: post-ritual, pre-Legalist.**

## 3. State of Wu as Sun Wu's Operational Environment

**3.1 Geography.** Southern Jiangsu (capital eventually Suzhou), Yangtze mouth; deltaic, marshy, Lake Tai system. Ruling lineage claimed Zhou ancestry (Taibo) but customs (cropped hair, tattooing) shared with Yue / Baiyue. Under King Shoumeng (r. 585–561 BCE) adopted northern administration / chariotry; under Helü (r. 514–496) and Fuchai (r. 495–473) projected force far inland.

**3.2 Terrain and unconventional tactics.** First recognizable Chinese navy with differentiated ship classes. Wu Zixu built canal network from Lake Tai into Yangtze/Huai systems (irrigation + military logistics). This backs Sunzi's detailed terrain chapters (Ch. 9 *Xingjun*, Ch. 10 *Dixing*, Ch. 11 *Jiudi*).

**3.3 Boju campaign / capture of Ying (506 BCE).** Helü, Wu Zixu, and (per Shiji) Sun Wu led Wu + Cai + Tang alliance; five victories; Chu capital Ying taken; Wu Zixu exhumed King Ping's corpse and whipped it 300 times. First time a "barbarian" peripheral state took the southern hegemon's capital. [documented, *Zuozhuan*, *Shiji*]

**3.4 Wu-Yue rivalry.** Helü killed 496 BCE by Goujian's Yue; Fuchai defeated Goujian at Fujiao (494 BCE) but let him live; Goujian rebuilt Yue; 482 BCE surprise attack on Wu capital while Fuchai campaigned north; Yue annexed Wu 473 BCE.

**3.5 Wu Zixu 伍子胥 as advisor archetype.** Chu exile (father/brother executed by King Ping), fled to Wu 522 BCE, orchestrated 514 BCE coup installing Helü, chief strategist. *Shiji* credits him with recommending Sun Wu to Helü. The template that becomes the Warring-States *shi* 士 and later *moushi* 谋士.

**3.6 Concubine-training anecdote.** *Shiji*: Helü asks Sun Wu to drill 180 concubines; Sun Wu executes the two favored company commanders when they disobey. Historicity disputed — *Zuozhuan* never mentions Sun Wu. [attested; disputed]

## 4. Pre-Sunzi Military Intellectual Tradition

**4.1 Sima Fa.** Attributed to Sima Rangju of Qi; received text = 4th c. BCE Qi compilation, likely preserving older ritual-military doctrine. Civil/military complementarity (*wen/wu*), only-just-war, protect enemy populace.

**4.2 Taigong / Liutao (六韬).** Attributed to Jiang Ziya (11th c. BCE Zhou founder-advisor); received text Warring-States / early-Han compilation.

**4.3 What Sun Wu drops / keeps / invents.**
- **Drops:** *tianming* framing, ritual-justification of war, divination as operational input.
- **Keeps:** lord's strategist figure, ruler-general relationship, hierarchical command, discipline, terrain/logistics.
- **Invents/foregrounds:** calculability of victory (5 factors + 7 comparisons, Ch. 1); deception as constitutive (*bingzhe guidao*); victory without battle as highest form; anti-omen injunction (禁祥去疑, Ch. 11); qualified commander-independence from sovereign ("there are orders of the sovereign that must not be obeyed," Ch. 8).

## 5. Ideological Environment

**5.1 Near-contemporary voices.**
- **Confucius (551–479 BCE)** — Lu; one generation after Sun Wu; different cultural zone. No evidence of mutual engagement. Sunzi uses *no* Confucian ethical vocabulary (ren, yi, li as moral category, junzi).
- **Laozi / Daodejing** — text is 4th–3rd c. BCE Warring-States; Laozi-contemporary-of-Confucius is a traditional claim. Naturalist / water / yielding themes may share late Spring-Autumn proto-Daoist sensibility in the Yangtze zone; direct textual influence indeterminable.
- **Mozi, Mencius, Xunzi, Han Fei, Sun Bin** all later. Sun Wu has no Legalist vocabulary, no Mohist to rebut, no systematic cosmology.

**5.2 Mandate of Heaven.** Zhou's founding ideological innovation — rule conditional on virtue, losing mandate justifies regime change. By Sun Wu's day, already hollowed out by the Zhou king's impotence. Sunzi writes as if heavenly sanction simply doesn't enter the calculation.

**5.3 Divination in military decision.** Turtle-shell (卜) and milfoil (筮, *Yijing*) divination standard before campaigns. *Zuozhuan* records dozens. Sunzi Ch. 11.26 *jin xiang qu yi* (禁祥去疑, "forbid omens, banish superstitious doubts") programmatically overrides default practice — not skepticism but disciplinary unit-cohesion move.

**5.4 Rise of 士 *shi* / strategist class.** Hereditary nobility monopolized office, but inter-state competition pushed rulers to appoint commoners and foreigners. Wu Zixu (Chu → Wu), Sun Wu (Qi → Wu), Fan Li (→ Yue), Guan Zhong (commoner → Qi chief minister). Crystallizes in Warring-States mobile *shi* — "serving all-under-heaven."

## 6. Economic Base and Cost of War

Iron-age agriculture (iron plowshares, mass irrigation), demographic growth, proto-commerce (Qi salt/iron monopolies, inter-state trade). Armies drew peasant labor via corvée-style levies; campaigns beyond winter/slack season bled productive capacity.

Sunzi Ch. 2 *Zuozhan*: "100,000 mailed troops thrown over 1,000 *li*, daily expense 1,000 gold ... sustained war never benefits a state" — **literal accounting, not rhetoric**. The prescription "forage on the enemy" is the operational conclusion.

## 7. What Sun Wu's Original Readers Took for Granted (that Modern Readers Do Not)

1. **Multi-state competition is the normal human condition.** No "China" yet as polity; "all-under-heaven" (天下) = the civilized Zhou world of competing states. Unified empire unthinkable.
2. **The ruler (君) is a singular concrete human being whose person IS the strategic decision-node.** "Advising the sovereign" is literal.
3. **Warfare is normal, recurring statecraft — not exceptional emergency.** Sunzi Ch. 1's "war is the great affair of state" is notable because rulers take war too *lightly*, not because war is rare.
4. **Divination is the default method of resolving uncertainty.** Sunzi's 禁祥去疑 is a *conspicuous* disciplinary move, not an obvious rationalist stance.
5. **The army's mass is peasant bodies under aristocratic command.** Every campaign-day costs harvest; every death costs a future tax-household. Sunzi's economic concern is structural, not humanitarian.
6. **Ritual-aristocratic warfare is living memory, not ancient history.** Hong River was ~130 years before Sun Wu — his readers *know* the Duke Xiang story; his instrumentalism is a deliberate break.
7. **Heaven (天), omens, cosmic resonance are ambient background, not operational inputs.** Sunzi's "heaven" in Ch. 1 = yin-yang / seasons / weather — natural, not theological.
8. **Advisors / strategists increasingly cross state borders.** Qi-man serving Wu against Chu raises no eyebrow; loyalty attaches to employer, not "nation."
9. **Chariots, infantry, navy, and walled cities coexist in one army's toolkit.** Readers have a working vocabulary for *sheng* (chariot-units), *zu* (infantry squads), riverine ops, sieges.
10. **Speed and deception are not dishonorable.** The moral break with the *Sima Fa* world. "All warfare is based on deception" — still contestable claim in Sun Wu's audience, not yet a cliché.

## 9. Bottom Line for the Echo

Sun Wu is a **transitional hinge figure.** He writes:
- *After* the *Sima Fa*'s ritual-warfare world has visibly failed (Duke Xiang of Song is his cautionary tale).
- *Before* the Warring States' fully professional mass-infantry bureaucratic war-state.
- From the margin (Wu, semi-barbarian Yangtze kingdom), not the Zhou-orthodox center.
- Inside a specific command relationship (King Helü + advisor-strategist, Wu Zixu as political patron).
- Into an audience that still believes in omens, ritual propriety, aristocratic honor — which is why his instrumentalism had to be *argued*, not assumed.

Every signature move in the *Art of War* maps onto a specific feature of the late Spring-and-Autumn world. Reading him without this context produces a sanitized life-coach; reading him with it produces a cold late-bronze-age state-advisor speaking in a room where the ruler is present, peasants are dying in rice fields, and a chariot-led army is 30 days from starving.
