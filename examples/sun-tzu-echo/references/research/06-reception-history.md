# Agent 6 — Reception History

Labels: `[documented]` (primary citation / dated artefact), `[attested]` (later credible transmission), `[modern-scholarly-inference]`.

---

## 1. Warring States to Han (c. 475 BCE – 220 CE): circulation and canonization

- *Sunzi* circulated as a recognized *bingjia* text by late Warring States. Andrew Meyer treats it as a Warring-States "masters" (*zi*) text rather than single-author Spring-and-Autumn product. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`
- **Han Feizi** (late 3rd c. BCE) notes the books of Sun Wu and Wu Qi are "in every household" — strong evidence of pre-Han canonical status. `[attested]`
- **Sima Qian, *Shiji* (c. 97 BCE)** preserves the ur-biography. Already ~400 years after the putative events. `[documented]`
- **Yinqueshan 银雀山 bamboo slips** (Linyi, Shandong, excavated 10 April 1972). Tombs sealed c. 140–118 BCE. 13 chapters of *Sunzi Bingfa* + 5 extras, plus a separate *Sun Bin Bingfa*. Settles that (i) the text was textually stable in early Han, (ii) Sun Wu and Sun Bin are distinct figures. `[documented]`

## 2. Three Kingdoms — Cao Cao's commentary (c. 200 CE)

- **Cao Cao 曹操 (155–220 CE)** wrote 《孙子略解》 / 《魏武帝注孙子》, the earliest surviving and most influential commentary. He explicitly edited/pruned the text; pre-Yinqueshan, the "received *Sunzi*" is effectively Cao Cao's edition. `[documented]`
- His reading is that of a practical general — logistics, terrain, tempo, speed vs. protractedness.
- **Reception flag:** Chinese culture's "Sun Tzu" is largely Cao Cao's Sun Tzu.

## 3. Tang (618–907): scholar-poet commentaries

- **Du Mu 杜牧 (803–852)**, major late-Tang poet, wrote a full thirteen-chapter commentary — literary/historically-illustrated register. `[documented]`
- Other Tang commentators preserved in the Song collation: Li Quan, Du You (*Tongdian*), Meng Shi, Chen Hao. `[attested]`

## 4. Song (960–1279): canon, collation, skepticism

- **《十一家注孙子》 *Shiyijia zhu Sunzi*** (Southern Song) canonizes 11 commentators: Cao Cao, Meng Shi, Li Quan, Du You, Du Mu, Chen Hao, Jia Lin, Mei Yaochen, Wang Xi, He Yanxi, Zhang Yu. Backbone of nearly every later scholarly edition. `[documented]`
- **《武经七书》 Seven Military Classics, 1080 CE** (Emperor Shenzong, ed. He Qufei). *Sunzi* placed first. Required for military exams ~800 years. `[documented]`
- **Ye Shi 叶适 (1150–1223)** — noticed Sun Wu absent from *Zuozhuan* despite Sima Qian's Boju claim. First sustained historicity doubt. Chen Zhensun 陈振孙 followed. `[documented]`

## 5. Ming–Qing (1368–1911)

- **Mao Yuanyi, 《武备志》 *Wubei Zhi* (1621)** — 240-*juan* encyclopedia; opening section anthologizes the Seven Classics, declaring *Sunzi* the apex. `[documented]`
- **Sun Xingyan 孙星衍** (Qing) produced the *Shijia zhu Sunzi* edition that became the base-text for Giles 1910 and most subsequent English translations. `[documented]`
- **Late Qing reform reading:** Self-Strengthening Movement (c. 1861–1895). **Zeng Guofan** read *Sunzi*'s five generalship virtues through a Confucian filter. `[attested]`

## 6. Japanese reception (8th c. onward)

- **Kibi no Makibi 吉備真備** (693–775) traditionally credited with bringing *Sunzi* to Japan c. 735 CE. `[attested]`
- **Takeda Shingen and *Fūrinkazan* 风林火山** compresses *Sunzi* Ch. 7 ("疾如风,徐如林,侵掠如火,不动如山"). **Critical caveat** — the four-character slogan itself is largely an **Edo / 20th-century reception overlay**, popularized by Inoue Yasushi's 1953 novel, Kurosawa's *Kagemusha* (1980), and NHK's 2007 taiga drama. Little contemporary Sengoku evidence Shingen actually flew a banner with this exact compressed slogan. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

## 7. Early Western reception (1772–1910)

- **Jean-Joseph-Marie Amiot**, Jesuit at the Qing court, *Art militaire des Chinois* (Paris, 1772) — first European-language translation; paraphrastic. Rumored Napoleon influence is uncorroborated. `[documented]`
- **E. F. Calthrop, *Sonshi* (Tokyo 1905; London 1908)** — first English, via Japanese mediation; Giles called it "excessively bad." `[documented]`
- **Lionel Giles (1910)**, British Museum curator — first philologically serious English, from Sun Xingyan's Ten-Commentator edition. Giles's text is the public-domain substrate for most 20th-c. pop reprints (including Clavell 1983). `[documented]`

## 8. 20th-c. revolutionary reception

- **Mao Zedong, 《论持久战》 *On Protracted War* (1938)** — deeply Sunzian in category (知己知彼, *shi*, turning weakness into strength); first modern revolutionary re-reading. `[documented]`
- **Võ Nguyên Giáp** — Điện Biên Phủ 1954; studied in China during WWII; cited *Sunzi*. 20th-c. anti-colonial asymmetric-warfare template. `[attested]`
- **Samuel B. Griffith, *Sun Tzu: The Art of War* (OUP 1963)**, foreword by B. H. Liddell Hart. Standard US service-academy text for decades; introduction frames *Sunzi* against Mao's guerrilla doctrine — so Sun Tzu reaches Cold War Western militaries **pre-framed by China and Vietnam**. `[documented]`
- **John Boyd (USAF, 1927–1997)** — OODA loop (1970s–80s); *Sunzi* his "Rosetta stone," owned seven translations with heavy marginalia. OODA is in large part a Western re-encoding of *Sunzi*'s *shi*/tempo/deception complex. `[documented]`
- **Harry G. Summers Jr., *On Strategy* (1982)** — launches the durable Clausewitz-vs-Sunzi pedagogic binary in US military education. `[documented]`
- **Michael I. Handel, *Masters of War* (1992/2001)** — scholarly corrective: Clausewitz and *Sunzi* overlap more than they differ; sharpest divergence is deception and winning-without-battle. `[documented]`

## 9. Post-Yinqueshan scholarly retranslation

- **Roger T. Ames, *Sun-Tzu: The Art of Warfare* (Ballantine 1993)** — first English to incorporate Yinqueshan; Daoist-adjacent philosophical framing. `[documented]`
- **Victor H. Mair (Columbia, 2007)** — text coalesced c. mid-4th–early-3rd c. BCE. `[documented]`
- **John Minford (Penguin, 2002)** — reintroduces the *Shiyijia zhu* commentator layer to English readers. `[documented]`
- **Derek M. C. Yuen, *Deciphering Sun Tzu* (OUP 2014)** — explicit reception-history restorative: Western reading "has rarely moved beyond facile references to short one-to-two-sentence axioms"; Daoist substrate stripped. `[documented]`

## 10. Pop-business and Hollywood (1980s–present)

- **James Clavell's 1983 introduction to Giles** (Delacorte) — on-ramp for the "Sun Tzu for managers" explosion. `[attested]`
- **Mark McNeilly, *Sun Tzu and the Art of Business* (OUP 1996)** and the derivative *Sun Tzu for X* shelf — reception, not *Sunzi*. `[attested]`
- **Oliver Stone, *Wall Street* (1987)** — Gordon Gekko's "Read Sun Tzu… every battle is won before it is ever fought" fixes "Sun Tzu = ruthless deception" in Anglophone pop imagination. `[documented]`
- Later echoes: *The Sopranos*, Robert Greene's *48 Laws of Power* (1998), RZA / Wu-Tang, hustle-culture quoting. Vice (2019) documented "Orientalism for the hustle-porn generation."

## 11. Popular misreadings and reception overlays (what pop "Sun Tzu" gets wrong)

1. **"All warfare is based on deception" as general amoral cunning.** 兵者,诡道也 is a *technical* statement about the operational domain of war, not an endorsement of lying in ordinary life. Pop readings (Gekko, Greene, hustle-quote culture) strip the framing.
2. **"Know your enemy and know yourself…" universalized and truncated.** The text (知彼知己,百战不殆) is **tripartite** — know both / know only self / know neither. Pop use drops clauses 2–3 and loses the whole epistemic structure. The "thousand battles, thousand victories" variant is apocryphal.
3. **"Subdue the enemy without fighting" misread as pacifism.** *Sunzi*'s 全 *quán* ideal is **economic** (war is catastrophically expensive, Ch. 2), not pacifist. *Sunzi* fights willingly; he insists on 先胜而后求战 — win first, fight second.
4. **Sun Tzu as timeless life-coach stripped of war.** The text presupposes concrete 4th–3rd c. BCE material conditions and a specific political metaphysics (sovereign/general/people aligned under 道). Axiom-extraction destroys this.
5. **"Sun Tzu" as a stable authorial personality.** The Western reader's "Sun Tzu" is in fact: a Warring-States masters-text → Cao Cao's 3rd-c. editorial hand → Song 11-commentators → Seven Military Classics canonization → Sun Xingyan's Qing edition → Giles 1910. **No unmediated voice underneath.**
6. **Clausewitz-vs-Sun-Tzu binary.** Pedagogically useful but overdrawn — a 20th-c. reception construct (Summers), not textual fact. Handel documents the large overlap.
7. **Apocrypha.** "Strategy without tactics is the slowest route to victory; tactics without strategy is the noise before defeat" and related invincibility-promising extensions are **not in any scholarly translation** and first appear in 1980s–2000s management literature.
8. **Fūrinkazan as "what Sun Tzu told Takeda Shingen to do."** Ch. 7's four phrases are genuine; the banner image is largely a 20th-c. Japanese literary/cinematic artefact retrojected.
9. **"Indirect approach"** (Lorge 2022) — Liddell Hart's pre-existing theory retrojected via Griffith 1963 foreword. Not dominant in Chinese or Japanese reading traditions.

## 12. Summary — reception layers vs. target figure

| Layer | Actually is | Pop treats it as |
|---|---|---|
| Cao Cao edition (c. 200 CE) | Wei-general editorial recension + commentary | "Sun Tzu's original words" |
| *Shiyijia zhu* (Song) | 11 Wei–Song commentators, Song editors' selection | (invisible to pop) |
| Giles 1910 | Late-Qing recension Englished in Victorian register | "The real Sun Tzu in English" |
| Clavell 1983 intro | Novelist repackaging Giles for business readers | "Sun Tzu for modern life" |
| *Wall Street* 1987 | One Hollywood scene | "Sun Tzu = ruthless deception" |
| Business-quote genre | Axioms stripped of military/material context | "Sun Tzu's timeless strategy" |
| *Fūrinkazan* imagery | Edo/20th-c. Japanese overlay on Sengoku Takeda | "What Sun Tzu told the samurai" |

**The historical/textual Sun Wu this Echo should target:** a Warring-States *bingjia* voice (or composite) transmitted via Cao Cao and the Song commentators, speaking about state warfare under pre-imperial Chinese material and political conditions, organized around *shi* 勢, *quán* 全, *zhī* 知, and cost-aversion toward prolonged campaigns.
