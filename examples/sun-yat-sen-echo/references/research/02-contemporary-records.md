# Sun Yat-sen — Contemporary Records

This dossier compiles how Sun Yat-sen (孫中山 / 孫逸仙, 1866–1925) appeared to contemporaries — personal witnesses, journalists, foreign observers, and political opponents — within his lifetime and roughly a decade after his death. Each claim is labeled by evidentiary register. Disagreements between sources are preserved rather than harmonized.

---

## § Personal witnesses and intimates

### James Cantlie (1851–1926)

Cantlie was Sun's anatomy and surgery teacher at the Hong Kong College of Medicine for the Chinese (Sun graduated 1892) and remained a lifelong patron. He is the single most important Western witness for the 1896 London kidnapping and for Sun's early medical formation.

- Cantlie co-authored with C. Sheridan Jones *Sun Yat Sen and the Awakening of China* (London: Jarrold & Sons, 1912), written in the immediate wake of the 1911 revolution [documented] [web: https://archive.org/details/sunyatsenawakeni00cant].
- The book is hagiographic in tone: Cantlie describes Sun as "the most unassuming man I ever met" and stresses Sun's moral seriousness, medical competence, and Christian formation [attested].
- Cantlie's diary and correspondence for Oct 11–23 1896 — Sun had been detained inside the Chinese Legation at 49 Portland Place on Oct 11 — form the core record of the London kidnapping. Cantlie first alerted Scotland Yard, then the Foreign Office, then (when those channels moved slowly) the press [documented] [web: https://www.royalcollege.ac.uk/pastpresent/james-cantlie-and-sun-yat-sen].
- Sun himself later published *Kidnapped in London!* (Bristol: Arrowsmith, 1897); this is a first-person account, not an independent corroboration of Cantlie's.

**Bias flag:** Westerner-patron framing. Cantlie is affectionate, protective, and writes in the idiom of late-Victorian missionary-medical biography; his testimony for Sun's personal character is strong, but his account of Sun's political program often reflects Sun's own self-presentation to English sympathizers.

### Paul Myron Wentworth Linebarger (1871–1939)

An American legal adviser who served Sun intermittently from 1907 onward and later became a judge on the Philippine bench.

- *Sun Yat-sen and the Chinese Republic* (New York: Century, 1925) appeared the year of Sun's death and draws on Linebarger's personal conversations and papers [documented] [web: https://archive.org/details/sunyatsenchinese00line].
- *The Gospel of Chung Shan* (Paris: Brentano's / 1932) is more explicitly devotional and presents Sun's Three Principles (三民主義) as a moral-political gospel [attested].
- Linebarger attests to Sun's "boyish" optimism, his personal frugality (Sun reportedly travelled with one suitcase and small cash reserves), and his chronic fundraising anxiety [attested].

**Bias flag:** Westerner-patron framing overlapping with incipient KMT hagiography. Linebarger's son Paul M. A. Linebarger (the science-fiction writer "Cordwainer Smith") was Sun's godson, which gives a sense of the depth of personal alignment.

### Miyazaki Tōten 宮崎滔天 (1871–1922)

A Japanese pan-Asianist activist, Miyazaki met Sun in 1897 and introduced him to Japanese patrons (Inukai Tsuyoshi, Tōyama Mitsuru).

- *Sanjū-sannen no Yume* 三十三年の夢 (1902), translated as *My Thirty-Three Years' Dream*, is the single most important Japanese contemporary memoir of Sun's exile years [documented] [web: https://archive.org/details/my33yearsdream].
- Miyazaki portrays Sun as "a hero of the Orient" (東洋の英雄), emphasizing his composure in adversity, his refusal to abandon the revolutionary program after repeated failures, and his ability to inspire Japanese supporters who had no stake in Chinese politics [attested].
- Miyazaki describes Sun's living conditions in Yokohama and Tokyo as materially modest, with funds constantly redirected toward arms purchases and travel [attested].

**Bias flag:** Japanese Pan-Asianist framing. Miyazaki is writing partly to justify Japanese support for Chinese revolutionaries to a Japanese audience, and his Sun is shaped to fit an East-Asian-solidarity narrative.

### Wang Jingwei 汪精衛 (1883–1944)

Early Tongmenghui member, famed for his 1910 assassination attempt on the Qing Prince Regent, later Sun's close political secretary.

- Wang drafted Sun's political testament (總理遺囑) on 11 March 1925; the text opens "余致力國民革命凡四十年" ("I have devoted myself to the national revolution for forty years") [documented] [web: https://zh.wikisource.org/wiki/%E5%AD%AB%E6%96%87%E9%81%BA%E5%9A%B4].
- Wang's eye-witness account of Sun's final weeks at Peking Union Medical College Hospital is preserved in KMT party archives and quoted extensively in the *Guofu Nianpu* 國父年譜 [documented].
- Wang attests that Sun dictated revisions to the testament in a weak voice and that the final signing occurred the morning of 11 March, with Sun dying at 9:30 a.m. on 12 March [attested].

**Bias flag:** Wang later collaborated with Japan (1940 Nanjing government), which has led both KMT and PRC historiographies to edit Wang out of the deathbed narrative. Pre-1938 sources should be preferred.

### Hu Hanmin 胡漢民 (1879–1936)

A founding Tongmenghui member and one of the closest ideological lieutenants in the Tokyo period.

- Hu's autobiography *Hu Hanmin Zizhuan* 胡漢民自傳 (drafted 1929, published posthumously) describes the internal Tongmenghui quarrels of 1907, including the "flag dispute" with Huang Xing [documented].
- Hu portrays Sun as stubborn on symbolic questions (the flag, the oath) but generous with credit when plans succeeded [attested].
- Hu records Sun's tendency to re-narrate failures as pedagogical stages, not as defeats — the "ten uprisings" narrative that Sun would later use in the 1922 English-language essay [attested].

**Bias flag:** Post-1925 KMT hagiography — Hu was writing as a senior KMT elder and his manuscript was filtered through party-archive editing before publication.

### Liao Zhongkai 廖仲愷 (1877–1925)

US-born, Sun's chief financial and organizational manager in the Canton years; assassinated 20 August 1925.

- Liao's speeches and correspondence (collected in *Liao Zhongkai Ji* 廖仲愷集) attest to Sun's handling of Soviet funds in 1923–25 and to Sun's instruction that Whampoa accept Soviet advisers without doctrinal surrender [documented].
- Liao records Sun's direct management of KMT finances: Sun personally signed receipts for small sums and demanded itemized accounts [attested].

### Soong Ching-ling / Song Qingling 宋慶齡 (1893–1981)

Sun's second wife (married 25 October 1915 in Tokyo, without Sun having divorced his first wife Lu Muzhen 盧慕貞 — though Lu consented and signed papers earlier that month).

- Soong's pre-1949 writings and letters — especially the 1927 Hankou statement breaking with Chiang — are the earliest-register source and should be distinguished from her post-1949 memoir material, which was edited in the PRC [documented].
- Her 1937 essay "The Struggle for New China" and the 1927 Wuhan speeches attest to Sun's insistence on the "Three Great Policies" (三大政策: alliance with Russia, alliance with the CCP, support of workers and peasants) [attested — but this formulation itself is contested; some scholars argue it was Soong's and Wang's formulation, not Sun's own words].

**Bias flag:** PRC-era revisions. Post-1949 editions of Soong's memoirs were systematically aligned with PRC orthodoxy on Sun as proto-socialist precursor; her daily letters from the 1920s do not always support that framing.

### Song Jiaoren 宋教仁 (1882–1913)

A founding Tongmenghui member, principal architect of the KMT as a parliamentary party in 1912–13; assassinated 20 March 1913 at Shanghai North Station.

- Song's *Wo zhi Lishi* 我之歷史 (diary, 1904–1907, published posthumously) describes the Tokyo Tongmenghui meetings and is one of the few near-daily primary sources for Sun's Japanese years [documented] [web: https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=gb&res=884858].
- Song's diary records disagreements with Sun on organizational structure (Song wanted a genuinely parliamentary party; Sun wanted a tightly disciplined revolutionary vanguard) [attested].

### Huang Xing 黃興 (1874–1916)

Sun's military counterpart; led most of the pre-1911 uprisings in person while Sun raised funds abroad.

- The 1907 **flag dispute**: Sun insisted on the青天白日 (blue-sky-white-sun) flag designed by Lu Haodong; Huang preferred an井-character flag representing equal-field land reform. The quarrel was severe enough that Huang briefly threatened to resign from the Tongmenghui [documented — Hu Hanmin memoir, Song Jiaoren diary entry for 22 Feb 1907].
- The 1914 **CRP oath refusal**: when Sun reorganized the defeated KMT as the Chinese Revolutionary Party (中華革命黨) in Tokyo and required members to swear a personal loyalty oath including a fingerprint impression, Huang refused and broke with Sun [documented] [web: https://archive.org/details/huangxingchinese00chun].
- Huang's surviving letters to Sun (1914) are cold and formal; he called the oath "incompatible with the dignity of free men" [attested].

### Chiang Kai-shek 蔣介石 (1887–1975)

- Chiang's early diaries (1918–1925, the so-called *Chiang Kai-shek Diaries* at the Hoover Institution) record Sun's instructions during the 1922 Yongfeng gunboat crisis and the 1923 Moscow mission [documented] [web: https://www.hoover.org/library-archives/collections/chiang-kai-shek-diaries].
- Pre-1925 entries are reverential but not yet cult-like; they record genuine admiration for Sun's persistence alongside private frustration at Sun's "carelessness with money" (not personal corruption but loose accounting) [attested].

**Bias flag:** Post-1925 Chiang-led hagiography. Chiang's 1925-onward writings reshape Sun into a transmitted founder-sage whose mantle Chiang personally inherits.

### Mikhail Borodin (1884–1951)

Comintern agent in Canton from October 1923 until July 1927.

- Borodin's reports to Moscow (preserved in RGASPI / Comintern archives, many translated in C. Martin Wilbur & Julie Lien-ying How, *Missionaries of Revolution: Soviet Advisers and Nationalist China, 1920–1927*, Harvard 1989) are the richest external record of Sun's last eighteen months [documented].
- Borodin described Sun as "a man with one idea" — national unification — and noted that Sun's grasp of Marxism was thin but his political instincts were strong [attested].
- Borodin reported that Sun repeatedly reworked party documents late into the night and was impatient with the Soviet advisers' insistence on mass organization before military consolidation [attested].

**Bias flag:** Soviet/Comintern framing. Borodin is writing for Moscow and emphasizes Sun's utility to Comintern strategy; his characterizations of Sun's ideology should be read as instrumental.

### Adolph Joffe (1883–1927)

Soviet diplomat; co-signed the 26 January 1923 **Sun-Joffe Manifesto** in Shanghai.

- The Manifesto's text is clear that "the Communist order or even the Soviet system cannot actually be introduced into China, because there do not exist here the conditions for the successful establishment of either Communism or Sovietism" — a formulation Sun accepted and Joffe endorsed [documented] [web: https://www.marxists.org/history/international/comintern/sections/china/documents/1923/joffe-sun.htm].
- Joffe's reports to Moscow before and after the Manifesto describe Sun as pragmatic about Soviet aid but unwilling to submit the KMT to Comintern discipline [attested].

### Maring / Henk Sneevliet (1883–1942)

Dutch Comintern agent; met Sun in Guilin in December 1921.

- Maring's 1922 Moscow report ("Report by Sneevliet to the Executive of the Communist International, 11 July 1922") describes Sun as "not a Marxist" but "a revolutionary nationalist" whose party could be infiltrated and reoriented through the "bloc within" strategy [documented] [web: https://www.marxists.org/archive/sneevliet/1922/report.htm].
- Maring records Sun's skepticism about peasant mobilization and his preference for a swift northern military expedition [attested].

### Dai Jitao 戴季陶 (1891–1949)

Sun's translator, secretary, and later the first systematizer of "Sun-Yat-sen-ism."

- *Sun Wen zhuyi zhi zhexue de jichu* 孫文主義之哲學的基礎 (The Philosophical Foundations of Sun-Yat-sen-ism, Shanghai 1925) is the earliest sustained attempt to extract a coherent doctrine from Sun's speeches [documented].
- Dai attests a crucial and repeatedly cited observation: **Sun was more effective in English one-on-one conversation than in formal Chinese oratory.** His Cantonese-accented Mandarin was not commanding on a podium, and his written Chinese was functional rather than elegant; in small English-language meetings with foreign sympathizers, however, he was reportedly magnetic [attested — Dai 1925; corroborated by Cantlie's 1912 portrait of Sun's conversational charm].
- Dai also attests Sun's habitual citation of the *Book of Rites* 禮記 (especially the 大同 passage) and the *Mencius* — the Confucian citation habit is not a post-hoc construction but is documented in Sun's Canton speeches of 1924 [documented].

### Homer Lea (1876–1912)

American military adventurer, author of *The Valor of Ignorance* (1909) and *The Day of the Saxon* (1912); met Sun in 1910 and accompanied him to London and back to China in late 1911.

- Lea was with Sun in Denver in October 1911 and travelled with him to London and then to Shanghai via Hong Kong [documented].
- Lea's surviving letters describe Sun as "serene under disappointment" and as possessed of "a curious hopefulness that is neither naïveté nor calculation" [attested] [web: https://www.hoover.org/library-archives/collections/homer-lea].

## § Journalists and foreign observers

### *The Globe* (London), 22 October 1896 — the London kidnapping exposé

- The headline "Startling Story from Portland Place: Conspirator Kidnapped in London" broke the story of Sun's detention inside the Chinese Legation [documented] [web: https://www.bl.uk/collection-items/the-globe-22-october-1896].
- *The Times* followed on 23 October; the Foreign Office had been pressured by Lord Salisbury by that point, and Sun was released the same afternoon.
- The episode made Sun an international celebrity almost overnight — before this, he was a minor Cantonese exile; after it, he had a Western press profile.

### *North-China Herald* (Shanghai) and *The Times* (London)

- *North-China Herald* covered Sun's movements from 1896 onward; its 1912 coverage of the Nanking provisional government is detailed but skeptical, treating Sun as a figurehead whose "cabinet of enthusiasts" lacked administrative experience [attested] [web: https://hpcbristol.net/collection/north-china-herald].
- *The Times* of London tracked Sun's diplomatic overtures and, through its Peking correspondent G. E. Morrison, was often a conduit for unfavorable assessments.

### W. H. Donald to G. E. Morrison, 4 July 1912

- The Australian journalist and adviser W. H. Donald wrote to Morrison calling Sun **"a maniac, without common sense"** — a now-famous line preserved in the Morrison papers at the Mitchell Library, Sydney [documented] [web: https://www.sl.nsw.gov.au/collection-items/papers-george-ernest-morrison].
- The full letter contextualizes the remark: Donald had just observed Sun proposing a 200,000-mile Chinese railway network on timelines Donald considered fantastical. Donald's critique is not about Sun's integrity (he does not question it) but about Sun's grasp of scale and finance — a theme that recurs in hostile contemporary assessments.

### The Denver / *Rocky Mountain News* datum — 12 October 1911

- Sun was in Denver, Colorado, staying at (or near) the **Brown Palace Hotel**, when the Wuchang Uprising broke out on 10 October 1911. He learned of it from a newspaper — the *Rocky Mountain News* or a similar Denver daily — on approximately 12 October [documented — Sun's own *Jianguo Fanglüe* preface; Homer Lea correspondence].
- This is a decisive and well-attested fact: **Sun was not in China when the revolution that bears his name broke out.** He was on a North American fundraising tour and took weeks to return, traveling via New York, London (where he lobbied for British neutrality and attempted unsuccessfully to negotiate loans), Paris, Singapore, and Hong Kong, arriving Shanghai on 25 December 1911 [documented].

### Japanese press coverage, c. 1900–1911

- The *Yorozu Chōhō* 萬朝報 and *Asahi Shimbun* 朝日新聞 carried sporadic coverage of Sun during his Tokyo years, often framed as "the Chinese Mazzini" (中國のマッツィーニ) [attested].
- The 1907 Japanese government expulsion of Sun from Tokyo (under Qing pressure) was covered in Japanese papers; the Japanese government gave Sun a 7,000 yen "farewell gift" which became ammunition in the 1907 Zhang Binglin quarrel [documented].

## § Political opponents

### Liang Qichao 梁啟超 (1873–1929)

- The 1905–1907 debates between Liang's *Xinmin Congbao* 新民叢報 and the Tongmenghui's *Min Bao* 民報 are the single richest printed contemporary critique of Sun's program [documented] [web: https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=gb&res=553932].
- Liang argued against racial revolution (anti-Manchu), against immediate republicanism, and against Sun's land-value-tax plan (平均地權), which Liang considered economically incoherent.
- Liang's personal remarks on Sun (in private correspondence) are less polemical than his printed critique: he acknowledged Sun's sincerity and persistence but doubted his administrative capacity [attested].

### Kang Youwei 康有為 (1858–1927)

- Kang and Sun were diaspora rivals, especially for overseas-Chinese donations (Honolulu, San Francisco, Singapore). Kang's *Baohuanghui* 保皇會 (Protect-the-Emperor Society) and Sun's Xingzhonghui / Tongmenghui competed for the same donor pool [documented].
- Kang reportedly described Sun privately as "an adventurer" (冒險家) [attributed — repeated in Hu Hanmin's memoir but not in Kang's own surviving papers].

### Zhang Binglin 章太炎 (1868–1936)

- A leading classical scholar and *Min Bao* editor, Zhang broke with Sun in 1907 over the disposition of the Japanese government's 7,000 yen "farewell gift" — Sun had kept 2,000 yen for personal travel and given 5,000 to *Min Bao*, which Zhang considered embezzlement [documented].
- Zhang published a leaflet titled or characterized as **《孫文罪狀》** ("Sun Wen's Crimes") circulating within Tongmenghui circles in Tokyo in 1907, demanding Sun's removal from the chairmanship [documented — cited in Feng Ziyou 馮自由, *Geming Yishi* 革命逸史, 1939].
- Zhang's quarrel was bitter and personal; he later reconciled selectively but never retracted the 1907 charges.

**Note:** Feng Ziyou's *Geming Yishi* (1939, in six volumes) is itself a major contemporary-witness compilation — Feng was a Tongmenghui founding member who preserved internal correspondence [documented].

### Chen Duxiu 陳獨秀 (1879–1942)

- Chen's assessments of Sun shifted across his career: in the *Xin Qingnian* 新青年 (*New Youth*) years 1915–1918 he treated Sun as a well-intentioned but outdated "old revolutionary"; during the CCP founding (1921) and the First United Front (1923–27), he worked with Sun while reporting privately to Maring that Sun was "authoritarian in small matters, vague in large" [attested] [web: https://www.marxists.org/chinese/chenduxiu/index.htm].
- After Sun's death, Chen's CCP-era writings emphasized Sun's 1924 "turn" as the genuinely revolutionary phase.

### Chen Jiongming 陳炯明 (1878–1933) — the 1922 Guangzhou shelling

This is the most contested episode in the contemporary record. **Both lines of primary-source testimony must be preserved.**

**KMT-orthodox line:** On the night of 16 June 1922, Chen Jiongming's subordinate Ye Ju 葉舉 ordered troops to attack the presidential residence (Yuexiu Lou 粵秀樓). Soong Ching-ling escaped first; Sun fled to the gunboat **Yongfeng** (永豐艦, later renamed Zhongshan); Chiang Kai-shek joined him on the gunboat on 29 June; Sun remained afloat in the Pearl River delta for 55 days. This narrative is canonized in the *Guofu Nianpu* and Chiang's own later writings [documented — KMT sources].

**Chen-defender line:** Chen Jiongming's own telegrams and statements (collected in *Chen Jiongming Ji* 陳炯明集, ed. Duan Yunzhang, Sun Yat-sen University Press, 1998, but drawing on 1920s primary documents) claim that (a) Chen was not in Canton on 16 June and had not ordered the attack; (b) the underlying quarrel was federalist — Chen wanted a "United Provinces of China" constitutional model (聯省自治) and opposed Sun's Northern Expedition; (c) Chen's troops acted to pressure Sun into negotiation, not to assassinate him [documented — Chen's 1922 telegrams] [web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chen_Jiongming (index only; see Duan 1998 for primary documents)].

Contemporary Hong Kong press (especially the *South China Morning Post* and the Chinese-language *Huazi Ribao* 華字日報) reported the event in terms closer to Chen's federalist framing than the later KMT-orthodox narrative [attested].

The two narratives cannot be fully harmonized. What is documented beyond dispute: shells were fired at the presidential residence; Sun took refuge on the Yongfeng; the quarrel was ideological (unification-by-force vs. federalist constitutionalism) as well as personal.

## § Key episodes with cross-witness reconstruction

### 1894 Li Hongzhang petition

- In June 1894, Sun (then 28) co-wrote with Lu Haodong a long reformist petition to Viceroy Li Hongzhang, proposing agricultural, educational, and industrial reform [documented — text preserved in *Sun Zhongshan Quanji* vol. 1].
- Sun travelled to Tianjin to deliver it; Li declined to meet him. The rejection is widely identified by contemporaries (Miyazaki, Cantlie) as the moment Sun turned from reform to revolution [attested].

### 1895 Guangzhou Uprising

- Planned for 26 October 1895 (double-ninth festival); betrayed before it began; Lu Haodong executed; Sun fled via Macao and Kobe to Yokohama [documented].
- This is the first armed attempt and the origin of Sun's exile.

### 1896 London kidnapping (11–23 October)

- See Cantlie, *The Globe*, and *The Times* above. The detention lasted twelve days. The released facts: Sun was lured or walked into the Legation; Legation staff attempted to arrange secret transport to China for execution; Cantlie's press mobilization made that impossible [documented].
- Whether Sun entered the Legation voluntarily or was "kidnapped" in a strict sense was debated at the time; Sun's own 1897 *Kidnapped in London!* insists on the stronger word. Legation counter-testimony (quoted in Slater's detective reports commissioned by the Chinese Legation) claimed Sun entered freely [documented].

### 1905 Tongmenghui 同盟會 founding, Tokyo

- Founded 20 August 1905 at the home of Uchida Ryōhei with about seventy founding members including Huang Xing, Song Jiaoren, Hu Hanmin, Wang Jingwei [documented — Song Jiaoren diary; Miyazaki memoir].
- Sun was elected Zongli 總理 (Director).

### The "ten uprisings" 1906–1911

- Ten armed attempts between the Pingliu Li Uprising (Dec 1906) and the Second Guangzhou / Huanghuagang Uprising (27 April 1911). **Huang Xing led most of them in person; Sun was abroad raising funds for nearly all.** This is well-documented in Hu Hanmin, Song Jiaoren, and Feng Ziyou.
- The 27 April 1911 Huanghuagang Uprising produced the "72 Martyrs" and was the most psychologically consequential failure before Wuchang.
- Huang Xing was wounded (two fingers shot off) at Huanghuagang; Sun was in the United States [documented].

### October 1911 Wuchang Uprising

- Broke out 10 October 1911 following the accidental explosion of a bomb in the Russian Concession at Hankou on 9 October, which exposed a revolutionary cell list [documented].
- Sun learned of it in Denver on approximately 12 October from a newspaper; he did not return to China until 25 December 1911 [documented — see Denver datum above].
- This is perhaps the single most-cited "chance" element in 1911 historiography: the revolution began without Sun's direction or presence.

### 1912 provisional presidency, oath, Yuan Shikai transfer

- Sun inaugurated 1 January 1912 as provisional president of the Republic at Nanking; his oath promised to step down upon the establishment of a constitutional government and Qing abdication [documented].
- Qing abdication 12 February 1912; Sun resigned 13 February in favor of Yuan Shikai; Yuan inaugurated at Peking on 10 March. Contemporaries (Song Jiaoren diary, Donald's letters) split on whether Sun was naïve or pragmatic in the transfer [attested].

### 1913 Second Revolution and Song Jiaoren assassination

- Song Jiaoren assassinated 20 March 1913 at Shanghai North Station; died 22 March. The Second Revolution (July–September 1913) was suppressed; Sun fled to Japan [documented].

### 1915 Soong Ching-ling marriage

- Married 25 October 1915 in Tokyo at the home of lawyer Wada Mizu. Sun was 49; Soong Ching-ling was 22. Lu Muzhen had signed a consent document earlier that month; contemporaries disagreed on whether this constituted a formal divorce under then-applicable Chinese family law [documented; contested].
- Soong Ching-ling's family — particularly her father Charlie Soong, who had been Sun's friend — broke with Sun over the marriage [documented].
- The marriage was controversial among Sun's own Christian circle and is rarely mentioned in early KMT hagiography; it is documented in Soong family correspondence [attested].

### 1917–18 first Guangzhou government

- Sun established a "Military Government for the Protection of the Constitution" (護法軍政府) in September 1917 after northern warlords dissolved the parliament. He was marginalized by the Guangxi clique within eight months and resigned in May 1918 [documented].

### 1922 Chen Jiongming crisis and Yongfeng gunboat

- See Chen Jiongming entry above. 16–17 June 1922 shelling; Sun on Yongfeng for 55 days; departed to Shanghai August 1922.

### 1923 Sun-Joffe Manifesto

- 26 January 1923, Shanghai. The Manifesto established the Soviet-KMT alignment while formally denying that Communism was applicable to China [documented].

### 1924 Whampoa / KMT First Congress

- KMT First National Congress 20–30 January 1924 at Canton; reorganized the party on Leninist lines, accepted CCP members as individuals ("bloc within"), promulgated the Manifesto that restated the Three Principles [documented].
- Whampoa Military Academy opened 16 June 1924 with Chiang Kai-shek as commandant and Liao Zhongkai as KMT representative; Zhou Enlai became political department deputy director [documented].

### 12 March 1925 death at Peking Union Medical College Hospital

- Sun arrived in Peking 31 December 1924 on a peace-conference mission with Feng Yuxiang and Duan Qirui; by mid-January 1925 he was clearly ill [documented].
- Admitted to Peking Union Medical College Hospital (PUMC) 26 January 1925; exploratory laparotomy same day; surgeons found an inoperable abdominal malignancy.
- **The autopsy (performed at PUMC) identified the tumor as gallbladder adenocarcinoma (膽囊腺癌), not primary liver cancer.** The "liver cancer" label common in popular accounts appears to derive from translation drift and from the tumor's hepatic invasion; the PUMC pathology report is preserved in the hospital's archives [documented] [web: https://www.pumc.edu.cn (institutional; original report referenced in J. Z. Bowers, *Western Medicine in a Chinese Palace*, 1972)].
- Death 09:30, 12 March 1925. The testament (drafted by Wang Jingwei, signed the previous day) was released; the "family testament" and a separate "letter to the Soviet Union" (co-drafted by Borodin's circle) were released simultaneously.

## § Personality cross-attestations

The following traits are attested by multiple independent witnesses, including hostile ones. This matters: a trait attested by both Cantlie and Zhang Binglin, or by both Borodin and Chen Jiongming, is load-bearing.

1. **Energy / persistence across failures.** Attested by Cantlie, Miyazaki, Hu Hanmin, Borodin, Linebarger, Homer Lea, Donald (grudgingly). Even Zhang Binglin's 1907 leaflet does not dispute Sun's tirelessness; it disputes his judgment [attested, cross-witnessed].

2. **Optimism bordering on grandiosity — the 大砲 ("Big Gun") nickname.** The Cantonese nickname 孫大砲 (Sun Dapao, "Sun the Big Gun" / "Sun the Windbag") circulated in Cantonese political circles from roughly 1912 onward [attested]. Donald's "maniac, without common sense" letter; Liang Qichao's "200,000 miles of railway" ridicule; Borodin's note that Sun's plans ran ahead of his resources — all triangulate the same trait [cross-witnessed].

3. **Personal frugality / financial honesty.** This is attested even by hostile sources and is therefore unusually load-bearing. Donald (hostile) does not accuse Sun of corruption. Chen Jiongming (the bitterest opponent) does not accuse Sun of personal enrichment. Zhang Binglin's 1907 quarrel concerned 2,000 yen of contested public funds, not personal embezzlement. Borodin reported to Moscow that Sun lived modestly even as millions of roubles passed through his hands. Linebarger and Cantlie both describe a man with a single suitcase [cross-witnessed].

4. **Speaking style.** Dai Jitao attests that Sun was more effective in small English-language conversation than in formal Chinese oratory; Cantlie and Linebarger corroborate the conversational charm; Song Jiaoren's diary notes that Sun's Tokyo speeches in Mandarin were sometimes labored by his Cantonese accent [attested].

5. **Confucian citation habits.** Sun regularly cited the *Li Yun* 禮運 "Great Unity" (大同) passage, Mencius, and the *Daxue* 大學 "eight items" (格致誠正修齊治平). This is documented in the 1924 Three Principles lectures stenographed at Canton and is not a post-hoc KMT editorial overlay [documented].

6. **Continuous fundraising.** Attested by every personal witness listed. Sun's travel schedule 1896–1911 is almost entirely diaspora fundraising (Hawaii, San Francisco, Vancouver, Penang, Singapore, Japan). Overseas Chinese ledgers from Honolulu, San Francisco, and Penang branches of the Xingzhonghui and Tongmenghui survive and document the pattern [documented].

## § Bias flag map

1. **Post-1925 KMT hagiography (Chiang-led).** The *Guofu Nianpu* 國父年譜 and the Guomindang's official compilations (1930s onward) reshape Sun into a sage-founder; episodes of failure, the 1915 marriage, and the 1907 Zhang Binglin quarrel are minimized or omitted. The "國父" (Father of the Nation) title itself is a 1940 official designation.

2. **PRC revisions of Soong Ching-ling's memoirs.** Post-1949 editions align Soong's framing of Sun with "proto-socialist precursor" narratives. Her pre-1949 letters are more politically heterogeneous.

3. **Japanese Pan-Asianist framing** (Miyazaki, Tōyama Mitsuru, Uchida Ryōhei). Sun is written as an East-Asian-solidarity hero; his Western formation and the tensions with Japanese policy (especially the 1907 expulsion, the 1915 Twenty-One Demands response) are de-emphasized.

4. **Soviet/Comintern framing** (Borodin, Joffe, Maring, later Soviet historiography). Sun is framed as a nationalist ripe for Comintern instrumentation; his explicit 1923 rejection of Communism as applicable to China is often soft-pedaled.

5. **Republican-era anti-Sun literature** (Chen Jiongming defenders; federalist constitutionalists; 1920s Hong Kong press). Sun is framed as a centralizing autocrat willing to invoke foreign aid and military adventure against constitutional process. This strand is under-weighted in both KMT and PRC traditions but is preserved in Hong Kong documentary collections.

6. **Liang Qichao constitutional-monarchist rival literature** (*Xinmin Congbao* 1905–07). Sun is framed as reckless and economically incoherent; useful as a contemporaneous intellectual critique from a non-Manchu rival tradition.

7. **Zhang Binglin's 1907 breakup literature** (*Sun Wen zuizhuang* leaflet; later references in Feng Ziyou's *Geming Yishi*). Accuses Sun of financial irregularity and autocratic leadership within the Tongmenghui; bitter but documentarily grounded.

8. **Foreign-observer Orientalist tone.** Even sympathetic Western observers (Cantlie, Linebarger, Homer Lea) frame Sun within a missionary-civilizational idiom that obscures the Chinese political registers in which he operated.

9. **Westerner-patron framing.** Cantlie's 1912 and Linebarger's 1925 biographies implicitly present Sun as a Western-educated, Christian-formed figure; the Japanese and overseas-Chinese dimensions of his formation are subordinated.

## § Cross-witness minimum (most robustly attested claims)

These survive triangulation across hostile, sympathetic, and instrumental witnesses and can be held with highest confidence:

- Sun was a Christian-educated, medically trained Cantonese exile who turned to armed revolution after the 1894 Li Hongzhang rejection [documented].
- The 1896 London kidnapping happened and made Sun internationally known [documented].
- From 1896 to 1911 he was primarily a fundraiser-in-exile; most on-the-ground uprisings were led by Huang Xing [documented].
- He was in Denver, not China, on 10 October 1911 [documented].
- He served as provisional president for six weeks (1 January – 13 February 1912) and handed power to Yuan Shikai [documented].
- He was personally frugal and not credibly accused of personal enrichment by any hostile witness [cross-witnessed].
- He was persistently optimistic to the point of being mocked as 孫大砲 [cross-witnessed].
- He was more persuasive in English conversation than in formal Chinese oratory [attested — Dai Jitao, Cantlie, Linebarger].
- He married Soong Ching-ling in Tokyo in October 1915 without a clean legal divorce from Lu Muzhen, and this was controversial among his circle [documented].
- He ran the 1917–18 and 1920–22 Canton governments with limited success and was shelled out by Chen Jiongming in June 1922 [documented].
- He signed the Sun-Joffe Manifesto in January 1923 and reorganized the KMT on Leninist lines in January 1924 [documented].
- He died on 12 March 1925 at PUMC of gallbladder adenocarcinoma, not primary liver cancer [documented].
