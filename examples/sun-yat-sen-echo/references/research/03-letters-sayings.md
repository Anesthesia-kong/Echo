# Sun Yat-sen — Letters, Speeches, and Personal Voice

Research Agent 3 output.

Tag key: `[documented]` = primary text / signed / recorded; `[attested]` = named contemporary source; `[attributed]` = reliable biography, no primary anchor; `[legendary]` = widely repeated, no primary source; `[modern-scholarly-inference]` = scholar's characterization.

## § Correspondence

**Scale and edition.** The standard scholarly edition of Sun's collected works — including the bulk of surviving correspondence — is the 11-volume `孫中山全集 Sun Zhongshan Quanji` (Zhonghua Shuju, Beijing, 1981–86). `[documented]` `[web: https://library.oapen.org/bitstream/id/b19fe771-01bd-4e23-a7aa-59676e477ab6/9780472903917.pdf]` A later 16-volume edition exists. Sun wrote in three languages: Chinese (most), English (to foreign supporters, press, financiers), and Japanese (functional, not literary).

**To foreign supporters.** The longest-running correspondence is with **Sir James Cantlie**, his Hong Kong medical-school teacher who organized the 1896 London rescue. Photocopies of Sun–Cantlie letters, written from Penang (1910), SS Korea SF→Honolulu (1910), Tokyo (1915–16), Shanghai (1918–c.1920), Canton (1921), are preserved in the Wellcome Collection. They cover the revolution, England's China policy, the dedication of *The International Development of China* (1921) to the Cantlies, and family matters. `[documented]` `[web: https://wellcomecollection.org/works/qu77agnv]` With **Miyazaki Tōten** (宮崎滔天), the Japanese pan-Asianist who sheltered Sun in exile. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C5%8Dten_Miyazaki]` **Homer Lea**, his "closest foreign advisor," met in London over British financing schemes; papers are at the Hoover Institution (Powers collection). `[documented]` `[web: https://oac.cdlib.org/findaid/ark:/13030/tf2489n54s/]` **Paul Linebarger Sr.**, American cavalry officer, resigned his judicial post in 1907 to become Sun's legal advisor; Sun godfathered his son (later the SF author Cordwainer Smith). `[documented]` `[web: https://hub.jhu.edu/magazine/2018/fall/cordwainer-smith-paul-linebarger/]`

**Overseas-Chinese fundraising.** A distinct genre: transactional, motivational, concrete about sums, fraternal. Xing Zhong Hui founded Honolulu 24 Nov 1894; 1894–95 Hawaii trip yielded ~125 members at $5 dues, $10 shares, $288 fees plus $1,100 raised. The 1895 Guangzhou uprising was funded by ~$6,000 from Hawaii diaspora. `[documented]` `[web: http://eng.chinamil.com.cn/CHINA_209163/Features_209191/10096769.html]` Networks: Hawaii, San Francisco's Chee Kung Tong, Singapore, Penang, Tokyo exile community.

**To Soviet figures, 1921–1925.** Sun's first letter to **Chicherin** (Soviet Foreign Commissar), 28 Aug 1921, in response to Chicherin's June 1921 communication: *"I would like to enter into personal contact with you and your friends in Moscow."* `[documented]` `[web: http://www.republicanchina.org/USSR-Comintern-KMT-CCP.pdf]` **Karakhan's** 23 Sept 1923 letter introduced Borodin. When Lenin died (Jan 1924), Sun sent condolences; Chicherin replied promptly. The **Sun–Joffe Manifesto** (26 Jan 1923, Shanghai) explicitly stated communism was unsuitable for China yet committed to cooperation — the hinge document. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun%E2%80%93Joffe_Manifesto]`

**To KMT associates.** Heavy correspondence with Chiang Kai-shek, Liao Zhongkai, Wang Jingwei, Hu Hanmin, Dai Jitao (who was Sun's translator and confidential secretary). `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dai_Jitao]` The Sun–Chiang correspondence around the 1922 Yongfeng escape and the 1924 Whampoa command shows Sun testing and negotiating Chiang's loyalty.

**To Lu Muzhen (first wife) and Song Qingling.** A 1915 letter to Lu asking for divorce consent, on Chinese Revolutionary Party letterhead, is preserved at the Dr Sun Yat-sen Museum, Hong Kong. `[documented]` `[web: https://www.info.gov.hk/gia/general/201204/20/P201204200485.htm]` Lu's reported reply — "If there is someone willing to take good care of him, I am willing to support that person and am therefore willing to divorce him" — is `[attested]` via museum exhibition materials.

**Bonhams has auctioned Sun letters repeatedly.** `[web: https://www.bonhams.com/press_release/32785/]`

**Tonal register.** Urgency of money and next-move logistics; moral framing (revolution as duty, comrades as brothers); occasional strategic frankness absent from speeches; private post-failure admissions (post-1895, post-1913, post-1922).

## § Major speeches

**The 1924 Three Principles lectures (三民主義).** 16 weekly lectures at Canton Normal High School, 27 Jan – 24 Aug 1924: 6 on Minzu (Nationalism), 6 on Minquan (Democracy), 4 on Minsheng (Livelihood). `[documented]` `[web: http://www.taiwan-database.net/PDFs/WTFpdf21.pdf]` Transcribed from delivery by Huang Changgu and Zou Lu, then revised by Sun. Minsheng reworked twice; **Minquan and Minzu were not personally re-revised before Sun's 12 Mar 1925 death.** `[documented]` The published volume includes Two Supplementary Chapters on Livelihood added posthumously by Chiang Kai-shek. `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Principles_of_the_People]` Implication: the oral tonality — repetition, digression, teacherly returns, concrete examples — survives editing, especially in Minquan.

**KMT First National Congress (20–30 Jan 1924).** Sun chaired. Opening speech stated reorganization had two objects: transform the KMT into "a powerful and organized political party," and address national reconstruction. `[attested]` `[web: https://chiculture.org.hk/en/photo-story/2760]` The Congress adopted the Three Great Policies (聯俄、容共、扶助農工).

**Whampoa Military Academy opening (16 June 1924).** Sun had proclaimed the cadets on 1 May. Motto: **親愛精誠** ("Camaraderie / Fraternity, Dexterity, Sincerity"). `[documented]` `[web: https://military-history.fandom.com/wiki/Whampoa_Military_Academy]` Tone: exhortation; "save the country"; principles left deliberately elastic — "follow the principles," "be diligent, courageous, trustworthy, and loyal." Part of the speech was later set as lyrics to what became the ROC national anthem. `[web: https://www.theworldofchinese.com/2023/06/how-the-whampoa-academy-gave-birth-to-famous-enemies-in-chinese-political-history/]`

**Northward Manifesto (10 Nov 1924).** 北上宣言書. Issued before leaving Guangzhou 13 Nov; stops in HK, Shanghai, Kobe; arrived Tianjin 4 Dec to a crowd of ~20,000. Themes: end warlordism, end imperialism, abolish unequal treaties, convene a national assembly. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen]`

**"Greater Asianism" / 大亞洲主義 — Kobe, 28 Nov 1924.** Sun's most-cited late-life speech outside the Three Principles. Canonical English translation: *Sun Yat Sen, China and Japan: Natural Friends, Unnatural Enemies* (Shanghai, 1941), copy in Library of Congress; Wikisource carries this translation. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Sun_Yat-sen's_speech_on_Pan-Asianism]` Core dyad: **"Oriental civilization is the rule of Right; Occidental civilization is the rule of Might."** The closing challenges Japan to choose the rule of Right — a careful, diplomatic provocation. Rhetorical signature: binary oppositions (Right/Might, benevolence/brute force), rhetorical questions, second-person address.

**1903–1905 US/Hawaii lecture tours.** First English-language and diasporic speaking campaigns; transcripts survive primarily in diaspora newspaper reports. `[documented]` `[web: https://guides.library.manoa.hawaii.edu/c.php?g=986504]`

**1911 Shanghai return.** Sun learned of Wuchang from newspapers in Denver, returned to Shanghai Dec 1911, elected Provisional President 29 Dec 1911. `[documented]` `[web: https://www.britannica.com/biography/Sun-Yat-sen/The-revolution-of-1911]` A formal return-speech text is not canonical in the way the 1924 lectures are.

**The only surviving voice recording.** 30 May 1924, Guangzhou, recorded by the *China Evening Post* — two 78-rpm records, one Cantonese, one Mandarin. Chai Wenhua's collection preserves them. Linguistic analysis by Sun Yat-sen University scholars: D1 = Zhongshan Cantonese (birth dialect), D2 = Standard Cantonese, D3 = Mandarin. Mandarin spoken with heavy Cantonese/Pearl River Delta accent; no trace of Hakka accent. `[documented]` `[web: https://languagelog.ldc.upenn.edu/nll/?p=2467]` `[web: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=yjTt1ugEQlM]`

## § Famous sayings and slogans — provenance verdicts

**天下為公 (tiānxià wéi gōng, "the world belongs to all").** Classical origin: *Liji · Liyun* 禮記·禮運 Datong passage — "大道之行也，天下為公." Zheng Xuan (Eastern Han) glosses 公 as 共 ("shared"). Sun's single most-requested calligraphic inscription; many extant examples at Wikimedia Commons. `[documented]` `[web: https://commons.wikimedia.org/wiki/Category:Calligraphy_by_Sun_Yat-sen]` **Verdict:** `[documented]` — classical phrase, Sun's use is documented in dozens of calligraphic pieces. The late-Qing / early-republican reading — fusing Confucian datong, peasant egalitarianism, Western democracy, socialism — is `[modern-scholarly-inference]`. `[web: https://gaodawei.wordpress.com/2023/07/30/everything-for-the-common-good-tianxia-weigong-%E5%A4%A9%E4%B8%8B%E7%82%BA%E5%85%AC-sun-yat-sens-lifelong-pursuit-of-ideals-and-putting-them-into-practice/]`

**革命尚未成功，同志仍須努力 ("The revolution is not yet complete; comrades must continue to strive").** The political testament (國事遺囑), drafted by **Wang Jingwei**, signed by Sun 11 Mar 1925. `[documented]` `[web: https://chinaheritage.net/journal/essays/sub-essays/sun-yat-sens-shade-%E9%9D%A9%E5%91%BD%E5%B0%9A%E6%9C%AA%E6%88%90%E5%8A%9F/]` Sentiment that the revolution is unfinished is Sun's own from 1911 forward `[attested]`; exact phrasing is Wang Jingwei's composition. **Verdict:** sentiment `[documented]`, wording `[attributed]` through Wang's drafting. Huang Wennong parodied it in *The Analects* within a decade.

**知難行易 ("knowing is hard, doing is easy").** Chapter 1 of *Sun Wen Xueshuo* 孫文學說 (1919); Sun proofread three drafts. `[documented]` Deliberate inversion of Wang Yangming / neo-Confucian 知易行難. Three-stage cognition model: doing without knowing → doing before knowing → knowing before doing. `[web: https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/review-of-politics/article/abs/wang-yangming-and-the-ideology-of-sun-yatsen/02442FC360B77BE147F39D698270D385]` Scholarly assessment: one-sided, under-dialectical, but the force is as a deliberate break from feudal knowing-easy-doing-hard. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` `[web: https://plato.stanford.edu/archIves/win2023/entries/chinese-epistemology/]` **Verdict:** `[documented]`.

**和平、奮鬥、救中國 ("Peace — struggle — save China").** Reported deathbed utterance, 12 Mar 1925. Contemporary eyewitness reports of final-day utterances are not fully consistent; the tripartite slogan shape looks polished for commemoration. `[web: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/300798544_Sun_Yat-sen's_Last_Words]` Global Times and CSIS/Interpret translations of commemorative speeches both lock it in as canonical. `[web: https://interpret.csis.org/translations/speech-at-the-conference-to-commemorate-the-150th-anniversary-of-the-birth-of-dr-sun-yat-sen/]` `[web: https://www.globaltimes.cn/page/202503/1329997.shtml]` **Verdict:** `[attested]` → `[attributed]`. Sun uttered words of this shape; the polished three-term slogan is posthumous commemoration.

**以俄為師 ("Take Russia as teacher").** Fits Sun's explicit 1923–24 framing that Russia represented "the new model of republicanism" vs. the US/France as "the old model." `[documented]` `[web: https://www.britannica.com/event/Sun-Joffe-Manifesto]` **Verdict:** sentiment `[documented]` via the Sun–Joffe Manifesto, First Congress speeches, and Three Great Policies; the exact four-character phrase is `[attested]` in later KMT/CCP writing, verification of precise authorship requires *Quanji* lookup.

**"I want to be a common man, with a boat on the West River" (paraphrase).** No specific primary citation surfaces; not in standard Chinese databases. **Verdict:** `[legendary]`. The underlying sentiment (reluctance to hold power) aligns with his Mar 1912 resignation in favor of Yuan Shikai, which is `[documented]`; the "West River boat" detail is not verifiable.

**先知先覺 / 後知後覺 / 不知不覺.** Trichotomy in *Sun Wen Xueshuo* and the 1924 lectures, framing the revolutionary pedagogical role. `[documented]` Sun positions himself implicitly as 先知先覺 without first-person self-nomination. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` The epithet 革命先行者 ("Forerunner of the Revolution") is a posthumous PRC/ROC title, not Sun's self-reference. `[documented]`

**一盤散沙 ("a plate of loose sand").** Minzu lecture, 1924. `[documented]` `[web: https://alphahistory.com/chineserevolution/sun-yat-sen-principle-nationalism-1924/]` Full analogy: foreigners say Chinese are loose sand — particles slipping about — but add cement and it hardens to rock; the sand loses freedom, the whole is firm; China must trade individual liberty for national cohesion to resist imperialism. Emblematic of Sun's analogy-based pedagogy.

**親愛精誠.** Whampoa motto, 16 Jun 1924. `[documented]`

**"I would like to enter into personal contact with you and your friends in Moscow."** Sun to Chicherin, 28 Aug 1921. `[documented]`

## § Voice characteristics — by language

**Chinese.** Phonetics: heavy Zhongshan Cantonese accent carrying into Mandarin (confirmed by the 1924 recordings); more comfortable in Cantonese than Mandarin. `[documented]` Register: classical-vernacular hybrid 文白並用 — 1924 lectures are near-vernacular pedagogical prose; manifestos (Northward, testament) shift semi-classical. Four-character set phrases (成語) deployed heavily — 天下為公, 一盤散沙, 先知先覺, 革命尚未成功 — density of four-character patterns is a stylistic signature. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` Rhetorical moves: repetition as pedagogical device (1924 lectures loop back repeatedly); analogies from technology and daily life (sand/cement, machine-operator constitutionalism, physician, engineer, "big cannon" railway plans); citation mixing (Liji, Mencius, Lincoln's "of/by/for the people" as gloss of 三民主義, Mill, Montesquieu, Rousseau, passing critical Marx); classical anchoring — when he wants something to stick he reaches into the canon.

**English.** Three registers:
- *Personal/narrative*: *Kidnapped in London* (1897) — first-person, chronological, readable; one contemporary reviewer compared vocabulary to Mark Twain. Written with James Cantlie and Edwin Collins as editorial collaborators. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Kidnapped_in_London/Chapter_7]` `[web: https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/abs/10.1080/17535654.2021.2108602]`
- *Technocratic/programmatic*: *The International Development of China* (dated 25 Apr 1921 Canton; pub. 1921). Dense infrastructure schemes — 160,000 miles of railway, three great seaports, canal and highway programs. Precise, list-heavy, engineering-report prose. `[documented]` `[web: https://www.gutenberg.org/files/45188/45188-h/45188-h.htm]`
- *Correspondence*: less polished, sometimes Chinese-influenced syntax; Cantlie letters preserve this middle register. `[documented]`

All Sun's English books were produced with editorial/co-authorial assistance. Raw voice is more audible in signed correspondence than in printed books. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` **Translator-layer caveat:** Frank Price's 1927 English translation of the Three Principles (canonical) is flat relative to the Chinese — it smooths oral repetition, digressions, anecdotes that give the lectures their teaching tone. Do not mistake flat English for flat Chinese: he was not flat in Chinese. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

**Japanese.** Functional, not literary. Used in exile (Tokyo years, Miyazaki and Umeya networks). Surviving Japanese correspondence is practical/logistical, not stylistically elevated. `[modern-scholarly-inference]` `[web: https://tabinaga.jp/wp-content/themes/tabinaga/pdf/sonbunumeya_en/history_4_1.pdf]` Do not reproduce a distinctive Japanese "voice" for him.

**Cross-language.** Key term **自由 (ziyóu)**: Sun's 1924 position is that China has **too much** individual liberty, to the point of becoming 一盤散沙 — a critique, not a celebration. A direct 自由 ↔ "freedom/liberty" map flattens this. `[documented]` The neologism **三民主義** is untranslatable cleanly; Sun himself glossed it via Lincoln's 民有、民治、民享 ("of/by/for the people"). `[documented]` `[web: http://www.taiwan-database.net/PDFs/WTFpdf21.pdf]`

**Voice-feature matrix.** Earnest / didactic: high. Pedagogical repetition: high. Concrete analogy: high. Certainty signals (必, 當, 不可): high. Classical anchoring: medium-high. Messianic / prophetic: medium. Sarcasm: very low. Humor: very low. Self-deprecation: medium (often strategic). Hedging and admitted doubt: medium in private letters, low in public. Grand-scale ("big cannon") plans: high. Four-character phrasemaking: high.

## § Private voice vs public voice

**Public voice:** confident, prescriptive, future-tensed, 必-heavy. Grand scale. The "**Sun Da Pao**" 大砲 ("Big Cannon") nickname was earned: c.1912, Yuan Shikai reportedly asked what Sun could do for China's railways; Sun answered 200,000 km. Yuan gave him the railway portfolio; Sun produced no track. In Cantonese idiom 大砲 = someone whose boast outruns his delivery. `[attested]` `[web: http://www.chinaknowledge.de/History/Rep/personssunyatsen.html]` The number cited varies (160,000–200,000 miles/km) — that imprecision is itself the "big cannon" stamp. Near-messianic self-location as 先知先覺 without explicit first-person claim. Revolutionary optimism as discipline.

**Private voice — where it leaks through:**
- *Post-1895 Guangzhou failure*: London-exile Cantlie correspondence, *Kidnapped in London* (1897). Gratitude, vulnerability, near-death. `[documented]`
- *Post-1913 Second Revolution failure*: Tokyo-exile letters to Cantlie (1915–16); the wilderness years; founding of the more authoritarian Chinese Revolutionary Party 中華革命黨 (1914); the Lu-divorce / Song-marriage transition. Admissions of failure most available here. `[documented]`
- *Post-1922 Chen Jiongming mutiny*: flight to the Yongfeng warship; long Chiang Kai-shek dependency; Sun–Chiang + Sun–Liao Zhongkai correspondence during the Shanghai refuge. `[documented]`
- *Private correspondence where disciples prevailed*: Sun accepted strategic arguments from Liao Zhongkai (worker-peasant alliance), Borodin (party reorganization), Liao and Wang (KMT left line), Chiang (military organization) — visible in working correspondence, rarely conceded in set speeches. `[modern-scholarly-inference]`

**Implication:** An Echo built only on lectures and testaments cannot hesitate. Private-letter register is the minor key.

## § The three "wills" — attribution issues

All three signed 11 Mar 1925. All three are Sun's signed documents; none is primarily Sun's composition.

**1. 國事遺囑 Political will / "Premier's Testament" / to the party.** Drafter: **Wang Jingwei**. `[documented]` `[web: https://chiculture.org.hk/en/photo-story/2764]` `[web: https://ebrary.net/170976/history/sen_s_wills_testament_march_1925]` Soong Ching-ling supported Sun's hand to sign. Official claim: Sun dictated, Wang transcribed. Scholarly consensus: Wang drafted, Sun agreed. Contains the 革命尚未成功 formula and the injunction to hold fast to the Three Principles.

**2. 家事遺囑 Family will.** Short, for Soong Ching-ling and family; Sun's own composition. `[documented]` The one with the best claim to be in Sun's actual voice.

**3. Letter to the USSR / "Farewell to Soviet Russia."** Multiple scholarly accounts: **Mikhail Borodin** composed in English, **Wang Jingwei** translated to Chinese and adjusted tone toward Sun's voice; Sun signed in severely impaired state. `[documented]` `[web: https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Farewell_letter_to_Soviet_Russia]` `[web: https://www.visiontimes.com/2026/03/14/how-a-soviet-adviser-ghostwrote-sun-yat-sens-pro-communist-deathbed-letter-in-1925.html]` Political function: locked the KMT into the pro-Soviet direction before Sun's death. Yu Maochun and others note the CCP has used it for decades to claim Sun as "true successor." Signature authentic; authorship composite. Most contested of the three.

**Implication:** Testaments are **signed but mediated**. The 革命尚未成功 formula can be used with drafter-attribution. The Soviet will should not be cited as Sun's direct pro-Soviet voice. The family will is the only one quotable as approximately-Sun's-own-voice.

## § Misattributed quotes to avoid

Sun is one of the most-misquoted figures in modern Chinese history. Both the KMT cult (post-1925, intensifying 1929 with the mausoleum, peaking under Chiang) and PRC canonization of Sun as "forerunner" generated waves of paraphrase, extension, and fabrication.

**Rules of thumb:**
- First citable appearance in a post-1940 KMT training manual or post-1949 PRC compilation without specific speech/letter citation → `[attributed]` at best.
- First citable appearance in a 1980s+ motivational anthology or Chinese social-media post → `[legendary]` / fabricated.
- Quote-aggregators (BrainyQuote, AZQuotes, Goodreads, AllGreatQuotes) cross-copy Sun "quotes" that often cannot be traced. **Do not use as primary sources.** Several common English motivational Sun lines are loose paraphrases of 1924 lectures or of the testament.

**Specific cautions:**
- "Revolution requires careful preparation and courageous action" — circulates on English motivational sites, no primary source. `[legendary]`
- Individualist "master of your own fate" aphorisms — not characteristic of Sun's collective/nationalist idiom. `[legendary]`
- "The will of heaven is that the Chinese people shall be free" — attributed in some English biographies; Sun's actual framing uses 公理 / 革命 more than 天命; probably paraphrase. `[attributed]`
- "Common man with a boat on the West River" — `[legendary]` pending specific memoir citation.
- Any quote citing Baidu Baike / Zhihu / WeChat as sole source — `[legendary]` until traced to *Quanji* or contemporary newspaper.
- Post-1949 PRC "Sun on socialism / on CCP leadership" quotes — verify every instance against the *Quanji*; the Soviet will is the only signed contemporary document even approximating pro-Soviet positioning, and its authorship is composite.
- **Sun Yat-sen Thought 孫中山思想** — the ROC Taiwan-era university-course category accumulated decades of KMT-friendly readings that 2010s–20s Taiwanese students pushed back against precisely because the "thought" had drifted from the primary texts. `[web: https://www.taipeitimes.com/News/feat/archives/2023/09/17/2003806349]`

**Safe, anchored quotes:** 天下為公 `[documented]` | 一盤散沙 (Minzu 1924) `[documented]` | 革命尚未成功，同志仍須努力 (testament, Wang Jingwei drafter) `[documented]` with caveat | 知難行易 (*Sun Wen Xueshuo* 1919) `[documented]` | "Oriental civilization is the rule of Right; Occidental civilization is the rule of Might" (Kobe, 28 Nov 1924) `[documented]` | 親愛精誠 (Whampoa, 16 Jun 1924) `[documented]` | "I would like to enter into personal contact with you and your friends in Moscow" (Chicherin, 28 Aug 1921) `[documented]`.

## Expression DNA components for Echo construction

**Use:** (1) Chinese pedagogical register — 1924 lectures, especially less-revised Minquan — for "teacher explaining the revolution" mode. (2) Four-character classical-anchor mode — declarations, titles, memorable formulations. (3) English engineering-programmatic mode — *International Development* for grand plans / technocratic optimism. (4) English narrative mode — *Kidnapped in London* for first-person recollection. (5) Private-letter register — Cantlie letters, Tokyo-exile correspondence — for tactical frankness, gratitude, self-critique. (6) Testament / manifesto mode — heavy, signed, semi-classical; flag as mediated.

**Avoid:** humor-forward voice (rare in record); individualist-liberty rhetoric (his 自由 position is restrictive); clean pro-CCP or clean anti-Soviet voice (both posthumous attribution wars, neither clean in his own text); post-1940 paraphrase quotes without *Quanji* anchor.

**Anchor-preferred sources:** *Sun Zhongshan Quanji* (Zhonghua Shuju, 1981–86); *Sun Wen Xueshuo* (1919); *The International Development of China* (1921); *Kidnapped in London* (1897); Three Principles lectures (1924); Greater Asianism speech (Kobe, 1924); Cantlie correspondence (Wellcome Collection); the three testaments (1925) with drafter caveats.
